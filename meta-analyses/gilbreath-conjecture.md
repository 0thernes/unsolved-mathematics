# Meta-Analysis: Gilbreath's Conjecture

> **AI-generated meta-analysis — not peer-reviewed.** Curated and accredited by **Alexander Donahue** (human-in-the-loop). A survey/assessment of an open problem; it makes no claim of a new result. Citations carry verification flags and require human source-checking.

## Abstract

Gilbreath's conjecture concerns the infinite triangular array obtained by writing the primes $2,3,5,7,11,\dots$ as a top row and repeatedly taking absolute differences of consecutive entries. The conjecture asserts that the first entry of every row below the top is $1$. Stated by François Proth in 1878 with a proof now judged invalid, and independently rediscovered by Norman L. Gilbreath around 1958, the problem is elementary to state yet remains open. This meta-analysis surveys its origin, the state of the art, the principal approaches, and the structural barriers to a proof. The decisive rigorous result is Andrew Odlyzko's 1993 reduction of the statement to a finite, bounded-width "persistence" check, verified for all primes up to $10^{13}$. Generic-sequence theorems (Cairns and others) show the leading-$1$ law is a robust combinatorial phenomenon depending only weakly on the primes' deep arithmetic. The obstruction to a full proof is the parity barrier: a proof must control the low-order binary structure of prime gaps uniformly, which lies beyond current analytic number theory. The consensus, echoing Erdős, is that the conjecture is almost certainly true but far from provable by present means.

## 1. Statement and significance

Define $d_0(n) = p_{n+1}$, the sequence of primes, and recursively $d_{k+1}(n) = |d_k(n+1) - d_k(n)|$. Gilbreath's conjecture is the assertion that $d_k(0) = 1$ for every $k \ge 1$. The first difference row is $1,2,2,4,2,4,2,4,6,\dots$; the next is $1,0,2,2,2,2,\dots$; and the leading $1$ persists at every observed depth.

The significance is twofold. First, iterated differencing is the classical tool for exposing latent regularity: for a degree-$d$ polynomial the $d$-th differences are constant, so the survival of a fixed $1$ in the leading column of the highly irregular prime sequence signals a subtle constraint on prime gaps. Second, the conjecture is a clean specimen of a "true but unprovable-by-current-means" elementary statement: trivial to formulate, overwhelmingly supported numerically, reducible to a finite check, yet resistant to proof. It sits alongside Goldbach, twin primes, and Polignac as a prime-gap problem whose difficulty is concentrated in parity information.

## 2. State of the art

The conjecture is **open**; no accepted proof exists. What is rigorously known divides into three strands.

**Massive verification.** Odlyzko (1993) confirmed the leading-$1$ property for the difference triangle generated by all primes up to $10^{13}$ — over $3 \times 10^{11}$ primes — to depths vastly exceeding any plausible counterexample. The earlier Gilbreath–Killgrove–Ralston study (1959) was among the first computer-assisted verifications and established the empirical credibility that motivated all later work.

**A finite sufficiency criterion.** Odlyzko proved that, up to a given prime bound, the conjecture reduces to checking a bounded-width, bounded-depth condition: once a difference row consists only of $0$s and $2$s within a window and begins with $2$, the leading-$1$ property propagates downward indefinitely. This converts an infinite statement into a finite verification on each range.

**Generic-sequence theorems.** Cairns (2008), Stoll, and others proved that Gilbreath-type laws hold for broad classes of sequences whose gaps are bounded and equidistributed modulo small powers of $2$, demonstrating the phenomenon is structural rather than special to the primes.

No nontrivial conditional theorem is known — not even one assuming the Riemann Hypothesis — that closes the problem.

## 3. Principal approaches and barriers

**Odlyzko's persistence method.** The dominant rigorous line. Its strength is the reduction to a self-sustaining $\{0,2\}$ structure; its limitation is that the criterion is sufficient but finite-range, certifying the pattern only up to the largest prime checked. Closing the conjecture demands a *uniform* guarantee that the $\{0,2\}$ structure never breaks for arbitrarily large primes.

**The "$0$s and $2$s" / parity-of-gaps heuristic.** After the first row, all gaps among odd primes are even; the residual content is whether they are $\equiv 0$ or $\equiv 2 \pmod 4$, and so on down the $2$-adic tower. Modeling these low-order bits as effectively random yields the Gilbreath law with probability $1$. The barrier is that the primes are not random: making this rigorous requires equidistribution of prime gaps modulo powers of $2$ that is out of reach. This is the **parity barrier** — sieve and elementary methods are structurally blind to the mod-$2$ information the conjecture lives on.

**Generalization to Gilbreath-type sequences.** Productive but non-transferable: the hypotheses (bounded, equidistributed gaps) are, for the primes specifically, theorems of comparable depth to what is sought.

**Direct analytic attack.** Post Zhang–Maynard–Tao bounded-gaps technology controls *small* gaps but not the fine parity structure of *all* gaps simultaneously, which is what Gilbreath requires across the entire infinite triangle.

## 4. Critical assessment

The dossier's framing is sound and matches the literature: the difficulty is not a missing trick but a missing theory of prime-gap parity. Three points warrant scrutiny. First, the headline verification bound of "$10^{13}$" and "$3.4 \times 10^{11}$ primes" recurs across the dossier and is attributed to Odlyzko (1993); the magnitude is plausible and widely cited, but the exact bound and the precise statement of Odlyzko's sufficiency theorem should be confirmed against the *Mathematics of Computation* paper rather than restated from secondary summaries. Second, the attribution of a reformulation jointly to "Hayes" and "Odlyzko" in the history file is vaguer than the rest and should be checked. Third, the claim that "no conditional theorem (even under RH) is known to imply Gilbreath" is consistent with the survey literature but is an absence-of-evidence statement that a referee cannot independently confirm without a literature sweep.

On balance the document neither overstates what is proved nor understates the obstruction. The reliance on Erdős's "200 years away" remark is appropriate as rhetoric but should not be mistaken for a quantitative estimate.

## 5. What a resolution would require / open directions

A proof must show that the $\{0,2\}$ structure of the difference rows, once established, never breaks for arbitrarily large primes — a uniform statement about the parity of prime gaps modulo $2$, then $4$, $8$, and so on, holding for the entire infinite triangle at once. Three plausible routes:

1. **Make the reduction uniform.** Prove Odlyzko's finite window has *provably bounded width at all heights*, converting per-range verification into a single argument. The window's empirical stability is encouraging but unproven.
2. **Transfer generic-sequence results to the primes.** Establish the equidistribution-of-gaps hypotheses used by Cairns-type theorems for the primes specifically — itself a problem of comparable depth.
3. **New analytic control of gap parity.** Develop methods that see past the parity barrier — a long shot, but the only route addressing the true obstruction.
