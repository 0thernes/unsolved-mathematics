# Top Mathematicians — Grimm's Conjecture

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Carl Albert Grimm | 1960s | originator | Posed the conjecture in a 1969 *American Mathematical Monthly* note: each member of a run of consecutive composites should receive a distinct prime divisor. |
| 2 | Paul Erdős | 1970s–1990s | breakthrough | With Selfridge, reframed the problem via distinct-prime-factor counting, linked it to prime gaps, and proved foundational partial results on prime factors of consecutive integers. |
| 3 | John L. Selfridge | 1970s | breakthrough | Co-author of the decisive 1971 analysis exposing the conjecture's connection to prime-gap bounds and the matching reformulation. |
| 4 | K. Ramachandra | 1970s | breakthrough | Co-author of the 1976 paper proving Grimm's Conjecture for short blocks; brought analytic and transcendence-flavored estimates to bear. |
| 5 | Tarlok Nath Shorey | 1970s–present | breakthrough | Co-author of the 1976 benchmark result and a sustained contributor to prime-factor problems for blocks of consecutive integers; the central figure in the modern theory. |
| 6 | Robert Tijdeman | 1970s–present | breakthrough | Co-author of the 1976 result; with Shorey, built the broader framework (transcendence methods, Diophantine equations) in which Grimm-type problems are studied. |
| 7 | Carl Pomerance | 1980s–present | frontier | Sharpened bounds on distinct prime factors and smooth numbers in blocks of consecutive integers, controlling the quantities that govern Hall's condition. |
| 8 | Ronald L. Graham | 1970s–1980s | frontier | Contributed to Erdős-circle work on prime factors of consecutive integers and binomial coefficients adjacent to Grimm's problem. |
| 9 | Richard K. Guy | 1980s–2000s | expositor | Catalogued and popularized the conjecture as problem B32 across editions of *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory*, fixing it in the open-problems canon. |
| 10 | Andrew Granville | 1990s–present | frontier | Through work on smooth numbers in short intervals and the multiplicative structure of consecutive integers, supplied tools relevant to the smooth-number obstruction in Grimm-type problems. |

## Notes

The active community around Grimm's Conjecture is small and concentrated in analytic and Diophantine number theory, descending directly from the Erdős–Selfridge and Shorey–Tijdeman schools. There is no large research group dedicated to the problem in isolation; rather, it is studied as one node in a network of questions about the prime factorization of blocks of consecutive integers, smooth numbers in short intervals, and prime gaps. Progress is gated by these neighboring areas — particularly by what can be proved about prime spacing and about smooth numbers in short intervals — so the people who move the problem tend to be those advancing those adjacent fields rather than specialists in Grimm's Conjecture per se. T. N. Shorey and collaborators remain the closest thing to a standing center of expertise.
