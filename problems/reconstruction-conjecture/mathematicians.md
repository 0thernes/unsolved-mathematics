# Top Mathematicians — The Reconstruction Conjecture (Ulam)

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Stanisław Ulam | 1940s–1960s | originator | Posed the underlying question and broadcast it in *A Collection of Mathematical Problems* (1960). |
| 2 | Paul J. Kelly | 1940s–1950s | originator | Gave the precise graph formulation, proved trees reconstructible, and established **Kelly's Lemma**, the field's core counting tool. |
| 3 | Frank Harary | 1960s | originator | Coined the modern vocabulary (deck, card, reconstructible) and stated the **Edge Reconstruction Conjecture** (1964). |
| 4 | László Lovász | 1970s | breakthrough | First strong edge-reconstruction bound ($m > \binom{n}{2}/2$), opening the dense-graph attack. |
| 5 | Vladimír Müller | 1970s | breakthrough | Sharpened the edge bound to $2^{m-1} > n!$, settling essentially all dense graphs. |
| 6 | W. T. Tutte | 1970s | breakthrough | Showed characteristic, chromatic, and rank polynomials are reconstructible; deep counting/algebraic structure. |
| 7 | Paul K. Stockmeyer | 1970s | breakthrough | Constructed non-reconstructible **tournaments/digraphs**, refuting the directed analogue and constraining all proof strategies. |
| 8 | Béla Bollobás | 1980s–1990s | breakthrough | Proved **almost every graph is reconstructible** with reconstruction number three. |
| 9 | J. A. Bondy | 1970s–1990s | expositor | Co-authored "All the king's horses" and the standard survey **"A graph reconstructor's manual"**; key recognizability results. |
| 10 | Brendan D. McKay | 1990s–present | frontier | Computational verification of all small graphs (up to and beyond $n = 11$) via the **nauty** software. |

## Notes

The active community is modest but persistent, clustered in **structural and algorithmic graph theory** and overlapping with combinatorialists who work on graph isomorphism, extremal graph theory, and graph polynomials. Foundational figures (Kelly, Harary, Tutte, Nash-Williams, Lovász) are deceased or long retired, but the problem remains a fixture of survey literature and open-problem lists (Bondy–Murty's textbook, the *Open Problem Garden*, and combinatorics conference problem sessions). Contemporary activity is split among three threads: (i) **computational** verification and reconstruction-number census work, where Brendan McKay's tooling is dominant; (ii) **structural** extensions to new graph classes and to digraph/hypergraph/infinite variants, where the negative results sharpen what is provable; and (iii) **claimed proofs**, mostly circulated as preprints and so far not community-accepted. There is no large research group "racing" to a solution; rather, the conjecture persists as a famous, well-defined, and stubborn benchmark that periodically draws fresh attention.
