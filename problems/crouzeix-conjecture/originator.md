# Originator(s) — Crouzeix's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

**Michel Crouzeix** is a French mathematician whose career has centered on numerical analysis, the theory of matrix and operator functions, and the approximation of solutions to differential equations. He was for decades a professor at the **Université de Rennes 1** (in the Institut de Recherche Mathématique de Rennes, IRMAR), where he became one of the leading figures of the French school of numerical analysis. His name is attached not only to the conjecture but to widely used results on the stability of numerical methods, including foundational work on **Runge–Kutta methods**, $A$-stability, and the analysis of stiff problems and analytic semigroups.

**Mathematical background.** Crouzeix's research grew out of the practical demands of computing functions of matrices and operators: when one approximates $e^{tA}$, resolvents $(zI-A)^{-1}$, or rational functions of a discretization matrix $A$, one needs *uniform* bounds on $\|f(A)\|$ that do not blow up with the dimension or the conditioning of the eigenvector basis. For normal matrices the spectral theorem gives $\|f(A)\| = \max_{z\in\sigma(A)}|f(z)|$, but for non-normal $A$ — exactly the case arising in stiff ODE solvers, convection–diffusion discretizations, and non-self-adjoint operators — the spectrum alone is a poor predictor of behavior. The eigenvalues can sit deep inside the stable region while $\|f(A)\|$ is enormous, the phenomenon of **non-normality** and transient growth studied via pseudospectra (Trefethen, Embree) and via the numerical range.

**The idea behind the conjecture.** Crouzeix's insight was that the numerical range $W(A)$ — a convex set strictly larger than the spectrum — is the *right* domain on which to measure $f$. The numerical range already controls the resolvent: $\|(zI-A)^{-1}\| \le 1/\operatorname{dist}(z, W(A))$ for $z\notin W(A)$. This single estimate, integrated against a Cauchy kernel, yields a functional-calculus bound $\|f(A)\| \le C\,\|f\|_{W(A)}$ with *some* constant. The deep question is how small $C$ can be made *universally*. Crouzeix recognized that the answer should be a pure absolute constant, and the family of $2\times 2$ matrices $\begin{psmallmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{psmallmatrix}$-type examples, whose numerical range is a disc, shows the constant cannot be smaller than $2$. He conjectured that this lower bound is also the truth: the sharp universal constant is exactly $2$.

This positions the conjecture as a refinement of von Neumann's spectral-set inequality, transplanted from the unit disc (where the relevant quantity is $\|A\|$) to the convex hull of the field of values. It is a statement about a **complete** $K$-spectral-set constant: by tensoring with matrix-valued functions, the conjectured bound is expected to hold in the completely bounded sense as well, tying the problem to dilation theory and the geometry of operator algebras.

**Broader legacy.** Crouzeix's work bridges hard analysis and computational practice. His resolvent and numerical-range estimates underpin rigorous error analysis for exponential integrators, contour-integral methods for matrix functions, and the convergence theory of Krylov subspace and rational-approximation algorithms (GMRES, Faber series, the Faber–Crouzeix circle of ideas). The 2007 explicit constant $11.08$ and the 2017 Crouzeix–Palencia constant $1+\sqrt 2$ are now standard reference points in numerical linear algebra. Beyond the named conjecture, Crouzeix shaped how a generation of analysts thinks about quantifying non-normality, and his program continues to motivate research in operator theory, complex approximation, and the rigorous foundations of scientific computing.
