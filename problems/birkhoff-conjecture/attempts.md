# Attempts — The Birkhoff Conjecture (Integrable Billiards)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Lazutkin's caustics (1973) — a clarifying near-miss

Vladimir Lazutkin proved that every smooth strictly convex billiard carries a positive-measure family of caustics accumulating on the boundary, via a KAM construction with the now-standard "Lazutkin coordinates." This was initially read by some as evidence that integrability might be common; it instead **sharpened** the conjecture by showing that abundant caustics exist universally, so Birkhoff's claim must concern the *complete* foliation (no gaps). The gaps are exactly where Aubry–Mather theory and Mather's destruction-of-invariant-curves results live, and closing them is the crux that no attempt has yet overcome in general.

## Bialy's global rigidity (1993) — settled the strongest form

Misha Bialy's Hopf-type theorem — global foliation by invariant curves forces the disk — is a genuine, accepted resolution of the *total* integrability version. It is a near-miss for the main problem only because its hypothesis (foliation of the entire phase cylinder) is much stronger than Birkhoff–Poritsky local integrability. Attempts to weaken the hypothesis to a one-sided neighborhood of the boundary have not succeeded; the integral inequality used loses control there.

## The Avila–De Simoi–Kaloshin and Kaloshin–Sorrentino theorems (2016–2018) — the major partial results

These are the landmark *positive* partial results, not disputed. Avila, De Simoi, and Kaloshin proved local rigidity for rationally integrable perturbations of low-eccentricity ellipses; Kaloshin and Sorrentino proved local Birkhoff rigidity for deformations of generic ellipses. Their methods (deformation theory, Mather's $\beta$-function, the action spectrum, and delicate Fourier analysis) are widely regarded as the state of the art. The honest caveat — stated plainly by the authors — is that all are *perturbative* and *local around conics*; they do not approach the global conjecture.

## Polynomial-integral program (Bialy–Mironov) — accepted, but a different hypothesis

Bialy and Mironov's resolution of the conjecture for integrals polynomial in momenta is a substantial, accepted body of work. The recurring point of scholarly caution is logical, not a dispute over correctness: a general integrable billiard is not known to admit a polynomial integral, so these theorems do not close the analytic conjecture. Their more recent announcements on the centrally symmetric ("rational") case have circulated as preprints; as with any frontier work, independent verification of the full argument is ongoing and should be cited as such.

## Status of claimed full proofs

To date there is **no accepted proof of the general Birkhoff conjecture**, and the dossier is aware of **no widely publicized retracted claim of a complete proof** in the refereed literature. The field's culture has been careful: results are stated with explicit hypotheses (global vs. local, polynomial vs. analytic, near-ellipse vs. arbitrary), so the usual pattern of a dramatic claimed proof followed by withdrawal has largely been avoided. The honest summary is that the problem has been *advanced incrementally and reliably* rather than punctuated by disputed announcements.

## Confusable problems (avoiding false attributions)

Two adjacent results are sometimes mis-cited as "solving Birkhoff." First, **Treschev's** investigations into formally integrable billiards with non-conic boundary (numerical/asymptotic evidence for analytic but non-elliptic local integrability of a degenerate map) concern a *different* setup and do not contradict the convex conjecture. Second, results on **outer (dual) billiards** and on **polygonal** or **non-convex** tables are separate problem classes. Care should be taken not to present any of these as progress on the strictly convex Birkhoff problem itself.
