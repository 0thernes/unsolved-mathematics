# Originator(s) — The abc Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The abc conjecture carries two names by convention — **Joseph Oesterlé** and **David Masser** — reflecting a near-simultaneous formulation around 1985 from two complementary vantage points: Oesterlé's from the theory of elliptic curves and the Szpiro conjecture, Masser's from transcendence theory and the function-field analogy. The pairing is itself a small lesson in how conjectures crystallize: not from a single flash but from a shared atmosphere of analogies whose time has come.

## Joseph Oesterlé

Joseph Oesterlé (born 1954) is a French number theorist, long associated with the Université Pierre et Marie Curie (Paris VI) and the Institut de Mathématiques de Jussieu. Trained in the elite tradition of French arithmetic geometry, he is known for deep work on modular forms, the class-number problem (the **Gross–Zagier–Oesterlé** resolution of the imaginary quadratic class-number-one and -two problems), and on Bernoulli numbers. Oesterlé arrived at abc while reflecting on **Lucien Szpiro's conjecture**, which bounds the minimal discriminant of an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ in terms of its conductor. Translating Szpiro's inequality through the Frey curve $y^2 = x(x-a)(x+b)$ attached to an abc triple, Oesterlé saw that a uniform discriminant–conductor bound was tantamount to an inequality between $c$ and $\mathrm{rad}(abc)$. He presented this circle of ideas in the Séminaire Bourbaki orbit, where the conjecture's potency — its capacity to encode Fermat, Mordell, and Roth at once — became visible. Oesterlé's instinct was structural: abc was, to him, the arithmetic content of a height inequality already glimpsed in the geometry of elliptic curves.

## David Masser

David William Masser (born 1948) is a British mathematician, a leading figure in **transcendence theory** and Diophantine approximation, who spent much of his career at the University of Basel after positions in the UK. A student of Alan Baker — whose theory of linear forms in logarithms reshaped effective Diophantine analysis — Masser inherited a sharp feel for how the size of integers is constrained by their prime factorizations. He is celebrated for the **Masser–Wüstholz** isogeny theorem (with Gisbert Wüstholz), a cornerstone of the modern theory of heights on abelian varieties, and for foundational results on elliptic functions and unlikely intersections. Masser was struck by the **Mason–Stothers theorem**, the polynomial identity in which the degree of a sum is controlled by the number of distinct roots of the product. Pressing the function-field/number-field analogy — in which "number of distinct roots" corresponds to "radical" and "degree" to "logarithmic height" — he proposed the integer inequality in its now-standard clean form $c < K_\varepsilon\,\mathrm{rad}(abc)^{1+\varepsilon}$, including the crucial recognition that the $\varepsilon$ cannot be removed.

## The joint formulation and its legacy

The two formulations are equivalent in substance, and the community fused them. What makes the conjecture extraordinary is not its statement, which a strong undergraduate can grasp, but its reach: it linearizes a swath of Diophantine geometry, converting hard finiteness theorems into near-corollaries. Both originators went on to broader legacies — Oesterlé in modular and analytic number theory, Masser in transcendence and unlikely intersections (the Zilber–Pink program owes much to his school). Neither claimed the conjecture would be easy; both understood it as a deep prediction about the multiplicative–additive tension at the heart of the integers. That tension, articulated in 1985, remains unresolved and continues to organize a large part of arithmetic geometry around a single inequality.
