# History — The Cherlin–Zilber Algebraicity Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The conjecture sits at the confluence of two traditions that converged in the late 1970s: the model theory of $\omega$-stable and totally categorical structures, and the structure theory of groups defined inside such structures. Its modern statement is:

> Every infinite simple group of finite Morley rank is isomorphic to an algebraic group over an algebraically closed field.

Morley rank is the model-theoretic notion of dimension introduced through Michael Morley's 1965 categoricity theorem. A group of finite Morley rank ($\mathrm{fMr}$) is a group equipped with a rank function on its definable sets behaving like a Noetherian dimension. The motivating fact is that any algebraic group over an algebraically closed field $K$ is a group of finite Morley rank, the rank coinciding with the algebraic dimension. The conjecture asserts the converse for the simple case: rank-theoretic simplicity should force algebraicity.

The two originators arrived independently. Boris Zilber, working in Kemerovo and Novosibirsk in the Soviet tradition of stability theory, was led to the question through his analysis of uncountably categorical structures and the "ladder" or "envelope" phenomena governing their coordinatizing groups. Gregory Cherlin, in the West, isolated the algebraicity question in his 1979 paper on groups of small Morley rank, classifying connected groups of rank $1$, $2$, and (partially) $3$. The synthesis of their viewpoints produced what is now the Cherlin–Zilber, or "Algebraicity," Conjecture.

A decisive strategic reframing came in the 1990s from the Borovik program, named for Alexandre Borovik, who proposed importing the methods of the Classification of the Finite Simple Groups (CFSG). One distinguishes groups by the structure of their $2$-Sylow subgroups into four types — even, odd, mixed, and degenerate — and attacks each separately. The even-type case, where the connected component of a Sylow $2$-subgroup is a nontrivial $2$-unipotent (elementary abelian) group, was brought to a successful conclusion: simple groups of even type were identified as Chevalley groups over algebraically closed fields of characteristic $2$. This is the principal unconditional triumph of the program.

### Timeline

- **1965** — Morley proves his categoricity theorem, introducing Morley rank.
- **1979** — Cherlin publishes "Groups of small Morley rank," classifying connected fMr groups of rank $\le 2$ and isolating the algebraicity question; Zilber's contemporaneous work on $\aleph_1$-categorical structures supplies the parallel formulation.
- **1980s** — Zilber and Cherlin develop the geometric and field-recovery machinery (Zilber's field theorem; indecomposability/Zilber's theorem analogue of generation).
- **1994** — Borovik proposes the "$2$-and-$3$" or finite-group-theoretic program; Borovik and Nesin's book "Groups of Finite Morley Rank" (1994, Oxford) becomes the field's standard reference.
- **1990s–2000s** — Systematic Sylow-type theory: Borovik–Poizat axiomatics, classification by $2$-Sylow type into even, odd, mixed, degenerate.
- **2008** — Altınel, Borovik, and Cherlin complete the classification of simple groups of **even type** in their monograph "Simple Groups of Finite Morley Rank" (AMS), the program's flagship unconditional result.
- **2008–2010s** — Mixed type is essentially eliminated; work concentrates on odd type (notably via Frécon, Deloro, Burdges, Cherlin) and the recalcitrant **degenerate type** (no involutions / trivial Sylow $2$-subgroup).
- **2008** — Borovik and Burdges/Cherlin show degenerate-type groups have no involutions, ruling out one feared pathology but leaving open whether they exist at all (the "bad group" question).
- **2010s–present** — Frontier work on small odd-type groups, "generic" identification theorems, and tameness hypotheses; Frécon's 2018 result that no "bad group" of Morley rank $3$ exists settles a long-standing low-rank test case. The full conjecture remains open.
