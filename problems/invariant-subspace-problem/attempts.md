# Attempts — The Invariant Subspace Problem

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

### The compact-operator chain (positive near-misses toward generality)

The earliest "attempts" were successful for restricted classes and repeatedly raised hopes of a general proof. Von Neumann's unpublished compact-operator theorem (c. 1935), the Aronszajn–Smith published version (1954), and the Bernstein–Robinson polynomially-compact result (1966) — proved with Abraham Robinson's non-standard analysis and immediately translated to standard language by Halmos — each looked like a step toward the full problem. **Lomonosov's theorem (1973)** was the most dramatic: its proof was so short and general that many expected it, or a refinement, to settle everything. The Hadwin–Nordgren–Radjavi–Rosenthal example (1980) of an operator with no compact operator in its commutant showed the Lomonosov route is intrinsically bounded, ending that hope.

### The Banach-space resolution (a celebrated negative result)

**Per Enflo's counterexample** is the most famous near-miss in the *opposite* direction. Enflo announced a Banach-space operator with no non-trivial invariant subspace in the mid-1970s; the manuscript was notoriously long and difficult, and its refereeing took over a decade, finally appearing in *Acta Mathematica* in **1987**. Before publication, its correctness was itself a matter of community discussion — an instructive case of a hard construction taking years to be validated. **Charles Read** then produced dramatically simpler counterexamples (1984–1986), including an operator on $\ell^1$ and quasinilpotent examples, which were quickly checked and accepted. These results are not disputed; they definitively answer the Banach-space question in the negative while leaving Hilbert space open.

### The Hilbert-space frontier and the 2023 Enflo claim

For Hilbert space, the strongest accepted positive results stop well short of the general case: subnormal operators (Scott Brown 1978), contractions with spectrum meeting the unit circle (Brown–Chevreau–Pearcy 1988), and various hyponormal and perturbation classes. None has been extended to all bounded operators despite sustained effort.

The most prominent recent development is a **2023 preprint by Per Enflo** claiming a proof that *every* bounded operator on a separable Hilbert space has a non-trivial invariant subspace. The claim drew wide attention given Enflo's stature and his earlier Banach-space work in the same area. **The dispute, stated neutrally:** as of this writing the proof has **not** been peer-reviewed, published, or accepted by the operator-theory community. Experts have noted that the manuscript is intricate and that independent verification of its central construction has not been completed; no specific, agreed-upon error has been published, but neither has the proof been confirmed correct. The standard interpretation is therefore that the problem **remains open** and the claim is unverified. A separate 2023 preprint by another author also circulated; it likewise lacks accepted verification. Readers should treat both as claimed-but-unconfirmed until peer review concludes.

### Other partial advances often cited

- **Almost-invariant subspaces** (Androulakis, Popov, Tcaciuc, Troitsky, and collaborators, late 2000s onward): every operator on an infinite-dimensional Banach space has almost-invariant half-spaces. A genuine theorem, but "almost" cannot yet be upgraded to "invariant."
- **Quasitriangularity and Apostol–Foiaş–Voiculescu theory** (1970s–80s): deep structural results on approximation of operators that constrain where counterexamples could live, without producing invariant subspaces directly.

No retracted *published* proof of the general Hilbert-space case is on record; the pattern instead is a series of correct partial results and, recently, a high-profile unverified claim. The honest summary: the affirmative Hilbert-space statement has never been established to the community's satisfaction.
