# Originator(s) — The KPZ Universality Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture carries the names of three physicists whose 1986 collaboration produced one of the most cited papers in statistical physics. The "KPZ" of KPZ universality is **Mehran Kardar**, **Giorgio Parisi**, and **Yi-Cheng Zhang**.

**Mehran Kardar** (b. 1957, Tehran) is a statistical and biological physicist. Educated at Cambridge and MIT, where he earned his PhD in 1983 and has been a professor since, Kardar works on disordered systems, polymers, fluctuation-induced (Casimir) forces, and interface dynamics. In the mid-1980s he was studying directed paths in random media and the statistical mechanics of growing surfaces. The KPZ equation emerged from his effort to write down the simplest local, nonlinear, stochastic evolution consistent with the symmetries of a driven interface — most importantly the lateral-growth nonlinearity $(\partial_x h)^2$, which encodes that growth proceeds normal to the surface and breaks the up–down symmetry of the linear Edwards–Wilkinson model.

**Giorgio Parisi** (b. 1948, Rome) is among the most influential theoretical physicists of his generation, awarded the **2021 Nobel Prize in Physics** for discovering "the interplay of disorder and fluctuations in physical systems from atomic to planetary scales." His replica-symmetry-breaking solution of the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick spin glass reshaped the understanding of disordered systems; he also contributed deeply to QCD (the Altarelli–Parisi/DGLAP equations), multifractality, and stochastic quantization. His instinct for how disorder and nonlinearity conspire to produce anomalous scaling was decisive in the KPZ analysis.

**Yi-Cheng Zhang** (b. 1958) is a physicist who, after working at the Brookhaven and the field-theoretic side of statistical mechanics, later became a pioneer of econophysics and recommender-system / information-filtering theory at the University of Fribourg. In 1986 he and Kardar were the active drivers of the growth-model computations that Parisi's framework helped interpret.

**The idea and its motivation.** The trio's paper, *Dynamic Scaling of Growing Interfaces* (Physical Review Letters 56, 889, 1986), addressed a concrete puzzle: simulations of ballistic deposition and Eden growth showed interface roughening with exponents that did *not* match the exactly solvable linear (Edwards–Wilkinson) theory. KPZ proposed that the missing ingredient was the nonlinear term, and using a dynamic renormalization-group (RG) calculation borrowed from the theory of randomly stirred fluids (Forster–Nelson–Stephen), they derived $\chi=1/2$, $z=3/2$ in $1+1$ dimensions. The conceptual leap was *universality*: the precise microscopic rules should not matter; any growth model in the same symmetry class should flow to the same fixed point with the same exponents and, as later understood, the same limiting distributions.

**From physical root to modern conjecture.** What KPZ wrote in 1986 was a statement about *exponents* derived non-rigorously. The modern conjecture is far sharper and is a theorem-shaped object: it predicts not only $t^{1/3}$ fluctuations but the exact limit laws (Tracy–Widom $F_2$, $F_1$), the Airy processes, and a single universal scaling limit — the KPZ fixed point / directed landscape — to which all class members should converge. That refinement is the work of a later generation (Spohn, Prähofer, Johansson, Baik, Deift, Tracy, Widom, Corwin, Quastel, Hairer, and others), drawing on random matrix theory, integrable systems, and stochastic analysis that the original authors did not use.

**Legacy.** The 1986 paper is foundational across surface science, turbulence, and probability. Kardar's textbooks shaped a generation of statistical physicists; Parisi's broader oeuvre earned the Nobel; and the universality class they named is now a magnet for some of the deepest mathematics in contemporary probability, including a Fields Medal (Hairer, 2014) for the analytic theory needed to make their equation rigorous.
