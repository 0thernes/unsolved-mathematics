# Originator(s) — The Volume Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture has three originators in two stages: Rinat Kashaev posed the original geometric form, and Hitoshi Murakami and Jun Murakami recast it in terms of the colored Jones polynomial, giving the statement now studied.

## Rinat M. Kashaev

Rinat Mavlyavievich Kashaev (b. 1965) is a mathematical physicist trained in the Soviet/Russian school of integrable systems and quantum groups. He worked at the Steklov Institute and later settled at the University of Geneva, where he is a professor. His early research centered on the quantum dilogarithm, the Faddeev–Volkov and discrete integrable models, and the Yang–Baxter equation — the algebraic machinery of two-dimensional exactly solvable physics. The quantum dilogarithm, a special function satisfying a pentagon identity, became the cornerstone of his knot invariant.

Kashaev's motivation was not knot theory per se but the search for invariants from quantum field theory and the representation theory of non-compact / cyclic quantum groups. In a series of papers (1994–1995) he attached to a knot diagram a "quantum-dilogarithm" state sum $\langle K\rangle_N$, structurally a discretized Chern–Simons path integral. Computing it for the simplest hyperbolic knots, he noticed that $\frac{2\pi}{N}\log|\langle K\rangle_N|$ approached their hyperbolic volumes. In his 1997 note he elevated this numerical coincidence to a conjecture, explicitly invoking Thurston's volumes. This was a bold cross-disciplinary leap: it asserted that a quantum invariant, defined purely combinatorially, "knows" the hyperbolic geometry of the complement. Kashaev's broader legacy includes Teichmüller TQFT (with Andersen), quantum Teichmüller theory, and continued work on state-integral models — much of it aimed at the same volume/Chern–Simons asymptotics.

## Hitoshi Murakami and Jun Murakami

Hitoshi Murakami (b. 1958) and Jun Murakami (b. 1957) are Japanese topologists (not brothers, despite the shared surname and frequent collaboration) who work on quantum invariants of knots and $3$-manifolds. Hitoshi Murakami has held positions at Osaka, Tokyo Institute of Technology, and Tohoku University; Jun Murakami is a professor at Waseda University. Both came out of the quantum-topology boom following the Jones polynomial and the Reshetikhin–Turaev construction, with deep expertise in the colored Jones polynomial, the Kontsevich integral, and finite-type invariants.

Their 2001 paper "The colored Jones polynomials and the simplicial volume of a knot" (*Acta Mathematica*) supplied the conceptual bridge. They proved Kashaev's $\langle K\rangle_N$ is, up to normalization, the $N$-colored Jones polynomial evaluated at $q=e^{2\pi i/N}$. This had two consequences. First, it placed the conjecture within the well-developed theory of quantum invariants, opening it to skein-theoretic, $R$-matrix, and holonomicity techniques. Second, it suggested the right invariant on the geometric side is the *simplicial* (Gromov) volume, valid for all knots — not only hyperbolic ones — since the colored Jones is defined for every knot. They further proposed the complexified conjecture, where the limit recovers the complex volume $\mathrm{Vol}+i\,\mathrm{CS}$, foreshadowing the link to Chern–Simons theory and the $A$-polynomial later developed by Gukov.

## Historical root vs. modern formulation

The historical root is Kashaev's quantum-dilogarithm invariant and its geometric guess; the modern formulation is the Murakami–Murakami colored-Jones statement. They are provably equivalent for the original invariant, but the modern phrasing is preferred because (i) it uses a standard invariant computable by many methods, (ii) it generalizes naturally to other roots of unity, other Lie algebras, and to $3$-manifolds (Chen–Yang's Turaev–Viro version), and (iii) it connects to Chern–Simons theory, where the conjecture acquires a physical interpretation as the semiclassical limit of a quantum partition function.
