# Originator(s) — The Bombieri–Lang Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture carries two names because two mathematicians arrived at the same higher-dimensional generalization of Mordell from different directions — Bombieri through the analytic and effective study of points on surfaces, Lang through a sweeping structural philosophy linking hyperbolicity and Diophantine geometry.

## Enrico Bombieri (b. 1940)

Born in Milan, Bombieri was a prodigy who published on number theory before completing his doctorate at the University of Milan (1963). He held chairs in Pisa before moving to the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton in 1977, where he remained as a permanent member. In 1974 he received the **Fields Medal**, principally for work on the large sieve, the Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem, and his contributions to the theory of minimal surfaces (including a famous counterexample, with De Giorgi and Giusti, to the Bernstein problem). His range is exceptional, spanning analytic number theory, several complex variables, the geometry of numbers, and Diophantine approximation; he later gave an influential elementary reworking of Vojta's proof of the Mordell conjecture ("The Mordell conjecture revisited," 1990).

Bombieri's route to the conjecture was concrete. Working on surfaces of general type and effective bounds for points, he recognized around 1980 that the right higher-dimensional statement could not be finiteness but rather *Zariski non-density*: the points must collapse onto a proper subvariety. He presented this in a Chicago seminar but, characteristically, never wrote it up formally — so the published record of the conjecture is largely Lang's.

## Serge Lang (1927–2005)

Born in Saint-Germain-en-Laye, France, Lang emigrated to the United States as a teenager, earned his Ph.D. under Emil Artin at Princeton (1951), and spent most of his career at Columbia and then Yale. A member of the Bourbaki group and one of the most prolific textbook authors in mathematics, Lang shaped Diophantine geometry as a unified subject — indeed the term "Diophantine geometry" is essentially his. His 1962 book of that title, and his later *Fundamentals of Diophantine Geometry* (1983), set the agenda for generations.

Lang's contribution to the conjecture was philosophical and architectural. From the 1970s he advanced a web of conjectures asserting an equivalence between three notions: a variety being **of general type** (positivity of the canonical bundle), being **Mordellic** (rational points non-dense over every number field), and being **Kobayashi hyperbolic** (admitting no entire curves, i.e. no non-constant holomorphic map from $\mathbb{C}$). The "special set" — the union of images of non-trivial maps from group varieties — was Lang's device for isolating exactly the part of a variety where points and entire curves can accumulate. Outside it, he predicted finiteness and hyperbolicity in lockstep. This vision, transferring intuition between value-distribution theory (Nevanlinna) and arithmetic, became the organizing principle of the entire field and was the soil from which Vojta's conjectures grew.

## The two formulations

The **historical root** is Mordell's finiteness for curves and Lang's general-type/hyperbolicity program; Bombieri supplied the sharp surface-level reformulation in terms of Zariski density. The **modern formulation** — "$X$ of general type over a number field has non-Zariski-dense rational points," together with Lang's exceptional-locus refinement — is the synthesis now universally attributed to both men. Vojta later showed the conjecture is a formal consequence of his height inequality, embedding it in an even larger structure.

## Legacy

Bombieri and Lang reframed Diophantine geometry as the study of how birational positivity governs arithmetic. Faltings's proof of Mordell, and his later proof of the Mordell–Lang conjecture for abelian varieties, realized fragments of the program; the full Bombieri–Lang conjecture remains one of the defining open problems of arithmetic geometry. Lang's relentless advocacy ensured the conjectures were written down, taught, and pursued; Bombieri's analytic instinct gave them their precise modern shape.
