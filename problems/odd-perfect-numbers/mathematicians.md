# Top Mathematicians — Existence of Odd Perfect Numbers

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Euclid | c. 300 BCE | originator | *Elements* IX.36: construction of even perfect numbers; defines the object whose odd analogue is in question. |
| 2 | Nicomachus of Gerasa | c. 100 CE | originator | First theory and catalogue of perfect numbers; crystallized the implicit "all perfect numbers are even" conjecture. |
| 3 | Leonhard Euler | 18th c. | breakthrough | Proved the converse of Euclid and derived the Euler form $p^\alpha m^2$, $p\equiv\alpha\equiv1\pmod4$, for any OPN — the structural basis of all modern work. |
| 4 | James Joseph Sylvester | 19th c. | breakthrough | Drove down feasibility via the "web of conditions"; early distinct-prime-factor bounds ($\omega \ge 5$). |
| 5 | Robert Daniel Carmichael | early 20th c. | breakthrough | Early systematic necessary conditions on OPNs and on $\sigma(n)=2n$. |
| 6 | Hans-Joachim Kanold | mid 20th c. | breakthrough | Rigorous structural theorems on the form of an OPN; corrected and extended earlier heuristic claims. |
| 7 | Peter Hagis Jr. | 20th c. | breakthrough | Proved $\omega(n)\ge 8$; bounds on largest and second-largest prime factors (with McDaniel). |
| 8 | Richard P. Brent / H. te Riele | late 20th c. | breakthrough | Large-scale computation establishing $n > 10^{300}$ (with G. L. Cohen). |
| 9 | Pace P. Nielsen | contemporary | frontier | $\omega(n)\ge 9$ (2007) then $\ge 10$ (2015); spoof-number finiteness; current frontier leader. |
| 10 | Pascal Ochem / Michaël Rao | contemporary | frontier | Proved $n > 10^{1500}$ and large component bounds (2012); modern computational frontier. |

Honorable mentions: **Douglas E. Iannucci** (second- and third-largest prime factor bounds), **Takeshi Goto & Yasuo Ohno** (largest prime factor $>10^8$), **Graeme L. Cohen** (abundancy-index methods, multiperfect numbers), **William L. McDaniel** (large-prime-factor results), **Carl Pomerance & Sergei Konyagin** (complementary upper-bound directions), and **Richard K. Guy** (whose *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory* curated the problem for generations).

## Notes

The active community is small, expert, and split between two complementary cultures: *structural number theorists* who tighten necessary conditions (distinct-prime-factor counts, special-prime constraints, cyclotomic/Diophantine reformulations) and *computational number theorists* who push magnitude and prime-factor lower bounds with verified factor-chain searches. Pace Nielsen (Brigham Young University) anchors the structural frontier and has mentored undergraduate research on spoof perfect numbers; Pascal Ochem and Michaël Rao represent the French computational school. Work circulates through *Mathematics of Computation*, *Integers*, and the *Journal of Number Theory*, and the problem features prominently in Richard Guy's problem collection and in OEIS/perfect-number reference compilations. There is broad consensus that no current technique can resolve existence outright; progress is incremental and the conjectured non-existence is widely believed but unproven.
