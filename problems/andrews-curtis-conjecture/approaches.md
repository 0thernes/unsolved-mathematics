# Approaches — The Andrews–Curtis Conjecture

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

Work on the conjecture splits into two opposed programs: attempts to **prove** AC for ever-larger classes of presentations (chiefly via computation and via topological reformulation), and attempts to **disprove** it by exhibiting a balanced presentation of $\{1\}$ that no sequence of AC-moves trivializes. No approach has resolved the problem in either direction; the candidate-counterexample strategy has repeatedly failed in the sense that every published candidate eventually fell.

## Topological reformulation (3-deformations of 2-complexes)

The core idea, due to Andrews and Curtis, is to translate the algebraic question into geometry: a balanced presentation of $\{1\}$ corresponds to a contractible (in the stable case, aspherical-quotient) finite $2$-complex, and AC-reducibility corresponds to $3$-deformability to a point. This reframes AC as a special case of the broader question of whether contractible $2$-complexes are collapsible after $3$-deformation, and links it to the **Zeeman conjecture** ($K \times I$ collapsible for contractible $2$-complexes $K$) and to questions in the Whitehead/Wall theory of simple homotopy. **Best result:** the reformulation is an exact equivalence and has clarified what a counterexample must look like (an "exotic" contractible complex). **Barrier:** it converts one hard problem into another equally hard one; Zeeman's conjecture is itself open, and the implication relationships among Zeeman, AC, and Poincaré-type statements are subtle and do not yield AC as a corollary of anything proved.

## Candidate counterexamples (Akbulut–Kirby and Miller–Schupp families)

The dominant disproof strategy isolates structured families of balanced presentations of $\{1\}$ that "look knotted" and conjectures they are not AC-trivial. The **Akbulut–Kirby** presentations $AK(n)=\langle x,y\mid x^n=y^{n+1},\,xyx=yxy\rangle$ ($n\ge 2$) arose from Dehn-surgery descriptions of homotopy $4$-spheres. The **Miller–Schupp** family $\langle x,y\mid x^{-1}y^n x = y^{n+1},\, x = w\rangle$ (with $w$ a word of zero exponent sum in $x$) is a second well-studied source. **Best result:** these families correctly identify the hardest small presentations and have driven all subsequent progress. **Barrier / negative outcome for the strategy:** the candidates keep falling. $AK(2)$ was shown AC-trivial; large initial segments of the Miller–Schupp family have been trivialized by search and reinforcement learning. Thus the very presentations proposed as counterexamples have instead become evidence *for* the conjecture — though $AK(3)$ remains stubbornly open, neither trivialized nor proven non-trivializable.

## Computational search (BFS, genetic, and Monte Carlo)

Because AC-moves generate a transformation graph, trivializing a presentation is a path-search problem. Approaches include breadth-first / iterative-deepening search over the move graph (Havas–Ramsay; Bowman–McCaul), genetic algorithms (A. D. Miasnikov, 1999), and large-scale heuristic search with length-bounded relators (Panteleev–Ushakov, 2019). **Best result:** explicit AC-trivializations of $AK(2)$ and of many Miller–Schupp presentations, plus exhaustive verification of AC-triviality for all sufficiently short balanced presentations. **Barrier:** the search space grows super-exponentially with relator length, and Bridson's lower bounds (below) show that even a *true* instance may require trivialization sequences and intermediate relator lengths far beyond any feasible search horizon. A failed search therefore can never certify non-triviality.

## Reinforcement learning and modern AI search

Since 2021, the path-search has been recast as a Markov decision process and attacked with deep reinforcement learning (policy/value networks, AlphaZero-style self-play, and proximal-policy-optimization agents). The work of Shehper, Halverson, and collaborators (and related independent efforts) resolved substantial new portions of the Miller–Schupp family that classical search could not reach. **Best result:** trivialization of many previously open Miller–Schupp instances and improved understanding of the "energy landscape" of AC-moves. **Barrier:** the same length-explosion obstruction applies; learned agents extend the feasible horizon but do not bound it, and they cannot prove a negative.

## Algebraic invariants and obstructions

A complementary program seeks computable invariants that are preserved by AC-moves and could certify non-triviality. Candidates have included representation-variety and group-ring (e.g. $K$-theoretic, Fox-calculus / Alexander-type) invariants, and the second homotopy module $\pi_2$. **Best result:** various necessary conditions for AC-equivalence and a sharper picture of what data the moves preserve. **Barrier — the central obstruction of the whole field:** *no invariant is known that distinguishes any balanced presentation of $\{1\}$ from the trivial one.* Many natural invariants (abelianization, Euler characteristic, Whitehead torsion in the relevant setting) vanish identically on balanced presentations of the trivial group, so they cannot separate a counterexample from the trivial presentation. This is the analogue, in this field, of a "barrier result": the absence of a separating invariant is exactly why a counterexample, if it exists, is so hard to *recognize*, and why disproof cannot proceed by invariant computation alone.

## Hardness and lower-bound results

Bridson (2015–2016) constructed balanced presentations that *are* AC-trivial but for which any trivialization must pass through relators of enormous length, giving non-elementary (tower-type) lower bounds on trivialization cost. **Significance:** this is a genuine negative-flavored theorem — it proves that brute-force feasibility is hopeless in general and that "we searched and found nothing" is evidentially weak. **Barrier:** it does not decide AC; it shows the decision problem, whatever its truth value, is computationally severe and that intuition calibrated on small cases is unreliable.
