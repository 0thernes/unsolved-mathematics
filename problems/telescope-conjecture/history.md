# History — The Telescope Conjecture (Disproved 2023)

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin and setting

The telescope conjecture lives inside **chromatic homotopy theory**, the program that organizes the stable homotopy category according to the "heights" $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ detected by the **Morava $K$-theories** $K(n)$ and the **Johnson–Wilson theories** $E(n)$. The chromatic filtration realizes the slogan that the stable homotopy category is assembled from layers, one per prime $p$ and per height $n$, with the $K(n)$-local categories as the simple "monochromatic" pieces.

Two natural localizations sit at each height. The **$K(n)$-localization** $L_{K(n)}$ is the Bousfield localization at Morava $K$-theory. The **finite (telescopic) localization** $L_n^f$ is built from the *telescope* $T(n) = v_n^{-1} F(n)$ of a type-$n$ finite complex $F(n)$: one inverts a $v_n$-self map and passes to the mapping telescope, an honest filtered colimit. There is always a natural comparison map $L_n^f X \to L_{K(n)} X$.

The **telescope conjecture** asserts that this comparison map is an equivalence for all $X$, equivalently that the localizing subcategory generated by $T(n)$ coincides with that generated by $K(n)$, i.e. that **$T(n)$ and $K(n)$ are Bousfield-equivalent**. The crux is the **$v_n$-periodic homotopy of finite complexes**: does the telescope $T(n)_*F(n)$ — a geometrically defined colimit — agree with its $K(n)$-local, more algebraically computable approximation? Heights $n = 0$ (rationalization) and $n = 1$ (classical, tied to $J$-homomorphism and image-of-$J$ computations) are true. The conjecture concerns $n \ge 2$.

## Formulation and reformulations

Ravenel introduced the statement in his 1984 *American Journal of Mathematics* paper "Localization with respect to certain periodic homology theories," where it appeared as one of a suite of conjectures (most of the others becoming the **Nilpotence** and **Periodicity** theorems of Devinatz–Hopkins–Smith and Hopkins–Smith). It was reframed categorically (smashing localizations, Bousfield lattices), arithmetically (via $v_n$-periodic homotopy and the $E(n)$-based Adams spectral sequence), and via **algebraic** models of the $K(n)$-local and telescopic categories. The "trace methods" reformulation — comparing telescopes to $\mathrm{TC}$ of suitable ring spectra — proved decisive.

## Timeline

- **1984** — Douglas Ravenel poses the telescope conjecture in "Localization with respect to certain periodic homology theories."
- **1988** — Devinatz–Hopkins–Smith prove the Nilpotence Theorem; Hopkins–Smith (1998 in print) prove the Periodicity Theorem, securing $v_n$-self maps and type-$n$ complexes.
- **1992** — Ravenel's "Nilpotence and Periodicity in Stable Homotopy Theory" (the "green book") frames the conjecture for a generation; Mahowald–Ravenel–Shick begin the $n=2$, $p=5$ program.
- **1990s–2000s** — Computations at $n=1$ confirm the conjecture there; Miller, Mahowald, and others develop the $v_1$- and $v_2$-periodic toolkit. Persistent numerical "discrepancies" are noted but inconclusive.
- **2001** — Mahowald–Ravenel–Shick, "The triple loop space approach to the telescope conjecture," lays out evidence and structure at height 2.
- **2014–2020** — Trace-methods technology matures: Hahn–Wilson on Lichtenbaum–Quillen for truncated Brown–Peterson spectra; redshift and chromatic Nullstellensatz lines (Hahn, Yuan, Burklund, Schlank, Levy) sharpen the comparison between telescopes and algebraic $K$-theory/$\mathrm{TC}$.
- **2023** — **Burklund, Hahn, Levy, and Schlank announce a disproof**: the telescope conjecture is **false for all heights $n \ge 2$** and all primes. Their argument detects the gap between $L_n^f$ and $L_{K(n)}$ through cyclotomic trace methods and the algebraic $K$-theory of $K(n)$-local rings, producing an unbounded discrepancy in $v_n$-periodic homotopy.
- **2023–present** — The frontier shifts to *measuring* the failure: the size and structure of the telescopic but not $K(n)$-local part, asymptotic growth of $v_n$-periodic homotopy, and consequences for the chromatic splitting conjecture and the systematics of the stable stems.
