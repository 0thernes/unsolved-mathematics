# Originator(s) — The Telescope Conjecture (Disproved 2023)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Douglas C. Ravenel

**Douglas Conrad Ravenel** (born 1947) is an American mathematician and one of the central architects of modern chromatic homotopy theory. He completed his Ph.D. at Brandeis University in 1972 under **Edgar H. Brown Jr.**, and after positions at Columbia and the University of Washington he has long been on the faculty at the **University of Rochester**, where he served as department chair. His name is attached to a remarkable cluster of conjectures that, between the mid-1970s and the late 1980s, reorganized stable homotopy theory around the "chromatic" picture.

### Background and the ideas behind the conjecture

Ravenel's vision grew out of the **Adams–Novikov spectral sequence** and the structure of the complex-cobordism Hopf algebroid $(MU_*, MU_*MU)$, where the formal-group-law stratification by height first becomes visible. With **Haynes Miller** and **W. Stephen Wilson**, Ravenel developed the *chromatic spectral sequence* (the Miller–Ravenel–Wilson "chromatic" paper of 1977), which decomposes the Adams–Novikov $E_2$-term into $v_n$-periodic layers and computed the periodic families it produces. That algebraic decomposition is the skeleton of the chromatic program.

In his 1984 paper Ravenel translated this algebra into a topological prediction about **localizations of spectra**. He distinguished two ways to isolate height-$n$ phenomena: the homologically defined $K(n)$- and $E(n)$-localizations, and the geometrically defined **telescope** obtained by inverting a $v_n$-self map on a finite complex and taking the mapping telescope. His telescope conjecture proposed that the two agree — that the concrete, computable colimit captures exactly the $K(n)$-local information. The motivation was both structural (it would make $L_n^f = L_n$ smashing localizations transparent) and computational (it would tame the $v_n$-periodic homotopy of finite complexes, the engine that produces the infinite periodic families in the stable stems).

Ravenel posed it as a conjecture, not a theorem, and over time grew genuinely skeptical: the height-2, prime-5 computations he pursued with **Mark Mahowald** and **Paul Shick** were designed in part to *test* it, and he repeatedly emphasized that the evidence was ambiguous. This honest agnosticism is part of the conjecture's legacy — it was never folklore-certain.

### The other Ravenel conjectures and the modern formulation

The telescope conjecture was the one "Ravenel conjecture" that resisted. The **Nilpotence Theorem** (Devinatz–Hopkins–Smith, 1988), the **Periodicity / thick subcategory** results, the **Smashing** and **localization** statements, and the **chromatic convergence theorem** were all proved, vindicating the chromatic philosophy. The telescope conjecture's modern formulation — Bousfield equivalence of $T(n)$ and $K(n)$, equivalently $L_n^f \xrightarrow{\sim} L_{K(n)}$ — is essentially Ravenel's original, sharpened by the language of localizing subcategories and trace methods. Its **2023 disproof** by Burklund–Hahn–Levy–Schlank closes the last of his conjectures, but in the negative, opening the quantitative study of how telescopic and $K(n)$-local homotopy diverge.

### Legacy

Ravenel's "green book" (*Nilpotence and Periodicity in Stable Homotopy Theory*, 1992) and "orange book" (*Complex Cobordism and Stable Homotopy Groups of Spheres*, 1986) trained a generation. The chromatic viewpoint he helped found now underlies redshift, $\mathrm{TMF}$, and the trace-methods revolution — the very tools that ultimately disproved his most stubborn conjecture.
