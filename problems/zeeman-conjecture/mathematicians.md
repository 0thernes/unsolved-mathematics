# Top Mathematicians — The Zeeman Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | E. C. Zeeman | 1960s | originator | Posed the conjecture in *On the dunce hat* (1964); identified $D$ as contractible non-collapsible and conjectured $K \times I$ collapsible. |
| 2 | J. H. C. Whitehead | 1930s–40s | originator | Founded simple-homotopy theory, collapses/expansions, and the theory of $2$-complexes underpinning the whole question. |
| 3 | J. J. Andrews | 1960s | breakthrough | Co-formulated the Andrews–Curtis conjecture, the statement ZC implies and the source of its candidate counterexamples. |
| 4 | M. L. Curtis | 1960s | breakthrough | Co-author of the Andrews–Curtis conjecture; framed balanced presentations of the trivial group. |
| 5 | Marshall M. Cohen | 1970s | expositor | Authored the standard reference on simple-homotopy theory, collapsibility and regular neighborhoods used throughout ZC work. |
| 6 | David Gillman | 1980s | breakthrough | With Rolfsen, proved the standard-spine reduction relating ZC to the Poincaré conjecture and verified families. |
| 7 | Dale Rolfsen | 1980s | breakthrough | Co-author of the Gillman–Rolfsen reduction; clarified the equivalence of standard-spine ZC with the general statement. |
| 8 | Wolfgang Metzler | 1980s–90s | expositor | Organized the two-dimensional homotopy / combinatorial group theory program linking ZC, AC and Whitehead asphericity. |
| 9 | Sergei Matveev | 1990s–2000s | frontier | Developed special-spine theory and complexity of $3$-manifolds, the natural setting for testing ZC on standard polyhedra. |
| 10 | Bruno Benedetti / Karim Adiprasito | 2010s | frontier | Proved collapsibility theorems for CAT(0) and non-positively curved complexes via discrete Morse theory, the modern toolkit adjacent to ZC. |

## Notes

The community working directly on the Zeeman conjecture is small and overlaps heavily with two larger groups: the **Andrews–Curtis / combinatorial group theory** community (Miasnikov, Havas, Ramsay, Bowman–McCaul, and more recently machine-learning teams attacking the $AK(n)$ presentations) and the **discrete Morse theory / PL combinatorics** community (Adiprasito, Benedetti and collaborators). Because ZC is logically stronger than AC, progress on ZC tends to come either as positive verification on special spines (the Matveev–Gillman–Rolfsen lineage) or as a byproduct of attempts to find AC counterexamples. There is no large dedicated "Zeeman conjecture" research program; rather, the problem functions as a high-value target that would fall out of, or be refuted by, advances on these neighboring fronts. Activity in the 2020s has been driven notably by reinforcement-learning and large-scale search efforts on Andrews–Curtis, keeping the candidate-counterexample question alive.
