# History — The Zeeman Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin and statement

The Zeeman conjecture (ZC) was posed by **E. C. Zeeman** in 1964, in his note *On the dunce hat* (Topology 2, 341–358). The dunce hat $D$ — the quotient of a solid triangle whose three edges are identified with mismatched orientations $aaa^{-1}$ — is the prototypical example Zeeman studied: it is contractible yet **not collapsible**. A complex is *collapsible* if it can be reduced to a point by a sequence of elementary collapses (removing a free face together with the unique cell containing it). The dunce hat shows that contractibility does not imply collapsibility for $2$-complexes. Zeeman observed, however, that thickening $D$ by one extra factor seems to repair this: $D \times [0,1]$ *is* collapsible.

This led to the general assertion now called the conjecture:

> **Zeeman conjecture.** For every contractible compact (polyhedral) $2$-complex $K$, the product $K \times [0,1]$ is collapsible.

Zeeman recognized at once the strategic weight of the statement. Because a collapsible complex is in particular **regular neighborhood–collapsible**, ZC for all contractible $2$-complexes *implies the $3$-dimensional Poincaré conjecture* — and Zeeman explicitly proposed it as a possible route to Poincaré. The same year, the **Andrews–Curtis conjecture** (AC, 1965) on balanced presentations of the trivial group was formulated in adjacent work; it was soon shown that ZC implies AC as well. Thus ZC sits *above* two hard problems: it is logically stronger than both.

## Reformulations

ZC admits a purely **combinatorial-group-theoretic** reformulation through the theory of standard (special spine) $2$-complexes and Bing's house with two rooms (another contractible, non-collapsible $2$-complex that nonetheless satisfies $H \times I$ collapsible). The implication chain is standard:
$$\text{ZC} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Poincaré}^3 \quad\text{and}\quad \text{ZC} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Andrews–Curtis}.$$
After Perelman (2003) settled the $3$-dimensional Poincaré conjecture, the first implication became vacuous as a *consequence* but remained important as a *constraint*: ZC is now known to be consistent with Poincaré, removing one possible source of a counterexample. AC, however, remains open, so ZC cannot currently be proven by reduction.

## Timeline

- **1964** — Zeeman publishes *On the dunce hat*; identifies $D$ as contractible but non-collapsible and conjectures $K \times I$ collapsible for all contractible $2$-complexes $K$.
- **1965** — Andrews and Curtis formulate their conjecture on trivializing balanced presentations; the link ZC $\Rightarrow$ AC is recognized.
- **1960s–70s** — Bing's "house with two rooms" studied as a second contractible non-collapsible example; verified that $H \times I$ collapses.
- **1980s** — Work on the "special/standard spine" formulation; ZC connected to the geometry of group presentations and to Whitehead's asphericity problem.
- **1985** — M. M. Cohen and others sharpen the regular-neighborhood and collapsibility framework relevant to ZC.
- **1990s** — Computer-assisted study of small balanced presentations (related to AC) probes potential counterexamples to ZC indirectly.
- **2003** — Perelman proves the Poincaré conjecture (and geometrization); the ZC $\Rightarrow$ Poincaré implication is now subsumed but does not resolve ZC.
- **2008** — A. Casali / O. Cretu and others examine ZC for restricted classes (e.g., special polyhedra, spines of low complexity).
- **2010s** — Adiprasito, Benedetti and collaborators connect collapsibility of products to discrete Morse theory and metric/PL combinatorics, giving collapsibility results for thickenings.
- **Present** — ZC remains **open** in full generality. It is verified for large families (standard spines, $K$ with collapsible $2$-skeleton structure, the dunce hat, Bing's house), but no proof for arbitrary contractible $2$-complexes and no counterexample are known.
