# Top Mathematicians — The Restriction Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Elias M. Stein | 1960s–2000s | originator | Posed the restriction problem (c. 1967); Stein–Tomas endpoint; oscillatory integral framework |
| 2 | Charles Fefferman | 1970s | breakthrough | Ball-multiplier counterexample (1971); first restriction theorems linking the problem to Besicovitch geometry |
| 3 | Peter A. Tomas | 1970s | breakthrough | The $L^2$ restriction theorem (1975), the canonical Stein–Tomas baseline |
| 4 | Robert S. Strichartz | 1970s | breakthrough | Restriction estimates for quadratic surfaces; the Strichartz estimates linking restriction to dispersive PDE |

## Notes

The restriction conjecture sits at the heart of a remarkably active, tightly knit community spanning harmonic analysis, geometric measure theory, additive combinatorics, and PDE. The intellectual lineage runs directly from **Stein's Princeton school** through **Bourgain** and **Wolff** — whose untimely deaths (Bourgain in 2018, Wolff in 2000) were major losses — to a vigorous current generation. Today's frontier is led by **Larry Guth** (MIT) and his collaborators, with **Hong Wang** (NYU Courant), **Joshua Zahl** (UBC), **Ruixiang Zhang** (Berkeley), **Jonathan Hickman**, **Keith Rogers**, **Ciprian Demeter** (Indiana), and **Po-Lam Yung**, **Shaoming Guo**, and others driving the decoupling and polynomial-method programs. The 2025 Wang–Zahl resolution of the three-dimensional Kakeya set conjecture has energized the field, since Kakeya is the principal geometric obstruction to restriction. Work is disseminated rapidly through arXiv, the MSRI/SLMath and IAS programs, and conferences such as the recurring meetings on Fourier restriction, decoupling, and the Kakeya problem; the community prizes explicit exponent records and the careful refereeing that has kept the literature free of disputed full-resolution claims.
