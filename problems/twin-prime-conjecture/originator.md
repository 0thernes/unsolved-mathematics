# Originator(s) — The Twin Prime Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Alphonse de Polignac (1826–1863)

The modern conjecture is conventionally credited to **Alphonse de Polignac**, a French mathematician born in 1826 into the aristocratic Polignac family (his father, Jules de Polignac, was a prime minister under Charles X). De Polignac studied at the École Polytechnique, the cradle of nineteenth-century French mathematical talent, and pursued number theory alongside a military career.

In **1849** de Polignac published the conjecture that now bears his name: *for every even integer $2k$, there are infinitely many pairs of consecutive primes whose difference is exactly $2k$.* The twin prime conjecture is the first and most famous instance, $2k=2$. De Polignac's general statement reflects a recurring nineteenth-century preoccupation — the apparent irregularity yet statistical regularity of the primes. Empirically, one observes that small even gaps (2, 4, 6) recur endlessly among the primes; de Polignac elevated that observation into a precise infinitude claim for each gap. He is also remembered for an erroneous companion assertion (that every odd number is the sum of a power of two and a prime, since refuted by counterexamples such as $127$), which is a reminder that he was a pattern-spotter rather than a builder of deep machinery. His mathematical legacy rests almost entirely on the gap conjecture, which has proven far more durable and consequential than its author's other work.

## Euclid and the ancient root

Metadata lists **Euclid** as an informal, antiquity-era originator, and the attribution deserves a careful gloss. Euclid (fl. c. 300 BCE), in *Elements* Book IX, Proposition 20, gave the celebrated proof that the primes are infinite. He did **not** state the twin prime conjecture; no surviving ancient text poses it. The connection is conceptual rather than documentary: the twin prime conjecture is the natural strengthening of Euclid's theorem from "infinitely many primes" to "infinitely many primes at minimal spacing." In this sense Euclid supplies the intellectual seed — the question of how primes are distributed once you know they never end — while de Polignac supplies the explicit formulation. Treating Euclid as the "informal" originator honors this lineage without overstating the historical record.

## From conjecture to its modern quantitative form

The version mathematicians actually work with today is sharper than de Polignac's qualitative claim. The **Hardy–Littlewood** formulation of 1923 predicts the exact asymptotic density of twin primes, $\pi_2(x)\sim 2C_2\int_2^x dt/(\ln t)^2$, derived heuristically from the circle method and a singular-series calculation. Thus there are two layers worth distinguishing: de Polignac's **existential** conjecture (infinitely many twins) and the Hardy–Littlewood **quantitative** conjecture (precisely how many). The existential statement is what remains formally "the twin prime conjecture"; the quantitative one is strictly stronger and would immediately imply it.

## Broader legacy

De Polignac's name endures less for his own theorems than for the question he crystallized. His conjecture sits at the head of a family — Polignac's conjecture, the Hardy–Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture, the bounded-gaps program — that has shaped a century of sieve theory and analytic number theory. The 2013–2014 bounded-gaps revolution (Zhang, Maynard, Tao, Polymath) proved a *uniform* version of Polignac's claim: some even number $2k \le 246$ occurs as a gap between consecutive primes infinitely often. That the field's most spectacular recent advances are framed as partial cases of de Polignac's 1849 statement is the truest measure of his influence.
