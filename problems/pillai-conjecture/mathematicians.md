# Top Mathematicians — Pillai's Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | S. S. Pillai | 1930s–40s | originator | Posed the conjecture; proved finiteness for fixed exponents and asymptotic solution counts for $a x^p - b y^q = k$. |
| 2 | Eugène Charles Catalan | 1844 | originator | Conjectured the $k=1$ case (consecutive perfect powers), the historical root Pillai generalized. |
| 3 | Alan Baker | 1960s–70s | breakthrough | Created the theory of linear forms in logarithms, the effective engine for all fixed-exponent results. |
| 4 | Robert Tijdeman | 1976 | breakthrough | Proved Catalan's equation $x^p - y^q = 1$ has effectively bounded solutions, a landmark for the $k=1$ case. |
| 5 | Tarlok N. Shorey | 1970s–80s | frontier | Co-author of *Exponential Diophantine Equations*; deep results on $S$-unit and power-gap problems. |
| 6 | Preda Mihăilescu | 2002–04 | breakthrough | Proved Catalan's conjecture via cyclotomic field theory, settling $k=1$ completely. |
| 7 | Michael A. Bennett | 2000s | frontier | Sharp bounds on the number of solutions of $|x^p - y^q| = k$ for fixed exponents; explicit Pillai-type results. |
| 8 | Maurice Mignotte | 1990s–2000s | frontier | Refined linear-forms-in-logarithms estimates enabling explicit resolution of Catalan- and Pillai-type equations. |
| 9 | Yann Bugeaud | 2000s | frontier | Modular + classical methods for exponential Diophantine equations; surveys connecting Pillai to $abc$. |
| 10 | Michel Waldschmidt | 2000s | expositor | Authoritative surveys ("Perfect powers: Pillai's works and their developments", "Open Diophantine problems") framing the conjecture and its $abc$ link. |

## Notes

The active community around Pillai's conjecture sits at the intersection of transcendence theory, Diophantine geometry, and computational number theory. The **transcendence school** descending from Baker — including Shorey, Tijdeman, Mignotte, Bugeaud, and Waldschmidt — supplies the effective linear-forms-in-logarithms toolkit that governs every fixed-exponent case. The **modular school** around Samir Siksek, Michael Bennett, Bugeaud, and Mignotte applies Frey-curve and level-lowering machinery (the Fermat-style approach) to resolve individual generalized-Fermat and Lebesgue–Nagell equations $x^p - y^q = k$. A vigorous **recurrence-sequence subcommunity** — Florian Luca and many collaborators (e.g. Mahadi Ddamulira, Carlos Gómez, and others) — studies "Pillai's problem" for Fibonacci, Tribonacci, and other linear recurrence sequences, producing a steady stream of papers. Finally, the **$abc$ community** keeps the conditional resolution in view: because $abc$ implies Pillai in full, progress (or lack thereof) on $abc$ directly bears on the conjecture. The unifying frontier — bounding the exponents for $k \ge 2$ unconditionally — remains untouched, and no single program currently threatens it.
