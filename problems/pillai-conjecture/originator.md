# Originator(s) — Pillai's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Subbayya Sivasankaranarayana Pillai (1901–1945)

**S. S. Pillai** was an Indian number theorist whose work, though cut short by an early death, left a durable mark on additive and multiplicative number theory. He was born on 5 April 1901 in Vallam, near Courtallam in the Tirunelveli district of Tamil Nadu. His early life was marked by hardship — he lost both parents young — but he secured scholarships that carried him through Scott Christian College in Nagercoil and then the University of Madras, where he came under the influence of the analysts K. Ananda Rau and S. Vaidyanathaswamy. He did not initially hold a permanent academic position; for several years he worked at the University of Madras on a research scholarship and later held posts at Annamalai University and the University of Travancore, eventually settling at Annamalai.

### Mathematical background and the path to the conjecture

Pillai is best remembered for two strands of work. The first is his solution, with R. Vaidyanathaswamy and independently of others, of problems in **Waring's problem**: he determined the value of $g(6) = 73$ and made fundamental contributions to the function $g(k)$ counting how many $k$-th powers are needed to represent every integer. This work placed him among the leading figures in Waring's problem in the 1930s, alongside Hardy, Littlewood, and Vinogradov.

The second strand — the one bearing his name as a conjecture — arose from his study of the equation
$$a x^p - b y^q = k.$$
Pillai's instinct was that perfect powers are sparse, and that two of them can be close together only sporadically. He proved that when the **exponents $p$ and $q$ are fixed**, the number of solutions is finite, and he obtained asymptotic counts in several configurations. From these fixed-exponent results he extrapolated the general conjecture: for each fixed difference $k$, only finitely many pairs of perfect powers (of any exponents $\ge 2$) differ by exactly $k$. The motivation was both aesthetic — a clean generalization of Catalan's 1844 conjecture — and structural, reflecting his belief that the multiplicative thinness of powers forces their gaps to grow.

### The historical root versus the modern formulation

The historical root is Catalan's conjecture (the $k = 1$ case, settled by Mihăilescu in 2002). Pillai's reformulation widens the difference to an arbitrary fixed $k \ge 1$ and, in its sharpest form, asks for the gaps between consecutive perfect powers to tend to infinity. The modern statement most often quoted — "$x^p - y^q = k$ has finitely many solutions with $p, q \ge 2$" — is faithful to Pillai's own conjectural statements, though he framed much of his published work with the more general coefficients $a, b$ and with fixed exponents where he could prove theorems.

### Legacy

Pillai died on 31 August 1945 in an air crash near Cairo while traveling to take up a visiting appointment at the **Institute for Advanced Study** in Princeton, at the invitation that recognized his stature. He was 44. His name survives in the **Pillai sequence**, in results on the **Jacobsthal function** and **Euler's totient** (Pillai's arithmetic function $P(n) = \sum \gcd(k,n)$), in Waring's problem, and above all in this conjecture, which remains open more than ninety years after he posed it. He is widely regarded as one of India's most important number theorists between Ramanujan and the postwar generation.
