# History — Grothendieck's Section Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The Section Conjecture is the most arithmetic of the prophecies in Alexander Grothendieck's anabelian program. Its setting is a smooth, projective, geometrically connected curve $X$ of genus $g \ge 2$ over a number field $k$. The étale fundamental group of $X$ fits into the **fundamental (homotopy) exact sequence**
$$1 \to \pi_1^{\mathrm{ét}}(X_{\bar k}) \to \pi_1^{\mathrm{ét}}(X) \to \mathrm{Gal}(\bar k / k) \to 1,$$
where the kernel $\pi_1^{\mathrm{ét}}(X_{\bar k})$ is the profinite completion of the topological surface group. Every $k$-rational point $x \in X(k)$ induces, via functoriality and a choice of base point, a section $s_x$ of this sequence, well-defined up to conjugation by the kernel. The conjecture asserts that this assignment
$$X(k) \;\longrightarrow\; \{\text{conjugacy classes of sections}\}$$
is a **bijection**: arithmetic (rational points) is fully encoded in group theory (splittings of a Galois extension). This is the "anabelian" creed in its sharpest finite form — that hyperbolic curves are reconstructible from their fundamental groups, including their points.

The genus $\ge 2$ hypothesis (more generally, hyperbolicity) is essential: for $\mathbb{P}^1$ or elliptic curves the statement fails, since these are not anabelian. There are several variants: the **birational** section conjecture (over function fields of curves), the **$p$-adic local** analogue, the **cuspidal** version for affine curves, and conjectures with restricted quotients (pro-$p$, metabelian, two-step nilpotent). A key subtlety is the existence of *packets* of sections at the boundary and the phenomenon of sections "at infinity" coming from real or $p$-adic places.

**Timeline**

- **1960s** — Grothendieck and collaborators develop the étale fundamental group (SGA 1) and the theory of the algebraic fundamental group, supplying the language for everything that follows.
- **1983** — Grothendieck writes his celebrated letter to Gerd Faltings (3 June 1983), articulating the anabelian conjectures and, within them, the section conjecture. This is the canonical origin date.
- **1984** — The *Esquisse d'un programme* circulates, embedding anabelian ideas in a broader vision of the Teichmüller tower and $\mathrm{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$.
- **1990** — Nakamura proves anabelian results for specific curves and studies Galois representations on fundamental groups, early evidence for the program.
- **1996** — Hiroaki Nakamura, Akio Tamagawa, and Shinichi Mochizuki establish the **Grothendieck anabelian (isomorphism) conjecture** for hyperbolic curves over sub-$p$-adic fields (Mochizuki's theorem), confirming the "$\pi_1$ knows the curve" half of the program — but *not* the section conjecture.
- **1990s–2000s** — Koenigsmann, Pop, and others develop local-global and birational techniques; the birational and $p$-adic local section conjectures become focal points.
- **2010** — Jakob Stix's work and the Bonn/Heidelberg school systematize obstructions; Stix shows curves with no points need not have sections only after subtle analysis (anabelian geometry of the boundary).
- **2010** — Esnault–Wittenberg connect sections to the Tate–Shafarevich group and finiteness questions.
- **2012** — Jochen Koenigsmann proves the **birational $p$-adic section conjecture** over $p$-adic fields, a landmark unconditional result.
- **2012** — Jakob Stix's monograph *Rational Points and Arithmetic of Fundamental Groups* consolidates the field.
- **2013** — Florian Pop establishes strong forms of the birational section conjecture over large/$p$-adically closed fields.
- **2015–2020s** — Saïdi, Tyler, Hoshi, and others push minimalist (pro-$p$, metabelian) and combinatorial versions; descent obstructions tied to $\Sha$ are clarified.
- **2020s (frontier)** — The full conjecture over number fields remains **open**. No counterexample and no proof exists; the strongest unconditional results are local and birational. Connections to the Lawrence–Venkatesh method and to obstruction-theoretic (Brauer–Manin, étale homotopy) refinements remain active.
