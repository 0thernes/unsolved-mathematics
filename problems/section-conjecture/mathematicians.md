# Top Mathematicians — Grothendieck's Section Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Alexander Grothendieck | 1960s–1980s | originator | Created the étale fundamental group (SGA 1); posed the anabelian and section conjectures in the 1983 letter to Faltings and the *Esquisse* (1984). |
| 2 | Shinichi Mochizuki | 1990s–present | breakthrough | Proved the anabelian isomorphism conjecture over sub-$p$-adic fields (1996); developed mono-anabelian reconstruction and the real section conjecture. |
| 3 | Akio Tamagawa | 1990s–present | breakthrough | Proved the profinite Grothendieck conjecture for affine and closed hyperbolic curves over number fields; deep results on $\pi_1$ in positive characteristic. |
| 4 | Hiroaki Nakamura | 1990s–present | breakthrough | Pioneered Galois rigidity of fundamental groups and braid groups; co-founder of the modern anabelian school. |
| 5 | Jochen Koenigsmann | 2000s–present | breakthrough | Proved the birational $p$-adic section conjecture (2005), a landmark unconditional result. |
| 6 | Florian Pop | 2000s–present | frontier | Established strong birational section results over large, $p$-adically and real closed fields; valuation-theoretic anabelian methods. |
| 7 | Jakob Stix | 2000s–present | frontier | Systematized obstructions to sections (Brauer–Manin, descent); authored the standard monograph LNM 2054; co-author of the IUT objections. |
| 8 | Mohamed Saïdi | 2000s–present | frontier | Cuspidalization of Galois groups and birational/$p$-adic section results; minimalist forms of the conjecture. |
| 9 | Hélène Esnault | 2000s–present | frontier | With Wittenberg, linked surjectivity of the section map to finiteness of $\Sha$ and cycle-theoretic conjectures. |
| 10 | Kirsten Wickelgren | 2010s–present | frontier | Developed $n$-nilpotent and Massey-product obstructions detecting non-geometric sections; homotopy-theoretic viewpoint. |

## Notes

The anabelian community working on the section conjecture is small, deeply technical, and internationally distributed, anchored historically in Japan (the Kyoto RIMS school of Mochizuki, Tamagawa, Nakamura, Hoshi) and in Europe (Bonn, Heidelberg, Frankfurt, Oxford, Philadelphia/Penn around Pop). Olivier Wittenberg, Yuichiro Hoshi, Ambrus Pál, and David Harari are further central contributors, with younger researchers entering through arithmetic homotopy theory, the étale homotopy type, and connections to the Lawrence–Venkatesh method for rational points. The field is characterized by exceptionally high entry cost — mastery of étale fundamental groups, $p$-adic Hodge theory, and profinite group theory is required — which keeps the active population in the low dozens. Activity remains steady at RIMS, in the European arithmetic-geometry seminars, and at conferences on anabelian geometry, though the IUT controversy has cast a long and contested shadow over the broader program's public reception. The section conjecture itself, distinct from IUT, retains broad respect as a clean, central open problem.
