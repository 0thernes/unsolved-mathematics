# Approaches — The Dixmier Conjecture

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

Let $A_1 = k\langle p,q\rangle/([p,q]-1)$ with $\mathrm{char}\,k = 0$. Because $A_1$ is a simple domain, any nonzero endomorphism is injective; the conjecture is the assertion that injectivity forces surjectivity. Below are the principal lines of attack.

## Reduction modulo $p$ and the Jacobian equivalence

The dominant strategy, and the one that reshaped the field, exploits the **positive-characteristic Weyl algebra**. Over a field of characteristic $p$, the elements $p^p$ and $q^p$ become central, so $A_1(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is a finite (Azumaya) algebra over its center, and an endomorphism induces a map on the center — a polynomial map of $\mathbb{A}^2$. Tracking this across all primes converts a hypothetical non-surjective Dixmier endomorphism into a polynomial self-map of affine space with constant invertible Jacobian that fails to be invertible.

- **Core idea:** translate noncommutative rigidity into commutative invertibility via $p$-curvature / characteristic varieties.
- **Best result:** Tsuchimoto (2005, full version 2008) and, independently, Belov-Kanel–Kontsevich (2007) proved that the *stable* Dixmier conjecture (all $n$) is **equivalent** to the *stable* Jacobian conjecture (all dimensions). In particular $\mathrm{DC}_n \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{JC}_{2n}$ in a suitable stable sense.
- **Barrier:** this is a two-edged result. It does not *prove* Dixmier; it shows Dixmier is **exactly as hard** as the Jacobian conjecture, which is itself open even for $\mathbb{A}^2$. Any approach now inherits all the known obstructions to the Jacobian conjecture.

## Symplectic and automorphism-group methods

Since $\mathrm{Aut}(A_1)$ is understood (it is generated by "tame" symplectic-type transformations, an analogue of the Jung–van der Kulk theorem for $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{A}^2)$), one can try to leverage the structure of automorphisms to constrain endomorphisms.

- **Core idea:** Belov-Kanel and Kontsevich conjectured and studied a **canonical isomorphism** between $\mathrm{Aut}(A_n)$ and the group of polynomial symplectomorphisms $\mathrm{Aut}(P_n)$ of the Poisson algebra, via a deformation-quantization / $\hbar \to 0$ tropical limit.
- **Best result:** the automorphism groups of $A_1$ and of the Poisson plane are known to be isomorphic (both "tame"); for $n \ge 2$ the conjectural isomorphism is partially established and ties the endomorphism problem to symplectic geometry.
- **Barrier:** an isomorphism of *automorphism* groups does not by itself control *non-surjective* endomorphisms, which are exactly the objects one must rule out. The hard case lives outside the group.

## Filtration, degree, and Bernstein-inequality bounds

The Bernstein filtration assigns to each operator a degree, and the **Bernstein inequality** bounds the dimension of the characteristic variety of any nonzero $A_1$-module by $1$ (holonomicity).

- **Core idea:** if $\varphi$ is an endomorphism, control the degrees of $\varphi(p), \varphi(q)$ and use holonomicity / GK-dimension to force surjectivity.
- **Best result:** the conjecture is confirmed for endomorphisms that respect the filtration or have small degree, and degree estimates rule out broad classes of would-be counterexamples; Joseph's and Dixmier's structural results handle the "associated graded is nice" case.
- **Barrier:** a general endomorphism need not be filtered, and the degree of the image can grow uncontrollably; no a-priori degree bound on a hypothetical counterexample is known, mirroring the absence of degree bounds in the Jacobian conjecture.

## Holonomic / D-module and microlocal methods

One can study an endomorphism through its action on holonomic $A_1$-modules and the induced correspondence of characteristic cycles.

- **Core idea:** a self-embedding of $A_1$ pulls back holonomic modules to holonomic modules; analyzing this functor (and its compatibility with duality and the de Rham / solution functors) should obstruct properness of the image.
- **Best result:** these methods recover holonomicity-based partial cases and connect to the integral / Fourier-transform structure of $A_1$; they underpin Tsuchimoto's machinery.
- **Barrier:** the functorial picture is faithful but not obviously essentially surjective; controlling all holonomic modules of the subalgebra $\varphi(A_1)$ is as hard as the original problem.

## Tropical / deformation-quantization limit

Belov-Kanel and Kontsevich proposed passing to the **tropical limit** of automorphisms (a $\log$-degeneration of the symplectomorphism group) to attack the $\mathrm{Aut}(A_n)\cong\mathrm{Aut}(P_n)$ conjecture and, through it, the endomorphism rigidity.

- **Core idea:** degenerate the (highly nonlinear) group of polynomial symplectomorphisms to a piecewise-linear / combinatorial object where invertibility might be visible.
- **Best result:** structural conjectures with supporting evidence in low rank; a research program rather than a theorem.
- **Barrier:** making the tropical limit rigorous and reconnecting it to non-tame endomorphisms remains open; it is a heuristic that has not yet closed the case.

## Summary of the obstruction landscape

Every serious approach now funnels into the Jacobian conjecture. There is **no known counterexample** and **no known unconditional proof** in any dimension, including $n = 1$. The absence of effective degree bounds, the failure of the simplicity argument to control images, and the equivalence to a famously stubborn commutative problem together explain why the conjecture is rated low in tractability.
