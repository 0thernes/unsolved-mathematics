# History — The Dixmier Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin and formulation

The conjecture grew out of Jacques Dixmier's systematic 1968 study of the first Weyl algebra $A_1 = A_1(k)$, the associative $k$-algebra generated by two elements $p, q$ subject to the canonical commutation relation $[p,q] = pq - qp = 1$, where $k$ is a field of characteristic zero. Over $k = \mathbb{C}$ this is the algebra of polynomial differential operators on the line, generated by $x$ and $\partial = d/dx$. In his paper *"Sur les algèbres de Weyl"* (Bulletin de la Société Mathématique de France, 1968), Dixmier proved a wealth of structural facts — $A_1$ is a simple, Noetherian domain with no nontrivial two-sided ideals and trivial center — and closed the paper with six problems. The first of these, **"Problème 1,"** asks whether every algebra endomorphism of $A_1$ is an automorphism. This is now called the **Dixmier conjecture**, conventionally stated affirmatively: every $k$-algebra endomorphism $\varphi: A_1 \to A_1$ is bijective.

Equivalently, because $A_1$ is a simple domain, any nonzero endomorphism is automatically injective; the content is **surjectivity**. In yet another form: if $P, Q \in A_1$ satisfy $[P,Q] = 1$, must they generate all of $A_1$? An affirmative answer would mean $A_1$ admits no "proper subalgebra isomorphic to itself via the generators," echoing the noncommutative analogue of asking whether a polynomial self-map with nonvanishing Jacobian is invertible.

## Reformulations and the Jacobian link

The decisive structural insight came decades later. In 2005–2007, **Yoshifumi Tsuchimoto** (Kochi) and, independently, **Alexei Belov-Kanel and Maxim Kontsevich** proved that the Dixmier conjecture $\mathrm{DC}_n$ for the $n$-th Weyl algebra $A_n$ is *equivalent* to the **Jacobian conjecture** $\mathrm{JC}_{2n}$ in $2n$ variables — more precisely, the family of Dixmier conjectures across all $n$ is "stably equivalent" to the family of Jacobian conjectures. The mechanism is reduction modulo $p$: in characteristic $p$ the Weyl algebra acquires a large center (the $p$-th powers of generators), and a $p$-curvature / characteristic-variety argument translates Weyl-algebra endomorphisms into polynomial symplectomorphisms. This placed Dixmier's question inside one of the most studied open problems in affine algebraic geometry and explained its resistance.

## Timeline

- **1968** — Jacques Dixmier publishes *Sur les algèbres de Weyl*; poses Problème 1 (the Dixmier conjecture) among six questions on $A_1$.
- **1970s–1980s** — The conjecture is verified for endomorphisms preserving extra structure (e.g. filtered/graded or symmetric cases); Joseph and others develop the automorphism theory of $A_1$, paralleling Dixmier–Moeglin work.
- **1982** — Joseph and others clarify the automorphism group $\mathrm{Aut}(A_1)$ and its relation to the symplectic/tame structure (analogue of the Jung–van der Kulk theorem for $\mathbb{A}^2$).
- **2005** — Tsuchimoto announces the equivalence of the stable Dixmier and Jacobian conjectures via $p$-curvature and the "Weyl algebra in positive characteristic" method.
- **2007** — Belov-Kanel and Kontsevich publish *"The Jacobian conjecture is stably equivalent to the Dixmier conjecture"* (Moscow Mathematical Journal), giving an independent and influential proof; Tsuchimoto's full account appears in Osaka J. Math.
- **2008–2015** — Belov-Kanel, Kontsevich and others pursue the "holonomic"/microlocal and tropical-limit programs; partial results bound the structure of hypothetical non-surjective endomorphisms.
- **2010s–2020s** — The conjecture remains open in every dimension $n \ge 1$; it is now widely regarded as exactly as hard as the Jacobian conjecture, which itself is unresolved even in two variables. No verified proof or counterexample exists as of the present frontier.

The historical arc is striking: a terminal "Problème 1" in a 1968 structural paper became, forty years on, a precise twin of a century-old question (the Jacobian conjecture traces to Keller, 1939), binding noncommutative algebra to commutative affine geometry.
