# Approaches — Schinzel's Hypothesis H

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

## Sieve methods (Brun, Selberg, the linear sieve)

The oldest and most productive line of attack treats the polynomial values $F(n) = f_1(n)\cdots f_k(n)$ as integers to be sifted of small prime factors. Brun's sieve and later Selberg's $\Lambda^2$ sieve give upper bounds of the correct order of magnitude for the number of $n \le x$ with all $f_i(n)$ prime — these upper bounds *prove the conjecture cannot fail by being too large*, and they match the Bateman–Horn prediction up to a constant. The deeper achievement is the production of *almost-primes*: the linear sieve, combined with a bilinear (Type II) input, yields infinitely many $n$ for which the $f_i(n)$ have few prime factors. Chen's theorem (1973), that there are infinitely many primes $p$ with $p+2$ a prime or product of two primes, is the canonical triumph of this method on a twin-type case. **Barrier:** every pure sieve runs into the *parity problem*, identified by Atle Selberg. Sieves cannot distinguish integers with an even number of prime factors from those with an odd number, so they cannot, by themselves, force the *exact* number of prime factors down to one. Producing a genuine prime (one prime factor) from a sieve alone is therefore obstructed; Chen's $P_2$ is, in a precise sense, the parity barrier's edge.

## Bilinear forms and the dispersion method

To break parity one injects arithmetic information beyond the sieve axioms — typically asymptotics for sums of the Möbius or von Mangoldt function twisted against the residues of $F(n)$. The Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem (1965) supplies precisely such equidistribution of primes in arithmetic progressions on average, and Bombieri's asymptotic sieve and the Friedlander–Iwaniec dispersion method convert bilinear (Type I/II) estimates into genuine prime detection. **Best result:** Friedlander and Iwaniec (1998) proved that $a^2 + b^4$ is prime infinitely often — a *thin* polynomial sequence (density $x^{3/4}$) representing primes, and the first case where the parity barrier was genuinely circumvented for a sparse polynomial. Heath-Brown (2001) followed with $a^3 + 2b^3$. **Barrier:** these breakthroughs exploit the *two-variable* structure, which provides the bilinear form the method needs; the one-variable polynomial values of Hypothesis H (e.g. $n^2+1$) offer no such factorization of the variable, and no analogous Type II information is available. The single-variable case is thus untouched by these methods.

## The circle method and the Hardy–Littlewood heuristics

The Hardy–Littlewood circle method (1923) gives the *expected* main term — the Bateman–Horn constant arises as the singular series $\mathfrak{S} = \prod_p (1 - \omega(p)/p)(1-1/p)^{-k}$, where $\omega(p)$ counts solutions of $F(n)\equiv 0 \pmod p$. The method rigorously delivers asymptotics when the number of variables is large relative to the degree (Birch's theorem, Waring-type settings), and it underlies the Green–Tao–Ziegler treatment of linear systems. **Barrier:** for a single polynomial in one variable, the minor arcs cannot be controlled — there are too few variables — so the circle method yields only the conjectural heuristic, not a proof. It tells us *what* to expect, not *that* it happens.

## Higher-order Fourier analysis (Green–Tao–Ziegler)

For *systems of linear forms*, Green and Tao (2008, primes in arithmetic progressions) and Green–Tao–Ziegler (2012, the inverse and Möbius–nilsequence theorems) developed higher-order Fourier analysis to prove the Hardy–Littlewood asymptotic for systems of *finite complexity*. This solves a large swath of Dickson's conjecture: any system of linear forms no two of which are "essentially the same" (in the precise sense that their complexity is finite) is handled unconditionally. **Barrier:** the twin-prime configuration $\{n, n+2\}$ — and any system containing two forms that are translates — has *infinite complexity*; the method's controlling norms degenerate, and it cannot produce two primes simultaneously from one variable. So even the linear, multiple-polynomial heart of Hypothesis H (twin primes) is out of reach.

## Bounded gaps and the GPY/Maynard–Tao sieve

Goldston–Pintz–Yıldırım (2009) and then Zhang (2014) and Maynard (2015) developed a multidimensional Selberg sieve that detects *at least one* (then several) primes within a bounded-length admissible tuple. **Best result:** for every $m$ there are infinitely many intervals containing $m$ primes whose pattern is one of a fixed admissible set; concretely, infinitely many prime pairs differ by at most $246$ (Polymath8b). This is the strongest unconditional progress on simultaneous primes. **Barrier:** the method finds primes *somewhere* in a tuple but cannot pin them to *prescribed* coordinates — it does not show that any single specified pair (like $n, n+2$) is prime infinitely often. The parity problem reasserts itself: forcing all coordinates prime is exactly what the sieve cannot do.

## Function-field and large-$q$ analogues

Replacing $\mathbb{Z}$ with $\mathbb{F}_q[t]$ converts the problem into one about polynomials over a finite field, where the Riemann Hypothesis is a theorem (Weil) and where geometric and Galois-theoretic tools (monodromy, the Katz equidistribution machinery) become available. **Best result:** Sawin and Shusterman (around 2018–2022) proved strong forms of Hypothesis H and the twin-prime and Bateman–Horn analogues over $\mathbb{F}_q[t]$ for $q$ large (and $q$ a prime power with mild conditions), including bounded-gap and exact-tuple statements with power-saving error terms. **Barrier:** these results crucially use that $q \to \infty$ or that $q$ avoids small characteristic, and they rely on geometric inputs (the Weil conjectures, monodromy computations) with no integer counterpart. The transfer principle from function fields to $\mathbb{Z}$ is heuristic only; the integer case has no Frobenius and no proven RH, so the analogues guide intuition without delivering the integer theorem.
