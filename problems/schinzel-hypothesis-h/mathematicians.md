# Top Mathematicians — Schinzel's Hypothesis H

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Andrzej Schinzel | 1937–2021 | originator | Stated Hypothesis H (1958) with Sierpiński, unifying the Bunyakovsky and Dickson conjectures into a single local-to-global law for prime values of polynomial systems. |
| 2 | Wacław Sierpiński | 1882–1969 | originator | Co-author of the 1958 formulation; senior figure of the Polish number-theory school whose work on prime-producing forms framed the hypothesis. |
| 3 | Viktor Bunyakovsky | 1804–1889 | originator | Conjectured (1850s) that a single irreducible polynomial with no fixed divisor represents infinitely many primes — the degree-$\ge2$ root of Hypothesis H. |
| 4 | Leonard E. Dickson | 1874–1954 | originator | Conjectured (1904) simultaneous primality for finitely many linear forms — the multiple-polynomial root of Hypothesis H. |
| 5 | Paul T. Bateman | 1919–2012 | breakthrough | With Horn, gave the quantitative Bateman–Horn density refinement (1962), turning Hypothesis H into a precise asymptotic prediction. |
| 6 | Roger A. Horn | b. 1942 | breakthrough | Co-author of the Bateman–Horn conjecture supplying the conjectural asymptotic count with explicit singular series. |
| 7 | Chen Jingrun | 1933–1996 | breakthrough | Proved (1973) the $P_2$ approximation to the twin-prime case — the closest unconditional approach, defining the parity-barrier frontier. |
| 8 | Henryk Iwaniec | b. 1947 | frontier | With Friedlander, proved $a^2+b^4$ prime infinitely often (1998), the first circumvention of the parity barrier for a sparse polynomial sequence. |
| 9 | James Maynard | b. 1987 | frontier | Multidimensional sieve (2015) producing bounded prime tuples for every $m$ — the strongest existential progress on simultaneous primes. |
| 10 | Will Sawin | b. 1990 | frontier | With Shusterman, proved Hypothesis H / twin-prime / Bateman–Horn analogues over $\mathbb{F}_q[t]$, isolating the integer obstruction. |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of analytic number theory, sieve theory, and arithmetic geometry. The bounded-gaps revolution (Goldston, Pintz, Yıldırım; Zhang; Maynard; Tao and the Polymath8 collaboration) remains the dominant unconditional engine, and Terence Tao continues to shape both the linear-systems program (with Ben Green and Tamar Ziegler) and the sieve frontier. John Friedlander and Henryk Iwaniec's bilinear-forms tradition is carried forward by researchers pushing thin-sequence prime detection. The most vigorous recent progress is in the function-field direction, led by Will Sawin and Mark Shusterman, whose geometric methods give the cleanest known route to statements equivalent to Hypothesis H, sharpening intuition for where the integer case's difficulty truly lies. No single group is close to the integer hypothesis; the field's consensus is that the parity problem must be broken in the one-variable setting before any nonlinear or prescribed-twin instance can fall.
