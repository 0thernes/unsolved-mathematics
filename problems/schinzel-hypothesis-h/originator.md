# Originator(s) — Schinzel's Hypothesis H

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Andrzej Schinzel (1937–2021)

Andrzej Bobola Maria Schinzel was a Polish number theorist, one of the most prolific researchers in elementary and analytic number theory of the twentieth century. Born in Sandomierz, he studied at the University of Warsaw, where he came under the influence of Wacław Sierpiński, the doyen of the Polish number-theoretic school. Schinzel completed his doctorate in 1960 and spent essentially his entire career at the Mathematical Institute of the Polish Academy of Sciences in Warsaw, where he led the number theory department for decades and edited the journal *Acta Arithmetica* — the same journal in which Hypothesis H was first published.

Schinzel's mathematical temperament was that of a master of the concrete: he was drawn to precise questions about polynomials, their factorizations, their values, and their reducibility. His best-known body of work concerns the arithmetic of polynomial values, the structure of solutions to Diophantine equations, primitive divisors of recurrence sequences (the Bilu–Hanrot–Voutier theorem grew from questions he pursued), and the reducibility of lacunary polynomials. He authored, with Sierpiński and later alone, hundreds of papers and the influential monograph *Polynomials with Special Regard to Reducibility* (2000). The "no fixed prime divisor" condition central to Hypothesis H is exactly the kind of clean local-to-global criterion that characterized his thinking: he had a deep instinct for separating the elementary congruence obstructions to a phenomenon from its genuinely deep arithmetic content.

The motivation for Hypothesis H was unification. Schinzel recognized that two separate folklore conjectures — Bunyakovsky's about a single high-degree polynomial and Dickson's about several linear ones — were instances of a single conjectural law: *prime values of polynomials are governed entirely by local obstructions*. By stating the joint hypothesis for any finite system of irreducible polynomials, Schinzel produced a statement implying, in one stroke, the twin-prime conjecture, the infinitude of primes of the form $n^2+1$, the Sophie Germain prime conjecture, and the existence of infinitely many primes in countless prescribed polynomial patterns. With Sierpiński he also stated the quantitative Hypothesis $H_1$, anticipating the density refinements later sharpened by Bateman and Horn.

## Wacław Sierpiński (1882–1969)

Sierpiński was the senior collaborator and one of the founders of the modern Polish mathematical school. Educated in Warsaw and Kraków, he made foundational contributions across set theory (the Sierpiński triangle, cardinal arithmetic), topology, and number theory, and co-founded the journal *Fundamenta Mathematicae*. By 1958 he was an elder statesman of the field; his role in Hypothesis H reflected a lifelong interest in prime-producing forms — he is independently remembered in this area for Sierpiński numbers (odd $k$ such that $k\cdot 2^n+1$ is composite for all $n$), which probe exactly the boundary between local and global obstructions that Hypothesis H formalizes.

## The historical root versus the modern formulation

The modern statement is essentially Schinzel and Sierpiński's 1958 formulation verbatim, and it has proved remarkably stable. The principal evolution has been the addition of the *quantitative* layer. Where Hypothesis H asserts mere infinitude, the Bateman–Horn conjecture (1962) predicts the asymptotic count with an explicit constant — a product over primes measuring the local densities — which in turn specializes to the Hardy–Littlewood constants for tuples. Schinzel's qualitative law and the Bateman–Horn density are now usually cited together as the canonical conjecture on prime values of polynomial systems. Schinzel's broader legacy — in *Acta Arithmetica*, in his students, and in the persistent fertility of the "no fixed divisor" paradigm — keeps the hypothesis at the center of analytic number theory.
