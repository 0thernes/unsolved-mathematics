# Attempts — Köthe's Conjecture

Köthe's conjecture has the unusual distinction of having generated very few *claimed* full proofs — a sign of how clearly the community understands where the difficulty lies. Most progress has come as honest partial theorems and as constructions that sharpen, rather than resolve, the question. The dominant pattern is: a statement *stronger* than Köthe's is disproved, leaving the exact conjecture standing.

## Levitzki's theorem and the chain-condition near-miss

Jacob Levitzki proved that a nil one-sided ideal in a ring with the ascending chain condition on right ideals (e.g. right Noetherian) is in fact nilpotent, hence contained in a nilpotent — a fortiori nil — two-sided ideal. This settles Köthe's conjecture for all Noetherian and Goldie rings and remains one of the cleanest positive results. It is a "near-miss" only in the sense that it shows the obstruction is entirely about rings *without* chain conditions; it gives no foothold on the general case.

## The PI-ring resolution

For rings satisfying a polynomial identity, the conjecture is a theorem, following from Levitzki's and Amitsur's structure theory (the Amitsur–Levitzki theorem and Kaplansky's theorem on primitive PI-rings show nil PI-ideals are locally nilpotent). This is widely regarded as the strongest unconditional positive evidence: the most-studied class of "tame" noncommutative rings obeys the conjecture without exception.

## Krempa's reductions (a methodological turning point)

Krempa (1972) did not prove the conjecture but transformed the search by establishing its equivalence to the nilness of $M_2(R)$ for nil $R$, and to $R[x]$ being Jacobson radical. After Krempa, attempts split cleanly into "prove $M_2$ preserves nilness" and "build a nil $R$ with $M_2(R)$ non-nil." Every serious modern attempt runs through one of these gates.

## Smoktunowicz's constructions and the disproof of stronger statements

Agata Smoktunowicz's work is the most consequential of the modern era. In 2000 she constructed a **simple nil ring** — a nonunital nil $\mathbb{Q}$-algebra that is simple — overturning the intuition that nil rings must be "small." Crucially, she also exhibited a **nil ring $R$ for which $R[x]$ is not nil** (2000/2002). This *disproves* the natural strengthening "$R$ nil $\Rightarrow R[x]$ nil," which had been a candidate stepping-stone toward Köthe. Because Köthe's conjecture only requires $R[x]$ to be *Jacobson radical* (a weaker property than nil), her counterexample does **not** refute Köthe's conjecture; it precisely delimits how much one can hope to prove. This is a textbook case of a near-miss in the *negative* direction: a beautiful counterexample that lands just short of the conjecture itself.

## Golod–Shafarevich algebras

The Golod–Shafarevich construction (1964) produced finitely generated, infinite-dimensional nil algebras that are not nilpotent, settling the Kurosh problem negatively and supplying the first reservoir of "wild" nil rings. These are the natural raw material for any attempted counterexample to Köthe's conjecture; to date none has been engineered to violate the $M_2$-nilness criterion.

## Status of claimed full proofs

There is **no widely accepted disputed or retracted full proof** of Köthe's conjecture in the literature comparable to the famous contested proofs attached to other open problems. Occasional preprints announcing a resolution have circulated informally over the decades, but none has survived expert scrutiny or entered the refereed record as a credible attempt; the consensus, reflected in survey literature (e.g. Smoktunowicz's survey "On some results related to Köthe's conjecture"), is that the problem is genuinely open and that no current technique controls the unbounded nil index that a general proof or counterexample must confront. Readers should treat any claim of resolution skeptically and check whether it addresses the $M_2(R)$ / Jacobson-radical reformulations, since those are the agreed reduction points.
