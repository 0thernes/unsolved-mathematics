# Attempts — Artin's Primitive Root Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Bilharz and the function-field shadow (1937)

Herbert Bilharz, a student of Hasse, proved the **function-field analogue** of Artin's conjecture in his 1937 dissertation — but *conditionally* on the Riemann Hypothesis for the relevant congruence zeta functions. When **André Weil proved the Riemann Hypothesis for curves over finite fields (1948)**, Bilharz's result became unconditional. This was the first genuine theorem in the Artin circle of ideas and it set the template: the number-field conjecture is "the same theorem" awaiting the (still-missing) analytic input. The episode is also a cautionary near-miss — a complete proof in a parallel universe that does not transfer.

## The Lehmer discrepancy (1957)

Derrick H. Lehmer and Emma Lehmer's machine computations were not a proof attempt but a decisive *empirical correction*. They found that for specific bases the observed density of primitive-root primes did not match Artin's product. Artin, on being shown the data, identified the missing dependency between the conditions "$q \mid p-1$" and "$a$ is a $q$-th power." This is the most famous "near-miss" in the conjecture's history: the original heuristic was *wrong as stated*, and only computation revealed it. The corrected constant is now standard.

## Hooley's conditional proof (1967)

Christopher Hooley's GRH-conditional proof is the central partial result. It is universally accepted as correct and is *not* disputed — but it is *conditional*, resting on the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis for infinitely many Dedekind zeta functions $\zeta_{\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q, a^{1/q})}$. It established the corrected density rigorously and remains the benchmark; an unconditional proof of the same statement for any single $a$ has never been given.

## Gupta–Murty and Heath-Brown (1984–1986)

The most celebrated unconditional progress. **Gupta and Murty (1984)** proved the conjecture for an infinite set of bases using sieve methods and rank estimates. **Heath-Brown (1986)** then proved the striking dichotomy: there are **at most two** primes $a$ for which Artin's conjecture fails. In particular at least one of $\{2,3,5\}$ — and at least one of any set of three multiplicatively independent integers — is a primitive root infinitely often. The "near-miss" character is sharp and frustrating: the conjecture is known for *almost every* base, yet **not a single specific integer** (not $2$, not $3$, not $10$) is known unconditionally to be a primitive root for infinitely many primes. No counterexample is suspected; the obstruction is purely the lack of GRH.

## Average and almost-all results (1968–1969)

Goldfeld (1968) and Stephens (1969) proved that the conjecture holds **on average** and for **almost all** $a$, unconditionally. These are honest theorems, widely accepted, but they sidestep the core difficulty by averaging away the dependence on individual zero-free regions.

## Disputed or retracted claims

There is **no widely circulated claimed unconditional proof** of Artin's conjecture for a specified base, and consequently no notable retraction in the literature. The problem has been comparatively free of erroneous "solutions," likely because the GRH barrier is so transparently the crux that experts recognize any unconditional claim must either invoke GRH or contain a gap. Occasional amateur preprints asserting elementary proofs surface but have not entered the refereed literature or attracted serious dispute; none has survived expert scrutiny, and the consensus remains that the conjecture is open for every individual $a$.
