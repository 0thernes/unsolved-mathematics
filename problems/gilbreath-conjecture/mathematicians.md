# Top Mathematicians — Gilbreath's Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | François Proth | 19th c. (1852–1879) | originator | First stated the leading-$1$ property of the prime difference table (1878) and gave an early proof now judged incorrect; historical root of the conjecture. |
| 2 | Norman L. Gilbreath | 20th c. (mid-1900s) | originator | Independently rediscovered the pattern (~1958) and posed it as a conjecture; the problem bears his name. Co-author of the first numerical study. |
| 3 | Ray Killgrove | 20th c. | breakthrough | Co-author of the 1959 *Mathematics of Computation* numerical investigation, one of the earliest computer-assisted verifications. |
| 4 | K. E. Ralston | 20th c. | breakthrough | Co-author of the 1959 numerical study extending verification far beyond hand computation. |
| 5 | Martin Gardner | 20th c. | expositor | Popularized the conjecture through *Scientific American*'s "Mathematical Games," cementing the name and broad awareness. |
| 6 | Paul Erdős | 20th c. | expositor | Assessed the conjecture as true but a proof "200 years away," framing it as a deep statement about prime-gap parity rather than a curiosity. |
| 7 | Andrew M. Odlyzko | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | Reduced the conjecture to a finite sufficiency condition and verified it for all primes up to $10^{13}$ (1993) — the strongest rigorous result. |
| 8 | Richard K. Guy | 20th–21st c. | expositor | Catalogued and propagated the problem in successive editions of *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory*, the standard reference. |
| 9 | Hannah Cairns | 21st c. | frontier | Proved Gilbreath-type laws for broad classes of non-prime sequences (2008), clarifying that the property is a generic combinatorial phenomenon. |
| 10 | Paulo Ribenboim | 20th–21st c. | expositor | Gave authoritative treatment of the conjecture in *The New Book of Prime Number Records*, situating it among prime-gap problems. |

## Notes

The active research community around Gilbreath's conjecture is small and intermittent. There is no ongoing "program" attacking it directly; instead, progress arrives sporadically from three adjacent communities. **Computational number theorists** in the tradition of Odlyzko periodically extend verification depth and probe whether the $\{0,2\}$-window width stays bounded. **Combinatorialists** (in the lineage of Cairns and Stoll) study difference-triangle dynamics and generic-sequence conditions, treating the primes as one instance of a robust self-similar structure. **Analytic number theorists** working on prime gaps (post Zhang–Maynard–Tao) supply the deep tools that a true resolution would need, but the conjecture's reliance on the parity of gaps places it on the far side of the parity barrier, so it is rarely a primary target. Much of the living knowledge resides in reference works and databases — Guy's *Unsolved Problems*, MathWorld, Caldwell's Prime Pages, and the OEIS difference-triangle entries — rather than in a stream of dedicated papers. The consensus is that the conjecture is true; the open question is whether any current method can prove the uniform persistence it demands.
