# Attempts — Gilbreath's Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Proth's 1878 "proof" (disputed / rejected)

The earliest claimed proof is **François Proth's**, published in the Paris Academy's *Comptes Rendus* in **1878**. Proth asserted both the statement and a demonstration. The argument is now regarded as **invalid**: it did not establish that the leading $1$ persists at every depth, but rather assumed a regularity in the difference rows that itself requires proof. Because Proth died young and worked outside academia, the error was never the subject of a famous public correction — the claim simply did not enter the literature, and the problem was rediscovered eighty years later. The neutral assessment in modern surveys is that Proth's proof "is generally considered to be incorrect," with the gap being exactly the propagation step that remains open today.

## Gilbreath–Killgrove–Ralston numerical study (1959)

Not a proof attempt but a foundational **near-confirmation**: Gilbreath, together with **Ray Killgrove** and **K. E. Ralston**, carried out one of the first computer-assisted verifications, reported in *Mathematics of Computation* (1959). It checked the leading-$1$ property far beyond hand range and established the empirical credibility that motivated all later work. Its limitation is intrinsic: a finite check cannot decide an infinitary statement.

## Odlyzko's reduction and large-scale verification (1993)

The most significant genuine advance. **Andrew Odlyzko** did not claim a proof of the full conjecture; rather, he proved a *sufficiency theorem* (a finite, bounded-width condition that, if met up to some height, would imply the law for all primes below the corresponding bound) and verified it for primes up to $10^{13}$. This is the canonical "near-miss": it reduces the problem to a clean structural condition and confirms it on an astronomically large range, yet leaves the genuinely infinite part — uniform persistence for arbitrarily large primes — untouched. No dispute attaches to Odlyzko's work; it is correct and is the standard reference for "what is rigorously known."

## Generic-sequence theorems (2000s)

Several authors, notably **Hannah Cairns (2008)** and **A. Stoll**, proved that Gilbreath-type laws hold for broad classes of sequences satisfying mild balance/equidistribution hypotheses on their gaps. These are correct theorems and genuine partial progress on the *phenomenon*, but they do not resolve the conjecture for the primes because verifying the hypotheses for the primes specifically is itself beyond current methods. They are best read as evidence that the conjecture is "true for structural reasons," not as a route to the primes.

## Erdős's assessment

Although not an attempt, **Paul Erdős's** widely quoted judgment — that the conjecture is surely true but that a proof is "perhaps 200 years away" — functions as a standing warning. Erdős's point was that the conjecture sits squarely on the parity structure of prime gaps, the precise terrain where elementary and sieve methods fail, so the apparent simplicity of the statement is deceptive. No serious, sustained claimed proof of the full conjecture by a credentialed mathematician has appeared since; the literature is one of reductions, verifications, and generalizations rather than completed proofs.

## Status of claimed proofs

There is **no accepted proof**. Occasional informal or amateur "proofs" circulate (as for many famous elementary conjectures), but none has survived scrutiny, and none is published in a refereed venue of standing. The honest summary: a single rejected historical proof (Proth, 1878), a powerful but finite-range reduction (Odlyzko, 1993), and a family of generic theorems that illuminate but do not close the problem.
