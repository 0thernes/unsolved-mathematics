# Originator(s) — Gilbreath's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Norman L. Gilbreath (the modern originator)

Norman L. Gilbreath is the figure whose name the conjecture bears. He posed it around **1958** while a student, by an act of recreational arithmetic that has become part of the folklore of number theory: he wrote the primes in a row and began taking absolute differences, iterating downward, and noticed that the leftmost entry stayed stubbornly equal to $1$ at every level. The story — that the computation was first scribbled on a napkin — captures the conjecture's defining character: it requires no theory to state, only patience and the willingness to look.

Gilbreath was not primarily a research number theorist of the academic-monograph kind; his lasting mathematical mark is precisely this observation. He did, however, follow it up rigorously for the era: together with **Ray Killgrove** and **K. E. Ralston** he produced an early machine-assisted numerical study, published in *Mathematics of Computation* (1959), one of the first venues where digital computation was brought to bear on a pure-mathematics conjecture. (Gilbreath is also known in a quite separate domain, as the inventor of the "Gilbreath shuffle" / "Gilbreath principle" in card magic — a genuine combinatorial result about how a single riffle shuffle preserves certain cyclic structures. The shared name reflects one person's taste for hidden order in seemingly random sequences, the same taste that produced the prime conjecture.)

What motivated Gilbreath was not a grand program but the empiricist's instinct: confronted with the primes — the most studied yet least predictable sequence in mathematics — he asked whether *iterated differencing*, a standard tool for taming polynomial sequences, would expose any residual regularity. For a polynomial of degree $d$, the $d$-th differences are constant; the primes are nothing like a polynomial, so the persistence of a $1$ in the leading column is genuinely surprising and points to a subtle constraint on the *parity* of prime gaps.

## François Proth (the historical root)

The conjecture has an earlier, long-forgotten origin in **François Proth** (1852–1879), a self-taught French farmer-mathematician from near Verdun. Despite no formal academic position, Proth made durable contributions to elementary number theory: **Proth's theorem** (a primality criterion for numbers of the form $k \cdot 2^n + 1$ with $k$ odd and $k < 2^n$) and **Proth primes** are named for him, and his work informed later primality testing. Working largely in isolation, Proth published short notes in the Paris Academy's *Comptes Rendus*.

In **1878**, Proth stated the very property now called Gilbreath's conjecture and offered a proof. The proof was subsequently found to be flawed — it did not establish the persistence of the leading $1$ — and the result was not absorbed into the mainstream literature. Because Proth died young (in 1879, at twenty-six or twenty-seven) and worked outside any institution, his statement of the problem went unnoticed for the better part of a century, which is why the conjecture is universally attached to Gilbreath rather than Proth.

## The two formulations

There is no substantive difference between the Proth and Gilbreath statements: both assert that the iterated absolute-difference table of the primes has a leading column of $1$s below the top row. The historical lesson is one of independent discovery and lost priority — a recurring pattern in elementary number theory, where statements simple enough to be found by anyone are often found by several people decades apart. Proth supplied the first (faulty) claim of proof; Gilbreath supplied the rediscovery, the name, and the first serious numerical confirmation, and through Gardner's popularization made it a permanent fixture among famous open problems about the primes.
