# Approaches — Gilbreath's Conjecture

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

The conjecture is unusual in that the dominant strategy is not to use deep properties of the primes at all, but to argue that *almost any* sequence resembling the primes mod small powers of $2$ would satisfy a Gilbreath-type law. The difficulty is converting "almost any" into "the primes, unconditionally."

## Reduction to a finite check (Odlyzko's persistence method)

The most important line of attack, due to **Andrew M. Odlyzko (1993)**, observes that the difference rows quickly become composed almost entirely of $0$s and $2$s. If one can show that for some depth $k$ every entry of row $k$ within a sufficiently wide window lies in $\{0, 2\}$ and the window's leading entry is $2$, then the leading-$1$ property *propagates downward forever* — the structure is self-sustaining. Concretely, Odlyzko proved a sufficiency criterion: it is enough to verify that a certain finite prefix of the triangle, of bounded width and depth, has the required $\{0,2\}$ form. He then verified the criterion computationally for all primes up to $10^{13}$, establishing that **no counterexample can appear in any row** for primes below that bound.

- **Best result:** Unconditional verification of the leading $1$ for the difference triangle generated by all primes $\le 10^{13}$ (over $3 \times 10^{11}$ primes), to depths far beyond any conceivable counterexample.
- **Barrier:** The criterion is *sufficient but finite-range*. It certifies the pattern only up to the largest prime checked. To finish the conjecture one needs a *uniform* guarantee that the $\{0,2\}$ structure, once established, never breaks for arbitrarily large primes — and that requires controlling prime gaps mod $2$ uniformly, which the method does not provide.

## The "$0$s and $2$s" / parity-of-gaps heuristic

The core phenomenon is that, after the first row, prime gaps are all even (every prime past $2$ is odd, so consecutive gaps are even), and the differences of even numbers stay even; the residual content is whether they are $\equiv 0$ or $\equiv 2 \pmod 4$, and so on down the $2$-adic tower. The heuristic models the gaps' low-order bits as effectively random and shows that a random $\pm$ sequence of this type obeys the Gilbreath law with probability $1$.

- **Best result:** Rigorous theorems for *random* and for *structured model* sequences (see below) confirming the law holds generically.
- **Barrier:** The primes are not random. Making the heuristic rigorous would require independence/equidistribution statements about prime gaps modulo powers of $2$ that are out of reach. This is a form of the **parity barrier**: sieve and elementary methods are notoriously blind to the parity (mod $2$) structure that the conjecture lives on, so the very features the conjecture depends on are those current sieve theory cannot control.

## Generalization to Gilbreath-type sequences

A productive strategy (pursued by **Hannah Cairns**, **A. Stoll**, **Martin Gardner**'s commentators, and others) abandons the primes and asks: *for which sequences* does iterated absolute differencing yield a leading column of $1$s? The answer is that the property is **generic** — it holds for any sequence that begins with an odd number and whose terms are sufficiently "balanced" in their low-order binary digits, regardless of the deeper arithmetic. Cairns (2008) and related work give clean sufficient conditions: roughly, if the gaps stay bounded and equidistribute mod $2$, the law holds.

- **Best result:** Theorems characterizing broad classes of sequences satisfying the conjecture, showing the primes are merely one instance of a robust combinatorial phenomenon.
- **Barrier:** These theorems require hypotheses (boundedness or equidistribution of gaps) that, for the primes specifically, would themselves be theorems comparable in depth to what is being attempted. The generic result does not transfer to the primes without independent input.

## Direct analytic / additive number theory

A frontal assault would try to prove that prime gaps satisfy the needed mod-$2^k$ equidistribution directly, via the circle method, sieves, or results in the spirit of **Zhang–Maynard–Tao** bounded-gaps technology.

- **Best result:** Modern gap theory (bounded gaps, Maynard's sieve) gives strong information about *small* gaps but not about the *fine parity structure* of *all* gaps simultaneously, which is what Gilbreath needs.
- **Barrier:** Again the parity barrier, plus the fact that the conjecture demands control of *every* row of the triangle uniformly — an infinitude of conditions — rather than a single asymptotic. No analytic input currently bridges this gap.

## Computational certification

Beyond Odlyzko, computation serves to (i) extend the verified range and (ii) test whether the $\{0,2\}$ window width grows or stabilizes, which would bear on whether a uniform argument is plausible. Empirically the required window stays narrow, strong evidence that the law is robust.

- **Barrier:** Computation alone can never close a statement quantified over all primes; it can only raise the depth of certainty and guide the search for a uniform mechanism.
