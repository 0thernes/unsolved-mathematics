# Originator(s) — The Erdős–Straus Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Paul Erdős (1913–1996)

Paul Erdős, born 26 March 1913 in Budapest to two mathematics teachers, was one of the most prolific mathematicians in history, author or co-author of roughly 1,500 papers with some 500 collaborators — the genesis of the celebrated "Erdős number." He earned his doctorate at the University of Budapest (Pázmány Péter University) in 1934 under Lipót Fejér, with a thesis on prime numbers that already contained an elementary proof of Bertrand's postulate. As a Hungarian Jew, he left Europe in the 1930s and spent the rest of his life essentially without a fixed home, travelling between institutions and collaborators, living out of a suitcase, and devoting himself wholly to mathematics.

Erdős's deepest instinct was for *elementary* problems whose statements a schoolchild could grasp but whose resolution demanded new ideas — in number theory, combinatorics, set theory, probability, and graph theory. He pioneered the probabilistic method and, with Atle Selberg, found an elementary proof of the Prime Number Theorem (1949). Crucially for the present problem, he was a tireless *collector and disseminator of problems*, often attaching small cash prizes; many famous conjectures bear his name precisely because he posed them publicly and pressed others to solve them.

Egyptian fractions were a lifelong interest. Erdős proved (1932) that the harmonic-like sums $\frac{1}{a_1}+\cdots+\frac{1}{a_k}$ with distinct denominators in $[n, 2n]$ are never an integer, and with Ernst Straus and others studied how economically rationals can be written as unit-fraction sums. The $4/n$ question is a natural extremal instance: the greedy algorithm shows three terms suffice for $3/n$ trivially and that $4/n$ generically needs at most a few terms, so asking whether *three* always work for the specific numerator $4$ is exactly the kind of sharp, deceptively simple question Erdős favored. He returned to unit-fraction problems repeatedly, including the related Erdős–Graham problems on Egyptian fractions.

## Ernst Gabor Straus (1922–1983)

Ernst Straus, born 25 February 1922 in Munich, fled Nazi Germany with his family, eventually settling in the United States. He completed his doctorate at Columbia University in 1948. Early in his career he served as an assistant to **Albert Einstein** at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton (1944–1948), collaborating on problems in unified field theory and general relativity — a striking pedigree for someone who would also become a deep combinatorialist and number theorist. He spent most of his career at UCLA, where he was a central figure and mentor.

Straus's mathematical range was wide: he co-authored with Einstein in mathematical physics, with Erdős and others in number theory and combinatorics, and contributed to approximation theory and the theory of inequalities. His collaboration with Erdős was extensive and warm; the $4/n$ conjecture is the most famous fruit of it, formulated around 1948 as both men were thinking about the economy of unit-fraction representations. Straus's facility with explicit constructions and parametric identities complemented Erdős's instinct for asymptotics and exceptional-set arguments, and the residue-class constructions that dominate the early literature are very much in his constructive idiom.

## Joint legacy

Neither man treated the conjecture as a private project; both folded it into Erdős's open circulation of problems, which is why it spread so quickly through Sierpiński, Schinzel, Mordell, and the wider community. The conjecture's enduring appeal — and its place in this Atlas — owes to the originators' shared aesthetic: a one-line statement, immune to brute force, that exposes a genuine gap in our understanding of how multiplicative structure (primes, quadratic residues) controls additive representations (sums of unit fractions). It remains a fitting monument to Erdős's problem-driven mathematics and to Straus's constructive number theory.
