# Top Mathematicians — The Erdős–Straus Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Paul Erdős | 1913–1996 | originator | Posed the conjecture (c. 1948); founded the Egyptian-fraction problem program and circulated it widely. |
| 2 | Ernst G. Straus | 1922–1983 | originator | Co-posed the conjecture; constructive number theory and parametric identities for $4/n$. |
| 3 | Louis J. Mordell | 1888–1972 | breakthrough | Codified the reduction modulo $840$, isolating the six hard square residue classes (*Diophantine Equations*, 1967). |
| 4 | Richard Obláth | 1882–1959 | breakthrough | Early verification and congruence-class solutions for $4/n$. |
| 5 | Wacław Sierpiński | 1882–1969 | expositor | Popularized the problem and the analogous $5/n$ conjecture; disseminated it in elementary number theory texts. |
| 6 | Andrzej Schinzel | 1937–2021 | breakthrough | General $a/n$ framework; solubility for all large $n$ outside finitely many residue classes. |
| 7 | R. C. Vaughan | b. 1945 | breakthrough | Exceptional-set bound $E(N)\ll N\exp(-c(\log N)^{2/3})$ (1970), the key analytic result. |
| 8 | William A. Webb | 20th–21st c. | frontier | Density and exceptional-set refinements; solvability theorems for the $a/n$ family. |
| 9 | Christian Elsholtz | b. 1968 | frontier | With Tao, sharp representation-count bounds; surveys and structural analysis of Egyptian fractions. |
| 10 | Terence Tao | b. 1975 | frontier | With Elsholtz (2011), definitive analytic study of the solution-count function and the obstruction. |

## Notes

The Erdős–Straus conjecture has no large, dedicated "research community" in the way a Millennium Problem does; it is instead a recurring touchstone within analytic and combinatorial number theory and the study of Egyptian fractions. The originators, Erdős and Straus, embedded it in Erdős's vast problem-circulation network, which is why mid-century progress is spread across Italian (Obláth, Rosati), Polish (Sierpiński, Schinzel), Japanese (Yamamoto), and Anglo-American (Mordell, Vaughan, Webb) contributors rather than a single school. The modern frontier is small but active: **Christian Elsholtz** and **Terence Tao**'s 2011 collaboration is the reference point for current work, and Elsholtz in particular has continued to write on Egyptian-fraction problems, including expository surveys. Activity today concentrates on (i) refining heuristics for the density and number of representations, (ii) extending computational verification (now past $n\sim10^{17}$), and (iii) seeking a route past the quadratic-residue obstruction in the six square classes modulo $840$ — the genuine open core. Richard K. Guy's *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory* (problem D11) remains the standard entry point for newcomers.
