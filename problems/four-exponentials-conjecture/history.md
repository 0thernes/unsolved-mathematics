# History — The Four Exponentials Conjecture

The four exponentials conjecture sits at the heart of transcendental number theory, in the circle of problems concerned with the algebraic independence of values of the exponential function. Its precise modern statement is: if $x_1, x_2$ are two complex numbers that are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$, and $y_1, y_2$ are likewise linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$, then at least one of the four numbers
$$
e^{x_1 y_1},\quad e^{x_1 y_2},\quad e^{x_2 y_1},\quad e^{x_2 y_2}
$$
is transcendental. Equivalently, in matrix form: if a $2\times 2$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} x_1 y_1 & x_1 y_2 \\ x_2 y_1 & x_2 y_2 \end{pmatrix}$ has the property that every entry is the logarithm of an algebraic number, then its rows (equivalently columns) are linearly dependent — the rank-one phenomenon. A common reformulation in terms of logarithms states that a $2\times 2$ matrix whose entries are $\mathbb{Q}$-linearly-independent logarithms of algebraic numbers cannot have rank $1$.

The problem arose as the "missing case" sitting just beyond a theorem that *can* be proved. The companion **six exponentials theorem** replaces the pair $y_1, y_2$ by a triple $y_1, y_2, y_3$ (and keeps $x_1, x_2$): under the same independence hypotheses, at least one of the six numbers $e^{x_i y_j}$ is transcendental. The six exponentials theorem is a genuine theorem; the four exponentials conjecture is its sharp, still-open analogue, and the gap between "six" and "four" measures precisely how far current methods fall short.

The roots reach back to the 1940s. The six exponentials theorem was, in essence, in reach of methods developed by Theodor Schneider and Carl Ludwig Siegel; Schneider's 1944/1949 work on transcendence and his later research monograph framed exactly the kind of two-by-many exponential statements from which the four/six dichotomy emerges. The result was rediscovered and given its definitive modern formulation independently by **Serge Lang** and **Kanakanahalli Ramachandra** in the 1960s, both of whom explicitly isolated the four exponentials conjecture as the natural unproved sharpening. Lang publicized it widely in his 1966 book *Introduction to Transcendental Numbers*, and Ramachandra's 1968 papers on the contributions to the theory of transcendental numbers gave a parallel treatment, including applications to the distribution of algebraic numbers. The "five exponentials theorem" (Waldschmidt) later carved out an intermediate unconditional result.

## Timeline

- **1944** — Schneider proves foundational transcendence theorems (Schneider's theorem on $\alpha^\beta$); the techniques underlying the six exponentials result take shape.
- **1949** — Schneider's research, later collected in *Einführung in die transzendenten Zahlen* (1957), frames two-variable exponential transcendence questions.
- **1960s** — Serge Lang formulates and disseminates the four exponentials conjecture and proves the six exponentials theorem.
- **1966** — Lang, *Introduction to Transcendental Numbers*, states the conjecture in its now-standard form alongside the six exponentials theorem.
- **1968** — K. Ramachandra independently establishes the six exponentials theorem and states the four exponentials conjecture, with arithmetic applications.
- **1970s** — Alan Baker's theory of linear forms in logarithms reshapes the field; the four exponentials problem is recognized as orthogonal to Baker's methods (which prove nonvanishing, not the needed rank statement).
- **1980s** — Michel Waldschmidt proves the **five exponentials theorem** and the "strong four exponentials" / sharp six exponentials refinements, the closest unconditional approaches.
- **1985** — Waldschmidt's monographs systematize the conjecture; the **strong four exponentials conjecture** and the **four exponentials conjecture** are related to Schanuel's conjecture, which implies both.
- **2000** — Waldschmidt, *Diophantine Approximation on Linear Algebraic Groups*, gives the canonical modern account and the matrix/rank reformulation.
- **Present** — The conjecture remains open. It is a known consequence of Schanuel's conjecture, and partial results (six exponentials, five exponentials, sharp variants) stand as the frontier; no method is known to remove the last logarithm.
