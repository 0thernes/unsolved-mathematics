# Attempts — The Four Exponentials Conjecture

The four exponentials conjecture has resisted direct proof for over half a century, and — unlike many famous open problems — it has *not* attracted a string of dramatic false proofs. The reason is structural: experts understand precisely why the standard machinery stalls one exponential short, so claimed elementary resolutions are rare and quickly recognized as flawed. The notable history is instead one of celebrated *partial* results that approach the conjecture without reaching it.

## The six exponentials theorem (the proven sibling)

The defining near-miss is the theorem that *can* be proved. Established by Serge Lang and independently by K. Ramachandra in the 1960s (with roots in Siegel and Schneider, and an antecedent in unpublished work attributed to Carl Siegel), the six exponentials theorem is the four exponentials conjecture with a third $y_3$ adjoined. Its proof is complete and uncontroversial; the entire interest of the four exponentials conjecture is the gap between this theorem and the sharper claim. No one has closed that gap.

## The five exponentials theorem (Waldschmidt)

Michel Waldschmidt's five exponentials theorem is the closest unconditional approach. It yields a four-exponentials-style conclusion at the price of adjoining one further quantity (an extra exponential or quotient), and is sharp in the sense that the methods cannot be pushed further without a genuinely new idea. It is a celebrated, fully verified partial result — a "near-miss" precisely because it shows four-exponentials conclusions are *almost* in reach, failing only by the single extra hypothesis.

## The strong six exponentials theorem and Roy's framework

Damien Roy proved the **strong six exponentials theorem** and reformulated the surrounding circle of statements in terms of the rank of matrices of logarithms of algebraic numbers. This is a substantive advance — the "strong" version is stronger than what the classical six exponentials theorem gives — and it sharpens the boundary, but the strong *four* exponentials conjecture remains open. Roy's work is the clearest map of exactly which increment is missing.

## Conditional resolutions

The conjecture is a proven consequence of **Schanuel's conjecture**; this is a legitimate, fully rigorous conditional theorem and is sometimes informally described as "almost solved." It is not a proof — Schanuel's conjecture is dramatically harder and itself unproved — but it means no specialist expects the four exponentials conjecture to be false. Anyone claiming an unconditional disproof would be contradicting a statement implied by a near-universally believed conjecture.

## Disputed or retracted claimed proofs

There is no widely circulated, peer-discussed claimed *proof* of the four exponentials conjecture that has been formally retracted in the mathematical literature, and no famous dispute of the kind seen around, say, the abc conjecture. The problem's folklore status and the clarity of the obstruction have kept it relatively free of false-proof controversy. Occasional preprints asserting elementary resolutions appear on preprint servers from time to time, but none has gained traction or survived expert scrutiny; the standard objection is always the same — the argument either silently assumes more independence data than the $2\times2$ configuration provides, or it implicitly proves only the six-exponentials count. In the interest of honesty, this dossier records no verified, community-accepted near-proof beyond the partial theorems above; any specific named "proof" should be treated as unverified absent publication in a refereed venue and confirmation by transcendence specialists.

## Why near-misses cluster at "five"

The recurring pattern — six provable, five provable with effort, four out of reach — is not coincidence. Every analytic attempt reduces to a counting inequality between interpolation conditions and unknowns, and that inequality is satisfied with a third logarithm, marginally satisfied with the fifth-exponential trick, and *violated* in the bare four-element case. The "attempts" record is therefore best read as a record of how sharply the community has localized the missing idea, not of failed proofs.
