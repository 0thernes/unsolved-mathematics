# Originator(s) — Sendov's Conjecture

## Blagovest Hristov Sendov (1932–2020)

**Blagovest Sendov** was a Bulgarian mathematician, academic administrator, and statesman, one of the most influential figures in 20th-century Bulgarian science. He was born on 8 February 1932 in Asenovgrad and died on 13 January 2020. He studied mathematics at Sofia University "St. Kliment Ohridski," where he spent much of his career, eventually serving as its Rector. He was a long-serving member and, from 1993 to 2003, **President of the Bulgarian Academy of Sciences**, and he held a range of public offices, including periods as a member and Chairman of the Bulgarian National Assembly and as Bulgaria's ambassador to Japan.

### Mathematical background and interests

Sendov's research lay primarily in **approximation theory**, **numerical analysis**, and the **constructive theory of functions** — the tradition descending from Chebyshev and developed in Bulgaria under figures such as Nikola Obreshkov. He worked extensively on Hausdorff approximation (introducing the so-called Hausdorff distance approach to approximation of functions, sometimes called "Sendov–Hausdorff" approximation), on splines, and on the metric theory of functions. He was also an early and energetic promoter of computer science and informatics in Bulgaria, founding institutions and educational programs in the field, and he authored widely used textbooks.

The conjecture that bears his name sits slightly apart from his main body of work. It belongs to the **geometry of polynomials** — the study of how the zeros of a polynomial constrain the zeros of its derivative. This area, classically associated with Gauss, Lucas, Laguerre, and later Marden and Walsh, attracted Sendov's interest as a clean extremal question. Around 1959 he asked: given that the Gauss–Lucas theorem confines all critical points to the convex hull of the roots, can one say something *local* — that each root individually has a critical point nearby? His conjectured answer (every root has a critical point within distance equal to the radius of the enclosing disk) is the strongest natural local refinement, with $p(z)=z^n-1$ showing the bound cannot be improved.

### The attribution story

Sendov communicated the problem privately, notably to his compatriot **Ljubomir Iliev**. When the problem reached the Western literature via Hayman's 1967 *Research Problems in Function Theory*, it was recorded under Iliev's name, and for two decades it circulated as the **"Ilieff conjecture."** Only later did Sendov's priority become the accepted standard, and the problem is now universally called **Sendov's conjecture**. The episode is a frequently cited example of how problem attribution can drift through informal communication.

### Broader legacy

Sendov's legacy is unusually broad for a mathematician. In pure and applied mathematics he is remembered for contributions to approximation theory and for the eponymous conjecture, which has spawned a substantial sub-literature and, through Tao's 2020 work, connections to potential theory and the analysis of equilibrium measures. Institutionally, he shaped Bulgarian higher education, the national academy, and the country's computing culture; he received numerous honors and honorary doctorates. The conjecture endures as his most internationally recognized single mathematical contribution — a deceptively elementary statement about polynomials and their derivatives that has resisted a complete proof for more than six decades.

There is no competing "historical root" formulation to disentangle: the modern statement is precisely the one Sendov posed (modulo the routine normalization to the unit disk). The only genuine historical wrinkle is the misattribution to Iliev, addressed above.
