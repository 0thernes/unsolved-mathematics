# Top Mathematicians — Sendov's Conjecture

Ten figures, past and present, who shaped the problem. Roles: `originator`, `breakthrough`, `frontier`, `expositor`. Only real, well-documented people are listed.

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Blagovest Sendov | 20th c. (1932–2020) | originator | Posed the conjecture (c. 1959); Bulgarian approximation theorist and academy president. |
| 2 | Walter K. Hayman | 20th c. | expositor | Published the problem in *Research Problems in Function Theory* (1967), bringing it to wide attention. |
| 3 | Ljubomir Iliev | 20th c. | expositor | Bulgarian mathematician through whom the problem reached the West; long misattributed as the "Ilieff conjecture." |
| 4 | Zalman Rubinstein | 20th c. | breakthrough | Settled the degree-3 case (1968), an early benchmark. |
| 5 | Q. I. Rahman | 20th c. | breakthrough | Numerous partial results; co-author of the canonical monograph *Analytic Theory of Polynomials*. |
| 6 | Gerald Schmeisser | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | Sustained degree-by-degree progress and exposition; co-author of *Analytic Theory of Polynomials*. |
| 7 | J. E. (Johnny) Brown | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | Verified the conjecture for additional low degrees (with Xiang, up to degree 8). |
| 8 | Julius Borcea | 20th–21st c. (1968–2009) | breakthrough | Structural results on critical points and related variance-type problems. |
| 9 | Jérôme Dégot | 21st c. | breakthrough | Proved the conjecture for high degree with the root bounded off the unit circle (2014). |
| 10 | Terence Tao | 21st c. | frontier | Proved Sendov's conjecture for all sufficiently large degree (2020) via limiting equilibrium measures. |

## Notes

The active community is small but presently energized by **Tao's 2020 theorem**, which transformed the landscape from "verified for small degrees only" to "open only in a finite intermediate band." Current work clusters around three goals: (i) making Tao's large-degree threshold **effective** and small enough to meet the computer-verified small-degree frontier — the natural route to a complete proof; (ii) understanding the **boundary regime**, where the distinguished root approaches the unit circle and the conjecture is sharpest (work by Chalebgwa and collaborators, among others, targets critical points near the circle); and (iii) developing the **potential-theoretic** machinery (equilibrium measures, logarithmic potentials) that Tao's proof opened up, both for Sendov and for adjacent questions in the geometry of polynomials. The field continues to draw on the classical estimation tradition (Rahman–Schmeisser school) alongside the newer measure-theoretic methods.
