# Attempts — Polignac's Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

**Polignac's own companion claim (1849).** In the same memoir that posed the prime-gap conjecture, Polignac also conjectured that every odd number is a sum of a prime and a power of two. This is false: $127$, $149$, $251$, $337$ and infinitely many others have no such representation (Erdős later exhibited an entire arithmetic progression of counterexamples via covering congruences). The failure is a cautionary near-miss—evidence that the prime-gap conjecture's empirical pedigree alone guarantees nothing—and it is occasionally, incorrectly, cited as "disproving Polignac." Only the second claim fell; the gap conjecture stands open.

**Brun's theorem (1919).** Brun proved $\sum 1/p$ over twin primes converges, the first rigorous result touching the $k=2$ case. It is a striking near-miss in the opposite direction: it shows twin primes are sparse, but is fully consistent with there being only finitely many, so it neither proves nor refutes Polignac.

**Chen's theorem (1973).** Chen Jingrun's proof that infinitely many $p$ have $p+2$ prime-or-semiprime is the most celebrated partial result. It "almost" proves the twin prime case—only the semiprime alternative stands between it and Polignac for $k=2$—but that gap is precisely the parity barrier, and no refinement of Chen's method has closed it.

**GPY (2005) and the distribution barrier.** Goldston–Pintz–Yıldırım proved $\liminf (p_{n+1}-p_n)/\log p_n = 0$ and showed that any level of distribution exceeding $1/2$ would give bounded gaps outright. For eight years the field was tantalizingly stuck at this threshold—a famous near-miss, since the needed improvement was "epsilon" beyond Bombieri–Vinogradov but seemed unreachable. An earlier 2003 GPY preprint contained a gap that was repaired; the corrected 2005 *Annals* version is canonical.

**Zhang (2013).** Yitang Zhang, then little known, crossed the distribution threshold and proved bounded gaps below $7\times 10^7$ occur infinitely often. The work was checked rapidly and accepted by *Annals of Mathematics*; it is one of the few genuinely surprising verified breakthroughs of the decade and the first unconditional result implying a (weak) form of Polignac.

**Maynard–Tao and Polymath8 (2013–2014).** Maynard's multidimensional sieve and the Polymath8b optimization brought the unconditional bound to **246**. This is a partial result, not a resolution: it shows some even $k \le 246$ recurs infinitely often without naming any specific $k$.

**Claimed elementary proofs.** As with the Riemann Hypothesis and Goldbach, the twin-prime/Polignac literature attracts a steady stream of claimed elementary proofs—on arXiv (general math / "vixra"-style), in preprints, and in self-published notes. None has survived expert scrutiny. The recurring fatal flaw is an implicit assumption of independence among the events "$n$ prime" and "$n+k$ prime," or an attempt to extract a lower bound from a sieve that the parity barrier provably forbids. **No specific case of Polignac—including the twin prime case—has any accepted proof or disproof as of 2026.** Claims to the contrary should be treated with the same skepticism as perpetual-motion designs until vetted by the analytic number theory community.
