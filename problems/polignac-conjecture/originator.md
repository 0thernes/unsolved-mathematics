# Originator(s) — Polignac's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

**Alphonse de Polignac** (Alphonse Armand Charles Georges Marie de Polignac, 1826–1863) was a French aristocrat and mathematician, born in Paris into the prominent Polignac family. He was a son of Jules de Polignac, the ultra-royalist prime minister under Charles X whose 1830 ordinances precipitated the July Revolution; Alphonse was thus born into political prominence and dynastic notoriety rather than into a scholarly lineage. He studied at the École Polytechnique, the elite French engineering school, where his mathematical interests took shape, and later served in the military, attaining the rank of officer. He died young, at 37, leaving a small but durable mathematical footprint.

Polignac's mathematics belonged to the empirical, table-driven tradition of mid-nineteenth-century number theory, before the analytic machinery of the Riemann zeta function (whose foundational 1859 memoir came a decade after Polignac's conjecture) had matured into a working toolkit. In 1849 he submitted a "Recherches nouvelles sur les nombres premiers" to the Académie des Sciences, communicated in the *Comptes Rendus*. Working numerically with tables of primes, he observed that the gaps between consecutive primes—2, 4, 6, and larger even numbers—each appeared to recur indefinitely as one ran further out among the integers. From this he extracted the conjecture that bears his name: **every even number is, in infinitely many ways, the difference between two consecutive primes.** The twin prime case ($k=2$) is the simplest and most famous instance, though the twin prime question itself is older and was not original to Polignac; his contribution was the bold generalization to all even gaps simultaneously.

The same 1849 work contained a second conjecture that has not fared as well: that every odd number greater than 1 can be written as $2^k + p$ for some prime $p$ and integer $k \ge 0$. This was a natural-looking empirical guess, but it is false—small counterexamples such as $127$ and $149$ resist any such representation, and infinitely many odd numbers (an arithmetic-progression family identified later) cannot be so written. The failure of this companion claim is a useful caution about the limits of numerical induction in number theory, and it is sometimes conflated with, but should be distinguished from, the prime-gap conjecture that survived.

It is worth separating the historical root from the modern formulation. Polignac stated his conjecture qualitatively—each even gap occurs infinitely often. The modern quantitative refinement, due to Hardy and Littlewood (1923), predicts not merely infinitude but a precise asymptotic count: the number of primes $p \le x$ with $p+k$ prime should be $\sim 2 C_2 \prod_{p \mid k, p>2} \frac{p-1}{p-2} \cdot \frac{x}{(\log x)^2}$, where $C_2$ is the twin prime constant. Polignac's conjecture is the (much weaker) statement that this count is unbounded for every even $k$, and it follows from the Hardy–Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture as a special case.

Polignac's broader legacy is modest in volume but disproportionate in influence: his name is attached to one of the canonical open problems of number theory, and the phrase "Polignac's conjecture" now organizes an entire research program on bounded prime gaps. The 2013–2014 breakthroughs of Zhang, Maynard and Tao are routinely described as proving "a weak form of Polignac's conjecture," giving a nineteenth-century empirical observation a central place in twenty-first-century analytic number theory.
