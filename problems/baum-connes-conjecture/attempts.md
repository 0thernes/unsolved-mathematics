# Attempts — The Baum–Connes Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The conjecture has a relatively clean record: it has not been plagued by famous false "complete proofs," in part because the community insisted from early on that any proof factor through verifiable $KK$-theoretic data. The drama has instead come from **the boundary of validity** — results that came tantalizingly close to the general statement and the discovery that a natural strengthening is outright false.

## The counterexamples to the conjecture with coefficients (2003)

The most important "near-miss" runs in reverse. To make proofs robust, Baum–Connes–Higson posed a **conjecture with coefficients**, replacing $C^*_r(G)$ by $A\rtimes_r G$. Many had hoped this was the "right" general statement. **Higson, Lafforgue and Skandalis** (Geom. Funct. Anal., 2003) used **Gromov's monster groups** — finitely generated groups that contain coarsely embedded **expander graphs** in their Cayley graphs — to construct $G$-$C^*$-algebras $A$ for which the assembly map with coefficients **fails to be surjective** (and related failures of injectivity). The mechanism is the failure of **exactness** of the reduced crossed product when expanders are present. This did not refute the original (coefficient-free) conjecture, but it permanently closed the door on proving the plain conjecture by reducing it to the coefficient version, and it identified non-exactness as the genuine enemy. (The full existence of Gromov monster groups itself rested on a long and delicate construction; rigorous accounts were supplied by Arzhantseva–Delzant and others.)

## Property (T) as the wall, and Lafforgue's circumvention

After Kasparov's Dirac–dual-Dirac method, it was widely felt that **Property (T)** groups might be genuine counterexamples, because the $\gamma$-element argument provably fails for them in $C^*$-theory: the obstruction $\gamma \neq 1$ is real. This was a near-miss of intuition rather than of proof — and it was reversed by **Vincent Lafforgue**, who showed (2002) that working in **Banach $KK$-theory** the obstruction need not survive, proving the conjecture for many Property (T) groups. His later **strong Property (T)** work (2012) extended this to all hyperbolic groups, including hyperbolic Property (T) groups, definitively showing that Property (T) alone is not an obstruction.

## The Connes–Kasparov conjecture as a confirmed special case

For connected (or almost-connected) Lie groups the conjecture specializes to the **Connes–Kasparov conjecture**, asserting that the assembly map computes $K_*(C^*_r G)$ from the maximal compact subgroup. This was proven in stages — Wassermann, Kasparov, Penington–Plymen, and finally **Chabert–Echterhoff–Nest (2003)** for the almost-connected case — and stands as a fully verified, deep instance. A modern, more conceptual proof for **reductive Lie groups** was given by **Peter Hochs and collaborators** and, via Mackey-analogy ideas, by **Nigel Higson** and others.

## No retracted complete proofs of the general case

There is no widely circulated, subsequently-retracted claim of a proof of the **full** conjecture for all locally compact groups; the problem's culture of demanding explicit $KK$-classes and homotopies has deterred premature announcements. Disputes have instead concerned **scope and definitions** — e.g., precisely which formulation (with/without coefficients, maximal vs. reduced, full $\underline{E}G$) a given theorem establishes — and these are now well-settled in the literature (Meyer–Nest's categorical reformulation being the standard clarifier). The honest current statement is that the conjecture is **confirmed in vast generality and refuted in its strongest coefficient form**, with the plain conjecture **open** for higher-rank lattices and similar groups.
