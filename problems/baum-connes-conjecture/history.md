# History — The Baum–Connes Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The Baum–Connes conjecture grew out of the confluence of three streams that matured in the 1970s and early 1980s: Alain Connes' program to extend index theory and differential geometry to the "bad" quotient spaces produced by group actions and foliations (noncommutative geometry); the Atiyah–Singer index theorem with its equivariant and families generalizations; and the K-theory of operator algebras. The central object is the **assembly map** (or analytic index map)

$$\mu \colon K^G_*(\underline{E}G) \longrightarrow K_*(C^*_r(G)),$$

which sends the equivariant $K$-homology of the classifying space $\underline{E}G$ for proper $G$-actions to the topological $K$-theory of the **reduced** group $C^*$-algebra $C^*_r(G)$. The conjecture asserts that $\mu$ is an isomorphism for every second-countable locally compact (Hausdorff) group $G$. Geometrically, it says that every $K$-theory class of $C^*_r(G)$ is the index of a $G$-equivariant elliptic operator and that the only relations among such indices are the obvious geometric ones — a sweeping "no exotic K-theory" statement that simultaneously encodes the strong Novikov conjecture, the Kadison–Kaplansky idempotent conjecture (for torsion-free $G$), and parts of the Gromov–Lawson–Rosenberg circle of positive-scalar-curvature questions.

The earliest formulation appeared in a 1982 preprint by **Paul Baum and Alain Connes** treating the discrete-group and foliation setting (using $BG$ rather than the proper classifying space). The decisive reformulation came after work of **Baum, Connes, Higson and Mislin** recognized that for groups with torsion one must use the classifying space for proper actions $\underline{E}G$; this is the version codified in the influential 1994 Oberwolfach survey by Baum, Connes and Higson, which remains the standard reference. A "conjecture with coefficients" — replacing $C^*_r(G)$ by the crossed product $A \rtimes_r G$ for a $G$-$C^*$-algebra $A$ — was introduced to obtain a statement stable under the operations needed in proofs; this stronger version was later shown to be **false** in general.

### Timeline

- **1976** — Connes develops the index theory of foliations and von Neumann algebras of measured foliations, seeding the noncommutative-geometry viewpoint.
- **1980–84** — Connes and Kasparov frame the conjecture for connected/amenable Lie groups (the "Connes–Kasparov conjecture"); Pimsner–Voiculescu's $\mathbb{Z}$-crossed-product computation gives early evidence.
- **1982** — Baum and Connes circulate the foundational preprint *Geometric K-theory for Lie groups and foliations*, posing the conjecture for discrete groups and foliations.
- **1984–88** — Kasparov's bivariant $KK$-theory and his "Dirac–dual-Dirac" method prove the conjecture (and Novikov) for discrete subgroups of Lie groups and groups acting properly on suitable nonpositively curved spaces.
- **1994** — Baum, Connes and Higson publish *Classifying space for proper actions and K-theory of group C\*-algebras*, fixing the modern $\underline{E}G$ formulation and the conjecture with coefficients.
- **2001** — **Higson and Kasparov** prove the conjecture for groups with the **Haagerup property** (a-T-menable groups), including all amenable groups, free groups, and many others.
- **2002** — **Lafforgue** introduces Banach $KK$-theory and proves the conjecture for many groups with **Property (T)**, including cocompact lattices in $Sp(n,1)$ and certain semisimple groups (Fields Medal, 2002, in part for this).
- **2003** — **Higson, Lafforgue and Skandalis**, building on Gromov's monster groups, produce **counterexamples to the conjecture with coefficients** via expander-based Gromov groups, while leaving the original (coefficient-free) conjecture open.
- **2003–07** — Lafforgue and others extend results toward hyperbolic groups; the bivariant Chern character program (Puschnigg, Voigt) advances rational statements.
- **2012** — **Lafforgue** establishes strong Property (T) and proves the conjecture for all (Gromov-)hyperbolic groups and their lattices.
- **2016–2020** — Chabert–Echterhoff–Nest and the Going-Down / restriction techniques verify the conjecture for **almost-connected groups** and linear reductive $p$-adic groups.
- **2020s** — The conjecture remains **open in general**; higher-rank lattices such as $SL_3(\mathbb{Z})$ and groups with strong Property (T) and bad coarse geometry are the active frontier.
