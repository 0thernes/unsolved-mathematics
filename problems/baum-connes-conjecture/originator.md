# Originator(s) — The Baum–Connes Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture bears the names of two mathematicians whose backgrounds — one rooted in algebraic topology and K-theory, the other in operator algebras and analysis — fused to produce its statement.

## Paul Baum (1936–2024)

Paul Frank Baum, born in Brooklyn in 1936, took his Ph.D. at Princeton in 1963 under **John Moore**, working in algebraic topology and homological algebra. His early career was spent in topology and algebraic geometry: the celebrated **Baum–Bott** formula for residues of holomorphic vector fields (with Raoul Bott) and the **Baum–Fulton–MacPherson** Riemann–Roch theorem for singular varieties date from this period and already show his instinct for index-theoretic and K-theoretic structure on singular spaces. Baum held a long professorship at Pennsylvania State University. From the late 1970s he became one of the principal architects of the geometric, topological side of noncommutative geometry: the construction of the classifying space for proper actions, the geometric model of $K$-homology by cycles (manifolds with maps and bundles), and the equivariant assembly map are very much in his idiom. His collaboration with Connes — and later with Higson — gave the conjecture its precise topological formulation and its geometric K-homology side. Baum continued to lecture on the conjecture and its index-theoretic content into the 2010s; he died in 2024.

## Alain Connes (b. 1947)

Alain Connes, born in Draguignan, France, in 1947, is a Fields Medalist (1982) and one of the founders of modern operator algebra theory and of **noncommutative geometry** as a discipline. His foundational work on the classification of factors (type III, the Tomita–Takesaki and modular theory, injective factors) earned the 1982 Fields Medal; he is a professor at the Collège de France and IHÉS and won the Crafoord Prize (2001). Connes' program reinterprets geometry through the lens of $C^*$- and von Neumann algebras: a "space" is encoded by an algebra, and the pathological quotients arising from group actions, foliations, and orbifolds — which have no good ordinary topology — become tractable noncommutative algebras (crossed products, groupoid algebras). For such an algebra the natural invariant is its $K$-theory, and the natural source of $K$-theory classes is index theory of equivariant elliptic operators. The conjecture is precisely the assertion that this index machine computes everything: that $K_*(C^*_r(G))$ is exhausted, without redundancy, by geometric indices. Connes' motivations were thus simultaneously to generalize the Atiyah–Singer index theorem to the equivariant/foliation setting and to provide a computational handle on the otherwise mysterious $K$-theory of reduced group $C^*$-algebras.

## The synthesis and its legacy

The 1982 Baum–Connes preprint married Baum's geometric $K$-homology and classifying-space technology to Connes' operator-algebraic index theory. The **modern formulation** differs from this root in one essential way: the original used $BG$ (which is correct only for torsion-free $G$), whereas the post-1994 statement — shaped together with **Nigel Higson** — uses the proper classifying space $\underline{E}G$, so that torsion in $G$ is accounted for. The conjecture became a unifying hub: it implies the **Novikov conjecture** (homotopy invariance of higher signatures), the **Kadison–Kaplansky idempotent conjecture**, the **Gromov–Lawson–Rosenberg** conjecture in many cases, and is intimately tied to the **Connes–Kasparov** theorem for Lie groups. Its influence reshaped K-theory, coarse geometry, and the theory of group $C^*$-algebras, and seeded the careers of a generation including Lafforgue, Yu, Tu, Skandalis, Nest, Echterhoff and many others.
