# Originator(s) — The Chowla Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Sarvadaman Chowla (1907–1995)

Sarvadaman D. S. Chowla was a British-born Indian–American number theorist whose career spanned analytic number theory, the theory of $L$-functions, character sums, and Diophantine problems. He was born on 22 October 1907 in London, where his father, Gopal Chowla, was studying; the family returned to India, and Chowla grew up in Lahore (then British India). He studied at Government College, Lahore, and at the University of the Punjab, before completing a doctorate at the University of Cambridge in 1931 under **J. E. Littlewood**, one of the towering figures of the Hardy–Littlewood school of analytic number theory. That training placed Chowla squarely in the tradition that produced the circle method, the study of the Riemann zeta function, and quantitative results on primes — the lineage from which his pseudorandomness conjecture naturally emerged.

Chowla held positions in India (Government College Lahore, Andhra University, the Tata Institute) before emigrating; from 1948 he worked in the United States, at the Institute for Advanced Study, the University of Kansas, the University of Colorado, and most enduringly at **Pennsylvania State University**, where he was a professor from 1963 until his retirement. He was a famously prolific collaborator — a frequent coauthor of **Paul Erdős** — and published more than 350 papers across an unusually broad range of topics. Several objects bear his name: the **Chowla–Selberg formula** (relating periods of elliptic curves with complex multiplication to values of the gamma function), **Chowla's conjecture on Artin $L$-functions and Siegel zeros**, the **Bruck–Ryser–Chowla theorem** in combinatorial design theory, and **Chowla's cosine problem**. He died on 10 December 1995 in Laramie, Wyoming.

## The ideas behind the conjecture

Chowla's correlation conjecture grew from a single governing intuition: the **parity of the number of prime factors should be "random."** The Prime Number Theorem already says the one-point average of $\lambda$ vanishes; the Riemann Hypothesis would control its square-root cancellation. Chowla recognized that genuine pseudorandomness demands more than cancellation in a single sum — it requires that **shifted copies of $\lambda$ be statistically independent**, so that products $\lambda(n+h_1)\cdots\lambda(n+h_k)$ also average to zero. This is the multiplicative-number-theory analogue of asserting that a random sign sequence has vanishing autocorrelations.

His framing in the 1965 monograph *The Riemann Hypothesis and Hilbert's Tenth Problem* was deliberately placed alongside the deepest open problems of the subject, signaling that he viewed correlation-vanishing as a structural truth about the primes, not a technical curiosity. The motivation was partly Diophantine: control over $\mu$ and $\lambda$ correlations governs the distribution of squarefree numbers, the parity barrier in sieve theory, and the behavior of multiplicative functions in arithmetic progressions and short intervals.

## Legacy and the modern reformulation

The conjecture Chowla stated for the **Möbius/Liouville** function in the language of *natural-density* averages is, in its original form, still open even in the two-point case. The modern research program differs in two respects. First, **Peter Sarnak** (2010) recast the underlying randomness as a *disjointness* statement in ergodic theory — Möbius orthogonality to all zero-entropy dynamical systems — providing a dynamical sibling now called the **Sarnak conjecture**. Second, **Terence Tao** and collaborators reframed progress around the **logarithmically-averaged** version, which proved tractable via the entropy decrement argument. Thus the field today distinguishes the historical natural-density Chowla conjecture (open) from the logarithmic Chowla conjecture (proven for odd orders). Chowla's original instinct — that prime-factor parity is indistinguishable from noise — remains the organizing principle of a now-vast literature.
