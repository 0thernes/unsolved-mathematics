# Attempts — Hall's Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

**The function-field success (Davenport, 1965).** The closest thing to a resolution is the polynomial analogue. Davenport proved that for coprime polynomials $f, g$ over a field of characteristic $0$ with $f^3 \neq g^2$, one has $\deg(f^3 - g^2) \geq \tfrac12 \deg f + 1$ — the exact strong-Hall inequality, with sharp constant. This is unconditional in the function-field world (and is essentially the Mason–Stothers / polynomial-$abc$ theorem). It is a genuine theorem, not an attempt at the integer case, but it is the principal evidence that the exponent $1/2$ is correct and that the strong form is morally right where archimedean obstructions vanish.

**Sharpness constructions (Davenport–Lewis–Schinzel 1965; Danilov 1982).** These are near-misses *against* the strongest constant rather than proofs. The 1965 identities and Danilov's 1982 explicit family produce infinitely many integer pairs with $|x^3 - y^2| < C\sqrt{x}$ for an explicit moderate $C$ (Danilov's examples give ratios below $1$). They establish that the exponent cannot be improved beyond $1/2$ and that any strong-form constant $C$ must be small — a hard empirical ceiling that constrains, but does not prove, the conjecture.

**Elkies's record example (1998).** Using lattice reduction (LLL applied to find cubes near squares), Noam Elkies found the celebrated pair with $x = 5853886516781223$ and $|x^3 - y^2| \approx 1.6\times 10^{12}$, giving Hall ratio $\approx 0.0211$ — for years the smallest known. This is a famous *partial result on the data side*: it does not prove anything about the lower bound but dramatically demonstrated how small the ratio can be, and it became the standard test case for any proposed strong constant.

**Computational catalogues (Jiménez Calvo, Herzog, and others, 2000s).** Systematic searches assembled lists of "good" examples with small Hall ratio, refining the record table and probing whether infinitely many examples with $r \to 0$ exist. These near-misses sharpen the question of the true infimum of the Hall ratio without settling it.

**Baker-method effective bounds.** For each fixed $k$, integral solutions of $y^2 = x^3 + k$ are effectively bounded via linear forms in logarithms (and curves resolved in tables up to large $|k|$, e.g. by Gebel, Pethő and Zimmer). These are real theorems but of the wrong shape: they bound $x$ exponentially in $k$ for individual $k$ and yield no uniform power-law lower bound on the gap.

**Disputed / unaccepted route via $abc$ (Mochizuki).** Because weak Hall follows from $abc$, Shinichi Mochizuki's claimed 2012 proof of $abc$ (published 2021 in *PRIMS*) would, if correct, settle weak Hall. The proof, built on **inter-universal Teichmüller theory**, is **not accepted by the broad mathematical community**. The principal objection, articulated by **Peter Scholze and Jakob Stix (2018)**, is that a key inequality in "Corollary 3.12" is unjustified — they argue the identification of certain objects collapses the claimed quantitative bound. Mochizuki disputes their reading. Pending resolution of this dispute, weak Hall cannot be regarded as proven via this channel; it is stated here neutrally as an open controversy, not an established result.

**No serious claimed proof of the integer case itself.** Unlike some open problems, Hall's conjecture has not attracted a body of retracted direct proofs; the difficulty is so transparently tied to $abc$/Vojta that essentially all credible work is either the function-field theorem, conditional deductions, or computation. The honest state is: the strong form is open with its constant in genuine doubt, and the weak form is open unconditionally (and would follow from any accepted proof of $abc$).
