# Originator(s) — The Hodge Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## William Vallance Douglas Hodge (1903–1975)

W. V. D. Hodge was a Scottish mathematician, born 17 June 1903 in Edinburgh. He studied at the University of Edinburgh and then at St John's College, Cambridge, where the influence of the Cambridge geometric tradition — and contact with the Italian school of algebraic geometry — shaped his early interests. After a period at Bristol, he returned to Cambridge, becoming the **Lowndean Professor of Astronomy and Geometry** in 1936 and, later, Master of Pembroke College. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1938 and knighted in 1959; he served as a leading figure in British mathematics until his death on 7 July 1975.

## Mathematical background and the road to harmonic integrals

Hodge's defining achievement was the **theory of harmonic integrals**, which he developed through the 1930s and presented systematically in his 1941 monograph *The Theory and Applications of Harmonic Integrals*. The core idea fused three strands:

- **Riemann's and Lefschetz's topology of algebraic varieties** — period integrals, the homology of complex manifolds, and Lefschetz's hard and weak theorems on hyperplane sections.
- **De Rham's theorem**, identifying cohomology with closed differential forms modulo exact ones.
- **Elliptic PDE / potential theory** — the recognition that within each de Rham class there is a *unique harmonic representative*, a form annihilated by the Laplace–Beltrami operator of a chosen (Kähler) metric.

On a compact Kähler manifold the Laplacian respects the bidegree $(p,q)$ of forms, and this is precisely what yields the **Hodge decomposition**. The original proofs in the 1941 book contained a gap in the existence theory for harmonic forms; this was repaired rigorously by **Hermann Weyl** (the "method of orthogonal projection") and, in greater generality, by the elliptic theory of **Kodaira** and others. The fully rigorous Hodge theory that resulted became one of the foundational pillars of modern complex and algebraic geometry.

## Why he posed the conjecture

Hodge understood that his decomposition gave a purely *analytic/topological* invariant — the spaces $H^{p,q}$ — sitting on top of a variety whose origin was *algebraic*. Algebraic subvarieties (cycles) contribute classes living in the diagonal pieces $H^{p,p}$. The natural and ambitious question was whether these geometric cycles **account for all** of the rational $(p,p)$ cohomology. The $p=1$ case was already a theorem (Lefschetz's $(1,1)$-theorem), tying $H^{1,1}\cap H^2(\mathbb{Z})$ to divisors. Hodge's conjecture is the bold extrapolation of that single known case to all codimensions, articulated in his **1950 International Congress of Mathematicians address** in Cambridge, Massachusetts, "The topological invariants of algebraic varieties."

## Historical root vs. modern formulation

Hodge's 1950 statement was, as posed, partly incorrect. He included an **integral** version (later disproved by Atiyah–Hirzebruch, 1962) and a "general" form about deeper levels of the Hodge filtration that **Grothendieck (1969)** showed to be false "for trivial reasons," replacing it with the amended general Hodge conjecture. The surviving, sharpened statement — rational coefficients, smooth projective varieties, codimension-$p$ Hodge classes — is the one enshrined as a Clay Millennium Problem.

## Legacy

Beyond the conjecture, Hodge co-authored with **Daniel Pedoe** the influential three-volume *Methods of Algebraic Geometry*, a bridge between the classical Italian school and modern foundations. **Hodge theory** — Hodge structures, variations of Hodge structure, mixed Hodge structures (Deligne), and the entire industry of period maps — descends directly from his work and underlies much of twentieth- and twenty-first-century algebraic geometry, transcendental number theory, and mathematical physics. The London Mathematical Society's Hodge Prize and the Hodge Institute in Edinburgh both honour his name.
