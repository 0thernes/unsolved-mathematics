# Top Mathematicians — The Hodge Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | W. V. D. Hodge | 1903–1975 | originator | Theory of harmonic integrals and the Hodge decomposition; posed the conjecture (1950). |
| 2 | Solomon Lefschetz | 1884–1972 | breakthrough | The $(1,1)$-theorem (1924), the proven codimension-1 case and template for the conjecture. |
| 3 | Michael Atiyah | 1929–2019 | breakthrough | With Hirzebruch, disproved the integral Hodge conjecture (1962), fixing rational coefficients. |
| 4 | Friedrich Hirzebruch | 1927–2012 | breakthrough | Co-author of the Atiyah–Hirzebruch counterexample via topological $K$-theory and Steenrod operations. |
| 5 | Alexander Grothendieck | 1928–2014 | breakthrough | Corrected the general Hodge conjecture (1969); standard conjectures framing cycles cohomologically. |
| 6 | Pierre Deligne | b. 1944 | frontier | Absolute Hodge cycles; Hodge classes on abelian varieties are absolutely Hodge; author of the Clay statement. |
| 7 | Phillip A. Griffiths | b. 1938 | frontier | Transcendental methods, variation of Hodge structure, normal functions; intermediate Jacobians. |
| 8 | Claire Voisin | b. 1962 | frontier | Kähler counterexample (2002); foundational textbooks; integral-Hodge obstruction results. |
| 9 | Eduardo Cattani / Aroldo Kaplan | 20th–21st c. | frontier | With Deligne, proved algebraicity of Hodge loci (1995), the strongest unconditional evidence. |
| 10 | James D. Lewis | b. 1953 | expositor | Authored the standard *Survey of the Hodge Conjecture*, shaping how the problem is taught. |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of **complex algebraic geometry, Hodge theory, and arithmetic geometry**. Core contemporary figures include Claire Voisin (Collège de France), Pierre Deligne (IAS, emeritus), and the **Hodge-loci / o-minimality** circle — Bruno Klingler, Benjamin Bakker, Jacob Tsimerman, and collaborators — who have reproved and extended Cattani–Deligne–Kaplan using definability (tame geometry). A second cluster works on **normal functions and singularities** (Gregory Pearlstein, Patrick Brosnan, Matt Kerr, Mark Green, and the late–career program of Griffiths). Arithmetic analogues (the **Tate conjecture**, motivated cycles) are pushed by Yves André, and by researchers on $K3$ surfaces and Shimura varieties. Much current activity is *indirect*: rather than attacking the full statement, the field accumulates structural results (algebraicity of Hodge loci, motivic realizations, special families) that the conjecture predicts. The problem is widely regarded as one of the hardest of the Millennium Problems, with no consensus route to resolution.
