# Approaches — The Hodge Conjecture

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

## The Lefschetz $(1,1)$-theorem and induction by codimension

The only fully proved instance is codimension $p=1$: the **Lefschetz $(1,1)$-theorem** identifies $H^{1,1}(X)\cap H^2(X,\mathbb{Z})$ with the classes of divisors, via the exponential sequence and the Picard group. The natural hope is to climb in codimension. **Hard Lefschetz** and the **Lefschetz hyperplane theorem** let one transfer cohomology between $X$ and its hyperplane sections, and the **Lefschetz standard conjecture** (that the inverse of hard Lefschetz is induced by an algebraic correspondence) would yield large parts of the Hodge picture. *Best result:* hard Lefschetz is a theorem (Hodge theory; Deligne in char $p$), but the standard conjecture itself is open, so this route stalls. *Barrier:* no general mechanism produces cycles in codimension $\ge 2$; products and intersections of divisors generate only a (generally small) subring of $H^{2p,p}$.

## Absolute Hodge cycles and the abelian-variety case

Deligne's theory of **absolute Hodge cycles** (Deligne–Milne–Ogus–Shih, 1982) provides Hodge classes that are simultaneously Hodge in every $\ell$-adic and de Rham realization. Deligne **proved every Hodge class on an abelian variety is absolutely Hodge**, a major structural result and the strongest unconditional evidence for the conjecture on abelian varieties. *Best result:* the Hodge conjecture is known for abelian varieties whose Hodge classes are generated by divisors (e.g. simple abelian varieties of prime dimension, products of elliptic curves; many cases via the **Hodge group / Mumford–Tate group** being large). *Barrier:* the **Weil and Mumford–Tate abelian varieties** carry "exotic" Hodge classes not built from divisors; for these the conjecture remains open, and absolute-Hodge-ness does not by itself produce a cycle.

## Mumford–Tate groups and the André–Deligne machinery

One reframes Hodge classes as the invariants of the **Mumford–Tate group** acting on cohomology; algebraicity questions become questions about which invariants are "motivated." André's theory of **motivated cycles** unconditionally realizes many Hodge classes (those obtained from hard Lefschetz and Künneth) as motivated, capturing properties expected of algebraic cycles. *Best result:* the standard conjectures hold for abelian varieties' motivated motives; Künneth and Lefschetz components are motivated. *Barrier:* "motivated" is weaker than "algebraic"; bridging that final gap is exactly the open problem.

## Normal functions and Griffiths' infinitesimal method

Griffiths revived **Lefschetz's normal functions** as a tool: a Hodge class spreads out over a family and defines a normal function (a section of a family of intermediate Jacobians); the class is algebraic iff a certain singularity / zero-locus condition holds. Green–Griffiths and Brosnan–Fang–Nie–Pearlstein turned this into a program: the Hodge conjecture would follow from the existence of **singularities of normal functions** along the right loci. *Best result:* the **Hodge conjecture is equivalent** to such a singularity statement after spreading out (Green–Griffiths, de Cataldo–Migliorini variants); the algebraicity of the zero locus of admissible normal functions (Brosnan–Pearlstein, building on Cattani–Deligne–Kaplan's algebraicity of Hodge loci) is established. *Barrier:* producing the singularities themselves — i.e. actually constructing the cycle — has not been achieved in general.

## Cattani–Deligne–Kaplan: algebraicity of Hodge loci

A landmark *unconditional* result: **Cattani, Deligne, and Kaplan (1995)** proved that the locus where a given Hodge class remains Hodge (the **Hodge locus**) is a countable union of algebraic subvarieties of the base. This is exactly what the Hodge conjecture predicts (Hodge loci should be cut out by the algebraicity of cycles) and is strong evidence, recently reproved and extended by o-minimality / **definability** methods (Bakker–Klingler–Tsimerman, 2020). *Barrier:* algebraicity of the *locus* does not produce an algebraic *cycle* realizing the class.

## Hodge theory in characteristic $p$ and the Tate conjecture

The **Tate conjecture** is the arithmetic analogue (Galois-invariant $\ell$-adic classes are algebraic). Progress on Tate (e.g. for $K3$ surfaces, abelian varieties by Faltings, Tate, Zarhin) informs Hodge via comparison theorems. *Barrier:* the two conjectures are independent in general; neither is known to imply the other unconditionally.

## Negative and limiting results (obstructions)

- **Integral Hodge conjecture is false** (Atiyah–Hirzebruch, 1962; refined by Kollár's degree examples and Totaro): torsion and non-torsion integral classes can be non-algebraic. This forces rational coefficients and is a hard ceiling on naive integral strengthenings.
- **Kähler (non-projective) version is false** (Voisin, 2002): there exist compact Kähler manifolds with Hodge classes not generated by classes of *analytic* cycles, so the conjecture is genuinely tied to **projectivity**.
- **Griffiths' non-finitely-generated / non-algebraic intermediate Jacobians** and **Mumford's infinite-dimensionality** of $0$-cycles show the Chow-group / cycle-class map is far from an isomorphism, dimming hopes for a soft surjectivity argument.
- There is no known **diagonal cycle / motivic** construction outputting codimension-$\ge 2$ cycles from cohomological input — the structural shortage of cycles, not a logical obstruction, is the central difficulty.
