# Top Mathematicians — The Catalan–Dickson Conjecture (Aliquot Sequences)

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Eugène Catalan | 1814–1894 | originator | Posed the original 1888 conjecture that every aliquot sequence terminates at $1$ or a perfect number. |
| 2 | Leonard Eugene Dickson | 1874–1954 | originator | Corrected the statement (1913) to the modern form — every aliquot sequence is bounded and ends in a cycle; documented the theory in his *History*. |
| 3 | Paul Poulet | 1887–1946 | breakthrough | Discovered long sociable cycles (1929), establishing that endpoints extend beyond perfect and amicable numbers. |
| 4 | Derrick Henry Lehmer | 1905–1991 | breakthrough | Pioneered machine computation of aliquot sequences, isolating the "Lehmer five" open small starters. |
| 5 | Richard K. Guy | 1916–2020 | originator | Co-authored the Guy–Selfridge counter-conjecture (1975); codified the problem in *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory* §B6. |
| 6 | John L. Selfridge | 1927–2010 | breakthrough | Co-developed the drivers/guides heuristic predicting that many aliquot sequences are unbounded. |
| 7 | Herman te Riele | b. 1947 | breakthrough | Major computational and theoretical study of aliquot sequences; extended open starters and analyzed amicable structure. |
| 8 | Paul Erdős | 1913–1996 | frontier | Founded the rigorous statistical theory of iterated $\sigma$/$s$, including density of abundant numbers and image regularities. |
| 9 | Carl Pomerance | b. 1944 | frontier | With collaborators, proved structural theorems on the image and fibers of $s$, untouchable-number density, and sociable-number distribution. |
| 10 | Wieb Bosma | b. 1958 | frontier | Determined the "aliquot constant" (average of $\log(s(n)/n)$, with Kane) and ran large first-step computations sharpening the divergence heuristic. |

## Notes

The active community is small but cohesive, sitting at the intersection of analytic and computational number theory. The *theoretical* frontier is led by Carl Pomerance, Paul Pollack, and their coauthors (Kobayashi, and earlier collaborators of Erdős), who supply unconditional results on the local structure of $s$ — fibers, image, untouchable numbers, sociable-number counts. The *statistical* strand, anchored by Wieb Bosma and Ben Kane, quantifies one-step dynamics. The *computational* strand — Herman te Riele, Wolfgang Creyaufmüller, Paul Zimmermann, and the volunteer FactorDB / "aliquot sequences" project — relentlessly extends the Lehmer five and tabulates open starters, with integer factorization (NFS, ECM) as the binding constraint. Richard Guy's *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory* remains the standard entry point, and the OEIS hosts the canonical reference sequences. No single group is positioned to prove the conjecture either way; progress is incremental and distributed across these complementary efforts.
