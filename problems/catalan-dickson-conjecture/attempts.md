# Attempts — The Catalan–Dickson Conjecture (Aliquot Sequences)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The conjecture has attracted no widely circulated *claimed proof* in either direction; its history is instead one of partial results, large computations, and the gradual hardening of expert opinion against it. The notable episodes are accumulations of evidence and structural theorems rather than disputed solutions.

## Catalan's original over-claim and Dickson's correction

The first "attempt" was Catalan's own 1888 statement that every aliquot sequence ends at $1$ or a perfect number. This was a *near-miss in formulation*: it ignored amicable and longer sociable cycles, which are genuine endpoints. Dickson's 1913 correction is best read as the first decisive partial advance — fixing the target so that the question became the durable one we have today.

## The Lehmer five and the limits of computation

The most sustained "attempt" is the open-ended computational program on $276, 552, 564, 660, 966$. D. H. Lehmer's mid-1960s machine runs first identified these as the obstinate small cases. Decades of extension by Guy, Selfridge, te Riele, Creyaufmüller, Zimmermann, and the FactorDB community have pushed $276$ past two thousand terms and beyond $200$-digit values without resolution. This is a near-miss only in spirit: every extension *could* have ended the sequence and did not. It is structurally incapable of proving divergence, so it cannot resolve the conjecture — a point repeatedly stressed by the practitioners themselves.

## The Guy–Selfridge counter-conjecture as a reasoned reversal

Guy and Selfridge (1975) did not attempt to prove Catalan–Dickson; they argued it is *false*. Their drivers-and-guides analysis is the closest thing to a "solution attempt" with a definite verdict, predicting unbounded sequences of positive density. The community broadly regards their heuristic as more likely correct than the original conjecture, but it remains unproved and is, by construction, not a proof. Stating the dispute neutrally: the heuristic's central assumption — that a driver persists long enough, and that the expected upward drift is never reversed over an infinite orbit — has no rigorous justification, and a skeptic can object that quasi-independence of successive terms is assumed rather than established.

## Genuine partial theorems (the real progress)

Several unconditional results stand as legitimate near-misses on *related* questions:

- **Erdős** established that the iterated $\sigma$/$s$ behavior obeys strong statistical regularities, and that abundant numbers have positive density — rigorous evidence consistent with divergence but far short of it.
- **Bosma and Kane** computed the precise average of $\log(s(n)/n)$ and the proportion of integers increasing under one step, sharpening the heuristic into measured fact for the first step.
- **Pomerance and Pollack** (with collaborators) proved positive density of untouchable numbers and sharp bounds on the fibers $s^{-1}(m)$, controlling the local structure of the map.

None of these touches the infinite-iterate boundedness question directly; they are honest partial results, not contested claims.

## Status of disputes

There is no famous retracted or disputed *proof* of the Catalan–Dickson conjecture to report. The honest summary is that the problem has resisted all attempts at a decisive argument; the live tension is between an unproved conjecture (Catalan–Dickson) and an unproved counter-conjecture (Guy–Selfridge), with the weight of computational and statistical evidence — but no proof — favoring the latter.
