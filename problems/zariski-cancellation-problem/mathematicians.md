# Top Mathematicians — The Zariski Cancellation Problem

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Oscar Zariski | 1899–1986 | originator | Raised the cancellation question for affine space (c. 1949) within his program to characterize $\mathbb{A}^n$ via commutative algebra. |
| 2 | Shreeram S. Abhyankar | 1930–2012 | breakthrough | With Eakin and Heinzer, settled the $n=1$ case: a curve whose cylinder is the plane is the line. |
| 3 | Paul Eakin | 20th c. | breakthrough | Co-author of the Abhyankar–Eakin–Heinzer cancellation results for one-dimensional rings. |
| 4 | William Heinzer | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | Co-author of the foundational $n=1$ cancellation and coefficient-ring uniqueness papers. |
| 5 | Masayoshi Miyanishi | 1940– | breakthrough | Structure theory of affine surfaces and $\mathbb{A}^1$-fibrations; with Sugie proved cancellation for surfaces in characteristic $0$. |
| 6 | Tohru Sugie | 20th c. | breakthrough | Co-proved the characteristic-zero surface cancellation theorem ($n=2$). |
| 7 | Takao Fujita | 1949– | breakthrough | Independent proof of Zariski cancellation for affine surfaces in characteristic zero. |
| 8 | Teruo Asanuma | 20th–21st c. | frontier | Constructed the characteristic-$p$ deformation threefolds whose cylinders are polynomial rings — the engine of the eventual counterexamples. |
| 9 | Leonid Makar-Limanov | 1944– | breakthrough | Invented the Makar-Limanov (AK) invariant; proved the Russell cubic is not $\mathbb{A}^3$, supplying the decisive tool for the field. |
| 10 | Neena Gupta | 1984– | frontier | Proved ZCP is *false* in positive characteristic for $n\ge 3$ (2014), the first counterexample in any setting; leading expositor of the open char-$0$ case. |

## Notes

The community is small but vigorous, centered on **affine algebraic geometry** and concentrated in India (ISI Kolkata, where Gupta works, and the school of S. M. Bhatwadekar and Amartya K. Dutta), Japan (the Osaka/Miyanishi tradition), Canada (Peter Russell and the Koras–Russell collaboration at McGill), France (Adrien Dubouloz, Jean Fasel, and the $\mathbb{A}^1$-homotopy/motivic wing in Dijon and Grenoble), and the U.S. (Makar-Limanov, Gene Freudenburg, Shulim Kaliman, and the locally-nilpotent-derivation circle). The toolkit — locally nilpotent derivations, $\mathbb{G}_a$-actions, the Makar-Limanov and Derksen invariants, and increasingly $\mathbb{A}^1$-homotopy theory — links cancellation to neighbouring open problems (the Jacobian conjecture, embedding and linearization questions, characterizations of $\mathbb{A}^n$). Gupta's 2014 breakthrough reinvigorated the area and sharpened attention on the still-open characteristic-zero threefold case, now the recognized frontier.
