# Originator(s) — The Tate Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## John Tate (1925–2019)

John Torrence Tate Jr. was born on 13 March 1925 in Minneapolis, Minnesota, the son of a physicist. He took his undergraduate degree at Harvard and entered Princeton intending to study physics, but moved to mathematics under the supervision of **Emil Artin**, completing his 1950 PhD thesis "Fourier analysis in number fields and Hecke's zeta functions." That thesis — universally known as **Tate's thesis** — recast Hecke's work on $L$-functions in the adelic language of harmonic analysis on $\mathbb{A}_k^\times$, and it remains the template for the modern analytic theory of automorphic $L$-functions. From the outset Tate's instinct was to find the *structural* object behind an arithmetic phenomenon.

Tate spent most of his career at Harvard (from 1954) and then, from 1990, at the University of Texas at Austin, where he held the Sid W. Richardson chair until his retirement. He was awarded the Wolf Prize (2002/2003) and the **Abel Prize in 2010** "for his vast and lasting impact on the theory of numbers."

## Mathematical background and motivation

Tate's mathematics sits at the meeting point of class field theory, Galois cohomology, and algebraic geometry. He co-developed (with Artin) the cohomological formulation of class field theory; introduced **Tate cohomology**, the **Tate module** $T_\ell$, **Tate twists**, $p$-divisible (Barsotti–Tate) groups, rigid analytic spaces and the **Tate curve**, and the **Tate–Shafarevich group** — a roster of named objects rivalled by few twentieth-century mathematicians.

The conjecture emerged directly from this milieu. Having reformulated $L$-functions adelically and absorbed Grothendieck's new étale cohomology, Tate asked the natural arithmetic question: the Weil conjectures predict that the zeros and poles of zeta functions encode geometry, so *which* cohomology classes come from honest subvarieties? The Hodge conjecture answered "the rational $(p,p)$ classes" over $\mathbb{C}$. Tate's analogue replaced Hodge theory's complex structure with the Galois action: the classes that "should" be algebraic are those fixed by Frobenius and the Galois group. He framed this in his 1962/1963 Woods Hole and Stockholm ICM material and then *proved the first hard case himself* in 1966, establishing the conjecture for endomorphisms of abelian varieties over finite fields. That proof, via $p$-divisible groups and a finiteness argument, became a model for the entire subject and directly inspired Faltings's 1983 work.

## Two formulations, one idea

It is worth distinguishing the historical root from the modern statement. Tate's **1966 theorem** is about $H^1$ of abelian varieties — equivalently, about the Tate module being a faithful invariant of the isogeny class. The **general conjecture** (as in his 1994 Motives survey) is about cycle-class maps into $H^{2i}(i)$ for arbitrary smooth projective varieties and is intertwined with the conjectured semisimplicity of Frobenius and with the "strong" pole-order statement. The abelian-variety case is the engine that drives nearly every later breakthrough.

## Legacy

Tate's broader legacy is foundational: through students such as Joe Buhler, Benedict Gross, Robert Kottwitz, V. Kumar Murty, Carl Pomerance, Ken Ribet, and Joseph Silverman, and through the objects bearing his name, he shaped arithmetic geometry as practiced today. The Tate and Hodge conjectures together form the central pair of "algebraicity of cohomology classes" problems, organizing the conjectural theory of motives. Tate died on 16 October 2019 in Lexington, Massachusetts.
