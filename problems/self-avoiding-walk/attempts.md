# Attempts — Self-Avoiding Walk (Scaling & Connective Constant)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The SAW literature is unusually clean: it has produced striking *partial* theorems rather than a trail of disputed "proofs." Because the central conjectures (exact 2D exponents, conformal invariance) are so hard, few have claimed full resolutions, and there is no famous retracted proof. The notable items are genuine near-misses and conditional successes.

**The honeycomb connective constant (success, but only one lattice).** Nienhuis predicted in 1982 that $\mu = \sqrt{2+\sqrt2}$ on the hexagonal lattice. For nearly thirty years this resisted proof despite being a clean algebraic number. Duminil-Copin and Smirnov closed it in 2010 (published 2012) with the parafermionic observable. The "near-miss" character is that the *method does not transfer*: the analogous constants for $\mathbb{Z}^2$, $\mathbb{Z}^3$, or the triangular lattice remain without exact rigorous values, even though the honeycomb proof shows the technique is sound. This is the field's defining tension — a beautiful exact result that stubbornly localizes to one geometry.

**Conformal invariance via LSW (conditional, not unconditional).** Lawler–Schramm–Werner (2004) proved that *if* a conformally invariant scaling limit exists, it is $\mathrm{SLE}_{8/3}$. This is rigorous and complete as a conditional statement, but the hypothesis — existence and conformal covariance of the limit — has never been verified for SAW. It stands in pointed contrast to percolation and the Ising model, where the analogous convergence *was* proven (Smirnov), and remains the most consequential open gap.

**Hara–Slade and the gap at $d=2,3,4$.** The lace expansion settles $d\ge 5$ completely. Repeated efforts to push it toward $d=4$ from above, or to find an analogous convergent expansion below the critical dimension, have not succeeded for the strictly self-avoiding model. The rigorous $d=4$ logarithmic-correction results of Bauerschmidt–Brydges–Slade apply only to the *weakly* self-avoiding (Domb–Joyce) variant; transferring them to the strict SAW is open. No claim of a $d=3$ or strict-$d=4$ proof has held up.

**Ratio limit and the "obvious" conjecture.** It is widely believed and numerically certain that $c_{n+1}/c_n \to \mu$ on $\mathbb{Z}^d$. Hammersley–Welsh (1962) proved the weaker $c_n \le \mu^n \exp(O(\sqrt n))$, and Kesten's pattern theorem (1963) gives $c_{n+2}/c_n\to\mu^2$. The clean statement $c_{n+1}/c_n\to\mu$ for hypercubic lattices is, surprisingly, still unproven — a famous "easy-looking" gap that has frustrated many. Kesten's bound was sharpened over the decades (e.g. Hammond and others reduced the subexponential correction), but the full ratio limit on $\mathbb{Z}^d$ has not fallen.

**Flory theory as a cautionary near-miss.** Flory's mean-field prediction $\nu=3/(d+2)$ matches the conjectured $\nu=3/4$ exactly in 2D and is close in 3D — but it is now understood to be *correct for the wrong reasons*, with two large errors that fortuitously cancel. Several attempts to "derive" the exact exponents by improving Flory's energy–entropy balance produced inconsistent values, illustrating that physical heuristics, however accurate, do not yield proofs and can mislead about mechanism.

**Numerical "determinations."** High-precision Monte Carlo (Clisby's pivot work) and exact series analysis report $\nu$ and $\mu$ to many digits, occasionally phrased as having "determined" the exponents. These are not disputed as numerics but are sometimes overstated: they are evidence, not theorems, and cannot certify exact rationality. The consensus remains that no exponent in $d=2$ or $d=3$ has a rigorous proof.
