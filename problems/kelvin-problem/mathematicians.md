# Top Mathematicians — The Kelvin Problem (Optimal Space Partition)

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) | 1824–1907 | originator | Posed the problem (1887); proposed the relaxed truncated-octahedron foam as the conjectured optimal partition. |
| 2 | Joseph Plateau | 1801–1883 | originator | Codified the geometric laws of soap-film equilibrium ($120°$ triple junctions, tetrahedral-angle vertices) that any minimal partition must obey. |
| 3 | Denis Weaire | b. 1942 | breakthrough | Co-discovered (1993) the Weaire–Phelan structure that beats Kelvin's; leading expositor of foam geometry. |
| 4 | Robert Phelan | b. 1960s | breakthrough | Co-discovered the Weaire–Phelan structure using Surface Evolver, refuting Kelvin's optimality conjecture. |
| 5 | Kenneth A. Brakke | b. 1954 | breakthrough | Created the Surface Evolver software, the computational engine that made the Weaire–Phelan discovery possible. |
| 6 | Jean E. Taylor | b. 1944 | breakthrough | Proved (1976) Plateau's laws rigorously, fixing the local singularity structure of any area-minimizing foam. |
| 7 | Frederick J. Almgren | 1933–1997 | breakthrough | Developed the geometric measure theory of $(M,\varepsilon,\delta)$-minimal sets underpinning foam regularity. |
| 8 | Thomas C. Hales | b. 1958 | breakthrough | Proved the planar honeycomb conjecture (1999/2001), the $2$D analogue and template for a possible $3$D proof. |
| 9 | John M. Sullivan | b. 1963 | frontier | Reframed the Kelvin problem in minimal-surface / TCP-structure terms; surveyed candidate optimal partitions. |
| 10 | Andrew M. Kraynik | contemporary | frontier | Large-scale computational studies of monodisperse foams and the Kelvin problem; tested candidate structures against WP. |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of geometric measure theory, computational geometry, and soft-matter physics. On the physics and computation side, the Dublin school around **Denis Weaire** and **Stefan Hutzler** (Trinity College Dublin) remains the center of gravity for foam structure, with **Kenneth Brakke**'s Surface Evolver still the standard tool for generating and relaxing candidate partitions; **Andrew Kraynik**, **Douglas Reinelt**, and collaborators contribute large-scale numerical foam studies. On the rigorous-mathematics side, the relevant expertise lives in minimal-surface and minimal-cluster theory — the lineage of **Almgren** and **Jean Taylor**, with **Frank Morgan** as a prominent expositor of isoperimetric and clustering problems, and **Thomas Hales** whose honeycomb proof defines what a $3$D resolution would have to achieve. The two cultures interact loosely: physicists supply candidate structures and high-confidence numerics (WP beats Kelvin, nothing beats WP), while geometers supply the regularity theory, but no group has bridged the gap to a global lower bound. The problem is low-traffic but durable, frequently cited as a benchmark open question in optimal partitioning, materials science, and architecture (the Beijing "Water Cube").
