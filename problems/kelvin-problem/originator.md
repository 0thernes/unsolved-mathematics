# Originator(s) — The Kelvin Problem (Optimal Space Partition)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## William Thomson, Lord Kelvin (1824–1907)

William Thomson was born in Belfast on 26 June 1824, the son of James Thomson, a mathematics teacher who became professor at the University of Glasgow. A prodigy, William matriculated at Glasgow at age ten, then read mathematics at Peterhouse, Cambridge, graduating Second Wrangler in 1845. He spent 1845 in Paris in Regnault's laboratory, absorbing the French tradition of mathematical physics (Fourier, Carnot, Laplace) that shaped his lifelong style. In 1846, aged just twenty-two, he was elected to the chair of natural philosophy at Glasgow, which he held for fifty-three years.

Thomson's output spans nearly every branch of nineteenth-century physical science. He formulated an absolute temperature scale (the kelvin bears his name), co-founded thermodynamics with the statement of its second law, and worked out the dynamical theory of heat. He was the scientific architect of the first successful transatlantic telegraph cable (1866), inventing the mirror galvanometer and siphon recorder to make it work — the achievement that brought him a knighthood in 1866 and, in 1892, a peerage as Baron Kelvin of Largs, the first British scientist elevated to the House of Lords. He held more than seventy patents and grew wealthy from his engineering.

## Why he posed the problem

By the 1880s Thomson was preoccupied with **vortex atoms** and with elastic models of the **luminiferous aether** — the hypothetical medium thought to carry light. He sought mechanical structures of space that were homogeneous, isotropic on average, and minimal in energy. A foam of equal-volume cells, with energy proportional to interfacial film area, was a concrete realization: the least-area partition would be the aether's natural ground state. This is the motivation behind the 1887 paper "On the Division of Space with Minimum Partitional Area."

Thomson approached the question as both geometer and experimentalist. He understood that a polyhedral honeycomb could not be a genuine energy minimizer, because soap-film equilibrium (Plateau's laws) forbids the sharp dihedral angles of the flat truncated octahedron. He therefore _relaxed_ the cell: curving the hexagonal faces and bowing the edges so that films meet three-at-a-time at $120°$. He built physical wire-frame and stereoscopic models to display the curved Kelvin cell, anticipating by a century the computational foam studies of Brakke and Weaire–Phelan. His conviction — never proven — was that this relaxed truncated octahedron was the optimal partition.

## Legacy and the modern reframing

Kelvin's conjecture stood unchallenged for $106$ years, becoming a textbook "known answer" in materials science and crystallography (the bcc Wigner–Seitz cell is exactly the truncated octahedron). Its fall in 1993 to the Weaire–Phelan structure is one of the more dramatic reversals in geometric problem-solving — a long-assumed optimum dethroned by computer experiment. Yet the underlying **question** Kelvin posed is sharper than his guessed answer, and it remains his: the modern Kelvin problem asks for the _true_ global minimizer, with WP now the leading candidate and Kelvin's foam the historical benchmark. The problem thus has a single clear root (Kelvin, 1887) and an evolving frontier; what changed in 1993 was the conjectured solution, not the formulation.

Thomson died on 17 December 1907 and is buried in Westminster Abbey beside Newton. His name endures in the kelvin, the Kelvin cell, the Thomson effect, and — fittingly for a man who modeled the void — in an open geometry problem about how best to fill space.
