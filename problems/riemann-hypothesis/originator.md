# Originator(s) — The Riemann Hypothesis

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Bernhard Riemann (1826–1866)

Georg Friedrich Bernhard Riemann was born on 17 September 1826 in Breselenz, in the Kingdom of Hanover, the second of six children of a Lutheran pastor. The family was poor and Riemann was shy, devout, and frequently ill; he would die of tuberculosis at thirty-nine. He showed early mathematical precocity—an oft-repeated anecdote has him reading and quickly mastering Legendre's hefty treatise on number theory as a schoolboy. Intending at first to study theology to support his family, he was permitted by his father to switch to mathematics.

He entered the University of Göttingen in 1846, where Gauss lectured, then moved to Berlin (1847–1849) to study under Dirichlet, Jacobi, and Eisenstein—an environment far more active in analysis than Göttingen at the time. Dirichlet's analytic methods in number theory, and his way of thinking conceptually rather than computationally, left a deep imprint. Riemann returned to Göttingen, completing his doctoral thesis on complex function theory (introducing Riemann surfaces) in 1851 under Gauss. His 1854 *Habilitation* lecture, *"Über die Hypothesen, welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen,"* founded Riemannian geometry and supplied, half a century later, the mathematical language of general relativity. He became a full professor at Göttingen in 1859, the same year as the prime-number memoir, and succeeded Dirichlet in the chair Gauss had held.

## The ideas behind the conjecture

Riemann's number-theoretic work was atypical for him—a single paper—but it grew organically from his mastery of complex analysis. Euler had related $\zeta(s)=\sum n^{-s}$ to the primes; Dirichlet had used $L$-functions to prove primes in arithmetic progressions. Riemann's innovation was to study $\zeta(s)$ as an analytic function of a *complex* argument and to exploit its global structure. He established the functional equation, recognized that the distribution of primes is governed by the complex zeros of $\zeta$, and derived an explicit formula in which each zero contributes an oscillatory term to the count of primes. From the symmetry of the functional equation about $\Re(s)=\tfrac12$ and from numerical evidence (he computed several zeros by hand, far more than he published), he conjectured that all non-trivial zeros lie exactly on that line. His motivation was thus concrete: optimal control of the prime-counting error term. RH is the cleanest possible statement that the primes are distributed "as regularly as possible."

Notably, the conjecture as Riemann stated it is essentially the modern one; unlike many problems whose formulation drifted over time, RH has remained verbatim for over 160 years. What has changed is the surrounding theory—generalizations to $L$-functions, spectral interpretations, and the random-matrix connection.

## Legacy

Riemann's collected works fill a single slim volume, yet they seeded entire fields: Riemann surfaces and algebraic geometry, the Riemann integral, Riemannian geometry, the Riemann–Roch theorem, the Cauchy–Riemann framework, and analytic number theory. After his death his Göttingen *Nachlass* was partly burned by a housekeeper before scholars could examine it; Siegel's 1932 reconstruction of surviving notes revealed the Riemann–Siegel formula, showing Riemann had developed deep computational machinery he never published. Riemann is widely regarded as among the most influential mathematicians in history, and RH stands as his most famous unsolved bequest—Hilbert placed it eighth on his 1900 list and reportedly said that, were he to awaken after a thousand years, his first question would be whether RH had been proved.
