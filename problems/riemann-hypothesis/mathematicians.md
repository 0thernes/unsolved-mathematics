# Top Mathematicians — The Riemann Hypothesis

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Bernhard Riemann | 1826–1866 | originator | 1859 memoir: complex $\zeta(s)$, functional equation, explicit formula, and the conjecture itself |
| 2 | Jacques Hadamard | 1865–1963 | breakthrough | Theory of entire functions; co-proof of the Prime Number Theorem via $\zeta(1+it)\ne0$ (1896) |
| 3 | Charles-Jean de la Vallée Poussin | 1866–1962 | breakthrough | Independent proof of the PNT and the first quantitative zero-free region (1896) |
| 4 | G. H. Hardy | 1877–1947 | breakthrough | Proved infinitely many zeros lie on the critical line (1914); with Littlewood, deep results on $\zeta$ |
| 5 | Carl Ludwig Siegel | 1896–1981 | breakthrough | Recovered the Riemann–Siegel formula from Riemann's *Nachlass* (1932), enabling modern computation |
| 6 | Atle Selberg | 1917–2007 | breakthrough | Positive proportion of zeros on the line (1942); Selberg trace formula; elementary proof of PNT |
| 7 | Norman Levinson | 1912–1975 | breakthrough | Showed more than one third of zeros lie on the critical line (1974) |
| 8 | Hugh Montgomery | b. 1944 | frontier | Pair-correlation conjecture (1973), opening the random-matrix connection |
| 9 | J. Brian Conrey | b. 1955 | frontier | Over two fifths of zeros on the line (1989); moments and random-matrix theory of $L$-functions |
| 10 | Alain Connes | b. 1947 | frontier | Spectral/trace-formula reformulation of RH via noncommutative geometry (1997–1999) |

## Notes

The active community spans several overlapping schools. The analytic-number-theory tradition—Conrey, Soundararajan, Young, Bui, and others, building on Levinson and Selberg—pursues critical-line proportions, moments, and zero-density estimates. The random-matrix program initiated by Montgomery and Dyson and developed by Keating, Snaith, Rubinstein, and the CFKRS collaboration links $\zeta$-statistics to the GUE and predicts moment asymptotics. The spectral/noncommutative-geometry direction follows Connes, with input from Berry and Keating on candidate Hamiltonians. Terence Tao and Brad Rodgers brought the de Bruijn–Newman constant program to a head (2020), and the Polymath15 collaboration sharpened its upper bound. Computational verification—Odlyzko historically, and Platt, Trudgian, and others today—continues to test RH to enormous heights and to certify zero-free regions rigorously. Peter Sarnak and Enrico Bombieri have shaped the modern framing of the problem and its place among the family of $L$-functions and the Langlands program.
