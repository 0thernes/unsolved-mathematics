# Originator(s) — The Palis Conjecture (Finitude / Density of Attractors)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Jacob Palis (born 1940)

Jacob Palis Jr. is a Brazilian mathematician, born 15 March 1940 in Uberaba, Minas Gerais. He trained as an engineer at the University of Brazil (Rio de Janeiro) before turning to mathematics, and completed his Ph.D. at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1967 under **Stephen Smale**, then the central figure of the emerging theory of dynamical systems. That apprenticeship placed Palis at the source of the hyperbolic paradigm — Smale's Axiom A, the horseshoe, structural stability — and shaped a lifelong project: to understand the *global* organization of the space of all dynamical systems, not merely individual examples.

Returning to Brazil, Palis became the architect of what is now one of the world's leading dynamics schools at **IMPA** (Instituto de Matemática Pura e Aplicada) in Rio de Janeiro. As researcher, director, and mentor he supervised or co-supervised a remarkable lineage — including Welington de Melo, Ricardo Mañé, Marcelo Viana, Jean-Christophe Yoccoz (collaborator), Carlos Gustavo Moreira, Enrique Pujals, and many others — that carried his program forward. He served as President of the Third World Academy of Sciences and of the International Mathematical Union (1999–2002), and received the Balzan Prize (2010) and the Wolf Prize in Mathematics (2014, with Yoccoz), among the highest distinctions in the field.

## The ideas behind the conjecture

Palis's central technical contributions are in **homoclinic bifurcation theory**. With Floris Takens he developed the systematic study of how dynamical complexity is *created and destroyed* at homoclinic tangencies — the moment a stable and unstable manifold touch non-transversally. Their work (and the famous "Palis–Takens" fractal-dimension and Hausdorff-dimension criteria) showed that the boundary between simple (hyperbolic) and complicated (chaotic, non-hyperbolic) behavior is governed by the geometry of these tangencies and the thickness of associated Cantor sets.

The conjecture is the natural capstone of that thinking. Having seen that hyperbolicity cannot be *topologically* dense — Newhouse's infinitely-many-sinks phenomenon forbids it — Palis asked the sharper question: what is true for *almost every* system, in the measure-theoretic sense that physicists care about? His answer, his "global view," is that the pathologies (infinitely many attractors, statistical instability) are confined to negligible, measure-zero sets, and that the typical system is *finitely describable*: a finite list of attractors, each with a physical SRB measure governing the time-averages of almost every orbit, and the whole picture robust under perturbation. This reframes the foundational goal of dynamics from "classify everything" to "the generic statistical behavior is tame."

## Legacy

The conjecture functions less as a single theorem to be proved than as a **research program** — "the Palis program" — that has organized the field for three decades. It motivated the theory of dominated splittings and partial hyperbolicity (Bonatti–Díaz–Viana), the $C^1$-generic dichotomies of Mañé, Pujals–Sambarino, and Crovisier, the one-dimensional density-of-hyperbolicity theorems of Kozlovski–Shen–van Strien, and the modern theory of SRB measures for non-uniformly hyperbolic systems. Even where the full statement remains far out of reach, its sub-questions have become some of the most fertile problems in smooth ergodic theory. The modern formulation is essentially Palis's own; what has shifted is the toolkit (from $C^2$ surface dynamics toward $C^1$ techniques and high-dimensional partial hyperbolicity) rather than the goal.
