# Top Mathematicians — The Erdős Unit Distance Problem

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Paul Erdős | 1946–1996 | originator | Posed the problem (1946); gave the grid lower bound $n^{1+c/\log\log n}$ and the conjecture $u(n)=n^{1+o(1)}$; sustained it as a central open problem for fifty years. |
| 2 | Endre Szemerédi | 1972– | breakthrough | Co-authored the first sub-$n^{3/2}$ improvement (with Józsa) and the $O(n^{4/3})$ bound; co-architect of the Szemerédi–Trotter incidence theorem underlying the field. |
| 3 | William T. Trotter | 1983– | breakthrough | Co-author of the Szemerédi–Trotter theorem and of the $O(n^{4/3})$ unit-distance bound — the record upper bound. |
| 4 | Joel Spencer | 1984 | breakthrough | Co-author of Spencer–Szemerédi–Trotter (1984), the paper establishing the still-standing $O(n^{4/3})$ upper bound. |
| 5 | László Székely | 1997 | breakthrough | Gave the elegant crossing-number proof of $O(n^{4/3})$, making the bound accessible and extensible to many variants. |
| 6 | János Pach | 1980s– | expositor | Central organizer of discrete geometry; co-author of the standard references and many refinements of incidence and unit-distance bounds. |
| 7 | Micha Sharir | 1990– | frontier | Co-developer of curve-arrangement incidence bounds and polynomial-partitioning incidence results bearing directly on point–circle counts. |
| 8 | Larry Guth | 2010– | breakthrough | With Katz, introduced the polynomial-partitioning method that settled the sibling distinct-distances problem and reshaped the toolkit for unit distances. |
| 9 | Nets Hawk Katz | 2010– | breakthrough | Co-author of the Guth–Katz resolution of distinct distances; key figure in the algebraic/polynomial approach to Erdős distance problems. |
| 10 | Adam Sheffer | 2010s– | frontier | Leading expositor and researcher of modern incidence theory; surveys and polynomial-method results on unit/distinct distances and structured configurations. |

## Notes

The active community sits at the interface of **discrete and combinatorial geometry**, **incidence theory**, and the **polynomial method**. Beyond those tabled above, frequent contributors include Joshua Zahl (sharp incidence and $\mathbb{R}^3$ unit-distance bounds), Frank de Zeeuw, József Solymosi, Orit Raz, and Gábor Tardos, with the broader Pach–Sharir school supplying the algorithmic and arrangement-theoretic backbone. The Guth–Katz breakthrough of 2010 redrew the field's expectations: it proved the polynomial method can crush one Erdős distance problem, which both energized attempts on the unit-distance count and clarified — through their repeated non-transfer — exactly why this problem is harder. Today the frontier is pursued in two registers: structural/arithmetic results on what an extremal set must look like, and barrier results (notably non-Euclidean norms achieving $\Theta(n^{4/3})$) that constrain which methods could ever succeed.
