# Attempts — Vaught's Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Morley's trichotomy (1970) — the defining near-miss

Michael Morley's theorem that $I(T,\aleph_0)\in\{0,1,\dots,\aleph_0,\aleph_1,2^{\aleph_0}\}$ is the result that *almost* settles the question: it removes every value except the single one in dispute. Morley showed via Scott analysis that if there is no perfect set of countable models then there are at most $\aleph_1$, so the only way the conjecture can fail is exactly $\aleph_1 < 2^{\aleph_0}$. This is the reference point against which every later attempt measures itself: one must rule out one cardinal.

## Harrington–Makkai–Shelah (1981) — $\omega$-stable theories

The proof of VC for $\omega$-stable theories is the deepest positive result. It is a genuine partial theorem, not a near-miss: for $T$ $\omega$-stable, $I(T,\aleph_0)$ is countable or $2^{\aleph_0}$. The argument fuses stability theory (prime models, the geometry of forking) with admissible-set theory and the model theory of $L_{\omega_1\omega}$. Its limitation is structural: it consumes $\omega$-stability essentially and offers no foothold in the strictly stable or unstable case.

## Steel (1978) — theories of trees

John Steel proved VC for the class of theories of trees, an early and influential confirmation in a combinatorially rich setting. The methods (well-founded analysis of tree models, perfect-set extraction) seeded later descriptive-set-theoretic treatments. It is a confirming instance rather than a step toward the general statement.

## Mayer (1988) — o-minimal theories

Laura Mayer proved Vaught's Conjecture for **o-minimal theories**, exploiting the strong structure theory (cell decomposition, monotonicity, finiteness) of o-minimal expansions of orders. Together with the linear-order results it shows VC is robust across the major "tame" model-theoretic classes — but again by class-specific invariants that do not generalize.

## Hjorth (2002) — refutation of the *topological* conjecture (a cautionary near-result)

Greg Hjorth constructed a **Polish group action with exactly $\aleph_1$ orbits** (consistently, under $\neg$CH), refuting the **Topological Vaught Conjecture for general Polish groups**. This is not a counterexample to Vaught's Conjecture — the group is not $S_\infty$ and the orbit relation is not isomorphism of first-order structures — but it is a decisive *negative* result: it proves that VC cannot follow from any purely topological dichotomy valid for all Polish groups. Any correct proof must use features special to $S_\infty$ or to first-order definability. It reoriented the field by closing off the most attractive "soft" strategy.

## Knight's claimed counterexample (announced c. 1998–2007) — DISPUTED, not accepted

**Claim.** Robin W. Knight (Oxford) announced and circulated a very long manuscript describing a purported **counterexample** to Vaught's Conjecture — a structure/theory built from an intricate topological "ell-space" construction engineered to realize exactly $\aleph_1$ countable models without a perfect set.

**Status.** The claim has **not** been accepted by the model-theory and descriptive-set-theory communities. The manuscript ran to hundreds of pages, was repeatedly revised, and was never published in a refereed venue as a confirmed counterexample. Specialists who examined it (in seminars and informal reviews) did not certify the construction; the consensus is that the proof is **not verified**, and the problem remains open. Stated neutrally: the construction is regarded as unconfirmed rather than as a refuted error — the burden of a fully checkable argument was not met to the community's satisfaction. No published erratum or formal retraction exists, but no expert treats VC as settled by it.

## Sustained partial programs (ongoing, no full proof)

- **Superstable finite-rank** and selected **stable** cases extend the 1981 result incrementally.
- **Counting-Scott-sentence** approaches in $L_{\omega_1\omega}$ aim to bound the number of models combinatorially; they recover Morley's theorem and sharpen it in special cases but do not close the $\aleph_1$ gap.
- **Martin's-Conjecture-flavored** degree-theoretic attacks give conditional structural constraints; they depend on another open problem.

The honest summary: there are strong, fully accepted **partial theorems** (Morley, Steel, Mayer, Harrington–Makkai–Shelah), one important **negative** result one level up (Hjorth), and one **disputed/unverified** claimed counterexample (Knight). The general conjecture is open.
