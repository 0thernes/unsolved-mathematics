# Attempts — Greenberg's Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The history of Greenberg's Conjecture is one of accumulating partial results and large-scale numerical confirmation rather than dramatic claimed proofs. The problem has not attracted the spate of erroneous "solutions" seen for more famous targets, partly because its statement is technical and the experts working on it have built incrementally and carefully. What follows are the substantive partial advances and the honest limits of each.

## Ferrero–Washington: the $\mu = 0$ near-resolution

The single most decisive result toward the conjecture is the theorem of Bruce Ferrero and Lawrence Washington (1979) that $\mu_p = 0$ for every abelian number field. This removed the $\mu$-invariant obstacle in the abelian case entirely. It is not a proof of Greenberg's Conjecture — the $\lambda$ half remains — but it is the reason the conjecture is usually phrased as "$\lambda_p = 0$ for abelian totally real fields." For non-abelian totally real fields, even $\mu_p = 0$ is not known in general, which is a frequently overlooked gap.

## Greenberg's own criteria and their reach

In the original 1976 paper Greenberg gave sufficient conditions for $\lambda = \mu = 0$ in terms of units and $p$-adic $L$-values, and verified the conjecture for various small cases. These criteria were sharpened over decades (Fukuda, Komatsu, Ozaki, Taya, Itoh, Nishino) to prove vanishing for infinitely many real quadratic and cubic fields under hypotheses on how $p$ splits and on cyclotomic-unit indices. Each such theorem is a genuine partial result, but each carries a splitting or index hypothesis that cannot presently be removed, so none extends to all totally real fields.

## Computational verification — confirmation, not proof

The conjecture has been checked numerically on a massive scale. Kraft and Schoof, Fukuda and Komatsu, Ozaki and Taya, and many others verified $\lambda_p = \mu_p = 0$ for thousands of real quadratic fields and small primes ($p = 3, 5, 7, \dots$), and for families of cubic and biquadratic fields. Fukuda's stabilization criterion makes such checks rigorous and finite. No counterexample has ever been found. This is strong evidence but, by its nature, cannot resolve a statement quantifying over all fields and primes.

## Reductions and conditional theorems

A recurring strategy has been to prove Greenberg's Conjecture under another (also open) hypothesis — typically the Gross–Kuz'min conjecture, the non-vanishing of a $p$-adic regulator, or Leopoldt's conjecture. Brumer's $p$-adic analogue of Baker's theorem yields Gross–Kuz'min (hence inputs toward Greenberg) in many abelian situations. These are honest implications, but they trade one open problem for another and do not constitute an unconditional proof.

## On claimed full proofs

There is no widely circulated, accepted unconditional proof of Greenberg's Conjecture in general, and there is no famous retracted claim comparable to those attached to other open problems. Occasional preprints assert proofs for special families or under extra hypotheses; these should be read as conditional or restricted results until refereed, and the burden — as with any such claim — is to control the zeros of the relevant $p$-adic $L$-function or the non-vanishing of a $p$-adic regulator without circularity. The community consensus, as of the present, is that the conjecture remains open: overwhelmingly supported, proven in many families, but not established in general for all totally real fields and all primes.
