# Approaches — Greenberg's Conjecture

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

## Cyclotomic units and the analytic class number formula

**Core idea.** For abelian totally real $k$, the Ferrero–Washington theorem gives $\mu_p = 0$, so only $\lambda_p = 0$ remains. By the Iwasawa Main Conjecture (Mazur–Wiles), the characteristic ideal of the relevant $\Lambda$-module equals the ideal generated by a $p$-adic $L$-function, and $\lambda_p$ equals the number of zeros (with multiplicity) of that $p$-adic $L$-function in the open unit disk. The strategy is to control these zeros by relating them to the index of cyclotomic units inside the full unit group and to special values of $p$-adic $L$-functions.

**Best result.** This line yields the cleanest sufficient criteria: e.g., for real quadratic $k = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ and a prime $p$ that does not split (or that splits with controlled behaviour), $\lambda_p = 0$ holds whenever a certain $p$-adic regulator or cyclotomic-unit index is a $p$-adic unit. Greenberg's original criteria and their refinements by Fukuda–Komatsu and Ozaki–Taya prove the conjecture for infinitely many fields and verify it computationally for vast tables.

**Barrier.** The criterion is sufficient but not necessary, and it can fail when $p$ divides the relevant index. There is no known mechanism forcing the $p$-adic $L$-function to be a unit in general; controlling its zeros is exactly as hard as the conjecture itself. The method also leans on $\mu_p = 0$, which is open outside the abelian case.

## Capitulation and the structure of $X_\infty$

**Core idea.** $\lambda_p = \mu_p = 0$ is equivalent to finiteness of $X_\infty = \varprojlim A_n$, where $A_n$ is the $p$-class group of $k_n$. By Iwasawa's control theory, finiteness is governed by capitulation: whether ideal classes of $k_n$ become trivial (capitulate) in $k_{n+1}$. One studies the maps $A_n \to A_{n+1}$ and the "Greenberg criterion": the conjecture holds iff a certain map becomes an isomorphism for large $n$, equivalently iff capitulation is total beyond some level.

**Best result.** Fukuda's stabilization theorem gives an effective stopping rule: if $A_n$ and $A_{n+1}$ have the same $p$-rank at one suitable level (with a splitting hypothesis on $p$), then the invariants vanish. This converts the infinite question into a finite computation and underlies essentially all numerical verifications.

**Barrier.** The criterion requires reaching a level where stabilization is visible; a priori bounds on that level are not available in general. For fields with large $p$-class groups or where $p$ ramifies intricately, the required level can be out of computational reach, and there is no theoretical guarantee that stabilization ever occurs without already assuming the conjecture.

## Genus theory and ambiguous classes

**Core idea.** Decompose $A_n$ using the action of $\mathrm{Gal}(k_n/k)$. Ambiguous (fixed) classes and the genus number can be computed from ramification and unit data, giving lower and upper bounds on $|A_n|$. For specific families (e.g., $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ with prescribed splitting of $p$), genus theory pins down the growth precisely.

**Best result.** Sharp results for real quadratic and biquadratic fields under conditions on the decomposition of $p$, recovering and extending Greenberg's criteria. Especially effective when $p$ is inert or has a single prime above it.

**Barrier.** Genus theory only sees the "ambiguous" part; the non-genus contribution to $\lambda$ is not controlled, so the method gives bounds rather than vanishing in general, and degrades as the number of primes above $p$ grows.

## Reduction to Gross–Kuz'min and $p$-adic regulators

**Core idea.** Greenberg's Conjecture is intertwined with the Gross–Kuz'min conjecture (finiteness of a related Galois module / non-vanishing of a $p$-adic regulator). One seeks to derive $\lambda_p = 0$ from non-vanishing of $p$-adic regulators of global units, or to prove implications between the two conjectures.

**Best result.** In several settings the conjecture is equivalent to, or follows from, the non-vanishing of a specific $p$-adic regulator; Gross–Kuz'min is itself known in many abelian cases (via Brumer's $p$-adic Baker theorem on linear forms in $p$-adic logarithms), giving conditional and some unconditional progress.

**Barrier.** $p$-adic regulators can in principle vanish or be highly divisible; ruling this out is a transcendence-flavored problem with no general solution. Gross–Kuz'min itself is open in general, so this is a reduction between hard problems rather than a resolution.

## Galois deformation and module-theoretic methods

**Core idea.** Study $X_\infty$ as a $\Lambda$-module via its Fitting ideals, projective dimension, and the structure of Selmer modules; use control theorems and the algebraic side of the Main Conjecture to constrain $\lambda$.

**Best result.** Module-theoretic results (Greenberg, Ozaki) classify possible $\Lambda$-module structures and prove vanishing under hypotheses on the number of ramified primes and on the triviality of certain cohomology groups; Ozaki has constructed fields realizing prescribed module structures, clarifying what is and is not possible.

**Barrier — a genuine obstruction.** Ozaki and others showed that totally real fields can have arbitrarily large class groups and intricate $\Lambda$-module structure, so no purely "soft" structural argument can force $\lambda = 0$; any proof must use the deep analytic/transcendence input (non-vanishing of $L$-values or regulators). This is the principal reason the conjecture has resisted: the algebraic side alone is insufficient, and the analytic side requires controlling zeros of $p$-adic $L$-functions, for which no general technique exists.
