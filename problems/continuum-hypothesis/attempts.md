# Attempts — The Continuum Hypothesis

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

**Cantor's own proofs (1880s–1890s).** Cantor repeatedly believed he had proved CH and announced it, then retracted. His genuine partial result was the Cantor–Bendixson theorem (1883), which establishes CH for closed sets: every uncountable closed set of reals contains a perfect subset and so has cardinality $\mathfrak{c}$. He could not extend this beyond the closed sets, and we now know — for arbitrary sets — that no such extension is provable in ZFC.

**König's claim (1904).** At the Heidelberg International Congress, Julius König presented a proof that the continuum cannot be well-ordered, which would have refuted CH (indeed contradicted the Axiom of Choice). The argument relied on a cardinal-arithmetic formula of Felix Bernstein, $\aleph_\alpha^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_\alpha\cdot 2^{\aleph_0}$, applied at a limit of cofinality $\omega$ where it fails. The error was identified within days (Zermelo and others); the salvageable core became **König's theorem**, which yields the one genuine ZFC constraint on the continuum, $\mathrm{cf}(2^{\aleph_0})>\aleph_0$, so e.g. $2^{\aleph_0}\neq\aleph_\omega$.

**Hilbert's attempt (1925/26).** In "Über das Unendliche," Hilbert sketched a proof of CH via his proof-theoretic program and a hierarchy of definable functions. The argument was incomplete and is not regarded as valid; it foreshadowed the constructible hierarchy but did not anticipate independence. It is historically important as a serious effort by a leading figure that nonetheless failed.

**The metatheorems as the decisive "near-miss."** The deepest reason all direct attempts failed is structural, made precise by Gödel (1938–40, CH consistent via $L$) and Cohen (1963, $\neg$CH consistent via forcing). After Cohen, the search for a ZFC proof or refutation was understood to be futile; this is the rare case where the impossibility of resolution within the stated axioms is itself a theorem.

**Modern "resolution" claims and the live dispute.** No retracted formal proof of CH exists in the modern era, because CH cannot be proved in ZFC. The active controversy is instead about which *new axioms* settle CH and whether the question even has a determinate answer:

- The **multiverse view** (Joel David Hamkins, 2012) holds that CH is settled in the relevant sense — by the existence of many set-theoretic universes in some of which CH holds and in others fails — so the question of CH's "true" value is, on this view, mistaken. Critics (notably the "universe" or absolutist camp, including Woodin and Koellner) object that this abandons the realist hope of a single intended universe and is methodologically premature.

- Woodin's program has itself shifted sides. His 1999–2001 $\Omega$-logic work argued for $\neg$CH ($\mathfrak{c}=\aleph_2$); his later **Ultimate-$L$** program points toward CH. This reversal is sometimes cited by skeptics as evidence that the "right axioms" question is not yet stable. Defenders counter that the reversal reflects new mathematical information (the centrality of inner-model theory at the supercompact level), not arbitrariness.

- The **Aspero–Schindler theorem** (2019/2021), proving $\mathrm{MM}^{++}\Rightarrow(*)$, removed a longstanding doubt on the $\neg$CH side by unifying Martin's Maximum with Woodin's axiom $(*)$, both of which give $\mathfrak{c}=\aleph_2$. It is widely regarded as the strongest recent evidence that, *if* an axiom decides CH, the answer is $\neg$CH — though it does not establish CH's negation as a theorem and is itself answered by the opposing Ultimate-$L$ considerations.

The neutral summary: there is no accepted proof or refutation of CH, none is possible from ZFC, and the dispute is genuinely open between principled programs reaching opposite verdicts.
