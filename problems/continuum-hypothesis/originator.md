# Originator(s) — The Continuum Hypothesis

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

**Georg Ferdinand Ludwig Philipp Cantor** (1845–1918) was the founder of set theory and the originator of the Continuum Hypothesis. Born in Saint Petersburg to a merchant family of Danish-Jewish and Russian-Lutheran descent, he moved with his family to Germany in 1856. He studied at the ETH Zürich and then in Berlin under Karl Weierstrass, Ernst Kummer, and Leopold Kronecker, completing his doctorate in 1867 on number theory. He spent essentially his entire career at the University of Halle, where the absence of a chair at a more prestigious institution — partly engineered by Kronecker's hostility — was a lasting frustration.

Cantor's path to CH ran through analysis. While working on the uniqueness of trigonometric series in the early 1870s, he was led to study point sets on the real line and their derived sets, building the iterated Cantor–Bendixson operation. This forced him to take infinite sets as objects in their own right. His revolutionary insight, published in 1874, was that infinity comes in **different sizes**: a diagonal-style argument (refined into the famous diagonal method by 1891) showed $\mathbb{R}$ cannot be put in bijection with $\mathbb{N}$, whereas the algebraic numbers can. The natural question — are there sizes strictly between $|\mathbb{N}|$ and $|\mathbb{R}|$? — is the Continuum Hypothesis, stated in 1878.

Cantor developed the full transfinite apparatus to attack it: ordinal numbers, the alephs $\aleph_0, \aleph_1, \dots$, cardinal arithmetic, and the well-ordering principle. He conjectured that the continuum sits at exactly the second infinite cardinal, $2^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_1$, and was convinced he could prove it. He repeatedly announced proofs and as often withdrew them. The mathematical resistance — and Kronecker's philosophical opposition to completed infinities — combined with Cantor's own bipolar illness to produce recurring breakdowns from 1884 onward. He died in a sanatorium in Halle in 1918.

Cantor's motivation was both technical and philosophical. He saw the transfinite numbers as a genuine extension of mathematics into the actual infinite, defended on theological as well as mathematical grounds, and held a strongly realist (Platonist) view: CH has a definite truth value waiting to be discovered. This realism is exactly the stance that animates the modern program — most explicitly in Gödel's 1947 essay and Woodin's work — to find new axioms that settle CH, on the conviction that independence from ZFC reflects the weakness of our current axioms rather than any indeterminacy in the mathematical universe.

The **historical formulation** (no intermediate cardinality between $\mathbb{N}$ and $\mathbb{R}$) and the **modern formulation** ($2^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_1$) coincide under the Axiom of Choice, which guarantees that $\mathfrak{c}$ is an aleph; without Choice the two can come apart, and CH is usually studied within ZFC. Cantor's legacy is foundational in the literal sense: set theory became the common language of mathematics, the alephs and cardinal arithmetic are his, and the independence phenomenon that CH first exhibited reshaped the philosophy of mathematics. Hilbert's tribute — "No one shall expel us from the paradise that Cantor has created" — captures the magnitude of what he began.
