# Originator(s) — Lehmer's Mahler Measure Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

**Derrick Henry Lehmer** (1905–1991) was an American mathematician whose career centered on computational and algorithmic number theory long before electronic computers made such work routine. He was the son of **Derrick Norman Lehmer** (1867–1938), himself a number theorist at the University of California, Berkeley, famous for building mechanical factoring devices (the "number sieve" machines). The younger Lehmer grew up immersed in the practical art of computing with integers — factoring, primality testing, and the construction of tables — and this hands-on sensibility shaped everything he did.

Lehmer studied at Berkeley and earned his Ph.D. at Brown University in 1930 under **Jacob Tamarkin**, with a thesis on the arithmetic of Lucas sequences and related recurrences. He held positions at Lehigh, the Institute for Advanced Study, and the National Bureau of Standards (where during the 1940s he was associated with early computing efforts, including the ENIAC), before settling at Berkeley, where he spent the bulk of his career. With his wife and frequent collaborator **Emma Lehmer**, he produced a long stream of work on primality, Diophantine equations, and computational verification of conjectures.

The 1933 paper "Factorization of certain cyclotomic functions" (Annals of Mathematics) grew directly out of this computational program. Lehmer was studying sequences $\Delta_n = \prod_i(\alpha_i^n - 1)$ attached to a monic integer polynomial — generalizations of the Pierce numbers — as a possible engine for producing large primes. The efficiency of such a prime search depends on the sequence growing slowly, and the asymptotic growth rate is exactly $\log M(P)$, the logarithm of what is now called the Mahler measure. Lehmer therefore sought integer polynomials with measure just barely above $1$. Kronecker's theorem told him that measure exactly $1$ forces the polynomial to be cyclotomic (roots of unity), which gives only periodic, useless sequences. The natural question — how close to $1$ can the measure of a non-cyclotomic polynomial be? — was thus a wholly practical concern dressed in deep arithmetic. After extensive hand computation he produced his degree-$10$ reciprocal polynomial with measure $\lambda_0 = 1.17628\ldots$ and could find nothing smaller. He famously remarked that whether such polynomials of arbitrarily small measure exist was a question he could not answer.

It is worth stressing what Lehmer did **not** claim. He did not conjecture that $\lambda_0$ is the minimum; he posed an open question about whether the infimum exceeds $1$. The strong assertion that $\lambda_0$ itself is optimal is a later folklore crystallization, supported by seventy years of failed searches but never asserted by Lehmer himself. This restraint is characteristic: Lehmer was an empiricist who reported what the numbers showed and resisted overreach.

The quantity he isolated had in fact been studied independently and more systematically by **Kurt Mahler** in the early 1960s, who introduced it in the context of polynomial heights and transcendence, which is why the measure bears Mahler's name rather than Lehmer's. The two strands — Lehmer's prime-hunting and Mahler's height theory — merged into the modern subject.

Lehmer's broader legacy is enormous: the **Lucas–Lehmer test** for Mersenne primes (the workhorse of modern record prime searches), foundational work in computational number theory, the Lehmer sieve machines, and a still-open companion problem on the totient function ("Lehmer's totient problem"). The polynomial $L(x)$, his number $\lambda_0$, and the conjecture they spawned remain among the most quoted unsolved problems in Diophantine number theory, a testament to how a question born from a concrete computation can outlive its original purpose and become a deep structural mystery about heights and algebraic integers.
