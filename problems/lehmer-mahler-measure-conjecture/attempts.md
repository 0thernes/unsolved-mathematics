# Attempts — Lehmer's Mahler Measure Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The history of Lehmer's problem is notable for the *absence* of a flood of disputed proofs that surrounds more famous conjectures. Because the statement is unusually crisp and the experts (Boyd, Smyth, Dobrowolski, Amoroso) are vigilant, the field has accumulated solid partial results rather than dramatic false claims. The "near-misses" are genuine theorems that fall just short of the full statement.

**Smyth's non-reciprocal theorem (1971)** is the most consequential near-miss. Smyth proved that any non-reciprocal algebraic integer has $M(\alpha) \ge \theta_0 = 1.32472\ldots$ (the plastic number / smallest Pisot number), a *complete* and sharp Lehmer-type bound for that class. It does not resolve the conjecture only because the extremal candidate $\lambda_0$ comes from a *reciprocal* polynomial, and reciprocal polynomials are precisely the case Smyth's method cannot reach. The result effectively cut the problem in half and remains the cleanest "almost-solution."

**Dobrowolski's bound (1979)** is the canonical near-miss in the opposite direction: it applies to *all* polynomials but only gives $M(\alpha) > 1 + (1-\varepsilon)(\log\log d/\log d)^3$, which tends to $1$. Repeated attempts over four decades to upgrade the auxiliary-polynomial method to a degree-independent constant have improved the implied constants (Cantor–Straus 1981, Louboutin 1983, Voutier 1996 — who gave the clean explicit form $M(\alpha) \ge 1 + \tfrac14(\log\log d/\log d)^3$ for $d\ge 2$) but never the asymptotic shape. This persistent failure is itself evidence that a new ingredient is needed.

**The Schinzel–Zassenhaus connection.** A closely related and historically intertwined problem asks for a lower bound on the *house* (largest conjugate modulus) of an algebraic integer. The **Schinzel–Zassenhaus conjecture** — that the house exceeds $1 + c/d$ — was a long-standing companion to Lehmer's problem and was **proved by Vesselin Dimitrov in 2019** (building on a "metric" / Pólya-type integral inequality, later streamlined by Dimitrov–Habegger and others). This is a genuine resolution of a sibling conjecture, and it raised hopes that similar metric ideas could attack Lehmer's measure conjecture. To date, however, Dimitrov's method does not yield the Mahler-measure gap: bounding the house is strictly weaker than bounding the measure, and the breakthrough has not transferred. It is important to state plainly that **Lehmer's conjecture itself remains open** — only the related Schinzel–Zassenhaus statement was settled.

**Abelian and structured-field results (Amoroso–Dvornicich 2000; Amoroso–Zannier)** are not disputed but are sometimes misread in popular accounts as "solving Lehmer." They prove a uniform height bound only under strong Galois hypotheses (abelian extensions, or numbers with controlled local behaviour). The neutral statement of their scope is essential: they do not bound $M(\alpha)$ uniformly for arbitrary reciprocal integer polynomials.

**Computational claims.** Periodic announcements of a Salem number smaller than $\lambda_0$ have surfaced informally over the decades; none has survived scrutiny. The exhaustive searches of Boyd, Mossinghoff, Rhin, Flammang, and Wu — extending past degree $40$ and across very large coefficient ranges — have repeatedly confirmed Lehmer's number as the smallest known measure exceeding $1$, with the next candidates (e.g., Lehmer's number's relatives and the degree-$18$ Salem numbers) all larger. No retraction of a *claimed proof* is on record; the discipline's caution has kept the literature clean. The honest summary: many sharp partial theorems, one resolved sibling (Schinzel–Zassenhaus), and the central conjecture untouched.
