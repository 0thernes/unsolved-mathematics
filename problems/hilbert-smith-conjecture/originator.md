# Originator(s) — The Hilbert–Smith Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture carries two names because it sits at the junction of a famous problem posed by **David Hilbert** and a precise reformulation supplied by **Paul A. Smith**.

## David Hilbert (1862–1943)

Hilbert, born in Königsberg and for most of his career the dominant figure at Göttingen, shaped nearly every branch of early-twentieth-century mathematics: invariant theory, algebraic number theory, the foundations of geometry, integral equations, mathematical physics, and the foundations of mathematics. His address to the 1900 International Congress of Mathematicians in Paris listed twenty-three problems that set much of the agenda for the century.

The **fifth problem** asked, in modern language, whether every locally Euclidean topological group is a Lie group — whether the differentiability assumed in Lie's theory of continuous groups could be derived rather than postulated. Hilbert's motivation came from the foundations of geometry (his _Grundlagen der Geometrie_, 1899) and from Lie's program: he wanted to know how much of the rigid analytic apparatus of transformation groups was forced by mere topology. The narrow group-theoretic question was settled affirmatively by Gleason, Montgomery–Zippin, and Yamabe around 1952. But the broader spirit of the problem — understanding which abstract groups can act on which spaces — generated the action-theoretic strengthening that is the Hilbert–Smith conjecture. In this sense Hilbert is the lineage originator: the conjecture is the still-open residue of his fifth problem once "group" is replaced by "transformation group."

## Paul Althaus Smith (1900–1980)

Smith, an American topologist, took his Ph.D. at Princeton in 1926 under Solomon Lefschetz, and spent much of his career at Barnard College and Columbia University. He was a foundational figure in the topology of transformation groups, and his name attaches to **Smith theory** — the homological study of the fixed-point sets of periodic maps, especially $\mathbb{Z}_p$-actions on spheres and other spaces. Smith theory shows, for example, that the fixed-point set of a $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action on a mod-$p$ homology sphere is again a mod-$p$ homology sphere, a result that became a cornerstone of equivariant topology.

It was precisely this mastery of $p$-primary periodic actions that let Smith see the heart of Hilbert's question. He recognized, building on M. H. A. Newman's 1931 theorem and the structure theory of compact groups, that the entire Hilbert-fifth-problem-for-actions could be reduced to ruling out a single hypothetical phenomenon: a faithful action of the compact group of **$p$-adic integers** $\mathbb{Z}_p = \varprojlim \mathbb{Z}/p^n$ on a manifold. A $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action would be an inverse limit of cyclic actions of order $p^n$ with arbitrarily small orbits — exactly what Newman's theorem makes implausible on a manifold but does not, by itself, forbid. Smith's 1941 formulation turned a vague structural worry into a crisp, testable conjecture, and that is the form mathematicians work on today.

## Legacy and the two formulations

The **historical root** is Hilbert's fifth problem: is a locally Euclidean topological group a Lie group? — now a theorem. The **modern formulation**, due to Smith, is the action-theoretic conjecture: a locally compact group acting effectively on a connected manifold is a Lie group, equivalently no $\mathbb{Z}_p$ acts effectively on a manifold. Smith's reduction is what makes the problem tractable enough to attack; Hilbert's name records its intellectual descent. Both men left vast legacies — Hilbert across all of mathematics, Smith across equivariant topology — and their joint conjecture remains one of the last open fragments of the 1900 list still resisting full resolution in dimensions four and above.
