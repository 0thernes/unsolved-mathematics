# Attempts — The Hilbert–Smith Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Confirmed special cases (genuine partial results)

- **Dimension 2 (surfaces).** The conjecture holds for actions on $2$-manifolds, established through Montgomery–Zippin-style structure theory and Smith theory; a $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action on a surface is impossible. This was understood relatively early and is uncontroversial.

- **Dimension 3.** Settled affirmatively by **John Pardon** in 2013 (_"The Hilbert–Smith conjecture for three-manifolds,"_ Journal of the AMS / arXiv:1112.2324). Pardon shows a free $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action on a $3$-manifold would produce a quotient of impossible cohomological dimension, ruling out all topological actions in dimension three. This is the headline modern result and is accepted; it is dimension-specific and does not extend to $n\geq 4$.

- **Lipschitz and quasi-conformal actions, all dimensions.** **Repovš and Ščepin** (1997) proved that no $\mathbb{Z}_p$ can act effectively by Lipschitz homeomorphisms on a Riemannian manifold, and related work extends this to quasi-conformal actions (Martin and collaborators). These are full-dimension theorems but only under regularity hypotheses far stronger than mere continuity.

- **Smooth and real-analytic actions.** The conjecture is classically true for actions by diffeomorphisms (a compact group acting smoothly and effectively on a manifold is a Lie group, via Bochner–Montgomery and Montgomery–Zippin). The genuine difficulty is entirely in the purely **topological** category.

- **Hölder-regularity results.** Subsequent work (e.g. by Maleshich and others) pushed the Repovš–Ščepin technique to certain Hölder exponents, narrowing — but not closing — the gap between Lipschitz and continuous.

## Near-misses and partial frameworks

- **Yang's dimension estimates (1957–60).** C. T. Yang's computation that a hypothetical $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action raises cohomological dimension by exactly $2$ is the closest the cohomological program comes to a contradiction; in dimensions $2$ and $3$ it suffices, but in higher dimensions the resulting $(n+2)$-dimensional orbit space is not by itself contradictory.

- **Newman's theorem (1931)** is repeatedly the "almost there" ingredient: it forbids the small-orbit behavior at every finite stage $\mathbb{Z}/p^n$, but the bounds need not survive the inverse limit in high dimension.

## On claimed full proofs

The general Hilbert–Smith conjecture (all dimensions, topological actions) has attracted **announced or preprinted claims of a complete proof** from time to time, as is common for long-standing problems. As of the current frontier none has been accepted by the community: the consensus, reflected in the literature and surveys, is that the conjecture remains **open for $n \geq 4$**. Where specific preprints have circulated claiming the full result, the standard objection has been a gap in passing from finite-stage estimates to the $p$-adic inverse limit, or an implicit regularity/manifold-structure assumption smuggled into a step that is only valid in the Lipschitz or low-dimensional setting. This dossier does not endorse any such claim; the verifiable record is the set of partial results above (surfaces, $n=3$, Lipschitz/quasiconformal, smooth), all of which are accepted, and the open status of the topological case in dimension four and higher.

The honest summary: there are no famous _retracted_ proofs that entered the canon, but there is a recurring pattern of optimistic announcements that do not withstand scrutiny at exactly the inverse-limit step. Readers should treat any "Hilbert–Smith solved" report with caution until it appears in a refereed venue with community vetting.
