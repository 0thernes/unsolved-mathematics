# Top Mathematicians — The Hilbert–Smith Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | David Hilbert | 1862–1943 | originator | Posed the fifth problem (1900) on continuous transformation groups, the lineage source of the conjecture. |
| 2 | Paul A. Smith | 1900–1980 | originator | Reduced the problem to ruling out effective $\mathbb{Z}_p$-actions on manifolds; founder of Smith theory. |
| 3 | M. H. A. Newman | 1897–1984 | breakthrough | 1931 theorem bounding small periodic transformations of manifolds, a core local obstruction. |
| 4 | Deane Montgomery | 1909–1992 | breakthrough | With Zippin, solved Hilbert's fifth problem and built the structure theory of transformation groups. |
| 5 | Leo Zippin | 1905–1995 | breakthrough | Co-author of the Montgomery–Zippin solution and the standard monograph _Topological Transformation Groups_. |
| 6 | Andrew M. Gleason | 1921–2008 | breakthrough | "Groups without small subgroups" (1952), the no-small-subgroups criterion central to the reduction. |
| 7 | Hidehiko Yamabe | 1923–1960 | breakthrough | Completed the Lie-group characterization of locally compact groups (Yamabe's theorem). |
| 8 | Chung-Tao Yang | 1923–2005 | breakthrough | Dimension-raising estimates for hypothetical $p$-adic actions; the cohomological engine of the problem. |
| 9 | Dušan Repovš & Evgenij Ščepin | contemporary | frontier | Proved the conjecture for Lipschitz / quasiconformal actions in all dimensions (1997). |
| 10 | John Pardon | contemporary | frontier | Proved the full topological conjecture in dimension three (2013). |

(Row 9 names a working pair; Gaven Martin's extension to quasiconformal actions belongs to the same frontier.)

## Notes

The active community is small but vigorous, sitting at the interface of geometric topology, transformation groups, and geometric dimension theory. The structural backbone was laid by the Princeton–Institute for Advanced Study circle (Smith, Montgomery, Zippin, Gleason, Yamabe) in the 1940s–50s. The modern frontier is carried by **Dušan Repovš** and **Evgenij Ščepin** (metric/regularity methods and dimension theory, Ljubljana and Moscow schools), **Gaven Martin** (quasiconformal analysis, New Zealand), and **John Pardon** (Princeton/Stanford), whose 2013 three-manifold theorem reignited interest. Adjacent expertise comes from **Alexander Dranishnikov** and the cohomological-dimension school, whose dimension-raising-map examples sharpen exactly what pathology the conjecture must exclude, and from expositors such as **Terence Tao**, whose graduate text on Hilbert's fifth problem made the structure theory broadly accessible. Current effort concentrates on dimension $\geq 4$ and on lowering the regularity hypotheses in the Repovš–Ščepin program toward genuine continuity.
