# History — The Hilbert–Smith Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The Hilbert–Smith conjecture grew out of the convergence of two distinct currents: Hilbert's fifth problem on the analytic structure of continuous transformation groups, and Paul A. Smith's study of periodic transformations and the algebra of finite group actions.

**Hilbert's fifth problem (1900)** asked whether every locally Euclidean topological group is necessarily a Lie group — equivalently, whether the requirement of differentiability in the definition of a continuous group is superfluous. Phrased in terms of group actions, a natural strengthening asks: if a locally compact group $G$ acts continuously, faithfully, and effectively on a (connected) manifold $M$, must $G$ be a Lie group? Hilbert's original problem concerns the case where $G$ itself is the manifold (acting on itself by translation); the action-theoretic version is strictly stronger and is the form the Hilbert–Smith conjecture takes.

The decisive structural reduction came from the theory of locally compact groups. By the **Gleason–Montgomery–Zippin–Yamabe** solution of Hilbert's fifth problem (1952–1953), a connected locally compact group with no small subgroups is a Lie group, and any locally compact group is an inverse limit of Lie groups. Consequently, the only obstruction to a locally compact transformation group being a Lie group is the presence of arbitrarily small compact subgroups; a structure-theoretic argument (due in this context to Newman, Smith, Bochner–Montgomery and others) shows that one may reduce the entire conjecture to a single test case: the group $\mathbb{Z}_p$ of **$p$-adic integers**. The conjecture therefore acquires its sharp modern form:

> **No $p$-adic integer group $\mathbb{Z}_p$ can act effectively (by homeomorphisms) on a connected topological manifold.**

**Paul Smith** isolated exactly this formulation in the 1930s while developing the theory of transformation groups and the cohomological invariants of periodic maps. His 1941 paper made the $p$-adic reduction explicit and framed the problem as the central open question of the field.

A key tool is **Smith theory** and the work of **M. H. A. Newman** (1931), whose theorem bounds how a finite cyclic group of homeomorphisms can act, forbidding actions with arbitrarily small orbits on a manifold — precisely the behavior a $\mathbb{Z}_p$-action would force.

## Timeline

- **1900** — Hilbert poses his fifth problem on continuous transformation groups and the role of differentiability.
- **1931** — M. H. A. Newman proves his theorem on periodic transformations of manifolds, bounding small group actions.
- **1933** — The conjecture in its transformation-group form crystallizes in the work surrounding Smith; commonly cited as the year posed.
- **1941** — Paul A. Smith makes the reduction to $p$-adic groups explicit, giving the conjecture its standard sharp statement.
- **1943** — Bochner and Montgomery study differentiable and analytic transformation groups, sharpening structural results.
- **1952–1953** — Gleason, Montgomery, Zippin, and Yamabe solve Hilbert's fifth problem; the no-small-subgroups criterion reduces the conjecture to $\mathbb{Z}_p$.
- **1955** — Montgomery and Zippin's monograph _Topological Transformation Groups_ codifies the field and the conjecture.
- **1957** — Yang proves cohomological constraints on hypothetical $\mathbb{Z}_p$-actions (dimension-raising of orbit spaces).
- **1963** — The conjecture is confirmed for actions on $2$-manifolds (Montgomery–Zippin methods; surfaces).
- **1969** — Confirmed for $3$-manifolds via Smith-theoretic and geometric arguments.
- **1997** — Repovš and Ščepin prove the conjecture for actions by **Lipschitz** (and quasi-conformal) homeomorphisms on Riemannian manifolds.
- **2013** — John Pardon proves the **three-dimensional** Hilbert–Smith conjecture in full (arbitrary topological actions), using $L^2$-cohomology / dimension-theoretic methods.
- **2010s–present** — Effort concentrates on dimension $\geq 4$ and on weakening regularity hypotheses (Hölder, quasi-conformal). The general case in dimension $\geq 4$ remains open.
