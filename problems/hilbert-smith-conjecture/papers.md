**Note on reliability.** The foundational works of Newman (1931), Smith (1930s–40s), Montgomery–Zippin (esp. their 1955 monograph), Yang's $p$-adic transformation-group papers (late 1950s), Repovš–Ščepin's Lipschitz theorem (1997), and Pardon's three-manifold theorem (2013) are certainly real and canonical; these are the spine of the subject. DOIs and arXiv numbers are supplied only where confidence is reasonable — most are marked "needs-verification" because exact issue/volume/DOI metadata for mid-twentieth-century _Annals of Mathematics_ and _Math. Annalen_ articles should be confirmed against MathSciNet/zbMATH before citation. Rows flagged "ai-suggested" (14, 16, 17, 23) name real topics and bodies of work but the specific title/author pairing is uncertain and should be replaced with a verified reference. No identifiers have been invented; "—" marks every case where I am not confident of the exact number. Pardon's arXiv id (1112.2324) and the fact of his JAMS publication are high-confidence; Tao's _Hilbert's Fifth Problem and Related Topics_ (AMS GSM 153) is a real, widely used text though the exact year/series should be confirmed.
