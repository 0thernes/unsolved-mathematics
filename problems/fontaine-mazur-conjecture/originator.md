# Originator(s) — The Fontaine–Mazur Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture bears the names of two mathematicians whose distinct programs intersected precisely at the question of which $p$-adic Galois representations "come from geometry."

## Jean-Marc Fontaine (1944–2019)

Fontaine was a French mathematician and the principal architect of $p$-adic Hodge theory. Born in Boulogne-Billancourt, he spent much of his career at the Université Paris-Sud (Orsay) and was elected to the Académie des sciences. Beginning in the late 1970s and through the 1980s he constructed the apparatus that makes the conjecture even possible to state: the period rings $B_{\mathrm{dR}}$, $B_{\mathrm{cris}}$, and $B_{\mathrm{st}}$, the classification of $p$-adic representations as Hodge–Tate, de Rham, semistable, or crystalline, and the comparison isomorphisms (the "$C_{\mathrm{dR}}$", "$C_{\mathrm{st}}$" conjectures, later theorems) relating étale and de Rham cohomology of $p$-adic varieties. His "mysterious functor" and the theory of $(\varphi,\Gamma)$-modules reorganized the local study of Galois representations entirely.

Fontaine's motivation was structural: he wanted an intrinsic, local-at-$p$ characterization of the cohomology of varieties. The de Rham condition is exactly the linear-algebra shadow that geometric representations must cast. From this vantage, the conjecture is the natural global completion of his local theory — it says the local de Rham condition, together with the obviously necessary unramified-almost-everywhere condition, is also sufficient for a representation to be of geometric/automorphic origin.

## Barry Mazur (b. 1937)

Mazur is an American mathematician at Harvard University, one of the most influential figures in modern arithmetic geometry. His earlier work spans differentiable topology (the Schoenflies problem, the Mazur swindle) before he turned to number theory, where his theorem on torsion of elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ and his theory of the Eisenstein ideal are landmarks. Crucially for this conjecture, Mazur founded the **deformation theory of Galois representations** in the late 1980s, introducing universal deformation rings — the engine that Wiles, Taylor, Kisin, and Emerton would later use to attack modularity.

Mazur brought to the collaboration the global Langlands-program perspective and the deformation-theoretic language. Where Fontaine supplied the right local condition, Mazur supplied the framework in which "all geometric representations of a fixed residual type" become a single algebro-geometric object whose closed points one can hope to identify with automorphic data.

## The joint conjecture and its legacy

The synthesis appeared in their 1993/1995 paper *Geometric Galois representations*. Its boldness is that it elevates Fontaine's purely local de Rham condition to a **global characterization**, fusing $p$-adic Hodge theory with the Langlands philosophy. The conjecture immediately reframed Wiles' modularity work as a special case of a vast organizing principle and set the agenda for two decades of progress: it is the conceptual target of Kisin's and Emerton's proofs in dimension two, of the potential-automorphy program of Taylor and collaborators, and of Calegari–Geraghty's derived generalizations. Fontaine's death in 2019 was widely marked as the loss of a founder of modern $p$-adic geometry; Mazur remains an active and central voice. The conjecture stands as the keystone arch between the local and global theories they each, separately, helped build.
