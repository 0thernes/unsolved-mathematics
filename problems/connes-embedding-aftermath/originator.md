# Originator(s) — The Connes Embedding Problem (Resolved 2020)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Alain Connes (b. 1947)

Alain Connes was born on 1 April 1947 in Draguignan, France. He entered the École Normale Supérieure in 1966 and completed his doctorate under Jacques Dixmier, the central figure of the French operator-algebra school. From the outset Connes worked at the deepest level of von Neumann algebra theory, the subject founded by Murray and von Neumann in the 1930s–40s to give operators an algebraic-probabilistic framework adapted to quantum mechanics.

Connes's defining achievement of the 1970s was the **classification of injective (hyperfinite) factors**. Building on Tomita–Takesaki modular theory and his own classification of automorphisms, he proved that the injective II$_1$ factor is unique (it is $R$, the hyperfinite factor) and classified injective factors of all types, including the celebrated analysis of type III$_\lambda$ factors via the modular flow. This work earned him the **Fields Medal in 1982**. He later received the Crafoord Prize (2001) and the Shaw Prize (in shared recognition of noncommutative geometry's broader impact), and has held the Léon Motchane Chair at the IHÉS and a professorship at the Collège de France.

## The idea behind the problem

The embedding remark of 1976 grew directly out of the classification program. Having shown that the hyperfinite factor $R$ is the canonical, maximally tractable II$_1$ factor — the one approximable by finite-dimensional matrix algebras — Connes was probing how far that tractability reaches. The ultrapower $R^\omega$ is the natural "limit object" capturing all matricial approximations at once. To ask whether every II$_1$ factor embeds in $R^\omega$ is to ask whether the rich, often non-amenable world of factors (free group factors $L(\mathbb{F}_n)$, property (T) factors, factors from ergodic theory) is nonetheless *visible through matrices*. It was a structural intuition — that finite-dimensional approximation might be universal — phrased with characteristic economy as a passing conjecture rather than a headline theorem.

The motivation connected to two currents Connes himself helped shape: **amenability/hyperfiniteness** (the dividing line his classification made precise) and the emerging study of **approximation properties** of groups and algebras (soficity, hyperlinearity, the Haagerup property). A group is *hyperlinear* exactly when its group von Neumann algebra embeds in $R^\omega$; CEP for group factors is thus the question of whether every countable group is hyperlinear, a cousin of Gromov's question on sofic groups.

## Broader legacy and the modern formulation

Connes's larger legacy is **noncommutative geometry**: the program (his 1994 book *Noncommutative Geometry*; the Connes–Chamseddine spectral-action approach to the Standard Model; the cyclic cohomology and index theory underlying it) that treats geometric spaces through their operator algebras. CEP sits at the foundational edge of this worldview — a test of how completely matrices model the noncommutative continuum.

The "modern formulation" is essentially Connes's original, but its center of gravity migrated. What Connes posed as a von Neumann algebra embedding question was, through Kirchberg's QWEP equivalence and the Tsirelson dictionary, recast as a question about **quantum correlation sets** and ultimately about **interactive proof complexity**. The 2020 negative answer arrived not from operator algebra at all but from quantum complexity theory — a vindication of the problem's centrality precisely because so many fields converged on it. Connes, still active, has frequently cited the resolution as a model of mathematics' unexpected unity.
