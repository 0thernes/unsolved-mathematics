# Attempts — The Littlewood Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Cassels–Swinnerton-Dyer (1955): conditional resolution

The first serious assault, by J. W. S. Cassels and H. P. F. Swinnerton-Dyer, proved the conjecture **conditional** on a rigidity statement about lattices invariant under the diagonal group, and unconditionally settled important special cases (notably pairs tied to cubic fields and pairs with $1,\alpha,\beta$ linearly dependent over $\mathbb{Q}$). It was a near-miss in the sense that it identified the *exact* hypothesis whose proof would close the problem — a hypothesis that, half a century later, became the subject of the EKL measure-rigidity programme. Its lasting contribution was the dynamical reformulation rather than a complete proof.

## Margulis's rigidity vision (1980s–1990s)

G. A. Margulis articulated the conjecture that bounded orbits of the diagonal group $A$ on $\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{R})/\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$ ($n\ge 3$) are extremely rigid, and observed that such rigidity would imply Littlewood. This was not a claimed proof but a program; it correctly predicted the route eventually taken and is the conceptual parent of the 2006 breakthrough. The gap Margulis could not close — handling invariant measures of zero entropy — is the same gap that remains today.

## Einsiedler–Katok–Lindenstrauss (2006): the dimension-zero theorem

The celebrated result of M. Einsiedler, A. Katok, and E. Lindenstrauss is the closest anyone has come. It proves the exceptional set has Hausdorff dimension $0$, so *almost every* pair satisfies the conjecture and any counterexample must live in a provably minuscule set. This is a genuine theorem, fully accepted, and central to Lindenstrauss's Fields Medal (2010). It is a near-miss only in the precise sense that "dimension zero" is not "empty": the method, resting on positive-entropy measure rigidity, cannot exclude a hypothetical orbit carrying only zero-entropy invariant measures. No retraction or dispute attaches to this work; the limitation is intrinsic and openly stated by the authors.

## Counterexamples to relatives (cautionary near-misses)

Several *variants* once hoped to be analogues of Littlewood have been **disproved**, which functions as evidence about why the original is hard rather than as progress on it:

- **Inhomogeneous / twisted analogues.** U. Shapira and collaborators produced explicit pairs and constructions showing that certain inhomogeneous and twisted versions of the multiplicative problem fail. These results are accepted and demonstrate that any correct proof of the original must exploit the homogeneous structure specifically.
- **$t$-adic and function-field experiments.** Computational and combinatorial work (e.g. around the $p$-adic / $t$-adic Littlewood conjecture in positive characteristic) has surfaced candidate counterexamples and delicate behavior in the function-field setting, again sharpening the boundary between true and false formulations.

## Disputed or erroneous claims

The Littlewood conjecture, like many famous open problems, periodically attracts **claimed elementary proofs**, typically circulated as preprints (e.g. on arXiv or vixra) or in self-published notes. As of the present writing none has gained acceptance; the recurring objection from experts is that such arguments either (i) silently assume a uniformity over all badly approximable pairs that is exactly the crux, (ii) prove only the (already classical) case where one number is well approximable, or (iii) conflate the multiplicative liminf with an averaged or generic statement. The honest summary is that **no claimed full proof has withstood scrutiny**, and the problem is universally regarded as open. Readers should treat any announcement of a short proof with strong skepticism until it is vetted in the peer-reviewed literature; the structural obstacle (the zero-entropy exceptional set) is well understood and any valid proof must address it explicitly.
