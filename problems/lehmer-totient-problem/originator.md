# Originator(s) — Lehmer's Totient Problem

**Derrick Henry Lehmer (1905–1991)** was an American mathematician who became one of the twentieth century's foremost figures in computational and analytic number theory. Born in Berkeley, California, he was the son of Derrick Norman Lehmer, himself a number theorist at the University of California who built mechanical factoring devices and tables of primes. The younger Lehmer thus grew up immersed in the concrete, computational side of number theory — sieves, factorization, primality testing — that would define his career. He took his undergraduate degree at Berkeley and his Ph.D. at Brown University in 1930, under Jacob Tamarkin, with a thesis on the theory of Lucas sequences and tests for primality.

Lehmer's mathematical temperament was distinctive: he combined deep theoretical insight with relentless computation, often building or commissioning special-purpose machines to attack problems. He and his wife and collaborator Emma Lehmer (née Trotskaia) constructed photoelectric and electromechanical sieve devices in the 1920s and 1930s, decades before general-purpose electronic computers, and he later became an early and influential user of ENIAC and other machines for number-theoretic searches. He spent most of his career at Berkeley, where he trained many students and helped shape the modern computational approach to number theory.

The totient problem grew directly out of Lehmer's work on **primality criteria and the structure of Euler's function**. Fermat's little theorem and its converse questions — when does $a^{n-1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ force $n$ to be prime? — were central to his lifelong study of Lucas sequences and pseudoprimes. Euler's theorem states $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for $\gcd(a,n)=1$, and for primes $\varphi(n) = n-1$. Lehmer asked the natural inverse question: could the *order-theoretic* coincidence $\varphi(n) \mid n-1$ ever hold for a composite $n$? If not, the relation would be a clean characterization of primality. In his 1932 paper "On Euler's totient function," he posed the question and derived the first structural constraints — that any composite solution is odd, squarefree, and has at least seven distinct prime factors — establishing the template (lower bounds on $\omega(n)$) that all subsequent work has followed.

This problem sits naturally beside the **Carmichael numbers**, which Lehmer also studied: a Carmichael number satisfies the weaker $\lambda(n) \mid n-1$. Lehmer's totient condition is the strict tightening obtained by replacing $\lambda$ with $\varphi$. Recognizing this hierarchy — and the contrast between the *abundance* of Carmichael numbers and the apparent *non-existence* of Lehmer numbers — is one of the conceptual legacies of his framing.

Lehmer's broader legacy is immense. He is remembered for the **Lehmer sieve** machines; for foundational work on **Lucas–Lehmer primality testing** of Mersenne numbers (the standard test bearing his name, building on Lucas); for **Lehmer's conjecture** on the Mahler measure of polynomials (an entirely separate, also-unsolved problem); for the **Lehmer pair** phenomenon in the spacing of Riemann zeta zeros; and for extensive tables and algorithms that seeded experimental number theory. He served as editor of *Mathematics of Computation* and was a guiding spirit of the computational tradition. The totient problem is characteristic of his style: a precisely stated, deceptively simple divisibility question whose resolution would have immediate algorithmic consequence, and which has resisted nearly a century of attack while spawning a rich literature of partial results.
