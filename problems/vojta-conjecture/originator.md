# Originator(s) — Vojta's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Paul Vojta

**Paul Alan Vojta** (born September 30, 1957) is an American mathematician at the University of California, Berkeley, where he has been a professor of mathematics since the late 1980s. He earned his Ph.D. at Harvard University in 1983 under **Barry Mazur**, with the thesis material that developed into his foundational 1987 monograph. (His arithmetic-discriminant and Mordell work was carried out as a young faculty member; he was an early MacArthur "genius" Fellow, awarded in 1987, and an invited speaker at the International Congress of Mathematicians.) Vojta is known for an unusually structural, almost architectural taste in mathematics: rather than attacking a single Diophantine question, he sought the organizing principle behind an entire class of them.

## Background and the central idea

Vojta's training under Mazur placed him at the heart of arithmetic geometry in the wake of Faltings' 1983 proof of the Mordell conjecture. The intellectual catalyst, however, came from outside the standard arithmetic toolkit. **Charles Osgood** had observed a numerical coincidence: the approximation exponent $2$ in Roth's theorem matched the constant in Nevanlinna's defect relation for holomorphic curves. Where others saw a curiosity, Vojta built a complete dictionary.

The key conceptual move was to identify the analytic notions of value-distribution theory with arithmetic ones, term by term:

- a holomorphic map $f:\mathbb{C}\to X$ $\;\leftrightarrow\;$ an infinite sequence of rational/integral points;
- Nevanlinna's characteristic function $T_f(r)$ $\;\leftrightarrow\;$ the Weil height $h_A(P)$;
- the proximity function $m_f(r,D)$ $\;\leftrightarrow\;$ the arithmetic proximity $m_S(P,D)$;
- the counting function $N_f(r,D)$ $\;\leftrightarrow\;$ the height contribution from finite places;
- the ramification term in the Second Main Theorem $\;\leftrightarrow\;$ the **canonical height $h_{K_X}$**, and the arithmetic discriminant $d_k(P)$.

Under this translation, Nevanlinna's Second Main Theorem becomes a precise conjectural inequality on heights — Vojta's conjecture. The genius of the formulation is that the single inequality, by varying $X$ and $D$, encodes $abc$, Mordell, Bombieri–Lang, Siegel, and the Subspace Theorem simultaneously, exposing them as facets of one phenomenon: that the arithmetic of a variety is controlled by the positivity of its canonical class.

## The two formulations

Vojta posed both a **rational-points** version (height inequality with the $\varepsilon h_A$ error) and a sharper **arithmetic-discriminant** version that incorporates $d_k(P)$ and handles points of bounded degree (algebraic points), the "general $(1+\varepsilon)$" form. The latter is strictly more powerful and yields, for instance, uniform statements over all extensions of bounded degree. Both descend from the same Nevanlinna template; the discriminant form corresponds to allowing the holomorphic curve to be ramified, mirrored arithmetically by the field of definition growing.

## Legacy

Vojta's own subsequent work supplied the strongest evidence for his vision. His **1991 reproof of the Mordell conjecture** via Diophantine approximation and Arakelov theory showed the program could prove deep finiteness theorems independently of Faltings' methods; his **1996 theorem on integral points on semiabelian varieties** remains the high-water mark of unconditional progress. The conjecture reorganized Diophantine geometry into a coherent research program pursued by Faltings, Bombieri, Ru, Wong, Corvaja, Zannier, Levin, Autissier, and others, and it gave the $abc$ conjecture a conceptual home inside higher-dimensional arithmetic. Vojta also co-authored, with Enrico Bombieri, the graduate text *Heights in Diophantine Geometry* (2006), now a standard reference. The conjecture is widely regarded as one of the deepest open problems in number theory; it remains unproven in essentially every nontrivial general-type case.
