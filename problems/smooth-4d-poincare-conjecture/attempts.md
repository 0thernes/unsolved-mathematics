# Attempts — The Smooth 4-Dimensional Poincaré Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

SPC4 has no famous "claimed proof and retraction" of the kind that punctuates the Riemann Hypothesis or the 3-dimensional Poincaré story. Instead, its history is one of *candidate counterexamples that were repeatedly shown to be standard*, and of strategies that came tantalizingly close to a counterexample without producing one. The notable episodes are near-misses on the construction side and progressive eliminations on the verification side.

## Cappell–Shaneson spheres: the long elimination

The Cappell–Shaneson homotopy 4-spheres (1976), built from punctured mapping tori of certain torus diffeomorphisms, were for decades the leading suspects for exotic structures. Akbulut and Kirby studied their handle diagrams in the 1980s. A genuine near-miss: for years it was *unknown* whether the simplest families were standard, and several mathematicians suspected they were exotic.

- **Gompf (1991)** showed large classes are standard.
- **Akbulut (2009–2010)** proved that an infinite family long believed to be the best exotic candidates are in fact diffeomorphic to $S^4$.
- **Gompf (2010)**, independently and with different handle calculus, confirmed and extended these results.

The cumulative effect was to retire the Cappell–Shaneson family as a source of exotic 4-spheres — a major partial result that *narrowed* the problem rather than solving it. No Cappell–Shaneson sphere is currently known to be exotic.

## The Gluck twist

Gluck's 1962 construction re-glues a tubular neighborhood of a knotted 2-sphere in $S^4$ using the nontrivial element of $\pi_1(SO(3))$, yielding a homotopy 4-sphere $\Sigma_K$. Many Gluck twists have been proved standard — for unknotted, ribbon, and various special 2-knots (Gluck himself, and later Gordon, Melvin, Sunukjian, Naylor–Schwartz, and others). This is a steady stream of partial results, each closing off a class of candidates. The general question — *is every Gluck twist standard?* — remains open and is a recognized sub-problem of SPC4.

## The Schoenflies and $s$-invariant near-miss

Freedman–Gompf–Morrison–Walker (2010) attempted to *manufacture* a counterexample using the Rasmussen $s$-invariant: if a specific knot were topologically slice in a homotopy 4-ball but had $s \neq 0$, an exotic 4-ball/sphere would follow. They ran explicit computations on candidate knots. Every tested knot turned out to be smoothly slice (so $s = 0$). This was a deliberate, well-publicized search for a counterexample that came up empty — a near-miss in the sense that the *machinery* worked but the *input* refused to cooperate.

**Manolescu–Piccirillo (2020)** systematized this, producing a finite list of explicit knots whose nontriviality under $s$ (or related invariants) would yield an exotic $S^4$. Subsequent analysis showed the listed candidates were in fact slice or standard. The episode is widely cited as the closest the community has come to a concrete, checkable attack — and as evidence of how resistant the problem is.

## Disputed and unverified claims

There is no widely recognized, peer-reviewed claimed *resolution* of SPC4 in either direction. Periodic preprints announce either a construction of an exotic $S^4$ or a proof of nonexistence; none has survived expert scrutiny, and the conjecture's status in the literature remains firmly **open**. Because no claim has reached the level of a refereed, community-acknowledged theorem, this dossier records no specific named dispute; the honest statement is that the problem has resisted every serious attempt and the expected answer is itself contested. Readers should treat any announcement of a solution with caution until checked by the 4-manifold community (Gompf, Manolescu, Akbulut's successors, Piccirillo, and others).
