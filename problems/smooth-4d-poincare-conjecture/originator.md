# Originator(s) — The Smooth 4-Dimensional Poincaré Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

SPC4 has two distinct intellectual parents: the *historical root* in Henri Poincaré, who introduced the topological question that bears his name, and the *modern formulation*, which became a precise standalone problem only through the work of Michael Freedman, whose 1982 topological resolution forced the smooth case into the open.

## Henri Poincaré (1854–1912) — the historical root

Henri Poincaré was a French mathematician, theoretical physicist, and philosopher of science, widely regarded as the last universalist. Born in Nancy into a distinguished family (his cousin Raymond Poincaré became President of France), he trained at the École Polytechnique and the École des Mines. His mathematical output ranged across automorphic functions, celestial mechanics (the three-body problem, for which he won King Oscar II's prize in 1889 and where he glimpsed deterministic chaos), differential equations, and the foundations of topology.

In his series *Analysis Situs* (1895) and its five supplements, Poincaré essentially created algebraic topology, introducing the fundamental group, homology, and Betti numbers. In the 1904 supplement he posed the question — phrased as a query, not a claim — of whether a closed 3-manifold with trivial fundamental group must be the 3-sphere, having earlier constructed the Poincaré homology sphere to show that homology alone does not suffice. Poincaré never considered the smooth/topological distinction (the modern apparatus of smooth structures and gauge theory lay decades ahead) nor dimension four specifically. His legacy here is the *shape* of the question — that simple connectivity should force sphericity — which propagates to every dimension and every category.

## Freedman and the modern formulation

The smooth 4-dimensional case became a sharply posed, isolated problem through Michael Freedman (b. 1951). Freedman, working at UC San Diego and later at Microsoft Station Q, proved the topological 4-dimensional Poincaré conjecture around 1982 by establishing the topological $h$-cobordism theorem in dimension four — a tour de force of infinite "Casson-handle" constructions and Bing-style topology. The result classified simply connected topological 4-manifolds via the intersection form and won him the Fields Medal in 1986.

Freedman's triumph created the problem in its current form. The instant his topological theorem landed, the question split: *topologically*, every homotopy 4-sphere is $S^4$; *smoothly*, the answer is unknown. The smooth case has no single "originator" in the sense of a person who conjectured it — it is the residue left behind by Freedman's theorem, the natural smooth refinement that everyone immediately recognized as open.

## The exotic-structure context

The reason SPC4 is hard, and the reason it became central, is the gauge-theoretic revolution that immediately followed. Simon Donaldson's 1983 theorem on the intersection forms of smooth 4-manifolds, using Yang–Mills instanton moduli spaces, exposed a vast gulf between the topological and smooth categories in dimension four — most dramatically the existence of uncountably many exotic $\mathbb{R}^4$'s (Donaldson, Freedman, Gompf, Taubes). This established that smooth four-dimensional topology is governed by analysis, not just algebra, and that $S^4$'s smoothness was no longer a foregone conclusion.

Thus the "originators" of SPC4 are best understood as a lineage: Poincaré supplied the founding question; Smale, Milnor, and Gluck supplied the higher-dimensional and exotic context; Freedman, by resolving the topological case, defined the smooth problem; and Donaldson supplied the tools and the evidence that it is deep. The conjecture's modern statement — "no exotic $S^4$" — carries Poincaré's name but is a product of the 1980s gauge-theory era.
