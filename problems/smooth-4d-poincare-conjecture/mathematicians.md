# Top Mathematicians — The Smooth 4-Dimensional Poincaré Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Henri Poincaré | 1854–1912 | originator | Posed the original Poincaré conjecture (1904); founded algebraic topology, supplying the "simple connectivity ⇒ sphere" template that generalizes to SPC4. |
| 2 | John Milnor | 1931– | breakthrough | Discovered exotic 7-spheres (1956), creating the smooth-vs-topological distinction that makes SPC4 meaningful. |
| 3 | Stephen Smale | 1930– | breakthrough | Proved the smooth generalized Poincaré conjecture for $n \geq 5$ via the $h$-cobordism theorem, isolating dimension four. |
| 4 | Herman Gluck | 1937– | originator | Introduced the Gluck twist (1962), the canonical construction of candidate exotic 4-spheres and a key open sub-problem. |
| 5 | Michael Freedman | 1951– | breakthrough | Proved the topological 4-dimensional Poincaré conjecture (1982), defining SPC4 as the open smooth case; co-launched the $s$-invariant strategy. |
| 6 | Simon Donaldson | 1957– | breakthrough | Used Yang–Mills gauge theory (1983) to expose the smooth/topological gulf in dimension four; revealed exotic $\mathbb{R}^4$'s. |
| 7 | Robion Kirby | 1938– | frontier | Developed Kirby calculus and handle diagrams (with Akbulut) for homotopy 4-spheres; co-developed trisections with Gay. |
| 8 | Robert Gompf | 1957– | frontier | Proved large families of Cappell–Shaneson spheres standard (1991, 2010); co-author of the definitive 4-manifold reference. |
| 9 | Selman Akbulut | 1949– | frontier | Showed prominent Cappell–Shaneson families are standard (2009–2010), retiring the leading exotic candidates. |
| 10 | Ciprian Manolescu | 1978– | frontier | With Piccirillo, reduced the existence of an exotic $S^4$ to explicit $s$-invariant/knot conditions; advanced skein-lasagna computations. |

## Notes

The active community around SPC4 is small but vigorous, concentrated in geometric topology and gauge theory. Beyond those tabled, central contemporary figures include **Lisa Piccirillo** (whose trace-embedding methods and resolution of the Conway knot's sliceness energized the $s$-invariant program), **Scott Morrison**, **Kevin Walker**, and **Paul Wedrich** (skein-lasagna and categorified 4-manifold invariants), **David Gay** (trisections), **Ronald Fintushel** and **Ronald Stern** (exotic-manifold construction techniques), and **Clifford Taubes** and **Edward Witten** (Seiberg–Witten theory, the engine behind exotic detection at $b_2 > 0$). The field is unusual in that its leading practitioners openly disagree on whether SPC4 is true: Freedman has expected exotic 4-spheres to exist, while much of the gauge-theory community is agnostic or leans toward standardness. Work proceeds mainly through annual conferences (e.g. Gauge Theory and Low-Dimensional Topology workshops), the Simons Collaboration on new structures in low-dimensional topology, and a steady stream of arXiv preprints testing specific candidate spheres and knots.
