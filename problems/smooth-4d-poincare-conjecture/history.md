# History — The Smooth 4-Dimensional Poincaré Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The Smooth 4-Dimensional Poincaré Conjecture (SPC4) is the last surviving case of the generalized Poincaré conjecture, and the only dimension in which the smooth question remains entirely open. Its modern formulation crystallized only after Michael Freedman's 1982 resolution of the *topological* 4-dimensional case made the smooth/topological gap in dimension four impossible to ignore.

**Statement.** SPC4 asserts: every smooth 4-manifold homeomorphic to the standard 4-sphere $S^4$ is diffeomorphic to it. Equivalently, $S^4$ admits no *exotic* smooth structure. This is sharper than the topological statement (proved) and orthogonal to it. In every dimension $n \neq 4$ the smooth generalized Poincaré question is settled: $n = 1, 2$ classically; $n = 3$ by Perelman; $n \geq 5$ via Smale's $h$-cobordism theorem. There can be exotic *spheres* in high dimensions, but a homotopy $n$-sphere is homeomorphic to $S^n$ and (for $n \geq 5$) the group $\Theta_n$ of homotopy spheres measures distinct smooth structures — yet $\Theta_4$ is unknown and conjecturally trivial. Dimension four is the lone genuinely open case.

**Roots.** Henri Poincaré posed his question in 1904 for 3-manifolds; its generalization to all dimensions, and the smooth-category refinement, are 20th-century developments. The discovery of exotic phenomena — Milnor's exotic 7-spheres (1956) and especially exotic $\mathbb{R}^4$'s (Donaldson, Freedman, Gompf, Taubes, 1980s) — showed that dimension four behaves like no other, making SPC4 a genuinely hard, isolated problem.

**Timeline.**

**1904** — Poincaré poses the original conjecture for 3-manifolds, later generalized to all dimensions.

**1956** — Milnor constructs exotic 7-spheres, opening the smooth category as distinct from the topological one.

**1961** — Smale proves the generalized Poincaré conjecture (PL and smooth) for $n \geq 5$ via the $h$-cobordism theorem.

**1962** — Gluck introduces the "Gluck twist," producing candidate exotic 4-spheres by re-gluing a tubular neighborhood of a knotted 2-sphere in $S^4$.

**1976** — Cappell and Shaneson construct homotopy 4-spheres (Cappell–Shaneson spheres) from punctured mapping tori — later prime suspects for exotic structures.

**1982** — Freedman proves the topological 4-dimensional Poincaré conjecture, isolating SPC4 as the open *smooth* case; awarded the Fields Medal (1986).

**1983** — Donaldson's gauge-theoretic diagonalization theorem reveals exotic smooth structures on $\mathbb{R}^4$ and severe constraints on smooth 4-manifolds.

**1986–1991** — Akbulut, Kirby, and Gompf give handle descriptions; Gompf shows many Cappell–Shaneson spheres are standard, progressively reducing the family.

**2009–2010** — Akbulut, and independently Gompf, prove that the most prominent infinite families of Cappell–Shaneson spheres are standard (diffeomorphic to $S^4$), eliminating the leading candidates.

**2010s** — Freedman, Gompf, Morrison, and Walker propose detecting exotic $S^4$'s via the Rasmussen $s$-invariant from Khovanov homology, linking SPC4 to the smooth slice genus of knots.

**2020** — Manolescu and Piccirillo formalize a strategy: an exotic $S^4$ would follow from a knot that is topologically but not smoothly slice with $s \neq 0$. They test candidates but find no counterexample, sharpening rather than resolving the problem.

**2024–present** — SPC4 remains open. Activity centers on Khovanov-homology obstructions ($s$-invariant, skein-lasagna invariants of Morrison–Walker–Wedrich), the Gluck-twist sub-problem, and trisection techniques (Gay–Kirby). No consensus exists on whether SPC4 is true or false.

A defining feature of SPC4's history: experts are genuinely divided on the expected answer. Freedman has voiced expectation that exotic 4-spheres exist; many gauge theorists lean the other way. Unlike most open problems, the *direction* of truth is itself unsettled.
