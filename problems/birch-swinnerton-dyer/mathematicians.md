# Top Mathematicians — The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Bryan Birch | 1960s–2020s | originator | Co-formulated BSD from EDSAC data; revived Heegner points, central to all proven cases |
| 2 | Peter Swinnerton-Dyer | 1960s–2010s | originator | Co-formulated BSD; pioneered large-scale computer experiment in number theory |

## Notes

The contemporary community is broad and highly active, even though the central rank-$\ge 2$ case is untouched. Key frontier figures beyond the table include **Christopher Skinner** and **Eric Urban** (Iwasawa Main Conjectures for $\mathrm{GL}_2$), **Wei Zhang** (arithmetic of $L$-functions, Gan–Gross–Prasad, positive-proportion BSD), **Karl Rubin** (Euler systems, CM main conjecture), **Kazuya Kato** (Euler system for modular forms), **Henri Darmon** and **Massimo Bertolini** (Stark–Heegner / Darmon points and $p$-adic $L$-functions aiming beyond rank $1$), and **Kartik Prasanna**, **Xinyi Yuan**, **Shou-Wu Zhang**, and **Daniel Disegni** (generalized Gross–Zagier and arithmetic intersection theory). Computational verification is sustained by **John Cremona** and the LMFDB collaboration. The problem sits at the heart of arithmetic geometry and the Langlands programme, and its generalizations (Bloch–Kato, Beilinson–Bloch–Kato for motives) keep a large international cohort engaged. The consensus is that genuinely new ideas — a higher Euler system or a rank-$\ge 2$ height formula — are required, and progress in the 2010s has been incremental and statistical rather than a breach of the core barrier.
