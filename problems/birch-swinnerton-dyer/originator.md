# Originator(s) — The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture bears the names of two British number theorists whose collaboration at Cambridge in the late 1950s and early 1960s fused classical Diophantine theory with the then-novel resource of electronic computation.

**Bryan John Birch** (born 25 September 1931, Burton-upon-Trent; died 28 November 2025) was an English mathematician educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he completed his doctorate under J. W. S. (Ian) Cassels, the dominant figure in postwar British number theory. Birch's early work already touched the questions that would define his career: his 1957 result that every sufficiently large integer is a sum of a bounded number of cubes (now "Birch's theorem" on forms in many variables) showed his command of the circle method and the arithmetic of equations. He held positions at Manchester before settling at Oxford, where he became Professor of Arithmetic and a long-serving fixture of the number theory community. Beyond BSD, Birch's name attaches to **Heegner points** — he revived and systematized Kurt Heegner's neglected construction of rational points on modular curves, a tool that later became central to the proven cases of his own conjecture via the Gross–Zagier formula. The Birch–Tate conjecture (relating $K_2$ of rings of integers to special values of zeta functions) is another of his contributions.

**Sir Henry Peter Francis Swinnerton-Dyer, 16th Baronet** (2 August 1927 – 26 December 2018) came from a different intellectual milieu but the same institution. Educated at Eton and Trinity College, Cambridge, he was a polymath whose work spanned the arithmetic of cubic and quartic surfaces, the theory of modular forms, analytic number theory, and the geometry of Diophantine equations. He was a master of explicit computation in the pre-computer and early-computer eras and an early, fearless user of EDSAC and EDSAC 2 for serious mathematical experiment. His administrative career was equally weighty: he served as Master of St Catharine's College, Vice-Chancellor of Cambridge University, and chairman of the University Grants Committee, shaping British higher education policy. He was knighted (KBE) and inherited a baronetcy, but among mathematicians his enduring fame is the conjecture.

The collaboration that produced BSD reflected complementary strengths. Both men were steeped in the theory of elliptic curves and the Mordell–Weil theorem, which guaranteed that the rational points of an elliptic curve form a finitely generated group $E(\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \oplus T$ but said nothing computable about the rank $r$. There was, at the time, no general algorithm — and there still is none proven to terminate — to compute $r$. Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer's decisive move was to seek an *analytic* signature of the rank in the local point counts $N_p$, encoded in the $L$-function. Their genius was statistical and experimental: they trusted patterns extracted from large-scale numerical data, then articulated those patterns as a precise arithmetic-analytic identity. This was a methodological landmark — among the first conjectures of the highest importance to be discovered by computer.

The modern formulation owes much to **John Tate**, who recast the conjecture in the language of $L$-functions and Galois cohomology and supplied the refined formula with the Tate–Shafarevich group and Tamagawa factors. Thus the "historical root" (an empirical law about $\prod N_p/p$) and the "modern statement" (equality of analytic and algebraic rank, with an explicit leading coefficient) are two faces of one insight. Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer's legacy is not merely a hard problem but a template: arithmetic invariants of varieties are governed by the analytic behaviour of their $L$-functions — the central creed of the Langlands era and of the Bloch–Kato conjectures that generalize BSD.
