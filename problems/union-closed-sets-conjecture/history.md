# History — The Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (Frankl)

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin and formulation

A finite family $\mathcal{F}$ of sets is **union-closed** if $A \cup B \in \mathcal{F}$ whenever $A, B \in \mathcal{F}$. The conjecture asserts that for any such family containing at least one non-empty set, there exists an element $x$ belonging to at least half of the members of $\mathcal{F}$; equivalently, some element lies in at least $\lceil |\mathcal{F}|/2 \rceil$ of the sets. The empty family and the family $\{\emptyset\}$ are the only excluded cases.

The problem is universally attributed to **Péter Frankl**, who is reported to have circulated it around **1979**. There is no founding research paper: like several of Frankl's combinatorial questions, it spread orally and through problem lists. Roland Hindman's 1992 note and Ron Graham's lectures helped fix the canonical statement. Stanley Burris and others later traced the earliest written appearance to a 1979 conference setting. The lattice-theoretic reformulation is older in spirit: by associating to $\mathcal{F}$ the join-closed structure it generates, the conjecture becomes a statement about **finite lattices** — every finite lattice with $n \ge 2$ elements contains a join-irreducible element lying below at most half of the elements (dually, above at least half). This equivalence, made precise by Poonen and by Reinhold, ties the problem to order theory.

Several equivalent formulations circulate: the **set form** above; the **lattice form**; an **intersection-closed (dual) form** asserting that some element is missing from at least half the sets of an intersection-closed family; and a **graph form** for the special case of families generated by edges. The framing as a question about the "abundance" of elements invites averaging and, more recently, **entropy** arguments.

## Timeline

- **1979** — Péter Frankl poses the conjecture; it begins circulating informally among combinatorialists.
- **1985** — Duffus and others discuss the lattice reformulation; the problem gains visibility in order-theory circles.
- **1989** — Bjorn Poonen establishes equivalences between the set-theoretic, lattice, and other formulations, clarifying the problem's scope.
- **1990–1992** — Renaud, Sarvate–Renaud, and Knill produce small-case verifications and structural results; Sarvate and Renaud publish a noted "counterexample to a stronger statement," underscoring that naive averaging fails.
- **1994** — Theodore Knill and others give bounds showing the conjecture holds when the largest set is small.
- **1998** — Robert Roberts and Ian Roberts, and separately Norton/Sarvate, verify the conjecture by computer for families on small ground sets (up to roughly 11 elements over time).
- **2001** — Giovanni Lo Faro and others extend computational verification; the "$n \le 11$" frontier is consolidated.
- **2001** — Piotr Wójcik proves that the largest set forces abundant elements under size conditions, an early density-type partial result.
- **2009–2012** — The conjecture is featured as a Polymath-style target and in surveys (Bruhn–Schaudt later catalogue the landscape). Czédli, and Czédli–Schmidt, push lattice-side bounds.
- **2013** — Henning Bruhn, Pierre Charbit, Oliver Schaudt, and Jan Arne Telle connect the conjecture to **stable sets in graphs** and prove it for families whose generated structure is suitably graph-like.
- **2014** — Bruhn and Schaudt publish the influential survey "The journey of the union-closed sets conjecture," organizing methods and partial results.
- **2018** — Improvements to the "frequent element" averaging bound; Knill-type and Wójcik-type density results refined. Best general lower bound on the maximum frequency remains a small fraction.
- **November 2022** — **Justin Gilmer** posts an entropy-based argument proving that some element appears in at least a constant fraction $c \approx 0.01$ of the sets — the first unconditional constant-fraction bound, a conceptual breakthrough.
- **November–December 2022** — Within weeks, several groups (Will Sawin; Zachary Chase and Shachar Lovett; Ryan Alweiss, Yang Liu, Mehtaab Sawhney, Kewen Wu; David Ellis–Maria-Romina Ivan–Imre Leader; Luke Pebody; Chris Cao and others) independently sharpen Gilmer's method, raising the constant to $\frac{3 - \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 0.38$.
- **2023** — Will Sawin and others show $0.38$ is essentially the natural barrier of the "one-step" entropy approach; refinements push slightly past it but $1/2$ remains out of reach. Sawin obtains a small improvement beyond $0.38$.
- **2024–2025** — Continued work on closing the gap to $1/2$, on the lattice and qualitative forms, and on whether entropy methods can in principle reach $1/2$. The conjecture remains **open** in full generality.
