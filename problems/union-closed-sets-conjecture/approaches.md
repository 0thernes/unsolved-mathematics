# Approaches — The Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (Frankl)

_Major strategies, partial results, and barriers._

Throughout, $\mathcal{F}$ is a finite union-closed family on ground set $X$, $m = |\mathcal{F}|$, and for $x \in X$ we write $d(x)$ for the number of members of $\mathcal{F}$ containing $x$. The conjecture is that $\max_x d(x) \ge m/2$.

## Entropy / information-theoretic method (Gilmer's approach)

The current dominant line, introduced by **Justin Gilmer** in November 2022. Sample two members $A, B$ of $\mathcal{F}$ independently and uniformly; union-closure forces $A \cup B \in \mathcal{F}$. Comparing the entropy of $A \cup B$ to those of $A$ and $B$ coordinate-by-coordinate yields, *unless some element is abundant*, a contradiction via the concavity/subadditivity of the binary entropy function. Gilmer proved unconditionally that $\max_x d(x) \ge c\, m$ with $c \approx 0.01$ — the first constant-fraction bound after four decades. The **core idea** is that abundance is the only way the entropy bookkeeping can balance. **Best result:** within weeks, Sawin; Chase–Lovett; Alweiss–Liu–Sawhney–Wu; and Pebody independently optimized the argument to $c = \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 0.3820$. **Known barrier:** Gilmer already observed that the *single-step* two-set entropy inequality cannot, by itself, exceed $\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2}$; the extremal configuration for the inequality saturates there. Reaching $1/2$ requires genuinely new input (a non-uniform sampling distribution, multi-set unions, or correlations the one-step method discards). Sawin gave a small improvement strictly beyond $0.38$, confirming the barrier is not absolute but showing how hard the remaining gap is.

## Averaging and "abundant element" counting

The oldest elementary approach. One seeks a weighting or counting identity forcing $\sum_x d(x) = \sum_{A \in \mathcal F} |A|$ to be large enough that some $d(x) \ge m/2$. **Core idea:** if the average set size is large, abundance follows by pigeonhole. **Best result:** the conjecture holds whenever the average set size is at least... is **not** generally true — naive averaging fails, because families exist whose average set size is small. Knill and Wójcik obtained density-type partial results (e.g. some element in roughly $m / \log_2 m$ sets), and Wójcik (2001) and Bošnjak–Marković handled size-restricted cases. **Known barrier:** Sarvate and Renaud exhibited union-closed families in which the *most frequent element of a generating set* is not abundant, showing that the natural averaging heuristics and several plausible strengthenings are simply false; any averaging proof must be subtle.

## Small-ground-set and "few sets" structural cases

Verify the conjecture for all families on a small ground set $|X| = n$, or with few members $m$, by structure plus computation. **Core idea:** the lattice of a union-closed family on few points has bounded structure, enabling case analysis or exhaustive search. **Best result:** the conjecture is verified for all union-closed families with $|X| \le 12$ (work of Bošnjak–Marković and predecessors Roberts–Roberts, Lo Faro, Morris) and for all families with $m \le 50$ members. It also holds when $\mathcal{F}$ contains a set of size $1$ or $2$ (an immediate strong case), when the largest set has size $\le$ a small bound, or when $|X| \le 2\log_2 m$ roughly. **Known barrier:** these results do not scale — the search space grows super-exponentially, and structural arguments for small $n$ give no leverage on large families.

## Lattice-theoretic formulation

Translate to: every finite lattice $L$ with $|L| \ge 2$ has a join-irreducible $j$ with at most $|L|/2$ elements above it. **Core idea:** induct on join-irreducibles, or exploit lattice congruences and the structure of the irreducible "spine." **Best result:** the conjecture holds for large classes of lattices — lower-semimodular lattices, relatively complemented lattices, modular lattices (Abe, Reinhold, Czédli–Schmidt), lattices of dimension/height bounds, and planar/2-dimensional lattices. Czédli proved cases for "large" lattices relative to their generating structure. **Known barrier:** the irreducibles can be numerous and their up-sets uncorrelated; no induction has been found that controls the general lattice, and the modular/semimodular successes rely on closure properties the general case lacks.

## Graph and stable-set reformulations

Bruhn, Charbit, Schaudt, and Telle (2013) recast a sub-case in terms of **stable (independent) sets**: a graph $G$ gives rise to the union-closed family of complements of its stable sets, and the conjecture restricted there becomes a statement that some vertex lies in at most half the stable sets. **Core idea:** transfer extremal graph-theoretic tools (and a known result that, in the "twin-free" graph setting, such a vertex exists) into the set-system world. **Best result:** the conjecture holds for all union-closed families arising from graphs (every union-closed family generated by sets of size $\le 2$, equivalently the graph case), and for chordal, bipartite, and several other graph classes. **Known barrier:** general union-closed families are generated by sets of *arbitrary* size; the graph reduction covers only the size-$\le 2$ slice, and no reduction of the general problem to the graph case is known.

## Salzborn / generating-family and "weight" approaches

Salzborn's formulation and various weighting schemes (Poonen's symmetric-group / averaging-over-families framework) attempt to find a probability distribution on $X$ under which the expected frequency is provably $\ge m/2$. **Core idea:** if *no* element is abundant, derive a contradiction from a clever convexity or symmetry argument over the space of union-closed families. **Best result:** Poonen characterized exactly when a fixed weighting "works" for *all* families with a given up-set, giving necessary-and-sufficient conditions but not a universal weighting. **Known barrier:** Poonen's own analysis shows no single fixed weighting can certify the conjecture for all families, so weighting must be family-dependent — which is essentially as hard as the original problem.
