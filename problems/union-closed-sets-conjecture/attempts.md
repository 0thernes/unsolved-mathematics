# Attempts — The Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (Frankl)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## The pre-2022 plateau

For more than four decades, every general result took the form "for every union-closed family, *some* element appears in at least $g(m)$ sets," with $g$ embarrassingly weak. The strongest unconditional general bounds before the entropy era guaranteed only an element of frequency on the order of $m/\log_2 m$ or a fixed small absolute count derived from the number of sets — a logarithmic gap from the conjectured $m/2$. The frustration was that no construction came close to violating the conjecture: in the extremal-looking families the most frequent element sits at exactly $m/2$ (power sets) or comfortably above. This combination — a true-looking statement with no near-counterexample and no proof — is what gave the problem its reputation as a trap.

## Sarvate–Renaud and the failure of natural strengthenings

A famous near-miss of a different kind: **D. G. Sarvate and J.-C. Renaud** (around 1989–1990) showed that several *plausible strengthenings* are false. In particular, the most frequent element need not be one of the singletons or small generators; there exist union-closed families where an element that "ought" to be abundant by symmetry or generation is not. This killed the most natural averaging proofs and is the standard cautionary example quoted in the literature. It is not a counterexample to the conjecture itself — the conjecture survives — but to the easy routes toward it.

## Verified special cases (genuine, accepted partial results)

These are accepted, not disputed: the conjecture holds when the family contains a singleton or doubleton; when $|X| \le 12$ (Bošnjak–Marković, extending Roberts–Roberts, Lo Faro, Morris); when $m \le 50$; for families generated by sets of size $\le 2$ (the graph case, via Bruhn–Charbit–Schaudt–Telle 2013); for lower-semimodular, modular, and relatively complemented lattices (Abe, Reinhold, Czédli–Schmidt); and when $m \ge \frac{2}{3} 2^{|X|}$ (large, "dense" families). Reimer's theorem on the average set size of union-closed families is a celebrated adjacent result often cited in attempts.

## Gilmer's breakthrough and its rapid sharpening

In **November 2022**, **Justin Gilmer** (then at Google) posted "A constant lower bound for the union-closed sets conjecture," proving unconditionally that some element appears in at least $\approx 1\%$ of the sets via an entropy argument. This is a genuine, accepted breakthrough — the first constant-fraction bound. Gilmer explicitly conjectured his method could be pushed to $\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2} \approx 0.38$. Within roughly two weeks, this was achieved independently by **Will Sawin**; **Zachary Chase and Shachar Lovett**; **Ryan Alweiss, Yang Liu, Mehtaab Sawhney, and Kewen Wu**; and **Luke Pebody**; with related observations by **David Ellis, Maria-Romina Ivan, and Imre Leader** and others. Sawin subsequently obtained a small improvement strictly beyond $0.38$. These are all peer-accepted; the episode is a model of fast, transparent collaborative verification (much of it on arXiv and blogs within days).

## Claimed full proofs and disputed announcements

The problem has attracted occasional claimed *complete* proofs, none of which has been accepted. Several preprints announcing a resolution of the full conjecture have appeared on arXiv and preprint servers over the years; the consistent community assessment is that each contains a gap — typically an unjustified averaging step, an inductive claim that fails for the irreducibles, or an implicit assumption equivalent to the conjecture. As of the latest reference frontier, **no claimed full proof has survived expert scrutiny**, and the conjecture is regarded as open. We state this neutrally: the standard objection to such announcements is that they reduce, at a crucial step, to exactly the abundance inequality being proved, or invoke a strengthening already refuted by Sarvate–Renaud-type examples. Readers should treat any claim of a complete proof as unverified until it appears in a refereed venue with independent confirmation. The accepted state of the art remains the entropy bound of roughly $0.38$, conditional improvements aside, with $1/2$ unproven.
