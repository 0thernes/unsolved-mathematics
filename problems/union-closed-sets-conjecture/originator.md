# Originator(s) — The Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (Frankl)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Péter Frankl

**Péter Frankl** (born 26 March 1953, Kaposvár, Hungary) is a Hungarian-born combinatorialist, one of the most prolific figures in twentieth- and twenty-first-century extremal set theory. He earned his doctorate in Hungary and built his early reputation within the Erdős school of combinatorics, working closely with Paul Erdős, Vojtěch Rödl, Zoltán Füredi, Ronald Graham, Gil Kalai, and many others. He held positions in France at the CNRS for much of his research career and later settled in Japan, where he is also widely known as a public figure — a street performer, juggler, and popularizer of mathematics fluent in many languages. His combination of deep research output and public visibility is unusual among working mathematicians.

Frankl's research centers on **extremal problems for set systems**: how large or how structured a family of subsets of an $n$-element ground set can be subject to intersection, union, or shadow constraints. He is a central contributor to the theory surrounding the **Erdős–Ko–Rado theorem** and its many generalizations, to **shadow and compression** techniques (the Kruskal–Katona circle of ideas), to **Δ-systems / sunflowers** (the Erdős–Rado sunflower problem, on which his bounds were long state of the art), and to **traces and VC-dimension**. The methodological signature of his work is the manipulation of set families by compression and averaging, and the extraction of an "abundant" or "popular" object from a constrained family. The union-closed sets conjecture sits squarely in this tradition: it asks for an abundant *element* inside a family closed under a natural operation.

The motivation for posing the conjecture, as Frankl has recounted, was disarmingly simple. Union-closed families are ubiquitous — they arise as lattices of closed sets, as collections of unions of a generating set system, and in countless concrete constructions. It is *easy* to verify the abundance property in every small example one writes down, and the extremal cases (such as the full power set, where every element lies in exactly half the sets, or a chain) all satisfy it with equality or comfortably. That tension — overwhelming empirical support against the apparent absence of any handle for a proof — is exactly the kind of clean, deceptively elementary statement Frankl was drawn to propose. He reportedly circulated it around **1979**; it was never the subject of a dedicated founding paper by him but propagated through problem sessions, correspondence, and the problem lists that Erdős-school combinatorialists maintained.

It is worth distinguishing the **historical root** from the **modern formulation**. Frankl's original phrasing is the element-abundance statement for finite union-closed families. The equivalent **lattice formulation** — every finite lattice with at least two elements has a join-irreducible element lying below at most half of its elements — was not Frankl's framing but emerged from the order-theory community (Duffus, Poonen, Reinhold, Czédli, Rival) as the problem was absorbed into lattice theory. The two are provably equivalent, but they motivate different toolkits: the set form invites entropy and averaging, the lattice form invites structural induction on irreducibles. Frankl himself has generally worked and spoken about the set-theoretic version.

Frankl's broader legacy is enormous: hundreds of papers, foundational results in extremal combinatorics, and a generation of collaborators and students influenced by his style. Among the many open problems bearing his name, the union-closed sets conjecture is the most famous precisely because it is the most elementary to state and the most stubbornly resistant — a single, crisp inequality that nearly half a century of combinatorics could not settle, until Gilmer's 2022 entropy breakthrough finally produced an unconditional constant-fraction bound and reopened the problem to systematic attack.
