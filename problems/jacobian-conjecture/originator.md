# Originator(s) — The Jacobian Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Ott-Heinrich Keller (1906–1990)

The conjecture bears the name of **Ott-Heinrich Keller**, a German mathematician whose 1939 note first raised the question in print. Keller was born in 1906 and trained in the rich German geometric tradition of the interwar period. His mathematical interests spanned algebraic geometry, topology, and the foundations of geometry, and he is remembered both for the conjecture that carries his name and for substantial work on algebraic varieties and discrete geometry. He held academic positions in Germany through a turbulent era, and after the Second World War his career continued in the German Democratic Republic, where he became a professor at the Martin Luther University of Halle-Wittenberg. He died in 1990.

## The setting of the 1939 note

Keller's paper, "Ganze Cremona-Transformationen" (entire Cremona transformations), published in the *Monatshefte für Mathematik und Physik*, sat at the intersection of two themes that occupied algebraic geometers of his generation: the **Cremona group** of birational transformations of projective space, and the more rigid class of *polynomial* (everywhere-regular) transformations of affine space. Classical Cremona theory, especially in dimension 2, had been developed extensively by the Italian school. Keller asked a sharper question about the polynomial subclass: when a polynomial transformation has Jacobian determinant equal to a unit, must it be a genuine automorphism with polynomial inverse?

The motivation was structural. A polynomial map with unit Jacobian is, infinitesimally, everywhere a local isomorphism — the inverse function theorem furnishes a *formal* or analytic local inverse at each point. The deep question is whether this purely *local, infinitesimal* invertibility forces *global, algebraic* invertibility. Keller's instinct — that it should — reflected a belief that the constant-Jacobian condition is a very strong rigidity constraint. That belief has proven remarkably hard to confirm or refute, and the gap between "locally invertible everywhere" and "globally polynomial-invertible" is precisely where the difficulty lives.

## Historical root versus modern formulation

Keller phrased his question in terms close to integral transformations and unimodular Jacobians, in a context where the base could be taken as the integers or a general field. The **modern formulation** — a polynomial map $F\colon \mathbb{C}^n \to \mathbb{C}^n$ with $\det DF \in \mathbb{C}^\times$ admits a polynomial inverse — crystallized over subsequent decades as mathematicians recognized two essential refinements that Keller's broad phrasing did not isolate:

1. **Characteristic zero is essential.** In positive characteristic the statement is false (the Frobenius-twisted example $x \mapsto x - x^p$ has Jacobian $1$ yet is non-injective), so the modern conjecture is restricted to fields of characteristic $0$, canonically $\mathbb{C}$.
2. **The right invariant is constancy.** Over $\mathbb{C}$, a nowhere-vanishing polynomial is automatically a nonzero constant, so "non-vanishing Jacobian" and "constant Jacobian" coincide; stating it as "constant Jacobian" makes the algebra transparent.

Thus the problem as studied today is a sharpened descendant of Keller's question rather than a verbatim restatement of it.

## Legacy

Keller's broader legacy includes contributions to the topology of manifolds and to tiling and lattice problems — his name also attaches to *Keller's conjecture* on cube tilings, a separate problem that was confirmed in low dimensions and disproved in high dimensions (a 1992 counterexample in dimension 10 by Lagarias and Shor, with the boundary later pinned down). But it is the Jacobian Conjecture that has made his name a permanent fixture of algebra and algebraic geometry. The problem has generated an enormous literature, a celebrated catalogue of false proofs, and deep connections to automorphism groups, the Dixmier conjecture on the Weyl algebra, differential operators, and combinatorics. Steven Smale's inclusion of it on his 1998 list of central problems for the twenty-first century cemented its status as one of the outstanding open questions Keller bequeathed to mathematics.
