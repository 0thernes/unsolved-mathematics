# Attempts — The Jacobian Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The Jacobian Conjecture is notorious less for a single dramatic near-miss than for a long catalogue of **erroneous "proofs"** — by amateurs and professionals alike — that have been announced and withdrawn over more than eighty years. Hyman Bass, Edwin Connell, and David Wright opened their 1982 survey with a warning to this effect, and the warning has aged well. What follows distinguishes genuine partial results from disputed or retracted general claims.

## Genuine partial results and near-misses

- **Moh's degree-100 theorem (1983).** Tzuong-Tsieng Moh proved the two-dimensional conjecture for polynomial maps of degree at most 100. This is a substantive, accepted result and remains a benchmark for the dimension-2 case; it does not, however, extend to a uniform bound, and dimension 2 in unbounded degree is still open.

- **Degree-2 (quadratic) case.** Any polynomial map with constant Jacobian whose components have degree $\le 2$ is invertible (a result associated with Stuart Wang and contemporaries). Clean and complete, but the reduction theorems show degree 2 is far from the general case.

- **Cubic reduction (Bass–Connell–Wright; Yagzhev).** Reducing the whole conjecture to cubic-homogeneous maps was hailed as a major simplification — and it is — yet it is a near-miss in the sense that it raised hopes the cubic case would be tractable. It was not; the difficulty merely concentrated.

- **Drużkowski cubic-linear reduction (1983).** A further sharpening to maps with $H_i = (a_i\cdot x)^3$. Important and correct, but again did not yield the general theorem.

- **de Bondt–van den Essen structural theorems (2000s).** Resolution of the symmetric/gradient case under nilpotent-Hessian hypotheses and of nilpotent Jacobians in low rank. Solid contributions that carve off large special families without closing the conjecture.

## Disputed and retracted general claims

A recurring pattern is the announcement of a *general* proof that does not survive scrutiny. Stating the disputes neutrally:

- **The "Abhyankar–Moh / two-dimensional folklore" pitfalls.** Several early two-variable arguments leaned implicitly on degree control that fails for higher-degree maps; the *Abhyankar–Moh embedding theorem* (a correct and celebrated result about embeddings of the line) was at times conflated with a proof of the plane conjecture, which it does not give.

- **Periodic arXiv preprints claiming a full proof.** Across the 2000s–2020s, multiple manuscripts asserting a complete proof in all dimensions have appeared on arXiv and elsewhere. The community's consistent response has been that these contain gaps — typically an unjustified passage from a *formal* or analytic local inverse to a *polynomial* global inverse, or an unproven nilpotency/integrability claim in the cubic reduction. Because such claims are numerous and individually short-lived, this dossier does not endorse any specific preprint as a proof; readers should treat any "solved" announcement as **unverified** until it appears in a refereed venue with independent confirmation.

- **Positive-characteristic confusion.** A class of mistaken arguments implicitly uses character­istic-zero facts (e.g., that a non-vanishing polynomial is constant) without flagging that the statement is *false* in positive characteristic, exposing where a putative proof must use $\mathrm{char}=0$ essentially.

## The methodological lesson

The standard objection that sinks general attempts is the **local-to-global gap**: the constant-Jacobian hypothesis gives an analytic/formal inverse everywhere, but turning that into a *polynomial* inverse requires global control (e.g., properness, a degree bound that is itself derived, not assumed) that has never been supplied in full generality. Bass–Connell–Wright's framing of "formulation of equations" was in part an attempt to make the obstructions explicit so that future arguments could be checked against them. As of the current frontier, no general proof has withstood refereeing, and the conjecture's status remains **open**.
