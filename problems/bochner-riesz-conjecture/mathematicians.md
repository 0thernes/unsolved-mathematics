# Top Mathematicians — The Bochner–Riesz Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Salomon Bochner | 1930s | originator | Introduced spherical Riesz means in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (1936); posed the summability question. |
| 2 | Marcel Riesz | 1910s–20s | originator | Created the Riesz (typical) means $(1-\lambda/R)^\delta$ underlying the operator. |
| 3 | Elias M. Stein | 1950s–2000s | breakthrough | Analytic interpolation; restriction program; $L^2$ restriction endpoint; framed the hierarchy. |
| 4 | Charles Fefferman | 1970s | breakthrough | Ball multiplier theorem (1971) linking the problem to Kakeya; planar contributions. |
| 5 | Lennart Carleson | 1970s | breakthrough | Carleson–Sjölin theorem: full resolution of the conjecture in the plane (1972). |
| 6 | Per Sjölin | 1970s | breakthrough | Co-author of the planar resolution; oscillatory-integral techniques. |
| 7 | Antonio Córdoba | 1970s | breakthrough | Square-function and $L^4$ Kakeya-maximal proof of the planar case. |
| 8 | Jean Bourgain | 1990s–2010s | breakthrough | Kakeya-maximal advances (1991); multilinear method with Guth; decoupling with Demeter. |
| 9 | Terence Tao | 1998–present | frontier | Bilinear restriction (with Vargas–Vega), sharp bilinear estimate, multilinear restriction. |
| 10 | Larry Guth | 2010s–present | frontier | Polynomial partitioning; Bourgain–Guth multilinear bounds; current best ranges (with Hickman, Iliopoulou). |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of harmonic analysis, additive combinatorics, and incidence geometry, organized around the linked triad of **Bochner–Riesz, restriction, and Kakeya** (with the cone, local smoothing, and decoupling nearby). Beyond those listed, central contemporary contributors include **Ciprian Demeter** (decoupling), **Jonathan Bennett** and **Anthony Carbery** (multilinear restriction, Brascamp–Lieb methods), **Sanghyuk Lee** (bilinear and maximal Bochner–Riesz), **Jonathan Hickman** and **Marina Iliopoulou** (polynomial methods, sharp oscillatory bounds), **Andreas Seeger**, **Christopher Sogge** (Fourier integral operators, eigenfunction estimates), **Nets Katz** and **Joshua Zahl** (Kakeya), and **Hong Wang** (restriction, recent Kakeya progress). The work is concentrated in a few centers (MIT, Princeton, Bonn, Edinburgh, Madison, Seoul) and advances through cross-pollination: a theorem on decoupling or Kakeya routinely tightens the Bochner–Riesz range. The two-dimensional case is closed; every result above $n=2$ is partial, and the field's culture is one of careful, incremental, heavily peer-checked progress.
