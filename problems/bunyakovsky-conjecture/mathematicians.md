# Top Mathematicians — Bunyakovsky's Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Viktor Bunyakovsky | 19th c. | originator | Stated the conjecture (1857), isolating the three necessary conditions (irreducibility, positive leading coefficient, no fixed prime divisor). |
| 2 | P. G. L. Dirichlet | 19th c. | originator | 1837 theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions — the degree-one case and conceptual ancestor. |
| 3 | Edmund Landau | early 20th c. | expositor | Promoted $n^2+1$ to fame as the fourth of his 1912 ICM problems, fixing it as the emblematic open case. |
| 4 | G. H. Hardy | early 20th c. | breakthrough | With Littlewood, Conjecture F (1923): the quantitative density of prime values via the singular series. |
| 5 | J. E. Littlewood | early 20th c. | breakthrough | Co-author of Conjecture F; circle-method heuristics for prime-value densities. |
| 6 | Andrzej Schinzel | 20th c. | breakthrough | Hypothesis H (1958, with Sierpiński) — the multi-polynomial generalization with Bunyakovsky as $k=1$. |
| 7 | Atle Selberg | 20th c. | frontier | The Selberg sieve and the identification of the parity problem — the central barrier to reaching primes. |
| 8 | Henryk Iwaniec | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | 1978 $P_2$ theorem for quadratics ($n^2+1=P_2$ i.o.); with Friedlander, the asymptotic sieve and $a^2+b^4$ primes (1998). |
| 9 | John Friedlander | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | With Iwaniec, broke parity for $a^2+b^4$ (1998) and co-authored the asymptotic sieve and *Opera de Cribro*. |
| 10 | D. R. Heath-Brown | 20th–21st c. | frontier | Primes of the form $a^3+2b^3$ (2001), extending parity-breaking technology to cubic forms. |

## Notes

The active community sits squarely within analytic number theory and sieve theory. The Friedlander–Iwaniec school (and their treatise *Opera de Cribro*) remains the methodological center of gravity for thin-sequence prime detection, and Heath-Brown's work on binary forms defines the multivariate frontier. The bounded-gaps revolution — **James Maynard** and **Terence Tao** (building on Goldston–Pintz–Yıldırım) — reshaped the adjacent $k$-tuple / Hypothesis-H landscape and keeps Bunyakovsky-type questions visible, even though their weights act on linear forms. A distinct and very active strand pursues the **function-field analogue**, where **Will Sawin** and **Mark Shusterman** (with antecedents in **Paul Pollack**'s work) have proven Bunyakovsky/Hypothesis-H statements over $\mathbb{F}_q[t]$ unconditionally for large $q$, sharpening the sense that the integer case is "true but currently out of reach." Computational and statistical work (e.g. Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan on prime biases) continually tests the Bateman–Horn predictions that encode the quantitative form of the conjecture.
