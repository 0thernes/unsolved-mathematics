# Originator(s) — Yang–Mills Existence and Mass Gap

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The problem has two layers of "origination": the **physical theory** (Yang and Mills, 1954) and the **mathematical problem statement** (Jaffe and Witten, 2000). Both are credited in the metadata.

## C. N. Yang (1922–) and Robert Mills (1927–1999)

**Chen Ning Yang** was born in Hefei, China, studied at the National Southwestern Associated University and Tsinghua, and completed his doctorate at the University of Chicago under Edward Teller, working close to the orbit of Enrico Fermi. He shared the 1957 Nobel Prize in Physics with Tsung-Dao Lee for the prediction of parity nonconservation in weak interactions. **Robert Mills** was an American physicist trained at Columbia (PhD under Norman Kroll); in 1953–54 he was a young postdoctoral colleague of Yang at Brookhaven National Laboratory.

The two were preoccupied with a structural question: electromagnetism is a gauge theory built on local $U(1)$ phase invariance, with the photon emerging as the connection field enforcing that invariance. Could the same principle be applied to isospin symmetry, where the gauge group is the non-abelian $SU(2)$? Their 1954 paper, "Conservation of Isotopic Spin and Isotopic Gauge Invariance," answered yes, introducing a connection whose curvature is **nonlinear** in the potential because the group does not commute. This non-abelian self-interaction is the source of all the difficulty and richness of the theory. At the time the obvious physical objection — the gauge bosons appear massless — seemed fatal, and Wolfgang Pauli had independently reached the same equations but withheld them for exactly this reason. The eventual resolution came through the Higgs mechanism (for the electroweak sector) and, for the strong force, through confinement and the conjectured mass gap. Yang–Mills theory is now the mathematical skeleton of the entire Standard Model.

## Arthur Jaffe (1937–) and Edward Witten (1951–)

The *problem*, as posed for the Clay Institute, is the work of two figures who bridge physics and rigorous mathematics. **Arthur Jaffe**, the Landon T. Clay Professor at Harvard and a founder of constructive quantum field theory, spent decades (with James Glimm and others) rigorously constructing lower-dimensional QFTs and codifying the axiomatic frameworks — Wightman and Osterwalder–Schrader — in which "a quantum field theory exists" can even be given precise meaning. His monograph with Glimm, *Quantum Physics: A Functional Integral Point of View*, is a touchstone. Jaffe understood acutely that no interacting four-dimensional QFT had ever been constructed, and that Yang–Mills was the most promising target because asymptotic freedom controls its short-distance behavior.

**Edward Witten** is the dominant mathematical physicist of his generation, a Fields Medalist (1990) at the Institute for Advanced Study, whose work reshaped topology, geometry, and quantum field theory (topological QFT, Seiberg–Witten theory, mirror symmetry, knot invariants from Chern–Simons theory). Witten supplied the physical insight and precise expectations — confinement, the spectrum, the role of the mass gap — that the formulation must capture.

Their joint **official problem description** (Clay, 2000) is careful to demand both a mathematically rigorous construction (a Hilbert space, a vacuum, a positive energy spectrum, the correct symmetries) and the dynamical statement that the spectrum has a gap above the vacuum. They explicitly note that even establishing existence — independent of the gap — would be a landmark.

## Two formulations, one problem

The **historical root** is a concrete classical/quantum field theory of physics, motivated by symmetry and the structure of the nuclear force. The **modern formulation** is an axiomatic existence-and-spectrum problem in analysis and mathematical physics, motivated by the gap between physicists' computations (perturbation theory, lattice, instantons) and rigorous mathematics. The legacy is immense in both registers: Yang–Mills underlies the Standard Model and won several Nobel Prizes downstream, while the Clay problem stands as the deepest open question about whether four-dimensional interacting quantum fields exist at all.
