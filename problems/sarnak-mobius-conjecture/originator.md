# Originator(s) — Sarnak's Möbius Disjointness Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Peter Sarnak (b. 1953)

Peter Clive Sarnak was born on 18 December 1953 in Johannesburg, South Africa. He completed his undergraduate studies at the University of the Witwatersrand and moved to the United States for graduate work at **Stanford University**, where he earned his Ph.D. in 1980 under the supervision of **Paul Cohen** (the Fields Medalist known for forcing and the independence of the continuum hypothesis). Sarnak held positions at the Courant Institute and Stanford before settling at **Princeton University**, where he is the Eugene Higgins Professor of Mathematics, with a long-standing concurrent appointment at the **Institute for Advanced Study (IAS)**.

Sarnak is one of the central figures of modern analytic number theory and its interface with automorphic forms, spectral theory, and dynamics. His honors include the **Wolf Prize in Mathematics (2014)**, the Ostrowski Prize, the George Pólya Prize, the Cole Prize in Number Theory, and election to the Royal Society and the U.S. National Academy of Sciences. He has shaped the field as much through ideas as through mentorship; his many doctoral students include several leading number theorists.

## Intellectual background and the genesis of the conjecture

Sarnak's research weaves together three threads that converge in the Möbius conjecture. First, **automorphic forms and $L$-functions**, where the distribution of zeros and the size of exponential sums over primes are governed by deep analytic structure. Second, **spectral theory and quantum chaos**, including his foundational work (with Rudnick, and with Luo) on quantum unique ergodicity and the equidistribution of Hecke eigenfunctions. Third, **homogeneous dynamics and equidistribution**, particularly horocycle and nilflows, where rigidity phenomena force orbits to be highly regular.

The conjecture grew out of Sarnak's recognition that the long-standing number-theoretic heuristic — that $\mu(n)$ is "pseudorandom" and orthogonal to any structured sequence — could be given a clean, universal formulation using the language of dynamical complexity. **Topological entropy** quantifies the exponential growth rate of distinguishable orbit segments; a system of *zero* entropy is exactly one whose orbits are predictable, i.e. "low complexity." Sarnak's insight was to declare: *the obstruction to $\mu$ correlating with $f(T^n x)$ is precisely positive entropy.* This packaged dozens of disparate special cases — circle rotations (Davenport), nilsequences (Green–Tao), automatic sequences like Thue–Morse — under one statement, and it made transparent the link to Chowla's conjecture, which is the disjointness of $\mu$ from the (entropy-zero) shift acting on its own correlation sequence.

He laid this out in his widely circulated 2010–2011 lecture notes, **"Three lectures on the Möbius function, randomness and dynamics,"** and in lectures at the IAS and elsewhere. The framing was deliberately unifying: it told ergodic theorists that a famous arithmetic function was a natural test object for their machinery, and it told number theorists that entropy was the right invariant to measure "structure."

## The historical root: Chowla

The modern formulation is Sarnak's, but its probabilistic heart is older. **Sarvadaman Chowla** (1907–1995), in his 1965 monograph *The Riemann Hypothesis and Hilbert's Tenth Problem*, conjectured that the higher autocorrelations $\sum_{n\le N}\lambda(n+h_1)\cdots\lambda(n+h_k)$ of the Liouville function vanish in the mean, the precise sense in which $\lambda$ (and hence $\mu$) is "random." Sarnak's contribution was to recognize that Chowla's statement is the disjointness instance for a particular zero-entropy system, and to generalize it to *all* such systems. The two conjectures are thus siblings: Chowla is the self-correlation case; Sarnak is the universal dynamical case.

## Legacy

The conjecture catalyzed an entire subfield at the boundary of ergodic theory and analytic number theory. It motivated the Bourgain–Sarnak–Ziegler orthogonality criterion, drew in researchers such as Tao, Frantzikinakis, Host, Lemańczyk, de la Rue, El Abdalaoui, and Kanigowski, and reframed Chowla's old conjecture as the dynamical "self-correlation" case. Even where the full statement remains open, the program has produced a rich theory of $\mu$-disjoint systems and sharpened our understanding of the Liouville and Möbius functions as quasi-random objects.
