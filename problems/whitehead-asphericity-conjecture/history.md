# History — The Whitehead Asphericity Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## How the problem arose

In his 1941 paper *On adding relations to homotopy groups* (Annals of Mathematics), J. H. C. Whitehead studied how the homotopy type of a two-dimensional complex changes when one attaches new $2$-cells (i.e. adds relations to a group presentation). A connected CW complex $K$ is **aspherical** when its higher homotopy groups vanish, $\pi_n(K)=0$ for $n\ge 2$; equivalently, for a $2$-complex, when its universal cover is contractible and $\pi_2(K)=0$, so that $K$ is a $K(\pi,1)$ on the $2$-skeleton. At the end of that paper Whitehead asked, almost in passing, whether asphericity is inherited by subcomplexes: **if a connected $2$-dimensional CW complex $K$ is aspherical, must every connected subcomplex $L\subseteq K$ also be aspherical?**

The question is deceptively elementary. Because $\pi_2$ of a $2$-complex is the relation module — the second homotopy as a $\mathbb{Z}[\pi_1]$-module generated by the attaching maps — the conjecture is at heart a statement in combinatorial group theory: adding relations to an aspherical presentation cannot "destroy" the asphericity already present, in the precise sense that removing $2$-cells keeps $\pi_2=0$. Whitehead himself did not claim it as a theorem; it was a query, and it has resisted resolution for over eighty years.

## Reformulations

Several equivalent or near-equivalent forms guide modern work. The **single-relator reduction**: it suffices to treat the case where $L$ is obtained from $K$ by removing a single $2$-cell, so one asks whether asphericity is preserved when one relator is deleted. The **group-theoretic form** via relation modules: a presentation is aspherical iff its associated chain complex over $\mathbb{Z}[\pi_1]$ is exact, and one studies whether sub-presentations stay exact. A closely linked statement is the conjecture that subcomplexes of aspherical $2$-complexes have **free** (or at least projective) $\pi_2$ as a module — the connection to coherence and to the Eilenberg–Ganea/Stallings–Swan circle of ideas. The conjecture also interacts with **Cohen–Lyndon** theory, one-relator groups, and the theory of **diagrammatically reducible** complexes (Gersten, Sieradski, Huck).

## Timeline

- **1939** — Whitehead introduces "asphericity" for $2$-complexes and the theory of attaching relations in early work on homotopy of $2$-complexes.
- **1941** — J. H. C. Whitehead, *On adding relations to homotopy groups*, poses the asphericity question for subcomplexes of aspherical $2$-complexes.
- **1949–1950** — Work of Whitehead and others on $\pi_2$ as a module clarifies the group-theoretic content of the problem.
- **1962** — Lyndon's study of one-relator groups and cohomological dimension; one-relator presentations are aspherical when the relator is not a proper power (Lyndon's Identity Theorem), supplying a key class of test cases.
- **1973–1981** — Cockcroft, Gersten, and others develop combinatorial/geometric reformulations; "Cockcroft complexes" and diagrammatic reducibility emerge as tools.
- **1980s** — S. M. Gersten and J. Howie connect the conjecture to small-cancellation and curvature conditions; Howie proves it for finite "staggered" and certain locally indicable cases.
- **1983** — J. Howie, *Some remarks on a problem of J. H. C. Whitehead* and related papers, reduce the finite case to specific configurations and prove substantial subcases.
- **1990s** — W. Bogley and collaborators survey the conjecture; the distinction between the **finite** and **infinite** versions is sharpened — a counterexample, if it exists, may require infinitely many cells.
- **2003** — Bogley–Pride and the *Two-dimensional homotopy and combinatorial group theory* volume (Hog-Angeloni, Metzler, Sieradski, eds.) consolidate the state of the art.
- **2000s–2020s** — The conjecture remains open; it is now widely connected to the **Eilenberg–Ganea**, **relation gap**, and **coherence** problems, and to questions about $L^2$-Betti numbers of one-relator and aspherical groups. No counterexample and no general proof has appeared.
