# Attempts — The Whitehead Asphericity Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

## Confirmed special cases

The conjecture is a theorem in several important classes, and these positive results structure the search for any counterexample.

- **One-relator presentations (Lyndon, 1950; Cockcroft, 1954).** Lyndon's Identity Theorem implies that a one-relator complex whose relator is not a proper power is aspherical, and Cockcroft analyzed when $\pi_2$ vanishes for such complexes. Deleting the single $2$-cell leaves a wedge of circles, trivially aspherical, so the conjecture holds here outright.
- **Locally indicable fundamental group (Howie, 1980s).** Building on Brodskii, Howie proved subcomplex-asphericity whenever $\pi_1$ is locally indicable. This covers a wide swath of torsion-free groups.
- **Small-cancellation and diagrammatically reducible complexes (Gersten; Sieradski; Pride; Huck).** Complexes satisfying $C(6)$, $C(4)\&T(4)$, or a valid weight test are DR, and DR passes to subcomplexes, confirming the conjecture in these geometries.

## Howie's reduction and the finite case

James Howie's papers of the early 1980s (notably *Some remarks on a problem of J. H. C. Whitehead*) are the most influential near-comprehensive attack. Howie showed that a *finite* counterexample, if one exists, is heavily constrained — forcing the ambient fundamental group to lie outside the locally indicable class and to carry torsion — and he reduced the finite version to the analysis of specific minimal configurations. This did not produce a proof or a counterexample, but it sharply narrowed where one could live and gave the field its working dichotomy between the finite and infinite versions of the conjecture.

## The role of acyclic and infinite constructions

A recurring near-miss program tries to *build* a counterexample as an infinite ascending union of relators, or from an acyclic but non-aspherical $2$-complex (echoing the Andrews–Curtis circle of ideas). The hope is that an aspherical limit could contain a non-aspherical subcomplex once infinitely many cells are present. No such construction has succeeded; controlling $\pi_2$ in the colimit has defeated every attempt, and these efforts are generally regarded as motivating evidence for *where* a counterexample might hide rather than partial proofs.

## On claimed proofs

The Whitehead asphericity conjecture has not, to date, accumulated a famous publicly retracted "proof" of the kind seen in some other open problems; the literature contains careful partial results rather than disputed grand claims. The standard cautionary note instead concerns **scope inflation**: results valid for diagrammatically reducible or locally indicable complexes are sometimes loosely paraphrased as settling "the Whitehead conjecture," when in fact they settle only a sufficient-condition subcase. The crucial gap — that asphericity does **not** imply diagrammatic reducibility — means no DR-based argument can be honestly upgraded to the full statement. Readers should treat any announcement of a general resolution with the same scrutiny: the unresolved core is precisely the class of aspherical, non-DR, non-locally-indicable complexes, and that class remains untouched.

## Connections that have NOT broken the problem

Attempts to derive the conjecture from, or reduce it to, the **Eilenberg–Ganea problem**, the **relation gap problem**, or **coherence** of one-relator groups have clarified the landscape but produced no implication strong enough to close it. In several cases the dependence runs the wrong way (a counterexample to one might yield a counterexample to another), which is itself a reason the problem is considered hard: it sits at a junction of multiple open questions in two-dimensional topology.
