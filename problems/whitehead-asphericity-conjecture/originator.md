# Originator(s) — The Whitehead Asphericity Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## J. H. C. Whitehead (1904–1960)

**John Henry Constantine Whitehead** — universally "Henry" Whitehead — was one of the founding figures of modern algebraic topology and a central architect of homotopy theory. Born in Madras (now Chennai), British India, on 11 November 1904, he was the nephew of the philosopher and mathematician Alfred North Whitehead. He was educated at Eton and then Balliol College, Oxford, where he initially read mathematics without exceptional distinction and, for a period, worked as a stockbroker before returning to research. He pursued doctoral study at Princeton under Oswald Veblen, with whom he co-authored *The Foundations of Differential Geometry* (1932), establishing the rigorous modern definition of a differentiable manifold.

Whitehead spent the bulk of his career at Oxford, where he was Waynflete Professor of Pure Mathematics from 1947. During the Second World War he worked on operational research and submarine warfare at the Admiralty. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1944, served as President of the London Mathematical Society, and was a generous, gregarious mentor whose Oxford seminar trained a generation of topologists. He died suddenly of a heart attack on 8 May 1960 while visiting Princeton.

## Mathematical background and motivations

Whitehead's deepest contributions lie in homotopy theory and the combinatorial topology of low-dimensional complexes. He introduced **CW complexes**, the now-standard category of spaces built by attaching cells, and proved the **Whitehead theorem**: a map between CW complexes inducing isomorphisms on all homotopy groups is a homotopy equivalence. He created **simple homotopy theory** and the **Whitehead group** $\mathrm{Wh}(\pi)$ and Whitehead torsion, foundational to geometric topology and surgery theory. The **Whitehead product** on homotopy groups and the **Whitehead link** also bear his name.

The asphericity conjecture grew directly out of his program to understand the homotopy types of $2$-dimensional complexes — the geometric avatars of group presentations. In the late 1930s and early 1940s Whitehead was developing the theory of how attaching $2$-cells (adding relators) changes $\pi_2$, which he understood as a module over the group ring of the fundamental group. He recognized that a $2$-complex is aspherical precisely when this module vanishes, encoding the exactness of a chain complex over $\mathbb{Z}[\pi_1]$. In his 1941 paper *On adding relations to homotopy groups*, having built this machinery, he asked whether passing to a subcomplex — deleting relators — could ever spoil asphericity. He posed it as an open question, not a claim; the modern formulation is essentially identical to his, with later authors making explicit the reductions to the single-cell and group-ring–exactness forms.

## Legacy

Whitehead's framework — CW complexes, the module-theoretic view of $\pi_2$, simple homotopy theory — is the language in which the conjecture is still studied. His name attaches to an extraordinary range of central objects, and the asphericity conjecture is the most stubborn of the open problems he bequeathed. That a question he tossed off at the close of a single paper has anchored decades of research in two-dimensional homotopy theory and combinatorial group theory is itself a measure of how fertile his geometric instincts were.
