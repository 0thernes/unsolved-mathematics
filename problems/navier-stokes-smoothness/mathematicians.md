# Top Mathematicians — Navier–Stokes Existence and Smoothness

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Claude-Louis Navier | 1785–1836 | originator | First derived the viscous flow equations (1822), adding internal friction to Euler's system. |
| 2 | George Gabriel Stokes | 1819–1903 | originator | Gave the modern continuum derivation (1845); founded the rigorous theory of Newtonian viscous flow. |
| 3 | Jean Leray | 1906–1998 | breakthrough | Constructed global weak solutions (1934) and posed the regularity/uniqueness problem; defined the Leray–Hopf class. |
| 4 | Eberhard Hopf | 1902–1983 | breakthrough | Extended weak existence to bounded domains (1951); co-namesake of Leray–Hopf solutions. |
| 5 | Olga Ladyzhenskaya | 1922–2004 | breakthrough | Proved global regularity and uniqueness in 2D; authoritative treatise on viscous incompressible flow. |
| 6 | James Serrin | 1926–2012 | breakthrough | Established the Prodi–Serrin conditional regularity criteria for weak solutions. |
| 7 | Luis Caffarelli | b. 1948 | breakthrough | Co-author of the partial-regularity theorem (1982): singular set has parabolic dimension $\le 1$. |
| 8 | Louis Nirenberg | 1925–2020 | breakthrough | Co-author of the CKN partial-regularity theorem; foundational figure in elliptic and parabolic PDE. |
| 9 | Charles Fefferman | b. 1949 | originator | Wrote the official Clay Millennium Prize problem statement (2000); harmonic-analytic framing. |
| 10 | Terence Tao | b. 1975 | frontier | Averaged-equation finite-time blow-up (2014), formalizing the supercriticality barrier; sharp critical-space results. |

## Notes

The active research community spans several centers and overlapping programs. The harmonic-analysis/critical-space line runs through Herbert Koch and Daniel Tataru (well-posedness in $BMO^{-1}$), with Vladimír Šverák, Gregory Seregin, and the late Luis Escauriaza on conditional regularity and backward uniqueness. The partial-regularity and weak-solution theory continues via Fang-Hua Lin, Vladimir Scheffer (its originator), and Igor Kukavica. The most visible recent surge is the convex-integration school — Camillo De Lellis and László Székelyhidi, with Tristan Buckmaster, Vlad Vicol, Philip Isett, and Tarek Elgindi — which produced non-uniqueness of weak solutions and Euler blow-up, reshaping expectations about what a resolution must look like. Computational blow-up search is led by Thomas Y. Hou and collaborators. The consensus view is that genuine progress now requires structure beyond energy and scaling, and the field is roughly split between those seeking a regularity proof and those hunting for a viscous singularity.
