# Originator(s) — The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture is jointly attributed to **George Szekeres** (1973) and **Paul D. Seymour** (1979), who arrived at the same statement from very different mathematical cultures. A third name, **W. T. Tutte**, hovers over the problem: the topological reformulation as the Circular Embedding Conjecture is his, and the flow theory that powers most attacks is his creation.

## George Szekeres (1911–2005)

Szekeres was a Hungarian-Australian mathematician of extraordinary range. Born in Budapest, trained originally as a chemical engineer, he was part of the legendary Budapest circle of young mathematicians around Paul Erdős and Esther Klein in the early 1930s. With Erdős he proved the Erdős–Szekeres theorem on monotone subsequences and the "Happy Ending Problem" on convex polygons in the plane (so named by Erdős because it led to Szekeres's marriage to Esther Klein). Fleeing the rise of fascism, Szekeres spent the war years in Shanghai under harsh conditions, then emigrated to Australia, taking positions at the University of Adelaide and later the University of New South Wales.

Szekeres's mathematics spanned combinatorics, number theory, relativity (the Kruskal–Szekeres coordinates for the Schwarzschild black hole bear his name), and group theory. His route to the cycle double cover came through *polyhedral decompositions*: he asked, for cubic graphs, when one can decompose the edge set into polygons so that the structure resembles the 2-dimensional skeleton of a polyhedron, with each edge shared by exactly two faces. In his 1973 paper "Polyhedral decompositions of cubic graphs" he formulated precisely the cubic case of CDC, motivated by the analogy with the boundary complex of a convex polytope and by classical map-colouring questions. For Szekeres the problem was fundamentally *geometric-topological*: a double cover is the combinatorial shadow of an embedding.

## Paul D. Seymour (born 1950)

Seymour, a British mathematician, is one of the architects of modern structural graph theory. Educated at Oxford, he held positions at Oxford, Ohio State, Rutgers, Bellcore, and (since 2007) Princeton. With Neil Robertson he proved the Graph Minor Theorem and the structure theorems underpinning it, work that reshaped the field; with Robertson, Sanders, and Thomas he gave a verified proof of the Four Colour Theorem; he co-developed the theory of perfect graphs (the Strong Perfect Graph Theorem, with Chudnovsky, Robertson, and Thomas).

Seymour reached the conjecture from the theory of *nowhere-zero flows and the cycle space*. In his 1979 paper "Sums of circuits" he studied when the edge set of a graph can be expressed as a sum of circuits, and the double-cover question arose as the natural extremal form: cover every edge exactly twice. Seymour's 6-flow theorem (1981) — every bridgeless graph has a nowhere-zero 6-flow — and Jaeger's 8-flow theorem are the deep companions to this circle of ideas, since the existence of a nowhere-zero 4-flow on a cubic graph is equivalent to 3-edge-colourability and yields a CDC immediately.

## Two formulations, one conjecture

Szekeres's polyhedral/embedding picture and Seymour's flow/cycle-space picture are equivalent, and the modern statement — *every bridgeless graph has a collection of cycles covering each edge exactly twice* — subsumes both. The fusion of these viewpoints is itself a large part of the conjecture's appeal: it ties surface topology (2-cell embeddings, the Circular Embedding Conjecture), additive structure (the cycle space over $\mathrm{GF}(2)$), and edge-colouring (snarks, the Berge–Fulkerson Conjecture) into a single problem. The legacy of all three originators — Szekeres's geometric instinct, Seymour's structural machinery, Tutte's flows and embeddings — remains visible in every serious attack on the problem.
