# Originator(s) — The Hadamard Matrix Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Jacques Hadamard (1865–1963)

**Jacques Salomon Hadamard** was one of the most influential French mathematicians of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, remembered above all for his 1896 proof of the **Prime Number Theorem** (obtained independently and simultaneously by Charles-Jean de la Vallée Poussin). Born in Versailles, he studied at the École Normale Supérieure, completed his doctorate in 1892 on functions defined by Taylor series, and held chairs at the Collège de France, the École Polytechnique, and the École Centrale. His mathematics ranged across complex analysis, analytic number theory, partial differential equations (where the notion of a **well-posed problem** is due to him), differential geometry, and what he called "the psychology of invention," the subject of his late book *An Essay on the Psychology of Invention in the Mathematical Field* (1945).

The matrix conjecture grew from a question in **analysis and determinants**, not combinatorics. In his 1893 paper *Résolution d'une question relative aux déterminants*, Hadamard sought the maximum possible determinant of a matrix whose entries are bounded in modulus by $1$. He proved the sharp inequality $|\det H| \le \prod_i \|r_i\| \le n^{n/2}$ and analyzed the equality case: a real matrix achieving $n^{n/2}$ must have entries $\pm1$ and mutually orthogonal rows, forcing the order to be $1$, $2$, or a multiple of $4$. Having found examples at orders $12$ and $20$ and recognizing Sylvester's powers-of-two family, Hadamard asked the natural converse — does a maximal-determinant $\{\pm1\}$ matrix exist for *every* order divisible by $4$? This existence question, peripheral to his analytic concerns, became the conjecture that bears his name. His broader legacy is immense: the Hadamard product, Hadamard three-circles theorem, Cartan–Hadamard manifolds, Hadamard's inequality, and a lineage of students (notably André Weil, Maurice Fréchet, and others through his famous seminar).

## Raymond Paley (1907–1933)

**Raymond Edward Alan Christopher Paley** was a brilliant English mathematician whose career was cut short by his death in an avalanche near Banff, Alberta, at age 26. A student at Trinity College, Cambridge, he worked with **J. E. Littlewood** and collaborated intensively with **Norbert Wiener** (the *Paley–Wiener theorems* in Fourier analysis) and with **Antoni Zygmund** (Littlewood–Paley theory, foundational to harmonic analysis). Paley's instincts were analytic and number-theoretic, and it was through **quadratic residues in finite fields** that he transformed the Hadamard problem.

In his 1933 paper *On orthogonal matrices*, Paley used the **Legendre symbol** to build the *Jacobsthal matrix* $Q$ of a prime power $q$ and from it constructed Hadamard matrices of order $q+1$ when $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ and of order $2(q+1)$ when $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. These **Paley constructions** were the first to settle infinitely many *new* orders beyond Sylvester's powers of two, and they recast the conjecture as a question about which integers $4k$ are reachable by combining a small toolbox of algebraic and number-theoretic constructions. Paley explicitly tabulated the smallest orders his methods left open (such as $92$, $116$, $156$), framing the research program that occupied the next century.

Thus the conjecture has a **dual origin**: Hadamard posed the existence question as the equality case of a determinant inequality (1893), and Paley gave it its modern constructive shape and the finite-field machinery (1933) that still underlies most known infinite families. The historical root (maximal determinants) and the modern formulation (existence for all $4k$) are two faces of the same statement.
