# Top Mathematicians — Goldbach's Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Christian Goldbach | 18th c. (1690–1764) | originator | Posed the original "every integer $>2$ is a sum of three primes" in his 1742 letter to Euler. |
| 2 | Leonhard Euler | 18th c. (1707–1783) | originator | Reformulated it as the strong binary conjecture: every even $n>2$ is $p+q$; judged it "certainly true." |
| 3 | Viggo Brun | early 20th c. (1885–1978) | breakthrough | Invented the Brun sieve (1920); first finite bound ("$9+9$"), founding the sieve attack. |
| 4 | G. H. Hardy | early 20th c. (1877–1947) | breakthrough | With Littlewood, applied the circle method and conjectured the quantitative asymptotic for representations. |
| 5 | J. E. Littlewood | early 20th c. (1885–1977) | breakthrough | Co-developer of the Hardy–Littlewood circle method and singular-series heuristics. |
| 6 | Lev Schnirelmann | early 20th c. (1905–1938) | breakthrough | Density method: every integer is a sum of a bounded number of primes (additive basis of primes). |
| 7 | I. M. Vinogradov | 20th c. (1891–1983) | breakthrough | Solved the ternary problem for large odd $n$ unconditionally (1937) via minor-arc estimates. |
| 8 | Chen Jingrun | 20th c. (1933–1996) | breakthrough | Proved "$1+2$" (1966/1973): every large even number is $p+P_2$ — the closest approach to the binary conjecture. |
| 9 | H. L. Montgomery & R. C. Vaughan | 20th–21st c. | breakthrough | Power-saving bound on the Goldbach exceptional set: $E(X)=O(X^{1-\delta})$ (1975). |
| 10 | Harald Helfgott | 21st c. (b. 1977) | frontier | Proved the weak (ternary) conjecture in full (2013) for all odd $n>5$, with David Platt's verification. |

## Notes

The current active community spans analytic number theory and computation. Helfgott (Göttingen/CNRS) and David Platt (Bristol) drove the ternary resolution and continue to refine explicit prime-counting and verified-computation tools that feed Goldbach-type problems. Olivier Ramaré (CNRS) remains central to explicit Schnirelmann-constant and sieve work, and Tomás Oliveira e Silva (Aveiro) leads the large-scale empirical verification ($4\times10^{18}$). On the binary frontier, the relevant expertise overlaps heavily with the school working on bounded gaps between primes and on improving exceptional-set savings (figures such as János Pintz, and the circle-of-ideas around Yitang Zhang, James Maynard, and Terence Tao on additive prime problems). The honest consensus is that no one currently has a credible route to the *binary* conjecture; effort concentrates on sharpening the partial results (exceptional set, explicit constants) rather than on a frontal proof.
