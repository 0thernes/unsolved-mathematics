# Originator(s) — Goldbach's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Christian Goldbach (1690–1764)

Christian Goldbach was born on 18 March 1690 in Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad) — the same city whose seven bridges would later inspire Euler's founding of graph theory. Trained in law and medicine, Goldbach was a polymath and traveler rather than a professional academic in the modern sense; in the 1710s he toured Europe and met leading mathematicians including Leibniz, Nicholas Bernoulli, and Abraham de Moivre, exchanges that drew him into mathematics. In 1725 he became a founding member and professor of mathematics at the newly established Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, where he also served as recording secretary. From 1728 he tutored Tsar Peter II in Moscow, and he later moved into the Russian foreign ministry, rising to privy councillor and a senior diplomatic post — so much of his mathematical life unfolded as learned correspondence alongside an official career.

Goldbach's mathematical output was eclectic rather than systematic: he worked on infinite series, the theory of equations, and number-theoretic curiosities (the "Goldbach–Euler theorem" on sums of reciprocals of perfect powers bears joint witness to the partnership, and "Goldbach's theorem" on Fermat numbers being pairwise coprime is also his). His enduring fame rests not on a body of theorems but on a single conjectural sentence. That is itself characteristic of the man: a gifted, well-connected amateur with a sharp eye for patterns in the integers, who posed the right question without the apparatus to answer it. He corresponded with Euler for over thirty-five years; the 7 June 1742 letter that contains the conjecture is one fragment of that vast exchange. Goldbach died in Moscow on 20 November 1764.

The **historical root** differs subtly from the **modern statement**. Goldbach actually wrote that every integer greater than 2 is a sum of three primes, working under the then-standard convention that $1$ counts as prime. Read literally with $1\notin\mathbb{P}$, his sentence is false; read with his own convention, it is equivalent to the even-number binary form. So the conjecture as schoolchildren now learn it — "every even number $>2$ is $p+q$" — is genuinely a joint Goldbach–Euler creation, with Euler supplying the clean, modern phrasing.

## Leonhard Euler (1707–1783) — author of the strong form

Euler needs little introduction: the most prolific mathematician of the eighteenth century, central to analysis, number theory, mechanics, and much else, and Goldbach's principal correspondent. Born in Basel and trained under Johann Bernoulli, Euler spent formative years at the St. Petersburg Academy (where Goldbach helped recruit him) and later at Berlin. His number-theoretic instincts were extraordinary — he proved Fermat's little theorem, studied the distribution of primes via the Euler product for $\zeta(s)$, and reanimated the study of perfect numbers and quadratic reciprocity's precursors.

When Goldbach floated his three-primes observation, Euler immediately recognized the cleaner equivalent and replied that the proposition "every even integer is a sum of two primes" followed from it and was, in his judgment, certainly true though he could not prove it. Euler's reformulation is why the *strong (binary)* conjecture is the canonical version, and metadata here credits him with the "strong form."

## Legacy

Neither man could attack the problem with the tools of their day; the conjecture's power lies in how it crystallized a hard truth about **additive** structure of the multiplicatively defined primes. It became a touchstone for Hilbert's 8th problem, for Hardy–Littlewood's circle method, for Brun's sieve, and ultimately for Vinogradov's and Helfgott's resolutions of the ternary case — a single sentence that seeded a century and a half of additive number theory.
