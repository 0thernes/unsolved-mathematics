_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Paul Seymour | 1990– | originator | Posed the general conjecture for all oriented graphs; defined the modern problem. |
| 2 | Nathaniel Dean | 1982– | originator | Posed the tournament case ("Dean's conjecture"), the historical root. |
| 3 | David C. Fisher | 1996 | breakthrough | Proved Dean's conjecture (tournament case) via a probabilistic squaring argument. |

## Notes

The active community around this conjecture is small but persistent, sitting at the intersection of **tournament theory** and **structural digraph theory**. The decisive early results came from two camps: Fisher's probabilistic argument and the **Havet–Thomassé median-order school**, the latter producing a reusable technique (the feed vertex) that propagates through nearly all later work. The only unconditional *general* progress — the $\approx 0.657$ multiplicative bound — is due to **Chen, Shen, and Yuster**, and it has stood essentially unimproved, marking the current ceiling of global methods.

Contemporary activity is dominated by **subclass verification**: authors such as **Salman Ghazal** and **Moussa Daamouch** have steadily enlarged the catalogue of oriented-graph families for which the conjecture is confirmed (sink-free digraphs, comparability-graph orientations, near-tournaments, degree-constrained classes), while problem lists maintained by figures like **Bang-Jensen** and **Gutin** keep the conjecture prominent. The frontier is not a single program but a distributed effort, with the recognized hard core being **sparse, balanced, digon-free** oriented graphs that defeat both median orders and weighting arguments. No researcher currently claims an accepted proof of the general case.
