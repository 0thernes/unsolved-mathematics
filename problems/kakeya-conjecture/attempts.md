# Attempts — The Kakeya Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

**Besicovitch's reversal (1928).** The first decisive "attempt" was against the original premise: Besicovitch showed the needle problem has no positive answer, the measure of a Kakeya set can be zero. This is not a partial result toward the modern conjecture but the event that created it, redirecting the question to dimension.

**Davies' planar theorem (1971).** Davies settled $n=2$ completely: planar Kakeya sets have Hausdorff dimension $2$. This remains the only classically "old" full case and the template for the conjecture.

**The bush bound (Bourgain, 1991).** Dimension $\ge \tfrac{n+1}{2}$ — the first general lower bound exceeding the trivial, and the entry point that connected Kakeya to restriction.

**The hairbrush bound (Wolff, 1995).** Dimension $\ge \tfrac{n+2}{2}$. For roughly a decade this was *the* best general bound, and it is still the cleanest geometric near-miss. Its inability to pass $\tfrac{n+2}{2}$ motivated the arithmetic and polynomial detours.

**The $\tfrac{5}{2}$ barrier in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (Katz–Łaba–Tao, 2000).** Hairbrush gives exactly $\tfrac{5}{2}$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$; KŁT pushed the Minkowski dimension to $\tfrac{5}{2}+\varepsilon$ using additive combinatorics. A small but conceptually important breach showing the geometric wall was not absolute.

**Finite-field Kakeya (Dvir, 2009).** A famous *near-miss-by-analogy*: Dvir's polynomial method gave a short, complete proof of the conjecture over $\mathbb{F}_q^n$. It raised hopes of an imminent Euclidean proof that did not materialize — the argument has no metric/scale content and does not transfer. It stands as a warning about how much the continuum structure matters.

**Multilinear Kakeya (Bennett–Carbery–Tao, 2006; Guth, 2010).** The sharp *multilinear* estimate was proved, but the linear conjecture lives in the non-transverse regime the multilinear bound cannot see. A genuine theorem adjacent to, but not implying, the target.

**The $\mathbb{R}^3$ resolution (Wang–Zahl, 2025).** In early 2025 **Hong Wang and Joshua Zahl** released a long manuscript proving that every Kakeya set in $\mathbb{R}^3$ has Hausdorff and Minkowski dimension $3$, via the sticky-Kakeya reduction and a multi-scale grains/projection analysis. This is reported as a complete resolution of the three-dimensional case and has been received as a major breakthrough; as with any very recent, very long proof, full community verification is ongoing and the result should be cited as the claimed/announced resolution of $n=3$ rather than a textbook-settled fact. The cases $n\ge 4$ remain genuinely open.

**Disputed / erroneous claims.** Because Kakeya is famous and easy to state, it attracts amateur "proofs," typically conflating measure with dimension or mishandling the $\delta\to 0$ limit; none has survived scrutiny and none is part of the literature. Within the professional literature, the notable corrected misconception is historical: the long-held belief (Kakeya, Fujiwara) that a *convex* or otherwise minimal region must have positive area — refuted by Besicovitch. No retracted *professional* proof of the full higher-dimensional conjecture is on record; the honest status is steady incremental progress plus the recent, still-settling $n=3$ result.
