# Originator(s) — The Kakeya Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture has three names attached to it because the *question*, the *phenomenon*, and the *precise dimensional formulation* each have a distinct author.

## Sōichi Kakeya (1886–1947)

Sōichi Kakeya was a Japanese mathematician, a professor at Tokyo Imperial University, who worked across analysis, the geometry of polynomials (the **Eneström–Kakeya theorem** on the location of roots of polynomials with positive monotone coefficients bears his name), and integral equations. In 1917 he posed the needle problem: minimize the area of a region inside which a unit segment can be reversed by continuous rotation. The problem was charming, concrete, and seemed to invite an extremal convex answer. Kakeya himself, and contemporaries such as Fujiwara, expected the deltoid (area $\pi/8$) to be optimal among convex bodies — a guess that turned out to be correct for *convex* regions but spectacularly wrong without the convexity constraint. Kakeya's role is that of the originator who asked the right naive question; the field still carries his name though the modern object is far from a rotating needle.

## Abram Samoilovitch Besicovitch (1891–1970)

Besicovitch is the mathematical heart of the story. Born in Berdyansk in the Russian Empire, he studied under **A. A. Markov** in St. Petersburg. Around 1919–1920, working in Perm during the upheaval of the Russian Revolution, he constructed a set of plane measure zero containing a line segment in every direction — not to answer Kakeya, whose problem he had not yet seen, but to settle a question on **Riemann integrability of functions of two variables**. He emigrated (escaping the Soviet Union in 1924), settled in Cambridge, and eventually held the Rouse Ball Chair. When he learned of Kakeya's problem he recognized that his construction, combined with a "sprouting/joins" device, demolished it: a needle can be turned in arbitrarily small area. His 1928 *Mathematische Zeitschrift* paper is the founding document. Besicovitch's deeper legacy is **geometric measure theory** — rectifiability, the structure of sets of finite perimeter, densities, and the projection theorems — a framework without which the dimensional reformulation could not even be stated. The measure-zero "Besicovitch sets" are named for him.

## Roy O. Davies (1927–2019)

The conjecture as we *now* state it — a statement about **Hausdorff dimension**, not area — is largely due to the British mathematician Roy Davies of the University of Leicester, a specialist in classical real analysis and measure theory and a student of the Besicovitch tradition. In 1971 Davies proved that although planar Kakeya sets can have measure zero, they must still have full Hausdorff dimension $2$. This crystallized the right question: measure is the wrong invariant; dimension is the rigid quantity. Davies' theorem is the $n=2$ case of the conjecture and the template for everything sought in higher dimensions.

## From root to modern problem

The historical root is a recreational planar minimization. The modern problem is a sharp lower bound on Hausdorff and Minkowski dimension in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and its weight comes from harmonic analysis: **Fefferman** (1971) showed Besicovitch sets break the ball multiplier, and **Bourgain** and **Wolff** later established Kakeya as a necessary ingredient for the **restriction**, **Bochner–Riesz**, and **local smoothing** conjectures. Thus three originators — Kakeya the questioner, Besicovitch the constructor, Davies the reformulator — between them define an object that today anchors a large region of modern analysis.
