# Originator(s) — The Elliott–Halberstam Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture is named for **Peter D. T. A. Elliott** and **Heini Halberstam**, who stated it jointly in 1968. Its intellectual roots, however, lie in the Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem of 1965 and in the broader sieve-theoretic tradition of which both men were part.

## Heini Halberstam (1926–2014)

Halberstam was born in Most (Brüx), Czechoslovakia, into a Jewish family; he came to Britain on a Kindertransport in 1939, escaping the fate of relatives murdered in the Holocaust. He read mathematics at University College London under **Theodor Estermann**, completing his doctorate in 1952. He held positions at UCL, the University of Newcastle, the University of Nottingham — where he was a professor for many years — and from 1980 at the University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign, where he chaired the department and remained until retirement.

Halberstam was one of the central figures in twentieth-century **sieve theory**. With **Klaus Friedrich Roth** he wrote *Sequences* (1966, Vol. I), the very book in which the conjecture appears; with **Hans-Egon Richert** he wrote the definitive monograph *Sieve Methods* (1974), which became the standard reference and trained a generation of analytic number theorists. His work ranged across additive number theory, the distribution of additive functions, and the average behaviour of arithmetic functions. He was known as an exceptionally generous mentor and expositor.

## Peter D. T. A. Elliott (b. 1941)

Elliott is a British-born number theorist who earned his Ph.D. at the University of Cambridge (1966), advised in the analytic-number-theory tradition associated with **Harold Davenport**. He spent the bulk of his career at the University of Colorado Boulder, where he is professor emeritus. Elliott is best known for his two-volume treatise *Probabilistic Number Theory* (1979–80), a foundational synthesis of the statistical theory of arithmetic functions descending from the work of Erdős, Kac, Kubilius, and Turán. He also made deep contributions to mean-value theorems for multiplicative functions and to the theory of arithmetic functions on arithmetic progressions — precisely the circle of ideas that makes the equidistribution of primes in progressions a natural object to push to its limit.

## The idea behind the conjecture

By the late 1960s, the Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem had shown that primes are distributed in arithmetic progressions, *on average over the modulus*, as well as GRH predicts — but only up to modulus $x^{1/2}$. The conceptual content of the Elliott–Halberstam conjecture is the recognition that the $x^{1/2}$ barrier in Bombieri–Vinogradov is an artifact of the **method** (the large sieve and bilinear-form bounds), not a real feature of the primes. The square-root range corresponds to the natural reach of the large sieve inequality; Elliott and Halberstam conjectured that the truth extends all the way to $x^{1-\varepsilon}$, the largest range in which the main term remains dominant. The hard limit at $\theta = 1$ — where $q$ is comparable to $x$ and a single progression contains $O(1)$ primes — is left out deliberately, reflecting the parity obstruction inherent to sieves.

## Legacy

The conjecture sat as a clean but seemingly inert strengthening of Bombieri–Vinogradov for nearly four decades. Its modern importance was crystallized by **Goldston, Pintz, and Yıldırım** (2005), who showed that *any* level of distribution beyond $1/2$ would force bounded gaps between primes, and under full EH gaps of at most $16$. After Zhang (2013) and Maynard (2013) it became the organizing target of the bounded-gaps program. Halberstam lived to see his conjecture become the linchpin of one of the most celebrated advances in number theory of the century; he died in January 2014, months after Zhang's breakthrough. Elliott's probabilistic methods continue to inform the analysis of multiplicative functions on which the sharpest sieve results depend.
