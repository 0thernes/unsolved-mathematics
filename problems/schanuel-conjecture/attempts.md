# Attempts — Schanuel's Conjecture

Schanuel's Conjecture has the unusual distinction of being a problem against which progress is measured by *partial* unconditional theorems and by powerful *analogues*, rather than by a sequence of claimed full proofs. Because the statement is so strong — it implies the algebraic independence of $e$ and $\pi$, itself untouched — the community treats any direct proof claim with extreme caution, and no serious, sustained claim of a general proof has survived scrutiny.

## Genuine partial results (proved cases)

- **Lindemann–Weierstrass (1885).** Schanuel restricted to $\mathbb{Q}$-linearly independent *algebraic* $z_i$. This is the entire algebraic case, unconditionally true. It remains the largest fragment that is fully established.
- **Gelfond–Schneider (1934).** The $n=2$ instance underlying $a^b$ transcendence. A true special case.
- **Nesterenko (1996).** Algebraic independence of $\pi, e^{\pi}, \Gamma(1/4)$ — equivalently $\operatorname{trdeg}_{\mathbb{Q}}\mathbb{Q}(\pi,e^\pi,\Gamma(1/4))=3$. This certifies a Schanuel-style trdeg lower bound for one specific, modular-function-related configuration, and is the deepest *algebraic independence* result in the vicinity.
- **Known to be transcendental, not independent.** It is famous that we cannot prove $e+\pi$, $e\pi$, $\pi^e$, or $2^{\sqrt2}$-style combinations all transcendental simultaneously; at least one of $e+\pi$, $e\pi$ is transcendental (trivially, since both algebraic would make $e,\pi$ algebraic), but *which* is unknown. These near-misses dramatize the gap.

## The decisive analogue (Ax–Schanuel, 1971)

James Ax's proof of the differential/formal version is the most important "attempt" in the sense of furnishing the strongest theoretical evidence that the arithmetic statement is true. It establishes exactly the trdeg inequality Schanuel predicts, but for power-series solutions of the exponential differential equation. Its repeated generalization (Pila–Tsimerman, Mok–Pila–Tsimerman) shows the *shape* of the conjecture is correct across many settings — yet none of these specialize back to $\mathbb{C}$.

## Conditional architectures

A substantial literature *assumes* Schanuel and derives consequences — Macintyre–Wilkie's conditional decidability of $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb{R},\exp)$; Zilber's pseudo-exponentiation, where a strong Schanuel is an axiom; Kirby's and Bays's reductions of Zilber's categoricity conjecture. These are not attempts to prove the conjecture but they sharpen exactly what an unconditional proof would buy, and they have, over time, isolated the conjecture as the lone arithmetic obstruction in an otherwise nearly complete model-theoretic picture.

## Disputed / withdrawn proof claims

There is no widely circulated, peer-engaged claim of a full proof of Schanuel's Conjecture that the community takes seriously; the problem is generally considered to be out of reach of present methods. Informal and preprint-level "proofs" appear from time to time (as for many famous open problems), but none has gained traction or named, documented refutation in the literature, so listing a specific disputed claim here would risk misrepresentation. The honest statement of the frontier is that the conjecture remains **open and unattacked at the level of a credible general proof**, with all real progress confined to special cases, analogues, and conditional reductions described above.

A related cautionary episode worth noting neutrally: certain strong *uniform* Schanuel-type statements (e.g. with explicit effectivity or with the constant field replaced) have been floated and later found to need additional hypotheses; the "Uniform Schanuel" and "Strong Schanuel" variants used in model theory are carefully distinguished from the bare conjecture precisely because naïve strengthenings can fail. This is a matter of careful formulation rather than a retracted proof.
