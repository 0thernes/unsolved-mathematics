# Originator(s) — The Inscribed Square Problem (Toeplitz)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Otto Toeplitz (1881–1940)

**Otto Toeplitz** was a German mathematician of the early twentieth century, best known today for the Toeplitz matrices and Toeplitz operators that bear his name and form a cornerstone of functional analysis and operator theory. He posed the inscribed square problem in 1911, and although it occupies a small corner of his output, it has become one of the most enduring open questions in elementary plane topology.

### Life

Toeplitz was born on 1 August 1881 in Breslau (then in the German Empire, now Wrocław, Poland) into a family of mathematicians — both his father and grandfather were Gymnasium mathematics teachers. He studied at the University of Breslau, earning his doctorate in 1905 with a dissertation in algebraic geometry. He then moved to Göttingen, the world center of mathematics under David Hilbert and Felix Klein, where he was profoundly influenced by Hilbert's program on infinitely many variables and the theory of integral equations. This Göttingen period turned Toeplitz toward the analysis of bilinear and quadratic forms in infinitely many variables — work that produced the "Toeplitz forms" and the spectral theory of what are now called Toeplitz operators.

He held a professorship at the University of Kiel from 1913, then at Bonn from 1928. As a Jewish mathematician, Toeplitz was dismissed from his Bonn chair in 1935 under the Nazi racial laws. He devoted his final years in Germany to supporting Jewish students and to emigration relief efforts before himself emigrating to Palestine in 1939, where he worked in an administrative role at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. He died in Jerusalem on 15 February 1940.

### Mathematical background and motivation

Toeplitz's deepest contributions lie in functional analysis: the Toeplitz–Hausdorff theorem (convexity of the numerical range), Toeplitz operators on Hardy spaces, and summability theory (the Silverman–Toeplitz theorem characterizing regular matrix summation methods). He was also a gifted expositor and historian of mathematics; with Hans Rademacher he wrote the classic popular book *Von Zahlen und Figuren* (1930, translated as *The Enjoyment of Mathematics*), and he championed the "genetic method" of teaching mathematics through its historical development.

It was this taste for vivid, geometrically intuitive problems — and the early-twentieth-century ferment around the Jordan curve theorem and the foundations of plane topology — that led him to the square peg question in 1911. The problem fit a broader contemporary interest in "inscribed configurations": questions about points on curves forced by topology rather than by metric rigidity. Toeplitz announced the conjecture together with a proof for convex curves, signaling from the outset that the hard case would be the wild, non-convex, non-smooth Jordan curves.

### Legacy

Toeplitz's name is attached to objects used daily across analysis, numerical mathematics (Toeplitz and circulant matrices), signal processing, and probability. The inscribed square problem is a comparatively minor part of that legacy in volume but an outsized one in fame: it is a canonical example of a problem that any schoolchild can understand yet that has defied a century of effort. Its persistence vindicates Toeplitz's instinct that the generality of continuous curves — not their geometry — is where the real difficulty lies. The problem is now a flagship illustration of how genuinely elementary statements can sit just beyond the reach of modern topology and symplectic geometry.
