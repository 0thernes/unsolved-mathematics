# Top Mathematicians — Furstenberg's ×2 ×3 Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Harry Furstenberg | 1967– | originator | Posed the measure-rigidity conjecture; proved the topological ($\times 2, \times 3$ closed-set) rigidity theorem that motivates it. |
| 2 | Daniel J. Rudolph | 1990 | breakthrough | Proved that any joint-invariant ergodic measure with positive entropy is Lebesgue — the central partial result, reducing the problem to the zero-entropy case. |
| 3 | Aimee S. A. Johnson | 1992 | breakthrough | Extended Rudolph's theorem to all multiplicatively independent $p, q \ge 2$, removing technical restrictions. |
| 4 | Bernard Host | 1995 | breakthrough | Gave an independent proof of positive-entropy rigidity via normal numbers and disjointness; clarified the underlying mechanism. |
| 5 | Russell Lyons | 1988 | breakthrough | Early Fourier-analytic study of $2$- and $3$-invariant measures, an entry point to the spectral approach. |
| 6 | Elon Lindenstrauss | 2000s | frontier | Measure-classification techniques in homogeneous dynamics (Fields Medal 2010) that generalize the rigidity philosophy; co-author on the Littlewood breakthrough. |
| 7 | Manfred Einsiedler | 2000s | frontier | Higher-rank measure rigidity for diagonalizable and toral-automorphism actions; co-author of the Littlewood result and the standard textbook. |
| 8 | Anatole Katok | 2000s | frontier | Foundational rigidity theory for higher-rank abelian actions (with Spatzier); co-author of the Littlewood breakthrough. |
| 9 | Pablo Shmerkin | 2019 | breakthrough | Proved Furstenberg's intersection conjecture (a $\times 2 / \times 3$ cousin) using $L^q$-norm methods. |
| 10 | Meng Wu | 2019 | breakthrough | Independent proof of Furstenberg's intersection conjecture via a CP-chains / dynamical argument. |

## Notes

The community around the $\times 2, \times 3$ conjecture sits at the intersection of **ergodic theory, homogeneous dynamics, and fractal geometry**. Its modern engine is the measure-rigidity school descending from Margulis and Ratner and crystallized by Einsiedler, Katok, and Lindenstrauss, whose Littlewood-conjecture work (2006) shares the conditional-measure and entropy machinery that any future resolution of the zero-entropy case would likely need. A second active wing — Hochman, Shmerkin, Wu, and collaborators — attacks the closely related dimension conjectures (intersections, slices, self-similar measures) and resolved several of them around 2019, sharpening the toolkit even though the core measure-classification problem persists. Researchers in additive combinatorics (sum–product, decoupling) and analytic number theory (Bourgain, Venkatesh, Michel) contribute quantitative equidistribution inputs. The shared, openly acknowledged obstacle across all these groups is the **zero-entropy regime**: there is broad consensus that no exotic measure exists, but no technique yet reaches it. Tobias Ward, Klaus Schmidt, and Ralf Spatzier are further frontier/expositor figures who shaped the higher-rank framework and its pedagogy.
