# History — Furstenberg's ×2 ×3 Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin

The conjecture grows out of Harry Furstenberg's 1967 paper *Disjointness in ergodic theory, minimal sets, and a problem in Diophantine approximation*, in which he proved a topological rigidity theorem: every closed subset of the circle $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ that is invariant under both $x \mapsto 2x$ and $x \mapsto 3x$ is either finite (a set of rationals) or all of $\mathbb{T}$. The key structural input is that the multiplicative semigroup generated by $2$ and $3$ is *non-lacunary* — the ratios $\log 3 / \log 2$ is irrational — so the two maps are "independent" in a way a single map $x \mapsto nx$ is not.

The natural measure-theoretic analogue, which Furstenberg posed as an open question, asks whether the same rigidity holds for invariant *measures* rather than closed sets. In modern form:

> **Conjecture (×2 ×3 measure rigidity).** Let $\mu$ be a Borel probability measure on $\mathbb{T}$ that is invariant and ergodic under the joint action of $T_2 : x \mapsto 2x \pmod 1$ and $T_3 : x \mapsto 3x \pmod 1$. If $\mu$ is non-atomic, then $\mu$ is Lebesgue measure.

Equivalently, the only $\langle T_2, T_3\rangle$-invariant ergodic measures are Lebesgue measure and measures supported on finite sets of rationals. The hypothesis that the two generators are multiplicatively independent ($p, q$ with $\log p/\log q \notin \mathbb{Q}$) is essential; for $\times 2$ alone there is a rich zoo of exotic invariant measures.

## Timeline

- **1967** — Furstenberg proves topological rigidity for $\times 2, \times 3$-invariant closed sets and raises the measure-rigidity question (Diophantine paper).
- **1980s** — The problem circulates as a touchstone for "stiffness/rigidity" of multi-dimensional commuting actions; it is recognized as deeply linked to Littlewood's conjecture in Diophantine approximation.
- **1990** — Daniel Rudolph proves the first major conditional result: a $\times p, \times q$-invariant ergodic measure with **positive entropy** for one of the maps must be Lebesgue. This becomes the central theorem of the subject.
- **1992** — Andrés Johnson (A. S. A. Johnson) extends Rudolph's argument to general multiplicatively independent integers $p, q \ge 2$, removing a coprimality restriction.
- **1996–1998** — Bernard Host gives an alternative proof of the positive-entropy rigidity via normal numbers and a different mechanism; Feldman and Lindenstrauss develop related approaches.
- **2001** — Manfred Einsiedler and Klaus Schmidt, and others, recast the problem in the language of commuting toral automorphisms and higher-rank abelian actions.
- **2006** — Einsiedler, Katok, and Lindenstrauss prove a celebrated partial result on **Littlewood's conjecture** using higher-rank measure rigidity (entropy/conditional-measure techniques in homogeneous dynamics), showing the exceptional set has Hausdorff dimension zero. This work shares the technical DNA of the $\times 2, \times 3$ problem.
- **2006** — Elon Lindenstrauss is awarded the Fields Medal (2010) in part for measure-rigidity results in homogeneous dynamics that descend from this circle of ideas.
- **2011** — Bourgain, Lindenstrauss, Michel, and Venkatesh, and related work, quantify equidistribution under semigroup actions, refining the positive-entropy regime.
- **2015–present** — Hochman, Shmerkin, and others connect the $\times 2, \times 3$ philosophy to fractal geometry (Furstenberg's intersection and slicing conjectures, proved 2019 by Shmerkin and independently Wu), but the **zero-entropy core** of the measure conjecture remains open.
- **Present frontier** — The conjecture is unproven in full. No example of a non-atomic, non-Lebesgue, jointly invariant ergodic measure is known, yet ruling out zero-entropy measures resists every existing technique. The problem is widely regarded as a "rigidity Everest" alongside Littlewood's conjecture.
