# Originator(s) — Furstenberg's ×2 ×3 Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Harry Furstenberg (1935–2025)

Hillel (Harry) Furstenberg was born in Berlin in 1935; his family fled Nazi Germany in 1939, settling in the United States. He completed his undergraduate and master's studies at Yeshiva University and earned his Ph.D. at Princeton in 1958 under Salomon Bochner. After positions at Princeton and the University of Minnesota, he moved in 1965 to the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, where he spent the remainder of his career and became one of the central figures of twentieth-century ergodic theory. He was awarded the Wolf Prize (2006/2007) and, jointly with Gregory Margulis, the Abel Prize in 2020 "for pioneering the use of methods from probability and dynamics in group theory, number theory and combinatorics." He died in 2025.

## Background and motivation

Furstenberg's signature was the discovery of deep bridges between ergodic theory and seemingly distant fields — number theory, combinatorics, Lie groups, and Diophantine approximation. His 1967 paper *Disjointness in ergodic theory, minimal sets, and a problem in Diophantine approximation* introduced the notion of **disjointness** of dynamical systems, an analogue of coprimality, and used it to attack a question of Diophantine flavor: the distribution of $\{2^m 3^n \alpha\}$ for irrational $\alpha$.

The $\times 2, \times 3$ problem arose directly from this work. Furstenberg observed that while a single linear map $x \mapsto nx$ on the circle admits a vast supply of invariant closed sets and invariant measures (its dynamics are "soft"), the *joint* action of two multiplicatively independent maps is far more constrained. His **topological** theorem — that the only closed jointly invariant sets are finite or everything — was a striking instance of rigidity. The conjecture is the natural measure-theoretic sharpening: rigidity should persist at the level of invariant measures, with Lebesgue measure as the unique non-atomic survivor.

The motivating intuition is that two independent expanding maps create so many "directions of mixing" that no exotic measure can survive being smeared by both. This is the prototype of what is now called **higher-rank rigidity**: actions of $\mathbb{Z}^2$ (or larger abelian groups) by commuting endomorphisms or by diagonal flows on homogeneous spaces tend to be dramatically more rigid than rank-one actions. The same philosophy animates Ratner's theorems on unipotent flows, the Einsiedler–Katok–Lindenstrauss work on Littlewood's conjecture, and Margulis's program in homogeneous dynamics — making the $\times 2, \times 3$ conjecture a conceptual seed for an entire research direction.

## The historical root vs. the modern formulation

The **historical root** is purely topological: closed invariant sets are trivial. This Furstenberg *proved*. The **modern conjecture** — the open problem — is its measure-theoretic counterpart and is genuinely harder, because measures see fine-scale "entropy" structure invisible to closed sets. Rudolph's 1990 theorem showed the conjecture holds the moment the measure carries positive entropy under one of the maps; what remains stubbornly open is the **zero-entropy** regime, where a hypothetical exotic measure would have to be invisible to all current entropy-based tools.

## Legacy

Furstenberg's framing turned a concrete question about digit expansions into a paradigm. The conjecture remains a benchmark for measure-rigidity techniques; progress on it (or on its homogeneous-dynamics cousins) has repeatedly signaled major advances, including a Fields Medal (Lindenstrauss, 2010) for closely related rigidity theorems. Even unproven, it organizes how mathematicians think about independence, entropy, and the limits of dynamical flexibility.
