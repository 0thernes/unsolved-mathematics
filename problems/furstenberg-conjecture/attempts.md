# Attempts — Furstenberg's ×2 ×3 Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The conjecture has resisted full proof for over half a century. Unlike some open problems, it has **not** attracted a notable stream of widely circulated false claims of a complete solution; the difficulty of the zero-entropy regime is so well understood that the community treats any claimed full proof with heavy skepticism. The history is instead one of genuine, carefully verified *partial* results that converge on — but do not cross — the entropy barrier.

## Genuine partial results and near-misses

- **Rudolph's positive-entropy theorem (1990).** The decisive near-miss. Rudolph reduced the entire conjecture to the zero-entropy case: any $\langle T_2, T_3\rangle$-invariant ergodic measure with positive entropy under one map is Lebesgue. This is as close as anyone has come, and it is universally accepted as correct. It simultaneously demonstrates *why* the remaining case is hard: positive entropy was exactly the leverage every method needs.

- **Johnson's extension (1992).** A. S. A. Johnson removed a technical coprimality assumption, proving the positive-entropy rigidity for all multiplicatively independent $p, q \ge 2$. No retraction; a clean strengthening.

- **Host's independent proof (1995).** Bernard Host's normal-numbers argument reproved the theorem by a different route, which served as a cross-check and clarified the role of disjointness. The agreement of two independent methods reinforced confidence in the positive-entropy result.

- **Lyons (1988) and the spectral side.** Russell Lyons and others studied the singularity of joint-invariant measures via Fourier analysis, obtaining partial constraints; these did not reach a full classification and stalled at the same zero-entropy wall.

## The standing of the zero-entropy case

The persistent obstacle is the **possible existence of zero-entropy, non-atomic, jointly invariant ergodic measures**. No such measure has ever been exhibited, and most experts believe none exists — but a rigorous proof of nonexistence is absent. Several research programs have aimed at it (higher-rank homogeneous-dynamics analogies, additive-combinatorial expansion estimates, sum–product methods), and each has produced valuable theorems in adjacent settings (notably the Einsiedler–Katok–Lindenstrauss work on Littlewood's conjecture, 2006) without resolving the core dichotomy.

## On claimed full proofs

There is no prominent **retracted** or **disputed** claimed complete proof of the conjecture in the mainstream literature comparable to the famous controversies surrounding other open problems. Occasional preprints asserting a resolution circulate, as with any high-profile conjecture, but none has gained acceptance or survived expert scrutiny; the standard objection is invariably that the argument tacitly assumes positive entropy, mishandles the conditional-measure structure in the non-invertible setting, or fails to rule out the deterministic zero-entropy case. In keeping with this dossier's honesty policy: **the conjecture remains open**, the metadata status is `active-progress`, and any source claiming otherwise should be regarded as unverified pending peer review.

## Adjacent "near-misses" worth noting

- Furstenberg's own **topological** theorem (1967) is the closed-set analogue and is fully proved — sometimes mistaken by newcomers for the conjecture itself.
- Furstenberg's **intersection/slicing conjectures**, resolved independently by Pablo Shmerkin and by Meng Wu in 2019, concern Hausdorff dimensions of $\times 2$ / $\times 3$ invariant sets. They are *cousins*, not the measure-rigidity conjecture, and their resolution did not yield the latter.
