# Top Mathematicians — Brennan's Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | James E. Brennan | 1970s–present | originator | Posed the conjecture (1978) on $L^p$ integrability of $\varphi'$; sharp exponent $4$ from weighted polynomial approximation. |

## Notes

The community around Brennan's conjecture is the geometric function theory and harmonic-measure circle, overlapping heavily with researchers in conformal dynamics, SLE, and multifractal analysis. It is not a large field — perhaps a few dozen active specialists worldwide — but it is well-connected to high-profile probability and analysis through the universal integral means spectrum, which links Brennan's conjecture to the work of Smirnov (Fields Medal 2010, for conformal invariance and SLE-adjacent results) and to the Carleson–Makarov school. Activity concentrates in Scandinavian and U.S. analysis groups (KTH Stockholm, Caltech, and former Soviet-school analysts now distributed across Europe and North America), with periodic computational pushes (snowflake and Julia-set simulations) and analytic refinements of $B(t)$. The conjecture's status as a clean, single-constant reduction of the universal spectrum keeps it a recurring topic in surveys and problem lists, even as the headcount of those attacking the sharp endpoint directly remains small. Ivan Kayumov and collaborators have contributed sharp lower-bound (negative-direction) estimates that constrain how any eventual proof must behave near $t=-2$.
