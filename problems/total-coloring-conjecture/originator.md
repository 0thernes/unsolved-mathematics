# Originator(s) — The Total Coloring Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture carries two names because it was posed independently, almost simultaneously, on opposite sides of the Iron Curtain.

## Mehdi Behzad (1936–)

Mehdi Behzad is an Iranian mathematician widely regarded as a founder of graph theory in Iran. He completed his Ph.D. at **Michigan State University in 1965**, advised by **Edward A. Nordhaus** and working closely with **Gary Chartrand**, then a vibrant center of combinatorial research. His dissertation introduced the notion of the **total graph** $T(G)$ — a single construction that unifies a graph's vertices and edges into one adjacency structure — and with it the **total chromatic number** $\chi''(G)$. Asking how large $\chi''(G)$ can be relative to the maximum degree $\Delta$ led him directly to conjecture the clean bound $\chi''(G)\le\Delta+2$.

The conceptual move was elegant: ordinary vertex coloring colors $V(G)$, edge coloring colors $E(G)$, and Vizing had just shown ($1964$–$1965$) that edge coloring needs at most $\Delta+1$ colors. Behzad's total graph let one treat vertices and edges *simultaneously*, and the natural conjecture was that the cost of doing both at once is only one extra color beyond Vizing's bound. After returning to Iran, Behzad held positions at institutions including Sharif University of Technology and was a leading figure in building the country's mathematical community, co-authoring textbooks and mentoring generations of students. He helped found Iranian mathematical institutions and competitions and remained an advocate for graph theory throughout his career.

## Vadim G. Vizing (1937–2017)

Vadim Georgievich Vizing was a Soviet (Ukrainian) mathematician whose name is attached to several of the deepest results in graph coloring. Born in Kiev in 1937, he studied at Tomsk State University and later worked at the Institute of Mathematics in Novosibirsk under the influence of the Akademgorodok combinatorics school. His celebrated **edge-coloring theorem** ($1964$–$1965$) — that every simple graph satisfies $\Delta \le \chi'(G) \le \Delta+1$, partitioning all graphs into just two classes — is the direct ancestor of the total coloring problem. In the same period of intense activity, Vizing recorded the conjecture $\chi''(G)\le\Delta+2$ as one of a list of open problems on chromatic numbers, publishing it in survey form (notably his 1968 "Some unsolved problems in graph theory"). Vizing's motivation was structural symmetry: the total chromatic number sits "above" both the chromatic and chromatic-index parameters, and he expected a uniform $+2$ slack to govern it, mirroring the $+1$ slack of his edge theorem.

Vizing made foundational contributions beyond coloring, including early work on list coloring (independently of Erdős–Rubin–Taylor) and on interval edge colorings. He spent much of his later career in Odessa. He died in 2017.

## Two formulations, one statement

The historical roots are essentially identical, so unlike some conjectures there is no divergence between a "historical" and a "modern" version: both Behzad and Vizing stated exactly $\chi''(G)\le\Delta+2$. What has accreted around the core statement are *strengthenings* posed by later workers — most importantly the **List Total Coloring Conjecture** (Borodin, Kostochka, Woodall, 1997), asserting that the list (choosability) version of the total chromatic number also never exceeds $\Delta+2$, and the **Type 1/Type 2** classification program, which seeks to determine exactly which graphs need the full $\Delta+2$ colors rather than $\Delta+1$.

## Legacy

The conjecture is now a textbook landmark and a benchmark for the probabilistic method in combinatorics: Molloy and Reed's 1998 proof that $\chi''(G)\le\Delta+C$ for an absolute constant became a showcase application of the Lovász Local Lemma. Both men's names endure — Vizing's across edge, total, and list coloring; Behzad's through the total graph construction that frames the entire subfield.
