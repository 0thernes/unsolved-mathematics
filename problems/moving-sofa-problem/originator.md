# Originator(s) — The Moving Sofa Problem

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The moving sofa problem is credited to **Leo Moser** (1921–1970), the Austrian-born Canadian mathematician who posed it in print in 1966. Moser was a prolific and inventive problem-poser whose name attaches to a remarkable number of small, sharp, and durable questions across number theory, combinatorics, and geometry.

## Life and mathematical background

Leo Moser was born in Vienna in 1921 and emigrated with his family to Canada as a child. He studied at the University of Manitoba and earned his Ph.D. in 1950 at the University of North Carolina under the supervision of Alfred Brauer, working in analytic and combinatorial number theory. He spent most of his career at the University of Alberta in Edmonton, where he was a central figure in the Canadian mathematical community until his early death in 1970, at age 48, following years of heart trouble. He was the younger brother of William O. J. Moser, himself a distinguished combinatorial geometer and co-author of the influential *Geometry and the Imagination* tradition of problem collections.

Moser's mathematical taste ran to problems that are elementary to *state* but resist elementary *solution* — questions whose surface simplicity conceals real geometric or combinatorial depth. He is associated with the **Moser spindle** (a key gadget in the Hadwiger–Nelson chromatic-number-of-the-plane problem), **Moser's worm problem** (the smallest region that can cover every unit-length arc), the **Moser–de Bruijn sequence**, **Moser's circle problem** (the deceptive $1,2,4,8,16,31,\dots$ region count), and **Moser's cube / Moser number** in extremal combinatorics. The moving sofa problem fits this pattern precisely: a homely, almost comic formulation that turns out to require involutes, envelopes, and computer-assisted optimization to attack.

## The ideas and motivation behind the problem

Moser's problem sits in a small family of *motion-planning* questions that ask for the extremal size of a rigid object subject to a confinement constraint during continuous motion. Closely related are his worm problem and the classical "ladder around a corner" calculus exercise — the latter asks for the *longest* line segment (a one-dimensional rod) that can be carried around a right-angled corner in a corridor, with answer determined by a simple optimization. The sofa problem is the natural two-dimensional escalation: replace the rod by a region and ask for maximal *area* rather than length. The leap in difficulty is enormous, because the optimal region's boundary is not a smooth conic but an envelope of swept lines whose pieces must be matched analytically.

It is important to separate the *historical root* from the *modern formulation*. The "ladder/rod around a corner" problem is older and elementary; Moser's contribution was to recognize that the area-maximization version — a genuinely hard extremal problem — was both natural and open. The modern statement (unit corridor, single right-angle turn, maximize area, sofa constant $\mu$) is exactly Moser's, and it has not drifted; later authors merely added variants (the ambidextrous sofa, sofas in corridors of unequal width, three-dimensional analogues) without displacing the original.

## Broader legacy

Moser's broader legacy is a body of crisply posed problems that have seeded entire subfields. Several of his questions — the chromatic number of the plane, the worm problem, the sofa problem — remain open or only recently resolved, and continue to attract serious work. His emphasis on problems that are "trivial to ask, hard to answer" influenced the problem-collection culture later carried forward by Croft, Falconer, and Richard Guy in *Unsolved Problems in Geometry*, where the moving sofa problem appears as a showcase example. That a single line of furniture-themed whimsy has, six decades later, demanded involute geometry and rigorous computer-assisted bounds is a fitting monument to Moser's instinct for the deceptively simple.
