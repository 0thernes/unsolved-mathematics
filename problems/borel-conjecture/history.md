# History — The Borel Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

The Borel Conjecture is a statement of **topological rigidity** for closed aspherical manifolds. A closed manifold $M$ is *aspherical* if its universal cover is contractible, equivalently if $\pi_n(M)=0$ for $n\geq 2$; such an $M$ is a $K(\pi,1)$, and its homotopy type is determined by the single group $\pi=\pi_1(M)$. The conjecture asserts that this homotopy-theoretic determination is in fact a *topological* one:

> If $M$ and $N$ are closed aspherical manifolds and $f:M\to N$ is a homotopy equivalence, then $f$ is homotopic to a homeomorphism. In particular, closed aspherical manifolds with isomorphic fundamental groups are homeomorphic.

The conjecture has no historical "paper of origin." It was communicated by **Armand Borel** in correspondence and conversation around 1953, motivated by the **Mostow rigidity** phenomenon then crystallizing for locally symmetric spaces. Borel's insight was to ask whether the rigidity Mostow established *geometrically* (isometry, requiring dimension $\geq 3$ and stronger hypotheses) might hold *topologically* under the far weaker hypothesis of asphericity alone. The conjecture entered the literature mainly through later attributions; **Wall** and others record Borel's question in print in the 1970s.

A central caution: the **homeomorphism** category is essential. The smooth and PL analogues are **false** — Browder, and later Farrell–Jones, produced closed aspherical manifolds that are homeomorphic but not diffeomorphic (e.g. via exotic tori and negatively curved manifolds with exotic smooth structures). Thus the Borel Conjecture is the topological rigidity statement precisely; it is the higher-dimensional aspherical analogue of the **Poincaré Conjecture** (which concerns the simply connected case, $\pi=1$).

**Timeline**

- **1953** — Armand Borel formulates the rigidity question, prompted by Mostow's emerging work on rigidity of locally symmetric spaces.
- **1968** — Mostow proves strong (geometric) rigidity for closed hyperbolic manifolds of dimension $\geq 3$, giving the conjecture its archetypal positive instance.
- **1969** — Kirby–Siebenmann torus trick and the structure theory of topological manifolds supply the surgery-theoretic framework in which Borel rigidity is naturally phrased.
- **1970s** — C.T.C. Wall's surgery theory recasts the problem: the conjecture is equivalent to the vanishing/assembly statement that the surgery **structure set** $\mathcal S(M)$ is trivial, controlled by the assembly map in $L$-theory.
- **1977–1983** — Farrell–Hsiang prove rigidity for flat and almost-flat manifolds and many infranilmanifolds; topological rigidity established for tori $T^n$.
- **1981** — Waldhausen's algebraic $K$-theory of spaces and the parametrized $h$-cobordism theorem provide the $K$-theoretic half of the picture.
- **1980s–1990s** — **Farrell–Jones** develop their controlled-topology / geodesic-flow method, proving the conjecture for closed manifolds of nonpositive curvature (Riemannian, dim $\neq 3,4$) and for many word-hyperbolic groups.
- **1993** — Farrell–Jones formulate the **Farrell–Jones Conjecture (FJC)** in $K$- and $L$-theory; its $L$-theoretic part implies the Borel Conjecture for the corresponding groups.
- **2004–2012** — **Bartels–Lück–Reich** prove FJC for word-hyperbolic groups (2008–2012) and, with Wegner, for CAT(0) groups, via controlled topology and flow spaces — yielding Borel rigidity for vast new classes.
- **2014–present** — FJC extended to lattices in virtually connected Lie groups, mapping class groups (partial), $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb Z)$, relatively hyperbolic and many normally/finitely-generated groups (Bartels, Lück, Rüping, Kammeyer, and collaborators). The conjecture is proven for an enormous family of groups yet remains **open in general**, with dimensions $3$ (entangled with geometrization) and $4$ (where surgery is obstructed) especially delicate.
