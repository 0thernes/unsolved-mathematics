# Originator(s) — The Unique Games Conjecture

## Subhash Khot

Subhash Khot (born 1978 in Ichalkaranji, Maharashtra, India) is the originator of the Unique Games Conjecture and the central figure in its development. He won a silver medal at the International Mathematical Olympiad in 1994 and ranked first in the Indian Institute of Technology Joint Entrance Examination, earning his B.Tech. in Computer Science from IIT Bombay in 1999. He moved to Princeton University for graduate study, completing his Ph.D. in 2003 under the supervision of Sanjeev Arora — one of the architects of the PCP theorem. After positions at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton, and Georgia Tech, Khot joined New York University's Courant Institute, where he is a professor of computer science.

Khot's intellectual milieu was the theory of *probabilistically checkable proofs* and *hardness of approximation*, then in a period of rapid maturation. Arora's circle had shown that approximation thresholds were not heuristic curiosities but exact, provable constants governed by the algebra of proof verification. Håstad's optimal inapproximability results (1997) had demonstrated the power of the *long code* and discrete Fourier analysis, but had also revealed a wall: the constraint structures emerging from parallel repetition were "projection" constraints, not bijections, and the cleanest analytic tests demanded bijective ("unique") constraints. Khot's insight, formulated during his doctoral work and published in 2002, was to *name the missing hypothesis* rather than re-derive each consequence from scratch. He recognized that a single clean assumption — the NP-hardness of distinguishing nearly-satisfiable from nearly-unsatisfiable unique games — would unlock a whole class of optimal hardness results at once.

The conceptual move was characteristic of Khot's style: identify the precise combinatorial object whose hardness is both *plausible* and *maximally leveraged*. Unique games sit at a sweet spot. They are simple enough to plug into long-code dictatorship tests — the bijective constraints let label assignments compose cleanly under the Fourier transform — yet not so simple as to be obviously easy. The subexponential algorithm of Arora–Barak–Steurer (2010) later confirmed that they straddle a genuine complexity boundary. Khot did not merely pose the conjecture; he drove much of the subsequent program, co-authoring the Max-Cut connection (with Kindler, Mossel, and O'Donnell), the Vertex Cover hardness (with Regev), and ultimately the 2-to-2 Games Theorem (with Dor Minzer and Muli Safra, 2016–2018), the deepest unconditional progress toward the conjecture to date.

Khot's broader legacy is the elevation of UGC into the organizing principle of modern approximation theory. Raghavendra's 2008 theorem made the conjecture *equivalent* to the statement that a single semidefinite program optimally approximates every constraint satisfaction problem — a unification that turned a technical hypothesis into a structural law. For this body of work Khot received the National Science Foundation's Alan T. Waterman Award (2010), the Rolf Nevanlinna Prize at the International Congress of Mathematicians (2014), and a MacArthur Fellowship (2016); he was elected to the U.S. National Academy of Sciences. His career illustrates how the act of *posing the right conjecture* — sharply, at the precise leverage point of a theory — can be as consequential as proving a theorem, reshaping a field's research agenda for two decades and counting.
