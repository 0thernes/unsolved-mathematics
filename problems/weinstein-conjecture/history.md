# History — The Weinstein Conjecture

The Weinstein conjecture is a fundamental existence statement in contact geometry: every Reeb vector field on a closed (compact, boundaryless) contact manifold carries at least one closed (periodic) orbit. It crystallized a half-century strand of work in Hamiltonian dynamics asking when energy hypersurfaces of mechanical systems must support periodic motions, and it became one of the central organizing problems for the symplectic-topological methods that emerged in the 1980s.

## Origin and precise formulation

The dynamical roots predate the contact-geometric statement. In the 1970s **Paul Rabinowitz** proved (1978) the existence of periodic orbits on star-shaped energy hypersurfaces in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, and **Alan Weinstein** proved (1978) existence on convex hypersurfaces. The two results applied to overlapping but distinct geometric classes, and Weinstein recognized that the essential feature was neither convexity nor star-shapedness but a contact-type condition on the hypersurface.

In **1979** Weinstein abstracted this into a conjecture. A contact form $\alpha$ on a $(2n-1)$-manifold $M$ satisfies $\alpha \wedge (d\alpha)^{n-1} \neq 0$; its Reeb field $R$ is defined by $\alpha(R)=1$ and $d\alpha(R,\cdot)=0$. The conjecture asserts that $R$ always possesses a periodic orbit. Equivalently, a hypersurface of contact type in a symplectic manifold carries a closed characteristic. The Reeb orbit is geometric — it depends on the contact form, not merely on the contact structure $\xi=\ker\alpha$ — but the conjecture asserts existence for every choice of $\alpha$.

## Timeline

- **1948** — Seifert proves periodic-orbit existence for a class of "kinetic + potential" energy surfaces and raises the general question of when such orbits must exist.
- **1978** — Rabinowitz (star-shaped hypersurfaces) and Weinstein (convex hypersurfaces) independently establish existence of periodic orbits in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.
- **1979** — Weinstein states the conjecture in "On the hypotheses of Rabinowitz's periodic orbit theorems," isolating the contact-type hypothesis as the right level of generality.
- **1987** — Claude Viterbo proves the conjecture for all hypersurfaces of contact type in $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, the first general result beyond convex/star-shaped cases.
- **1993** — Helmut Hofer introduces pseudoholomorphic curves into the problem, proving the conjecture in dimension three for $S^3$, for overtwisted contact structures, and whenever $\pi_2(M)\neq 0$.
- **1990s–2000s** — Floer-theoretic and contact-homology programs (Eliashberg–Givental–Hofer symplectic field theory) attack the three-dimensional case; partial results accumulate for many contact structures.
- **2007** — Clifford Taubes proves the Weinstein conjecture in **dimension three** unconditionally, using Seiberg–Witten Floer theory; the closed orbit appears via a limit of solutions to a perturbed Seiberg–Witten equation.
- **2010–2012** — Taubes, with Hutchings, Kutluhan and Lee, establishes the isomorphism between embedded contact homology (ECH) and Seiberg–Witten Floer cohomology, embedding the three-dimensional theorem in a broad structural framework.
- **2010s** — Cristofaro-Gardiner, Hutchings and collaborators sharpen the count: every contact form in dimension three has at least two Reeb orbits, and generically infinitely many.
- **2017–present** — In higher dimensions results remain restricted to special classes (overtwisted structures, fillable contact manifolds, prequantization bundles); the general statement in dimensions $\geq 5$ is the live frontier.

## Status of the frontier

In dimension three the conjecture is a theorem of Taubes (2007). In dimensions $\geq 5$ it remains open in general, although it holds for many classes: overtwisted contact structures, Boothby–Wang prequantization bundles, and contact manifolds admitting suitable symplectic fillings where holomorphic-curve or contact-homology machinery applies. The unconditional higher-dimensional statement is the central open problem.
