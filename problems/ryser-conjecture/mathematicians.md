# Top Mathematicians — Ryser's Conjecture (Hypergraph Covering)

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Herbert J. Ryser | 1948–1985 | originator | Combinatorial-matrix viewpoint from which the conjecture grew; the inequality bears his name. |
| 2 | J. R. Henderson | 1971 | originator | Stated the conjecture in his Caltech thesis and proved the case $r=3$. |
| 3 | Dénes König | 1931 | originator | König's bipartite min–max theorem — the $r=2$ base case the conjecture generalizes. |
| 4 | László Lovász | 1970s | breakthrough | Fractional cover theory; identified projective planes as extremal and proved fractional bounds. |
| 5 | Zoltán Füredi | 1980s | breakthrough | Fractional matching/cover inequalities $\tau \le (r-1)\nu^*$; foundational survey work on matchings vs covers. |
| 6 | Ron Aharoni | 2001 | breakthrough | Topological proof of the full conjecture for $r=3$ (tripartite 3-graphs). |
| 7 | Penny Haxell | 2000–present | frontier | Aharoni–Haxell hypergraph Hall theorem; extremal characterization and partial results for $r=4,5$. |
| 8 | Tibor Szabó | 2009–present | frontier | Extremal and equality analysis; constructions of non-projective-plane Ryser-extremal hypergraphs. |
| 9 | Alexey Pokrovskiy | 2010s–present | frontier | Improved intersecting-case bounds and new extremal constructions. |
| 10 | Anurag Bishnoi | 2017–present | frontier | Sub-$(r-1)$ covers for intersecting hypergraphs in ranks without projective planes. |

## Notes

The active community around Ryser's Conjecture is small but vigorous, clustered at the intersection of **extremal combinatorics, topological combinatorics, and finite geometry**. Two intellectual lineages dominate. The first is the **topological school** descending from Ron Aharoni and Penny Haxell, whose Hall-type theorem for hypergraphs (and its connectivity machinery, developed with Eli Berger and Ran Ziv) underlies the only known proof beyond $r=2$. The second is the **finite-geometry / extremal school**, centered on Tibor Szabó, Alexey Pokrovskiy, Anurag Bishnoi, and collaborators (Shagnik Das, Patrick Morris, Ahmad Abu-Khazneh, János Barát), which attacks the intersecting case by exploiting the presence or absence of projective planes of order $r-1$ and by constructing extremal examples that refute the strongest uniqueness conjectures. Design theorists such as Ian Wanless, Brendan McKay, and collaborators contribute on the Latin-square and design-enumeration side, and Zsolt Tuza connects the intersecting case to his own covering conjectures. Progress is incremental and rank-by-rank; the field is candid that no route to general $r$ is currently visible, which keeps the problem a benchmark for new methods rather than a target of imminent resolution. Names and dates above reflect established attributions; precise authorship of the most recent papers should be confirmed against MathSciNet/zbMATH.
