# History — The Slice–Ribbon Conjecture

_Origin, formulation, and timeline._

## Origin and setting

The conjecture lives in the part of knot theory that touches four-dimensional topology. A knot $K \subset S^3 = \partial B^4$ is **smoothly slice** if it bounds a smoothly embedded disk $D \subset B^4$. It is **ribbon** if it bounds an immersed disk in $S^3$ whose only self-intersections are *ribbon singularities* — arcs of double points along which one sheet passes cleanly through the interior of the other (no clasps, no triple points). Pushing a ribbon disk's interior into $B^4$ resolves the ribbon singularities and produces a slice disk, so **ribbon $\Rightarrow$ slice** is elementary. The conjecture asserts the converse.

A useful reformulation: a slice disk in $B^4$ is *ribbon* precisely when it admits a Morse (radial) function with no local maxima — equivalently, the disk is built from minima and saddles only. Thus "every slice knot is ribbon" is the handle-theoretic statement that **every slice disk can be isotoped to one without maxima**. This is also equivalent, via Casson–Gordon and the work of others, to claims about which 3-manifolds bound certain 4-manifolds.

## Formulation by Ralph Fox

Ralph Fox introduced ribbon knots and the surrounding circle of questions in the early 1960s. The conjecture is universally attributed to **Problem 25** in Fox's influential list "Some problems in knot theory," published in the proceedings of the 1961 Georgia topology conference (Fox, 1962). Fox asked whether the class of slice knots coincides with the class of ribbon knots; the question crystallized work he and his Princeton students (notably John Milnor and others in the orbit of his seminar) had been doing on the algebra of knots and the not-yet-named smooth concordance group.

## Timeline

- **1957** — Fox and Milnor circulate the "Fox–Milnor condition": the Alexander polynomial of a slice knot factors as $f(t)f(t^{-1})$ (published in full in 1966), giving the first algebraic obstruction to sliceness.
- **1961–1962** — Fox poses the slice–ribbon question as Problem 25 in *Some problems in knot theory* (Georgia conference proceedings).
- **1966** — Fox and Milnor publish "Singularities of $2$-spheres in $4$-space and cobordism of knots," formalizing slice knots and the determinant/Alexander obstruction.
- **1969** — Levine defines the algebraic concordance group and computes it as $\mathbb{Z}^\infty \oplus (\mathbb{Z}/2)^\infty \oplus (\mathbb{Z}/4)^\infty$, giving systematic obstructions to (algebraic) sliceness.
- **1975** — Casson and Gordon construct non-slice knots that are algebraically slice, showing Levine's invariants do not detect sliceness; their metabelian invariants become a key tool for the conjecture.
- **1980s** — Topological category diverges: Freedman proves Alexander-polynomial-one knots are *topologically* slice, so any program must be smooth.
- **1982** — Casson–Gordon-style and Donaldson-theoretic obstructions begin to constrain which knots can be slice.
- **2007** — Lisca proves the slice–ribbon conjecture for **2-bridge (rational) knots**, using Donaldson's diagonalization theorem and Heegaard Floer $d$-invariants — the first large, infinite, nontrivial family verified.
- **2011** — Greene and Jabuka verify the conjecture for **3-stranded pretzel knots** $P(p,q,r)$ with $p,q,r$ odd.
- **2010s** — Lecuona, and others, extend confirmations to broad families of Montesinos knots; Aceto, and collaborators, handle further classes via lattice embeddings and $d$-invariants.
- **2020s** — Frontier work uses knot Floer, instanton/Khovanov-style concordance invariants, and the (non-orientable / exotic) 4-genus toolkit; the conjecture remains **open** in general, with no proposed proof and persistent searches for a counterexample among candidate knots.

The problem is now over six decades old and is regarded as one of the central open questions tying together knot concordance, Donaldson/Heegaard-Floer obstructions, and the smooth topology of the 4-ball.
