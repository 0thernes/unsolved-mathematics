# Top Mathematicians — The Slice–Ribbon Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Ralph H. Fox | 1913–1973 | originator | Defined ribbon knots and posed the conjecture as Problem 25 (1962); founded the U.S. knot-theory school. |
| 2 | John W. Milnor | b. 1931 | breakthrough | Co-author of the Fox–Milnor condition (1966), the first algebraic obstruction to sliceness. |
| 3 | Jerome Levine | 1937–2014 | breakthrough | Built the algebraic concordance group (1969), the systematic algebra against which sliceness is tested. |
| 4 | Andrew Casson | b. 1943 | breakthrough | Casson–Gordon invariants (1975): metabelian obstructions distinguishing slice from algebraically slice. |
| 5 | Cameron Gordon | b. 1945 | breakthrough | Co-developer of Casson–Gordon theory; long-standing expositor of slice/ribbon questions. |
| 6 | Michael H. Freedman | b. 1951 | breakthrough | Topological 4-manifold theory forced the conjecture into the smooth category (Alexander-poly-one knots are topologically slice). |
| 7 | Simon K. Donaldson | b. 1957 | breakthrough | Diagonalization theorem (1983), the gauge-theoretic engine behind the lattice-embedding confirmations. |
| 8 | Paolo Lisca | contemporary | frontier | Proved the conjecture for all 2-bridge knots (2007) — the landmark partial result. |
| 9 | Joshua Greene | contemporary | frontier | With Jabuka, proved the conjecture for odd 3-pretzel knots (2011); lattice-embedding methods. |
| 10 | Ana G. Lecuona | contemporary | frontier | Extended confirmation to broad families of Montesinos knots (2012–). |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of **knot concordance** and **smooth 4-manifold topology**. Beyond the table, central contemporary contributors include **Stanislav Jabuka** (pretzel-knot case with Greene), **Brendan Owens** and **Ciprian Manolescu** ($\delta$-invariant), **Paolo Aceto** (rational cobordism / lattice embeddings), and the **Heegaard Floer** founders **Peter Ozsváth** and **Zoltán Szabó**, whose $d$-invariants and concordance invariants ($\tau$, $\Upsilon$, $\nu^+$) are indispensable tools. **Jacob Rasmussen** ($s$-invariant) and **Lisa Piccirillo** (sliceness detection) sharpened the broader sliceness toolkit, and **Charles Livingston** and **Jennifer Hom** are leading expositors/surveyors of knot concordance, maintaining (with collaborators) the **KnotInfo** database that tracks the slice status of low-crossing knots where any counterexample would most plausibly be found. The field is small but highly active, advancing the conjecture family-by-family rather than by a single decisive method.
