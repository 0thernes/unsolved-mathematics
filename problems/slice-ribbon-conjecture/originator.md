# Originator(s) — The Slice–Ribbon Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Ralph Hartzler Fox (1913–1973)

The conjecture is due to **Ralph Fox**, one of the architects of modern knot theory in the United States. Born in 1913 in Morrisville, Pennsylvania, Fox studied at Swarthmore and Johns Hopkins before earning his Ph.D. at Princeton in 1939 under **Solomon Lefschetz**, with a dissertation on topology and the theory of homotopy groups of fibers. After positions at the Institute for Advanced Study, the University of Illinois, and Syracuse, he returned to **Princeton University**, where he spent the bulk of his career and built the school of low-dimensional topology that produced a remarkable lineage of students — including **John Stallings, Barry Mazur, John Milnor** (a close collaborator), **Lee Neuwirth, Guillermo Torres, David Gillman, and Francisco González-Acuña**, among others.

### Background and mathematical style

Fox's instinct was to make knot theory *computable* and *algebraic*. He is remembered for the **Fox $n$-colorings** (an elementary yet powerful invariant), the **free differential calculus** (Fox derivatives), which turns the fundamental group of a knot complement into a tool for computing Alexander matrices and polynomials, and for systematizing the study of knot groups and their presentations. He co-authored, with Richard Crowell, *Introduction to Knot Theory* (1963), the textbook that trained a generation. His viewpoint — that a knot is best understood through the algebra of its complement and through explicit surfaces it bounds — directly shaped the slice–ribbon question.

### The ideas behind the conjecture

By the late 1950s Fox and Milnor were studying when a knot bounds a disk in the 4-ball: the genesis of **knot concordance** and the **(smooth) concordance group**. They isolated the *Fox–Milnor condition* on the Alexander polynomial as a necessary algebraic obstruction to sliceness. In parallel, Fox formalized **ribbon knots** — those bounding an immersed disk in $S^3$ with only ribbon (clasp-free) singularities — a class that is manifestly, constructively "slice in an obvious way." The natural question was whether this *constructive* class exhausts the *existential* class of all slice knots. He recorded it as **Problem 25** in his 1962 problem list from the Georgia topology conference: *Is every slice knot ribbon?* The phrasing reflects Fox's conviction that the seemingly four-dimensional condition (bounding a disk in $B^4$) ought to be detectable by three-dimensional, combinatorial means.

### Legacy

Fox died in 1973 following surgery. His mathematical legacy is enormous: the Fox calculus and Fox colorings are standard tools; his students reshaped geometric topology; and the slice–ribbon conjecture remains one of the most cited open problems bearing his name. The conjecture has aged into a litmus test for the gap between the smooth and topological categories in dimension four — a gap Fox could not have anticipated, since the dramatic divergence between *topologically slice* and *smoothly slice* knots emerged only after **Freedman's** (1982) and **Donaldson's** (1983) work, a decade after his death.

### On the modern vs. historical formulation

Fox's original wording predates the careful distinction between the **smooth** and **topological** categories. In the topological category the statement is false in spirit — Freedman's theorem produces topologically slice knots (e.g., untwisted Whitehead doubles of many knots) that are not ribbon — so the conjecture is now always understood in the **smooth** category: *every smoothly slice knot is ribbon*. The handle-theoretic reformulation ("every slice disk is isotopic to one with no local maxima") and the equivalence to questions about 4-manifolds bounding given 3-manifolds are later refinements that sharpen, but do not change, Fox's original question.
