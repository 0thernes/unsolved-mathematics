# Top Mathematicians — Hilbert's Sixteenth Problem (Second Part)

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | David Hilbert | 1862–1943 | originator | Posed the problem (Paris ICM, 1900); framed limit-cycle counting as a topology-of-solutions question. |
| 2 | Henri Poincaré | 1854–1912 | originator | Created the qualitative theory of planar ODEs and the very notion of a limit cycle, the object Hilbert asked to count. |
| 3 | Henri Dulac | 1870–1955 | breakthrough | 1923 finiteness claim for individual systems; the gap in it defined a 60-year subproblem. |
| 4 | Nikolai N. Bautin | 1908–1993 | breakthrough | Proved cyclicity $\le 3$ for foci/centers of quadratic systems (1952); introduced the Bautin ideal. |
| 5 | Ivan G. Petrovskii | 1901–1973 | expositor | With Landis, the famous (erroneous) $H(2)=3$ program; spurred the field by being refuted. |
| 6 | Vladimir I. Arnold | 1937–2010 | expositor | Formulated the weakened/infinitesimal Hilbert 16th problem; set the modern research agenda. |
| 7 | Yulij S. Ilyashenko | b. 1943 | breakthrough | Exposed Dulac's gap (1981); proved individual finiteness (1991); wrote the definitive centennial survey. |
| 8 | Jean Écalle | b. 1950 | breakthrough | Independent 1992 proof of individual finiteness via resurgent functions / transseries. |
| 9 | Robert Roussarie | b. 1944 | frontier | Theory of limit periodic sets and cyclicity; co-disproved the Lins–de Melo–Pugh conjecture (2007). |
| 10 | Sergei Yakovenko | b. 1958 | frontier | With Novikov and Binyamini, explicit uniform bounds for zeros of Abelian integrals (infinitesimal problem). |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of dynamical systems, real algebraic geometry, and transcendental number theory / tame geometry. The "Moscow school" lineage of Arnold–Ilyashenko–Yakovenko (now including Gal Binyamini and Dmitry Novikov at the Weizmann Institute) drives the infinitesimal/effective-finiteness program via o-minimality and point-counting. A strong "Catalan/Belgian–French" school around Roussarie, Freddy Dumortier, Jaume Llibre, Armengol Gasull, and Joan Torregrosa develops bifurcation theory, slow–fast/canard methods, and explicit cycle constructions. A large and productive Chinese school (Jibin Li, Yulin Zhao, Maoan Han, Chengzhi Li and many others) holds most of the current lower-bound records for cubic and higher-degree systems. Lubomir Gavrilov and Pavao Mardešić contribute on the algebraic-geometry side of Abelian integrals. Despite this breadth, the central existence question — is $H(2)$ finite? — has seen no decisive movement, and the field's recent energy is concentrated on the tractable infinitesimal proxy and on computer-assisted constructions.
