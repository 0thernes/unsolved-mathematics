# Attempts — Hilbert's Sixteenth Problem (Second Part)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

**Dulac's 1923 "theorem" (gap, later repaired).** Henri Dulac published *Sur les cycles limites* claiming that every analytic planar vector field has finitely many limit cycles. The proof studied the return map near a polycycle through normal forms of the Dulac maps at the singular points. In **1981 Yu. Ilyashenko** showed the argument was incomplete: the asymptotic series used did not rule out limit cycles accumulating on a polycycle (the function might oscillate beyond all orders of its expansion). The statement was eventually re-proved correctly and independently by **Ilyashenko (1991)** and **Écalle (1992)** with entirely new transcendental machinery. This is the canonical example of a long-accepted "result" that was in fact open for nearly six decades.

**Petrovskii–Landis (1955–1957), the false $H(2)=3$.** I. G. Petrovskii and E. M. Landis published a proof that quadratic systems have at most three limit cycles and that $H(n)$ obeys an explicit formula. The method used complexification and estimates on the variation of the return map along complex paths. By the early 1960s the authors themselves acknowledged a fundamental error in the argument; the claimed bound was wrong. The refutation became concrete around 1979–1980 when **Shi Songling** and, independently, **Chen Lansun and Wang Mingshu** exhibited explicit quadratic systems with **four** limit cycles (a small-amplitude triple near one focus plus a large cycle around another), proving $H(2)\ge 4$. Whether $H(2)=4$ remains open; $4$ is the widely held conjecture.

**Bautin's center cyclicity (1952), a genuine and durable partial result.** N. N. Bautin proved that no more than three small-amplitude limit cycles can bifurcate from a single focus or center of a quadratic system, via the structure of the ideal generated by the focal quantities (the "Bautin ideal"). This is correct and remains a cornerstone, but it is *local*: it bounds cycles near one equilibrium, not the global Hilbert number, so it does not bound $H(2)$.

**The Lins–de Melo–Pugh conjecture (1977), disproved 2007.** For the Liénard equation with $f$ of degree $2k+1$ they proved exactly $k$ cycles in the classical case and conjectured this count as a general upper bound for polynomial Liénard systems. **Dumortier, Panazzolo and Roussarie (2007)** disproved the conjecture by exhibiting Liénard systems with more limit cycles than predicted, using slow–fast/canard techniques. This near-miss reshaped expectations about how cycle counts grow.

**Periodic claims of complete solutions.** As with several famous open problems, preprints announcing a full resolution of Hilbert's 16th (a finite explicit $H(n)$, or even just finiteness of $H(2)$) appear from time to time on arXiv and elsewhere. None has been accepted by the research community as of this writing; the consensus is that the existence of a finite bound for quadratic systems is unproven. Such claims should be treated with caution: the history above shows that even expert arguments in this area have concealed fatal gaps for decades, and the burden of independent verification is high.

**Lower-bound records as honest progress.** Distinct from disputed upper bounds, the constructive lower bounds are uncontested. Cubic systems with $11$ and later $13$ limit cycles, and general-degree configurations realizing $\sim n^2\log n$ cycles, are accepted constructions. They sharpen the conjectured growth rate but, by their nature, cannot resolve the existence question that lies at the heart of the problem.
