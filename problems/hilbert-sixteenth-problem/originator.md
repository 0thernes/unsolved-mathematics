# Originator(s) — Hilbert's Sixteenth Problem (Second Part)

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

**David Hilbert** (23 January 1862, Königsberg, Prussia — 14 February 1943, Göttingen) was the preeminent mathematician of the turn of the twentieth century and, with Poincaré, one of its two universalists. He took his doctorate at Königsberg in 1885 under Ferdinand von Lindemann, was shaped intellectually by his lifelong friendship with Hermann Minkowski and by Adolf Hurwitz, and in 1895 was appointed to the chair at Göttingen, which under his leadership became the world's center of mathematics until the Nazi expulsions of 1933 destroyed it.

Hilbert's career is often divided into phases, each of which he entered, transformed, and left: invariant theory (where his 1888 finite-basis theorem overturned the computational program of Gordan), algebraic number theory (the 1897 *Zahlbericht*), the foundations of geometry (*Grundlagen der Geometrie*, 1899, which axiomatized Euclid with full rigor), integral equations and spectral theory (whence "Hilbert space"), the foundations of mathematics and proof theory (the "Hilbert program"), and mathematical physics, where he worked in parallel with Einstein on the field equations of general relativity. This breadth is essential context: when Hilbert posed his problems he was not a specialist in any one of the fields they touched but a synthesist mapping the whole terrain of mathematics.

The sixteenth problem grew directly out of two strands alive in 1900. The first was the **topology of real algebraic curves**: Hilbert had himself contributed to the study of the ovals of sextic curves, sharpening the question of which arrangements permitted by Harnack's bound ($\tfrac{(n-1)(n-2)}{2}+1$ ovals) are actually realizable. The second was **Poincaré's qualitative theory of differential equations**, the *Mémoire sur les courbes définies par une équation différentielle* (1881–1886), which had introduced limit cycles and the index theory of singular points and had shown that the global structure of phase portraits is a topological object worthy of study. Hilbert's insight — characteristic of his taste for problems that join distant fields — was that the *position of the ovals of an algebraic curve* and the *position of the limit cycles of a polynomial vector field* are two instances of one question: how complicated can the real solution set of a degree-$n$ object be, and how are its pieces arranged in the plane? He grouped both under a single heading precisely to advertise this analogy.

Hilbert expected, as the rhetoric of his Paris address makes clear, that a sufficiently developed method would yield finite, computable bounds. He did not anticipate that the second part would resist all comers for over a century, that even the existence of a bound for quadratic systems would stay open, or that the auxiliary "individual finiteness" statement (each fixed system has finitely many limit cycles) would itself require the deep transcendental machinery later supplied by Ilyashenko and Écalle. The problem's difficulty is in some sense a measure of how far the analytic theory of differential equations lagged behind the algebraic geometry that inspired it.

Note on formulation: Hilbert's original wording asks for the "maximum number and position" of limit cycles for each degree $n$ — the existence of the Hilbert number $H(n)$ as a finite quantity is implicitly assumed. The modern reading separates this into (a) the **existence** of a finite $H(n)$, (b) its **value** or growth rate, and (c) the auxiliary individual-finiteness statement. Hilbert did not distinguish these; the century of work since has shown them to be of very different depth.

Hilbert's broader legacy needs little rehearsal: the axiomatic method, the program for the foundations of mathematics (whose limits Gödel exposed in 1931), the formalism that organizes functional analysis, and above all the **list of 23 problems** itself, which set much of the agenda of twentieth-century mathematics. His epitaph in Göttingen reads *"Wir müssen wissen. Wir werden wissen."* — "We must know. We will know." — the closing words of his 1930 Königsberg address. For the sixteenth problem, at least, the "will" remains a hope rather than a fact.
