# Originator(s) — The Caccetta–Häggkvist Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture carries the names of two combinatorialists who introduced it together in 1978: the Australian operations researcher **Louis Caccetta** and the Swedish graph theorist **Roland Häggkvist**. Both were early-career researchers at the time, working in the rich extremal- and structural-combinatorics environment of the late 1970s, and the joint paper that bears their names became the most cited work of an otherwise quite different pair of careers.

## Louis Caccetta

Louis Caccetta is an Australian mathematician long associated with **Curtin University** in Perth, Western Australia, where he built a substantial career in combinatorics, optimization, and operations research. His research spans graph theory, network design, integer and combinatorial optimization, and applications to industrial and resource-management problems — a breadth characteristic of the applied-combinatorics tradition in Australia. Within pure graph theory his most enduring contribution is the 1978 conjecture, but his wider body of work emphasizes extremal problems, hamiltonicity, and the structure of graphs and digraphs under degree constraints. The directed-girth question fit naturally into his interest in how local degree conditions force global cyclic structure, a theme that recurs across extremal graph theory. Caccetta remained an active researcher and supervisor in Perth for decades, contributing to the Australian combinatorics community and to the optimization literature well beyond the single famous conjecture.

## Roland Häggkvist

Roland Häggkvist is a Swedish mathematician, for much of his career a professor at **Umeå University** in northern Sweden, and one of the most influential figures in modern combinatorics there. He is known across several areas: Hamiltonian cycles and the structure of dense graphs, **Latin squares** and edge-colourings of graphs, list colouring, and extremal questions about cycles. He was a frequent collaborator and a notable problem-poser, and his name attaches to results and conjectures throughout graph theory. Häggkvist's instinct for the right extremal threshold — the precise boundary at which a degree condition forces a structural conclusion — is visible in the Caccetta–Häggkvist statement, which guesses an exactly tight constant ($n/k$) rather than merely an asymptotic order of magnitude. That confidence in tightness is part of what has made the conjecture so durable and so attractive: it is not a vague heuristic but a sharp, falsifiable prediction with a clean extremal example ($\vec{C}_n$) saturating it.

## The idea behind the conjecture

The motivation was a Turán-type question in the directed world. Classical extremal graph theory asks how dense an (undirected) graph can be while avoiding a fixed subgraph; the directed analogue Caccetta and Häggkvist confronted asks how large a degree condition can be while avoiding *short directed cycles*. They approached this through the equivalent lens of **minimal digraphs with given girth**: given that the girth exceeds $k$, how small must the minimum out-degree be? The directed cycle $\vec{C}_n$ supplied the conjecturally extremal configuration, and small-case computation supported the guess that $n/k$ is exactly the threshold. The elegance lies in the single uniform constant $1/k$ governing all cases simultaneously.

## Legacy

The conjecture has far outgrown its origins. The triangle case ($k=3$) became a magnet for new methods — flag algebras, additive combinatorics, probabilistic arguments — and connects to questions studied by Paul Seymour, Maria Chudnovsky, and others, including the Second Neighbourhood Conjecture and Cayley-digraph problems in additive number theory. For Caccetta and Häggkvist alike, it stands as a problem whose statement is elementary enough to explain in a sentence yet deep enough to have resisted every major technique of extremal combinatorics for over forty years.
