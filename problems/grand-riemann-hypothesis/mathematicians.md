# Top Mathematicians — The Grand Riemann Hypothesis

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Bernhard Riemann | 1826–1866 | originator | 1859 memoir: analytic continuation, functional equation, explicit formula, and the hypothesis for $\zeta(s)$ — the seed case. |
| 2 | Adolf Piltz | 1855–1940 | originator | 1884 generalization of the hypothesis to Dirichlet $L$-functions; first recognition that RH concerns a *class* of $L$-functions. |
| 3 | Erich Hecke | 1887–1947 | breakthrough | $L$-functions with Größencharakteren; analytic theory extending RH to general number fields (ERH). |
| 4 | Emil Artin | 1898–1962 | breakthrough | Artin $L$-functions and the holomorphy conjecture; opened the non-abelian / automorphic horizon of GRH. |
| 5 | Atle Selberg | 1917–2007 | originator | Positive proportion of $\zeta$-zeros on the line; defined the Selberg class $\mathcal{S}$ and stated GRH in axiomatic "grand" form. |
| 6 | Pierre Deligne | b. 1944 | breakthrough | Proof of the Weil conjectures (1974): RH as a theorem in the function-field analogue, the model for what GRH would mean. |
| 7 | Robert P. Langlands | b. 1936 | frontier | Langlands program: automorphic $L$-functions as the natural home of GRH; functoriality unifying the class. |
| 8 | Hugh L. Montgomery | b. 1944 | frontier | Pair-correlation conjecture (1973) linking zeros to random-matrix statistics; reframed GRH probabilistically. |
| 9 | Alain Connes | b. 1947 | frontier | Noncommutative-geometry trace formula reformulating GRH for $L$-functions with Größencharakter as a positivity statement. |
| 10 | J. Brian Conrey | b. 1955 | frontier | Record proportions of on-line zeros ($>40\%$); with Katz–Sarnak and others, leader of the moments/RMT program for $L$-functions. |

## Notes

The active community spans several overlapping clusters. **Analytic number theorists** (Conrey, Soundararajan, Iwaniec, Sarnak, Bui, Young, Pratt, Robles) push the critical-line proportion and moment conjectures, increasingly via the connection to random matrix theory pioneered by Montgomery, Odlyzko, Katz, and Sarnak. **Selberg-class structure theory** is driven by Kaczorowski and Perelli, who classify low-degree elements and pursue the degree conjecture. **Spectral / noncommutative approaches** center on Connes and the Berry–Keating circle, seeking a Hilbert–Pólya operator. The **Langlands-program** side (Langlands, Arthur, Ngô, and the broader automorphic-forms community) supplies the functoriality results that make "every automorphic $L$-function" a coherent target. Polymath-style collaborations (e.g., the Rodgers–Tao work on the de Bruijn–Newman constant) and large-scale numerical verification (the LMFDB collaboration) keep both the theoretical and computational frontiers active. Despite this breadth, no group claims a route to a full proof; consensus holds that a genuinely new idea — most likely an arithmetic analogue of $\ell$-adic cohomology — is required.
