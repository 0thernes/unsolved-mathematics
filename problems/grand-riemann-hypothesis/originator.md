# Originator(s) — The Grand Riemann Hypothesis

GRH has no single author; it is the cumulative widening of one idea by several figures. The Atlas records two: **Adolf Piltz**, who first generalized Riemann's hypothesis to a family of $L$-functions, and **Bernhard Riemann**, whose original 1859 conjecture is the seed case. The axiomatic "grand" formulation belongs to **Atle Selberg**, and the conceptual frame that makes it grand — automorphy — to **Robert Langlands**.

## Bernhard Riemann (1826–1866)

Born in Breselenz, Hanover, the son of a Lutheran pastor, Riemann studied at Göttingen and Berlin under Gauss, Dirichlet, and Jacobi. His 1859 Berlin Academy memoir, written on the occasion of his election, introduced the analytic continuation of $\zeta(s)$, its functional equation, the explicit formula linking zeros to primes, and — almost in passing — the hypothesis that all non-trivial zeros lie on $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$. He framed it as a remark he could not prove and "set aside" for his main goal, the Prime Number Theorem. Riemann died of tuberculosis at 39 in Selasca, Italy; his collected works fill a single slim volume, yet seeded analytic number theory, complex geometry, and general relativity. The original RH is the degree-one, level-one, trivial-nebentypus case of GRH.

## Adolf Piltz (1855–1940)

Far less famous, Piltz was a German mathematician who studied at Jena and worked on the distribution of primes. In 1884 he formulated what is now the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis: that Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s,\chi)$ — Riemann's $\zeta$ being the principal character case — should also have all non-trivial zeros on the critical line. His motivation was concrete: control of primes in arithmetic progressions, where the error term in the prime-counting function $\pi(x;q,a)$ is governed precisely by the zeros of the relevant $L(s,\chi)$. GRH for Dirichlet $L$-functions would give essentially square-root-error equidistribution of primes across residue classes. Piltz's name is also attached to the Piltz (Dirichlet) divisor problem. His 1884 step is the first recognition that RH is not about $\zeta$ alone but about a *class* of objects sharing an Euler product and functional equation — the conceptual move that, iterated, becomes GRH.

## Atle Selberg (1917–2007) and the grand formulation

Norwegian-born, a permanent member of the Institute for Advanced Study and a 1950 Fields Medalist, Selberg gave GRH its modern axiomatic shape. In lectures and the 1992 Amalfi paper he defined the **Selberg class** $\mathcal{S}$ by four axioms — Dirichlet series convergence, analytic continuation with finite-order growth, a functional equation of Riemann type, and an Euler product with a Ramanujan-type prime bound. He conjectured RH for *every* element of $\mathcal{S}$, and added deep "orthonormality" conjectures (Selberg's conjectures A and B) about the statistical independence of zeros of distinct primitive elements, which imply unique factorization in $\mathcal{S}$ and Artin's conjecture. This is the cleanest statement of GRH not depending on the (still partly conjectural) automorphic dictionary.

## Robert Langlands (b. 1936) and the automorphic home

Langlands' program (1967 onward) supplies the structural reason the class is "grand": all arithmetically meaningful $L$-functions should be automorphic $L$-functions $L(s,\pi)$ for cuspidal representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ over a number field. Under functoriality, the Selberg class and the automorphic class are expected to coincide, so GRH is one conjecture wearing two notations.

## Historical root vs. modern formulation

The historical root (Riemann 1859, Piltz 1884) concerns a concrete list — $\zeta$, Dirichlet, later Dedekind and Hecke $L$-functions. The modern formulation (Selberg, Langlands) is *axiomatic and uniform*: GRH is a single hypothesis quantified over an entire class, so that any new $L$-function entering the class inherits the prediction automatically. The legacy is that "the Riemann Hypothesis" is now understood not as a fact about one function but as a structural law about all $L$-functions.
