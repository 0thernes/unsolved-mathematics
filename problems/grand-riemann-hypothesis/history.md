# History — The Grand Riemann Hypothesis

The Grand Riemann Hypothesis (GRH, "Grand" rather than "Generalized") is the maximal generalization of Riemann's 1859 conjecture: **every** $L$-function in a suitably broad class has all of its non-trivial zeros on the critical line $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$. In modern form it is asserted for all automorphic $L$-functions attached to cuspidal automorphic representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{Q}})$ (more generally over any number field), and, in axiomatic form, for every element of the **Selberg class** $\mathcal{S}$.

## Origin and successive reformulations

The seed is Riemann's *Über die Anzahl der Primzahlen unter einer gegebenen Größe* (1859), which conjectured RH for $\zeta(s)$. The first nontrivial generalization is due to **Adolf Piltz** (1884), who, studying the distribution of primes in arithmetic progressions, extended the hypothesis to Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s,\chi)$ — now called the **Generalized Riemann Hypothesis** (GRH$_{\text{Dirichlet}}$). The acronym is genuinely ambiguous: "Grand" is reserved for the version over the full automorphic/Selberg class, while "Generalized" usually means the Dirichlet (or Dedekind/Hecke) case.

Three structural reformulations drove the problem toward its grand form:

1. **Dedekind and Hecke.** Dedekind zeta functions $\zeta_K(s)$ of number fields, and Hecke's $L$-functions with Größencharakteren (1918–1920), placed RH inside class field theory; their RH is the **Extended Riemann Hypothesis** (ERH).
2. **Artin and automorphy.** Artin $L$-functions (1923–1930) and the conjectural correspondence with automorphic forms — crystallized in the **Langlands program** (1967–1970) — recast all arithmetic $L$-functions as automorphic $L$-functions $L(s,\pi)$ for cuspidal $\pi$ on $\mathrm{GL}_n$. GRH became the statement that each such $L(s,\pi)$ satisfies RH.
3. **Selberg's axioms.** In 1989–1992 **Atle Selberg** isolated an axiomatic class $\mathcal{S}$ of Dirichlet series (Euler product, analytic continuation, functional equation, Ramanujan-type growth) and conjectured RH for every $F\in\mathcal{S}$, together with orthonormality conjectures governing how zeros of distinct primitive elements interact. The Selberg class is believed to coincide with automorphic $L$-functions, making GRH and "RH for $\mathcal S$" effectively the same conjecture.

## Timeline

- **1859** — Riemann conjectures RH for $\zeta(s)$ in his eight-page memoir.
- **1884** — Piltz extends the hypothesis to Dirichlet $L$-functions (origin of the "generalized" form).
- **1896** — Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin prove the Prime Number Theorem via zero-free regions — the template later used for all $L$-functions.
- **1917** — Hardy proves infinitely many zeros of $\zeta$ lie on the critical line.
- **1918–1920** — Hecke develops $L$-functions with Größencharakteren; ERH crystallizes.
- **1923–1930** — Artin introduces non-abelian $L$-functions; Artin's holomorphy conjecture links them to automorphy.
- **1942–1956** — Selberg proves a positive proportion of $\zeta$-zeros lie on the critical line.
- **1967–1970** — Langlands formulates his program; automorphic $L$-functions $L(s,\pi)$ become the natural home of GRH.
- **1974** — Deligne proves the Weil conjectures (RH for varieties over finite fields); the *function-field* analogue is a theorem, sharpening the contrast with the open number-field case.
- **1974** — Levinson shows $>34\%$ of $\zeta$-zeros are simple and on the line.
- **1989** — Conrey raises the proportion past $40\%$.
- **1989–1992** — Selberg defines the class $\mathcal{S}$ and states the Selberg conjectures; GRH attains its axiomatic "grand" formulation.
- **1999–2011** — Kaczorowski–Perelli classify low-degree elements of $\mathcal{S}$ (degrees $0$, $1$, and gaps in $(1,2)$), confirming structural expectations without resolving RH.
- **2011–2020** — Bui–Conrey–Young, Pratt–Robles, and others push the critical-line proportion past $41\%$ for $\zeta$; random-matrix and pair-correlation heuristics (Montgomery 1973, Katz–Sarnak 1999) frame GRH statistically.
- **Present** — GRH remains **open** for every degree-$\ge 1$ element beyond what RH/ERH already cover; it is unproven even for a single $\mathrm{GL}_2$ cusp form's $L$-function.
