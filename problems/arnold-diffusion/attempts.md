# Attempts — Arnold Diffusion (Genericity)

The history of this problem is unusually marked by long, influential, but only partially published manuscripts, and by results whose statements were correct while the writing-up lagged years behind the announcement. The notes below are neutral summaries; where claims were disputed or incomplete, the objections are stated as recorded in the community.

**Arnold's example (1964).** Not a flaw but a deliberate limitation: Arnold proved *existence* of diffusion in one engineered a priori unstable system and explicitly left genericity as a conjecture. Much subsequent confusion stemmed from later authors over-reading the example as evidence that the general case was nearly settled, when in fact the typicality question was untouched.

**Mather's announcements (2002–2004).** John Mather circulated a long manuscript announcing generic Arnold diffusion for nearly integrable convex Hamiltonians in the two-and-a-half-degree-of-freedom setting, using his variational theory. The results were widely believed and deeply influential, and Mather lectured on them extensively, but a complete, fully refereed write-up of the entire program did not appear under his name; portions were filled in, corrected, and published by others over the following fifteen years (notably the Bernard–Kaloshin–Zhang and Kaloshin–Zhang programs). The episode is best described not as a retracted claim but as a correct vision whose full proof was a community effort spanning many papers. Some experts noted gaps in the double-resonance analysis as originally sketched; these were the hardest part to complete.

**The double-resonance gap.** Across multiple attempts, the recurring near-miss is the *double-resonance* region, where two resonant lines cross and the local model becomes a mechanical system on the 2-torus. Several early arguments handled single resonances cleanly but treated double resonances only heuristically or under unverified non-degeneracy hypotheses. Kaloshin–Zhang's monograph (2020) is generally regarded as the first place where the double-resonance case for $n=2$ is controlled with full rigor for a generic perturbation.

**A priori unstable proofs and their scope (2004–2010).** Delshams–de la Llave–Seara (geometric), Treschev (separatrix-map / normal form), Cheng–Yan and Bernard (variational) each gave rigorous diffusion results. The honest caveat is scope: these are theorems for a priori *unstable* systems (hyperbolicity present in the unperturbed model) and do not by themselves resolve Arnold's a priori *stable* conjecture. Occasional secondary literature blurred this distinction; the primary papers are careful about it.

**Computer-assisted contributions.** Gidea, de la Llave, Capiński, Zgliczyński and collaborators produced computer-assisted proofs of diffusion in concrete systems (including the planar elliptic restricted three-body problem). These are rigorous existence results in specific systems; they are not claims of genericity, and have not been disputed, but they should not be cited as resolving the conjecture.

**Status of "full resolution" claims.** As of the present frontier, **no claimed complete proof of Arnold's genericity conjecture for all $n \ge 3$ exists**, and therefore none has been disputed in that sense. The strongest standing results are genericity in two and a half degrees of freedom (smooth convex category). Any announcement of the full conjecture — in particular in the analytic category or in arbitrary dimension — should be treated as needing verification; to date none has passed community refereeing. Practitioners are explicit that higher-dimensional and analytic cases are open, so extraordinary care is warranted before treating any preprint as a resolution.
