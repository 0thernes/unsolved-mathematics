# History — The Zilber–Pink Conjecture

The Zilber–Pink conjecture is the apex of the modern theory of *unlikely intersections*. It asserts that when a subvariety $V$ of a semiabelian variety, a Shimura variety, or more generally a mixed Shimura variety meets the *special subvarieties* of the ambient space in a way that violates the expected dimension count, the resulting "unlikely" intersection is not Zariski dense — it is contained in a finite union of proper special subvarieties of $V$. It simultaneously generalizes the Manin–Mumford, Mordell–Lang, and André–Oort conjectures, fusing the abelian (torsion / division-point) world with the Shimura (special-point) world under a single dimension-theoretic principle.

The heuristic is elementary: if $V \subseteq S$ has dimension $d$ and a special subvariety $T$ has codimension $> d$, one expects $V \cap T = \varnothing$ generically; intersections that nonetheless occur are "unlikely" and should be explained by an *atypical* component lying in a special subvariety of $S$. Making this rigorous across all the relevant settings is the conjecture.

## Origins and reformulations

The two formulations arose independently and from different cultures.

- **Boris Zilber** reached the conjecture through model theory. In his work on the model theory of the complex exponential and on what he called *pseudo-exponentiation*, he was led (circa 2002, published 2002) to a "Conjecture on Intersection with Tori" (CIT) for semiabelian varieties, motivated by questions of categoricity and by Schanuel's conjecture.
- **Richard Pink**, working in arithmetic geometry, formulated a sweeping generalization for mixed Shimura varieties in unpublished manuscripts and a 2005 preprint, explicitly designed to contain André–Oort and Mordell–Lang as special cases.
- **Enrico Bombieri, David Masser, and Umberto Zannier** had, in a celebrated 1999 paper on multiplicative tori, already proved the first non-trivial unlikely-intersection theorem, seeding the "anomalous subvarieties" framework Zannier later systematized.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Bombieri–Masser–Zannier prove finiteness of points on a curve in $\mathbb{G}_m^n$ satisfying two independent multiplicative relations; the founding result of the field.
- **2002** — Zilber circulates "Exponential sums equations and the Schanuel conjecture," stating the CIT.
- **2005** — Pink's manuscripts and preprint state the general conjecture for (mixed) Shimura varieties.
- **2008** — Pila–Zannier give a new proof of Manin–Mumford via o-minimality and the counting theorem, launching the dominant modern method.
- **2011** — Jonathan Pila proves the **André–Oort conjecture for $\mathbb{C}^n = \mathbb{A}^n$ (products of modular curves)** unconditionally, using o-minimality and point-counting.
- **2014** — Habegger–Pila prove Zilber–Pink for curves in abelian varieties under suitable conditions; the o-minimal Pila–Wilkie machinery becomes standard.
- **2016** — Zannier's book *Some Problems of Unlikely Intersections in Arithmetic and Geometry* consolidates the field.
- **2016** — Habegger–Pila establish Zilber–Pink for curves in $Y(1)^n$ assuming a large Galois-orbit / arithmetic hypothesis.
- **2021** — Pila–Shankar–Tsimerman (with appendix by Esnault–Groechenig), building on Andreatta–Goren–Howard–Madapusi Pera averaged Colmez and Tsimerman's earlier work, prove **André–Oort for all Shimura varieties of abelian type**.
- **2022–2024** — Daw–Orr, Gao, and others push Zilber–Pink for curves and obtain unconditional cases via height bounds (Gao–Habegger functional transcendence and the Betti map), and Binyamini–Schmidt–Yafaev develop effective/quantitative point-counting (PILA / sharply-o-minimal structures).

The conjecture remains **open** in essentially all its higher-dimensional generality, even for surfaces in abelian varieties.
