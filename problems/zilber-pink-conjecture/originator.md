# Originator(s) — The Zilber–Pink Conjecture

The conjecture carries two names because it was reached twice, by two mathematicians working in nearly disjoint traditions who converged on the same dimension-theoretic principle.

## Boris Zilber

Boris Iosifovich Zilber (born 1949) is a model theorist, trained in the Soviet logic school and long associated with the University of Oxford. His early fame rests on deep structural results in model theory: the Zilber trichotomy, his work on totally categorical and $\aleph_1$-categorical theories, and the broader program of *geometric stability theory*, in which he argued that the combinatorial geometry of a definable set should reflect classical algebraic or analytic structure (a "field hidden in the geometry").

Zilber's route to unlikely intersections was philosophical as much as technical. Seeking to give a model-theoretic axiomatization of the complex exponential field $(\mathbb{C}, +, \cdot, \exp)$ — his theory of *pseudo-exponentiation* — he found that categoricity and the analysis of solution sets of exponential-polynomial systems required a number-theoretic input governing how algebraic subvarieties meet algebraic subgroups of tori. He distilled this into his **Conjecture on Intersection with Tori (CIT)** around 2002, recognizing it as a transcendence-flavored statement closely allied to Schanuel's conjecture. For Zilber the conjecture was not an isolated Diophantine curiosity but a structural necessity of the logical theory of $\exp$.

## Richard Pink

Richard Pink (born 1962) is an arithmetic geometer at ETH Zürich, known for work on Shimura varieties, Galois representations, Drinfeld modular varieties, and the arithmetic of function fields (including the Pink–Roessler approach and contributions around the Mumford–Tate and André–Oort circle). Where Zilber came from logic, Pink came from the heart of the Langlands–Shimura world.

In unpublished notes and a 2005 preprint, Pink proposed a single conjecture for **mixed Shimura varieties** designed to encompass the André–Oort conjecture (about special points), the Manin–Mumford conjecture (about torsion), and the Mordell–Lang conjecture (about finitely generated subgroups) as instances of one principle: *atypical intersections with special subvarieties are non-Zariski-dense*. Pink's formulation is the more general and is the version most often cited in Shimura-variety contexts; it makes precise the notions of *special subvariety*, *weakly special subvariety*, and *optimal/atypical component*.

## Historical root versus modern formulation

The "historical root" is twofold: the **Manin–Mumford conjecture** (Raynaud's theorem, 1983) and the **Mordell–Lang conjecture** (Faltings, Vojta, McQuillan), together with **André–Oort** (André's 1989 question, Oort's 1990s emphasis). Zilber–Pink subsumes all of these. The crucial conceptual reframing — due to Bombieri–Masser–Zannier and crystallized by Zannier — is the language of *anomalous and atypical subvarieties*, which converts the disparate classical statements into one uniform dimension count.

## Legacy

The conjecture has reorganized Diophantine geometry around o-minimality and functional transcendence. Zilber's insistence that logic governs arithmetic geometry was vindicated when the Pila–Zannier method (o-minimal point counting) became the field's central engine; his name now attaches to one of the most influential open problems linking model theory and number theory. Pink's framework remains the standard vocabulary in the Shimura-variety community. Both originators are active, and the conjecture is widely regarded as a guiding problem for the coming generation.
