# Top Mathematicians — The Hardy–Littlewood k-tuple Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | G. H. Hardy | 1877–1947 | originator | Co-posed the conjecture in *Partitio Numerorum III* (1923); singular-series heuristic via the circle method. |
| 2 | J. E. Littlewood | 1885–1977 | originator | Co-originator; deep analytic-number-theory machinery underpinning the asymptotic prediction. |
| 3 | Viggo Brun | 1885–1978 | breakthrough | Created sieve theory (1919); first correct order-of-magnitude bounds; convergence of the twin-prime reciprocal sum. |
| 4 | Atle Selberg | 1917–2007 | breakthrough | Selberg sieve giving sharp upper bounds; articulated the parity barrier that blocks lower bounds. |
| 5 | Paul T. Bateman | 1919–2012 | expositor | With Horn, generalized the asymptotic to polynomial systems (Bateman–Horn), unifying the conjecture. |
| 6 | Chen Jingrun | 1933–1996 | breakthrough | Chen's theorem (1973): infinitely many $p$ with $p+2 = P_2$ — the closest classical approach to the twin case. |
| 7 | Dan Goldston | b. 1954 | breakthrough | GPY method (Primes in Tuples); proved $\liminf (p_{n+1}-p_n)/\log p_n = 0$. |
| 8 | János Pintz | b. 1950 | frontier | Co-architect of GPY; ongoing work on gaps, clusters, and the structure of small gaps. |
| 9 | Yitang Zhang | b. 1955 | breakthrough | First unconditional bounded gaps (2013); equidistribution beyond level $1/2$ over smooth moduli. |
| 10 | James Maynard | b. 1987 | frontier | Multidimensional GPY sieve; many primes in admissible tuples; Fields Medal 2022. (Terence Tao co-developed the method independently.) |

## Notes

The active community sits at the intersection of sieve theory and the analytic distribution of primes. James Maynard (Oxford) and Terence Tao (UCLA) remain central, the latter having both co-invented the modern multidimensional sieve and coordinated the **Polymath8** collaboration that drove the prime gap below 246. Kannan Soundararajan, Ben Green, Kaisa Matomäki, and Maksym Radziwiłł contribute the surrounding technology (multiplicative functions, equidistribution, large/small gaps), while Dan Goldston, János Pintz, and Cem Yıldırım continue to refine the GPY framework. In the function-field direction, Will Sawin and Mark Shusterman have proved strong analogues over $\mathbb{F}_q[t]$. The defining feature of the present community is honesty about the **parity barrier**: every group works on the *qualitative shadow* (bounded gaps, many primes in tuples, almost-primes) while openly regarding the full asymptotic — for even a single admissible pair — as requiring a genuinely new idea that no current program has produced.
