# Attempts — The Graceful Tree Conjecture (Ringel–Kotzig)

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The GTC is unusual among famous open problems in that it has attracted many *partial* successes and a steady trickle of *flawed claimed proofs*, but no widely accepted general resolution. The pattern is instructive: constructions that work beautifully on structured trees break exactly where trees become "generic."

**Caterpillars and the diameter ladder (genuine partial results).** Rosa's early result that all caterpillars are graceful is the prototype near-universal success. The ladder then climbs by diameter: diameter $\le 3$ and $\le 4$ trees are graceful, and **Hrnčiar and Haviar (2001)** proved all trees of **diameter $\le 5$** are graceful via an explicit, intricate transfer of labels — a celebrated near-miss precisely because diameter $6$ already resists the same scheme. Each rung required a fresh, longer argument, dramatizing why a uniform proof is hard.

**Bermond's lobster conjecture (open sub-conjecture).** Bermond (1979) conjectured that all lobsters are graceful. Many subfamilies are settled (e.g., lobsters with specified leg/spine constraints, by Morgan, Ng, and others), and no counterexample exists, but the full lobster case remains open — a strict subproblem whose difficulty already approximates the general one.

**$\alpha$-labeling limits (a structural near-miss).** The drive to prove every tree has an $\alpha$-labeling — which would imply gracefulness with strong extra properties — failed for a concrete reason: there exist trees that are graceful but provably admit **no** $\alpha$-labeling. This was a productive negative result, redirecting effort away from the false hope that the stronger property is universal.

**Stanton–Zarnke and product constructions (real, with limits).** Stanton and Zarnke, and later Koh–Rogers–Tan, built graceful trees by product/composition operations. These are correct and generate infinite families, but the constructions presuppose factor structure that arbitrary trees lack, so they extend the catalog without closing the conjecture.

**The Ringel Conjecture resolution (a near-miss for GTC specifically).** **Montgomery, Pokrovskiy, and Sudakov (2020)** proved that $K_{2n+1}$ decomposes into copies of any given tree for all large $n$ — the original motivation for graceful labeling. It is frequently *misreported* as resolving the GTC. It does not: the decomposition is produced by probabilistic absorption that tolerates a controlled defect and does not yield a graceful labeling of the individual tree. The two statements remain logically distinct, and the GTC stays open.

**Claimed general proofs (disputed / not accepted).** Over the decades several manuscripts — mostly on the arXiv or in unrefereed venues — have announced a proof that *all* trees are graceful. None has been accepted by the community. A recurring, neutrally stated objection applies to many of them: the argument establishes a **range-relaxed** or near-graceful labeling (distinct vertex labels in some interval $[0,k]$ with $k>n-1$, or distinct edge differences without surjectivity onto $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$) and then asserts, without a valid argument, that the labels can be compressed back into exactly $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$. The compression step is precisely where the difficulty lives, and it is where these proofs fail. Other claimed proofs rely on an unjustified induction on tree size that does not preserve the exact edge-difference bijection when a vertex or branch is reattached. As of mid-2026 there is **no peer-reviewed, community-accepted proof** of the GTC, and authoritative surveys (Gallian's *Dynamic Survey of Graph Labeling*) list it as open. Readers should treat any "solved" announcement with caution until it appears in a refereed journal and is independently checked.

**Computational non-counterexamples.** Aldred–McKay (1998–99) verified all trees up to 27 vertices; later searches pushed the bound higher (Fang and others) with no counterexample. This is a strong empirical near-certainty but, of course, not a proof.
