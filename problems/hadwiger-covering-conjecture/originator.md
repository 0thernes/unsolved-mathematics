# Originator(s) — The Hadwiger Covering Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

The conjecture has no single author; it crystallized from four mathematicians working on related questions across the 1950s and 1960s, which is why it carries the joint name **Hadwiger–Levi** (covering/illumination) and **Gohberg–Markus** (covering by homothets).

## Hugo Hadwiger (1908–1981)

Born in Karlsruhe and raised in Bern, Hadwiger spent his career at the University of Bern, where he became a leading figure in integral geometry, convexity, and combinatorial geometry. He is best known for the **Hadwiger theorem** characterizing the continuous, rigid-motion-invariant valuations on convex bodies as linear combinations of intrinsic volumes — a cornerstone of integral geometry. His 1957 monograph-style writings on combinatorial geometry posed numerous extremal problems; among them the covering conjecture. Hadwiger's instinct was to look for the *worst* convex body for a covering task and to conjecture that the cube/parallelepiped — the body with the most "flat" structure and corners — is the unique extremizer. He also lent his name to the (unrelated) Hadwiger conjecture in graph coloring and to the Hadwiger–Nelson problem, making him one of the most prolific conjecture-posers of the century.

## Friedrich (Frigyes) Levi (1888–1966)

A German–Hungarian algebraist and geometer, Levi held a chair at Leipzig until he was dismissed under the Nazi regime in 1935; he emigrated and spent productive years in India (Calcutta), influencing a generation of Indian mathematicians, before returning to West Germany. Levi worked across group theory, ordered structures, and geometry. In the 1950s he studied the boundary-illumination of plane convex sets and established the planar case, observing that a convex body in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is covered by $4$ smaller homothets, with $4$ needed only for parallelograms. His framing — covering the boundary by "shadows" — is the conceptual seed of Boltyanski's illumination reformulation.

## Israel Gohberg (1928–2009) and Alexander Markus

Gohberg, born in Bessarabia (then Romania, now Moldova), became one of the most influential operator theorists of the century, founding (with collaborators) the modern theory of Toeplitz and integral operators and emigrating to Israel in 1974, where he built a major school at Tel Aviv University. Early in his career, working in Kishinev (Chișinău) with **Alexander Markus**, a fellow functional analyst, he considered the purely geometric question of covering a convex body by smaller homothetic copies. Their 1960 paper independently stated the $2^n$ conjecture in the covering-by-homothets language, which is why Eastern European literature attributes it to **Gohberg–Markus**. That two operator theorists landed on the same extremal-geometry conjecture as Hadwiger is a striking instance of convergent discovery.

## Boltyanski and the illumination bridge

Although not an "originator" of the statement, **Vladimir Boltyanski** (1925–2019) supplied the reformulation that organized all later work: he defined the **illumination number** $I(K)$ — the minimum number of directions illuminating the whole boundary — and proved $I(K) = c(K)$. This converted a covering problem into a question about boundary points and supporting hyperplanes, opening the door to the local/topological arguments that dominate the modern attack.

## Legacy

The conjecture sits at the crossroads of convex geometry, combinatorics, and the geometry of numbers. Its appeal is the contrast between a clean, dimension-uniform conjecture ($2^n$, parallelepipeds extremal) and the brutal difficulty of even the $3$-dimensional case. It has driven the development of illumination theory, belt-body theory, and probabilistic covering methods, and remains one of the headline open problems of discrete geometry.
