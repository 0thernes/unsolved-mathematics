# Top Mathematicians — The Collatz Conjecture

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Lothar Collatz | 1937– | originator | Formulated the $3n+1$ iteration problem (c. 1937); the conjecture and its name derive from him. |
| 2 | Stanisław Ulam | mid-20th c. | expositor | Circulated the problem at Los Alamos; one source of the "Ulam conjecture" name. |
| 3 | Shizuo Kakutani | mid-20th c. | expositor | Popularized the problem in the 1950s–60s; source of the "Syracuse / Kakutani" names. |
| 4 | Helmut Hasse | mid-20th c. | expositor | Lectured on the problem (the "Hasse algorithm"), spreading it through European departments. |
| 5 | Riho Terras | 1976 | breakthrough | Proved almost all $n$ have finite stopping time (natural density $1$) — first major theorem. |
| 6 | C. J. Everett | 1977 | breakthrough | Independent almost-all stopping-time result; sharpened the density framework. |
| 7 | Ray P. Steiner | 1977 | breakthrough | Excluded nontrivial $1$-cycles using transcendence/continued-fraction methods. |
| 8 | Jeffrey C. Lagarias | 1985– | expositor | Definitive surveys and annotated bibliographies; with Krasikov, the $x^{0.84}$ counting bound. |
| 9 | Ilia Krasikov | 1989–2003 | frontier | Difference-inequality / nonlinear-programming bounds on the convergent set, refined with Lagarias. |
| 10 | Terence Tao | 2019 | frontier | "Almost all orbits attain almost bounded values" — the strongest unconditional result to date. |

## Notes

The Collatz conjecture has never had a single sustained research "school"; it is worked on episodically by number theorists, ergodic theorists, and combinatorialists, alongside an unusually large population of amateurs. The modern professional frontier is small but distinguished. **Terence Tao**'s 2019 entropy-and-density argument reinvigorated rigorous interest and connected the problem to additive combinatorics and probabilistic number theory. **Jeffrey Lagarias** remains the field's historian and organizer, his surveys and the edited volume *The Ultimate Challenge* (2010) functioning as the community's shared reference. Work on the **cycle problem** continues in the transcendence-theory tradition of Steiner, Simons, and de Weger (linear forms in logarithms, $\log_2 3$ approximation). Large-scale **computational verification** is carried forward by Tomás Oliveira e Silva and, more recently, David Bařina (verified to $2^{68}$ as of 2020). Adjacent communities in **arithmetic dynamics** (extending $T$ to $\mathbb{Z}_2$ and studying generalized Collatz maps) and in **theoretical computer science** (undecidability of generalized iterations, following Conway and Kurtz–Simon) supply the structural and negative results. The consensus, echoing Erdős, is that the problem sits beyond current methods and awaits a genuinely new idea.
