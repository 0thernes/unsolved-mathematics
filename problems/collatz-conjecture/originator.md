# Originator(s) — The Collatz Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Lothar Collatz (1910–1990)

**Lothar Collatz** was a German mathematician born on **6 July 1910** in Arnsberg, Westphalia, and died on **26 September 1990** in Varna, Bulgaria, while attending a conference. He is best remembered by the general public for the iteration problem that bears his name, but within mathematics his enduring reputation rests on **numerical analysis** and **applied mathematics**, fields he helped shape in the mid-twentieth century.

### Education and mathematical background

Collatz studied at several German universities — Greifswald, Munich, Göttingen, and Berlin — during the late 1920s and early 1930s, an era when Göttingen and Berlin were among the world's leading mathematical centers. He completed his doctorate in **1935** at the University of Berlin under **Alfred Klose** (within the orbit of the Berlin applied-mathematics school associated with Erhard Schmidt and Richard von Mises). His early work concerned eigenvalue problems for differential equations and the numerical treatment of boundary-value problems. After holding positions in the 1930s and 1940s, he became professor at the Technical University of Hannover and, from **1952**, founded and led the Institute for Applied Mathematics at the **University of Hamburg**, which he built into a major center.

### The ideas and motivation behind the problem

The $3n+1$ problem grew out of Collatz's broad fascination with **graph-theoretic and combinatorial iteration**. As a young researcher around **1937** he experimented with number-theoretic functions defined by simple arithmetic case-splits — maps that, when iterated, generate directed graphs on the integers. His interest was in the *structure of the resulting orbits and trees*: which functions produce a single connected tree rooted at a small cycle, and which fragment into many components. The map $n \mapsto n/2$ or $3n+1$ was one such experiment. Crucially, Collatz's motivation was not a grand conjecture about number theory but a concrete question about the **global shape of an iteration graph** — exactly the kind of object that later became central to arithmetic dynamics. He reportedly posed the problem during the **International Congress of Mathematicians in 1950** at Cambridge, Massachusetts, which helped seed its international spread.

Because he never published it, the precise statement entered the literature only through the people who carried it: Ulam, Kakutani, Hasse, Thwaites, and finally **Lagarias**, whose 1985 survey definitively attributed the problem to Collatz and standardized the name "Collatz conjecture." The historical root (a question about iteration graphs) and the modern formulation (convergence to $1$ for all positive integers) are the same statement viewed from two angles: the orbit structure of $T$ is a single tree rooted at the $1$-$4$-$2$ cycle **if and only if** the conjecture holds.

### Broader legacy

Collatz's lasting technical contributions lie far from the conjecture. He is the originator (with Ulrich Sinogowitz, 1957) of **spectral graph theory's foundational question** — bounding graph eigenvalues — and of the **Collatz–Wielandt formula**, a variational characterization of the Perron eigenvalue of a nonnegative matrix that remains standard in Perron–Frobenius theory and numerical linear algebra. His textbooks on the numerical treatment of differential equations and on functional analysis and numerical mathematics were widely influential in postwar Germany. He helped found the **Gesellschaft für Angewandte Mathematik und Mechanik (GAMM)** community of applied mathematicians and trained many doctoral students.

It is a notable irony that a mathematician of serious applied stature is most famous for a recreational-looking problem he never published. Collatz himself reportedly regarded it as a curiosity. Yet the problem's resistance to every modern technique — ergodic theory, $p$-adic analysis, additive combinatorics — has elevated it to a benchmark for the limits of our understanding of deterministic iteration, ensuring that his name is attached to one of mathematics' most stubborn open questions.
