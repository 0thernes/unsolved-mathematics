# Top Mathematicians — Legendre's Conjecture

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Adrien-Marie Legendre | 1752–1833 | originator | Stated the conjecture (prime between $n^2$ and $(n+1)^2$) within his work on prime distribution. |
| 2 | Pafnuty Chebyshev | 1821–1894 | breakthrough | Proved Bertrand's postulate, the weaker constant-ratio interval result that frames the problem. |
| 3 | Edmund Landau | 1877–1938 | expositor | Canonized the conjecture as one of four "unattackable" Landau problems at the 1912 ICM. |
| 4 | Guido Hoheisel | 1894–1968 | breakthrough | First proof of primes in $(x, x+x^\theta)$ with $\theta<1$ (1930), opening the short-interval program. |
| 5 | Albert E. Ingham | 1900–1967 | breakthrough | Zero-density methods; proved a prime between consecutive cubes (1937), the nearest fully proved analogue. |
| 6 | Harald Cramér | 1893–1985 | frontier | Conditional gap bound $O(\sqrt{x}\log x)$ under RH and the probabilistic Cramér model predicting tiny gaps. |
| 7 | Hugh L. Montgomery | b. 1944 | frontier | Zero-density and pair-correlation results that sharpen short-interval prime estimates. |
| 8 | Martin N. Huxley | b. 1944 | breakthrough | The long-standing $7/12$ exponent for prime-containing short intervals (1972/1979). |
| 9 | Glyn Harman | b. 1956 | frontier | Co-architect of the sieve underlying the $x^{0.525}$ record; author of the Harman-sieve monograph. |
| 10 | Roger C. Baker & János Pintz | contemporary | frontier | With Harman, proved the standing record: a prime in $(x, x+x^{0.525})$ for large $x$ (2001). |

## Notes

The active community around Legendre's conjecture is essentially the analytic number theory community working on **prime gaps and primes in short intervals**, since the conjecture is a uniform short-interval statement rather than an isolated problem. Central figures of the modern frontier include **Roger Baker, Glyn Harman, and János Pintz** (holders of the $0.525$ record), alongside **D. R. Heath-Brown** and **Henryk Iwaniec**, whose short-interval and sieve techniques underpin the record. The dramatic recent progress on *small* gaps — **Daniel Goldston, János Pintz, Cem Yıldırım** (GPY), **Yitang Zhang**, **James Maynard**, **Terence Tao**, and the **Polymath8** collaboration — reshaped the field's tools and personnel, though it bears on bounded gaps rather than the large-gap uniformity Legendre requires. On the zero-density side feeding short-interval bounds, the **Guth–Maynard (2024)** improvement has reinvigorated hope of nudging the exponent, and **Kannan Soundararajan** and collaborators contribute to the statistical understanding of gaps. No single research group "owns" the conjecture; progress is expected to come as a by-product of better zero-density estimates and sieve identities, so the relevant community is broad and overlaps heavily with work on the Riemann Hypothesis and the Hardy–Littlewood conjectures.
