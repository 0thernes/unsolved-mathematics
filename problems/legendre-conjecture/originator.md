# Originator(s) — Legendre's Conjecture

## Adrien-Marie Legendre (1752–1833)

Adrien-Marie Legendre was a French mathematician whose work spans number theory, analysis, celestial mechanics, geodesy, and the theory of elliptic functions. Born in Paris on 18 September 1752 to a comfortable family, he was educated at the Collège Mazarin (Collège des Quatre-Nations), where he received a strong grounding in mathematics and physics. His early independence — a modest inherited fortune allowed him to pursue research without immediate need of employment — let him devote himself to mathematics, though he later lost much of that fortune during the French Revolution.

Legendre held a series of academic and administrative posts: he taught at the École Militaire in Paris in the late 1770s and early 1780s, was elected to the Académie des Sciences in 1783, and over his career participated in major scientific projects of the era, including the geodetic survey connecting the Paris and Greenwich observatories and the metric-system commissions. He served on numerous committees and, like many savants of his generation, navigated the political upheavals of the Revolution and the Napoleonic and Restoration periods.

## Mathematical background and the route to the conjecture

Legendre's central contributions to number theory are collected in his treatise *Essai sur la théorie des nombres* (first edition 1798, with expanded later editions), one of the first comprehensive monographs devoted to the subject. In it he stated an early form of the law of **quadratic reciprocity** (later proved rigorously by Gauss), introduced the **Legendre symbol** $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$, and studied the representation of integers by quadratic forms.

It was within this program of understanding the distribution and structure of primes that Legendre proposed two famous empirical statements. First, his approximation $\pi(x) \approx \dfrac{x}{\log x - B}$ with $B \approx 1.08366$ — an early quantitative guess at the **prime-counting function**, ultimately superseded by the prime number theorem (Legendre's constant turned out to be wrong; the correct limiting value is $1$). Second, the interval statement now bearing his name: that a prime always lies between consecutive squares $n^2$ and $(n+1)^2$. Both arose from the same motivation — extracting regularity from the seemingly erratic appearance of primes by tabulating and fitting their density. The square-interval conjecture is best read as a bold *uniform* refinement of the density picture: if primes thin out only as slowly as $1/\log x$, then even the relatively short window $(n^2,(n+1)^2)$ should never be empty.

## Broader legacy

Legendre's name is attached to an extraordinary range of mathematical objects: **Legendre polynomials** (arising from his work on gravitational potential and the figure of planets), **Legendre's relation** for elliptic integrals, the **Legendre transformation** of convex analysis and physics, the three-squares and **Legendre's theorem** on ternary quadratic forms, and the Legendre symbol. His textbook *Éléments de géométrie* (1794) reshaped the teaching of geometry across Europe and America for generations. He also did foundational work, contemporaneously with Gauss, on the **method of least squares**.

His relationship with Gauss was marked by repeated priority disputes — over reciprocity, least squares, and the prime number theorem heuristic — in which Gauss's deeper or earlier (often unpublished) results overshadowed Legendre's published ones. Legendre died in Paris on 10 January 1833, somewhat embittered by these episodes, yet his influence is permanent.

The conjecture itself is a fitting emblem of his style: an elementary, precisely stated, empirically motivated assertion about primes that turned out to encode a depth of difficulty far beyond the tools of his time — and beyond ours. More than two centuries later, the modern formulation is identical to Legendre's, which is part of its enduring appeal.
