# Attempts — The Novikov Conjecture

_Notable attempts, near-misses, retracted proofs._

The Novikov Conjecture has a distinctive sociology: rather than a string of disputed "proofs of the whole," its history is a sequence of celebrated *partial* theorems, each enlarging the class of groups for which the statement holds. There is no widely circulated false claim of a full proof, and no counterexample. The drama lies in the boundary between Novikov and its stronger cousin, the **Baum–Connes Conjecture**.

## Foundational partial results (near the start)

**Lusztig (1972)** proved the conjecture for $\pi=\mathbb{Z}^n$ via families of elliptic operators parametrized by the torus of characters — the first substantive case beyond the trivial group, and the template for the analytic approach. **Mishchenko (1974)** introduced the symmetric signature in the $C^*$-algebra of the group and proved the conjecture for groups acting freely on contractible manifolds with non-positive curvature, importing index theory into the problem.

## The major breakthroughs

**Kasparov's** $KK$-theoretic Dirac–dual-Dirac machinery (1988) settled the conjecture for discrete subgroups of Lie groups; **Connes–Gromov–Moscovici (1990)** and **Connes–Moscovici (1990)** handled hyperbolic groups and Gromov-norm-bounded cohomology classes through cyclic cohomology. **Guoliang Yu's (2000)** theorem for groups coarsely embeddable into Hilbert space is the broadest unconditional result and is sometimes loosely described as having "almost settled" the conjecture, since the class of embeddable groups is vast. It is a genuine, fully-accepted theorem — not a near-miss — but it is *not* the whole conjecture, because of the expander obstruction below.

## The expander / monster-group frontier

The sharpest near-miss is conceptual. **Gromov's random groups** (announced ~2000, developed by Arzhantseva–Delzant, Ollivier, and others) contain coarsely embedded **expander graphs** and therefore do **not** coarsely embed into Hilbert space. This places them outside the reach of Yu's theorem. Crucially, **Higson, Lafforgue, and Skandalis (2002)** used these groups to construct genuine **counterexamples to the Baum–Connes Conjecture with coefficients**. This was a real refutation — but only of the *coefficient* (and Gromov-monster) form of Baum–Connes, **not** of the Novikov Conjecture. The Novikov Conjecture for monster groups remains **open**; no counterexample is known, and the index-theoretic obstruction that kills Baum–Connes-with-coefficients does not transfer to the homotopy-invariance statement. Stating this neutrally: the failure is of a stronger conjecture and of a specific method, and is sometimes misreported as casting doubt on Novikov itself — it does not.

## Other partial milestones

**Carlsson–Pedersen (1995)** proved split injectivity of the assembly map for groups with finite $B\pi$ admitting suitable boundary compactifications, via controlled topology. **Bartels–Lück–Reich (2008–2012)** proved the **Farrell–Jones Conjecture** — which *implies* Novikov — for hyperbolic and CAT(0) groups, later extended to mapping class groups, $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$, and lattices; each extension recovers Novikov for those groups as a corollary. **Guentner–Higson–Weinberger (2005)** established Novikov for all **linear groups** (subgroups of $\mathrm{GL}_n(K)$ over any field), an exceptionally broad result.

## Status of "claimed proofs"

No retracted full proof of the Novikov Conjecture is part of the standard record. The conjecture's honesty-preserving feature is precisely that it has resisted shortcut claims: every advance has been a theorem for an explicitly delimited class of groups, refereed and absorbed into the literature. The single most consequential "negative" result — Higson–Lafforgue–Skandalis — is a counterexample to a neighboring conjecture, and is correctly understood as evidence about the limits of the coarse-geometric method rather than about Novikov's truth.
