# Originator(s) — The Novikov Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## Sergei Petrovich Novikov (b. 1938)

Sergei Petrovich Novikov was born on 20 March 1938 in Gorky (now Nizhny Novgorod) into a family of distinguished mathematicians: his father Pyotr Novikov solved the word problem for groups (proving its undecidability), and his mother Lyudmila Keldysh was a noted topologist; his uncle Mstislav Keldysh was a leading applied mathematician and president of the Soviet Academy of Sciences. Novikov studied at Moscow State University, completing his doctorate in 1964 under Mikhail Postnikov. He rose rapidly within the Soviet topological school surrounding Pontryagin, Rokhlin, and Adyan.

Novikov's early work was in differential and algebraic topology. He computed cobordism rings, studied the Adams spectral sequence, and made deep contributions to the classification of smooth structures. His towering early achievement, for which he was awarded the **Fields Medal in 1970** (the first Soviet recipient), was the **proof of the topological invariance of the rational Pontryagin classes** (1965–66). This result was startling: Pontryagin classes are defined via the smooth (or at least PL) structure through characteristic classes of the tangent bundle, yet Novikov showed their rational values depend only on the underlying topological manifold. The proof was a tour de force combining the structure of the diffeomorphism group of the torus with surgery techniques.

## The ideas that led to the conjecture

The topological-invariance theorem immediately raised the question of *homotopy* invariance. Pontryagin classes are not homotopy invariants — fake projective spaces and exotic manifolds show this. But the Hirzebruch signature theorem expresses the signature, a homotopy invariant, as a specific characteristic number $\langle L(M),[M]\rangle$. Novikov asked: when one couples the $L$-class to the cohomology of the fundamental group via a classifying map $M\to B\pi$, which resulting "higher signatures" survive as homotopy invariants? He recognized that surgery theory, which he had helped build, predicts these higher signatures should be exactly the homotopy-invariant part of the rational Pontryagin information detectable through $\pi_1$. The conjecture is thus the natural "fundamental-group-twisted" extension of Thom's homotopy invariance of the signature.

A second motivation was Novikov's deep engagement with the algebraic structure of surgery obstruction groups (Wall groups) and his collaboration-by-influence with the emerging $C^*$-algebraic index theory of Mishchenko and Kasparov. Novikov saw that the higher signatures could be packaged as the image of a symmetric signature in the $L$-theory or $K$-theory of the group ring, transforming a manifold problem into one about assembly maps.

## Broader legacy

Beyond topology, Novikov reinvented himself as a mathematical physicist. In the 1970s and 1980s he developed the theory of soliton equations and finite-gap (algebro-geometric) integration, introduced the **Novikov ring** and **Morse–Novikov theory** for closed 1-forms, and contributed to the theory of the Korteweg–de Vries hierarchy. The **SUSY-Novikov inequalities**, Novikov homology, and the Novikov conjecture in $L$-theory all bear his name. He has held positions at the Steklov Institute, Moscow State University, and the University of Maryland. He received the Wolf Prize in 2005 and the Lobachevsky Prize, and remains one of the defining figures of twentieth-century geometry and topology.

The conjecture's modern formulation — rational injectivity of assembly maps — was sharpened by others (Wall, Mishchenko, Kasparov, Quinn, Ranicki), but its conceptual seed is precisely Novikov's insight that the signature's homotopy invariance ought to propagate through the fundamental group. That single observation organized half a century of subsequent work in geometric topology, operator $K$-theory, and coarse geometry.
