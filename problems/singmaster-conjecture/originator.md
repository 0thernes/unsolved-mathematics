# Originator(s) — Singmaster's Conjecture

_Biography, background, and the ideas that led here._

## David Singmaster (1938–2023)

David Breyer Singmaster was an American-British mathematician, recreational-mathematics polymath, and historian of mathematical puzzles. Born in the United States in 1938, he took his doctorate in number theory at the University of California, Berkeley, and spent most of his career as a professor of mathematics at South Bank Polytechnic (later London South Bank University) in London. He died in February 2023.

Singmaster's mathematical persona straddled two worlds that he treated as one: serious number theory and the history and analysis of mathematical recreations. To the general public he is best known as the man who, in the early 1980s, popularized and systematized the solution of the Rubik's Cube. His booklet *Notes on Rubik's Magic Cube* (1979–1981) introduced the move-sequence notation — F, B, U, D, L, R for the six faces — that remains the worldwide standard ("Singmaster notation"). He also coined or codified terminology and documented the cube's group-theoretic structure for a mass audience, turning a toy into a vehicle for teaching permutation groups.

## Mathematical background and the idea behind the conjecture

Singmaster's research instincts were those of an elementary number theorist with an exceptional eye for the curious. His most cited contribution to pure mathematics is the conjecture that bears his name, posed in 1971 in a short note titled "How often does an integer occur as a binomial coefficient?" (published in the *American Mathematical Monthly* in 1971). The question is quintessentially Singmaster: it requires no machinery to state, can be illustrated to a schoolchild via Pascal's triangle, and yet sits on top of deep Diophantine geometry.

The motivation was direct experimental observation. Scanning Pascal's triangle, Singmaster noticed that while most values appear only the "forced" number of times, a handful recur surprisingly often. He identified the parametric Fibonacci family that yields $N(a) \ge 6$ infinitely often, and singled out $3003$ as the smallest sextuple occurrence. The natural follow-up — is there any ceiling at all? — became the conjecture. He was careful to frame it as an upper-bound question $N(a) = O(1)$, not a claim about the exact constant; indeed he conjectured the true maximum might be quite small (Erdős later guessed $8$).

## Legacy and the modern formulation

The conjecture root has not changed since 1971: the statement is exactly Singmaster's. What has changed is the *framing* under which mathematicians attack it. Abbott, Erdős, and Hanson (1974) reinterpreted it as a problem about counting integral points on a family of low-degree curves, which subsequently brought Siegel's theorem, Baker's effective methods, and Faltings's theorem to bear, and most recently the analytic-number-theory machinery of Matomäki–Radziwiłł–Shao–Tao–Teräväinen. Singmaster's original elementary statement and these geometric/analytic reformulations are equivalent; the geometric language is the one in which partial results are now proved.

Beyond this single problem, Singmaster's broader legacy is as a bridge figure. He maintained an enormous annotated bibliography, *Sources in Recreational Mathematics*, tracing puzzles across centuries and cultures; he edited and contributed to the recreational-mathematics literature for decades; and through the Rubik's Cube he introduced a generation to group theory. His name endures both in cube notation and in a conjecture that, half a century on, remains genuinely open — a fitting monument to a mathematician who specialized in deceptively simple questions.
