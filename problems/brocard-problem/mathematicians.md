# Top Mathematicians — Brocard's Problem

_Ten figures who shaped the problem, past and present._

| # | Name | Era | Role | Key contribution |
|---|------|-----|------|------------------|
| 1 | Henri Brocard | 19th–20th c. | originator | Posed the question $n!+1=m^2$ (1876, restated 1885); identified $n=4,5,7$. |
| 2 | Srinivasa Ramanujan | early 20th c. | originator | Independently posed the same question (Question 469, JIMS, 1913). |
| 3 | Richard Obláth | early–mid 20th c. | frontier | Studied $n!+1=m^k$ and related factorial–power Diophantine equations. |
| 4 | Andrzej Dąbrowski | modern | breakthrough | Proved $n!+A=m^2$ has finitely many solutions for every non-square $A$ (1996). |
| 5 | Bruce C. Berndt | modern | frontier | Co-author of the definitive computational search; Ramanujan-notebook scholar. |
| 6 | William F. Galway | modern | frontier | Co-author (with Berndt, 2000) verifying no Brown numbers for $n\le 10^9$. |
| 7 | Marius Overholt | modern | frontier | Showed a weak form of the abc conjecture implies Brocard's conjecture. |
| 8 | Richard K. Guy | modern | expositor | Catalogued the problem in *Unsolved Problems in Number Theory*, fixing its canonical status. |
| 9 | Paul Erdős | 20th c. | frontier | Drove the surrounding factorial-Diophantine program ($n!+m!=k!$, etc.) that frames the problem. |
| 10 | G. H. Hardy | early 20th c. | expositor | As editor of Ramanujan's *Collected Papers*, preserved and disseminated Question 469. |

## Notes

There is no large, dedicated "Brocard community" — the problem is a single hard equation rather than a research program, so it is worked on intermittently by number theorists at the boundary of Diophantine analysis, transcendence theory, and computational number theory. The most active threads today are (i) **computational verification**, where ongoing searches (in the lineage of Berndt–Galway, with contributions from researchers such as Daniel Gabric and various distributed-computing efforts) keep extending the no-solution range; (ii) the **abc-conditional / Diophantine-approximation** circle, where the problem appears as a standard corollary in surveys of abc consequences and is revisited whenever effective methods advance; and (iii) **generalization specialists** following Dąbrowski, who probe $n!+A=m^2$, $n!+1=m^k$, and Erdős–Obláth-type equations hoping a technique will transfer back to the recalcitrant $A=1$ case. The realistic expectation in the community is that unconditional resolution awaits either a proof of abc (which would settle finiteness immediately) or a genuinely new idea for injecting effective structure into the factorial — neither of which is on the near horizon.
